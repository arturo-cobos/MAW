Task Descriptor: Harvest knowledge data and reusable assets (IMSP240 and IMSP711)
Describes the identification, harvesting and submission of intellectual capital at the time of project closure. This also includes the lessons learned and other reusable asset collection and its submission in knowledge repositories.

Main Description

This task involves the following:

  • Collect, compute and consolidate end of project measurement data that was developed throughout the project lifecycle and store it in the organization's measurements repository.   This includes end of project measures defined in the project quality plan, and common causes found as part of root cause analysis and defect prevention activities.
  • Collect and submit performance data (estimates and actual data) in designated repository. 
  • Harvest intellectual property or assets. During the project and especially at the end of the project, the Project Manager and Project team will identify, cleanse and then submit reusable artifacts to Knowledgeview (the Global Business Services intellectual capital repository).   Examples of reusable artifacts include:
    • Project deliverables, including proposals, work plans, reports, meeting agendas, presentations, designs, instructional materials and process maps
    • Tools used to implement a process such as checklists, surveys, questionnaires, models, templates, and automated tool capabilities
    • Software code, class libraries, models and architectures
    • Project profiles and lessons learned
  • Collect and documenting lessons learned:
    • Identify the experience gained and lessons learned from the project, to be passed on to future projects and incorporated into existing processes, standards, procedures, methodology and tools.
    • Report the success story to the repositories such as IBM Rational Asset Manager (iRAM), if reusable assets were applied within the project which resulted in higher quality output (faster, cheaper, smarter, better due to the usage of proven assets)
    • If productivity for the project varies significantly from the established trend, review and document the key contributing factors.
Steps
Review and analyze the stakeholder feedback
Review and analyze project end-of-project feedback received from all sources.
Collect, compute and consolidate end-of project measurement data
Collect, compute and consolidate end-of-project measurement data that was developed throughout the project lifecycle and store it in the organization's measurements repository, except for estimation data which should be summarized and cross referenced in the project evaluation report. This includes end of project measures defined in the project quality plan, and common causes found as part of root cause analysis and defect prevention activities.
Collect project estimates and actual data
Collect project estimates and actual data. This involves following activities:
  • Gather data from the baselined estimates captured in the estimation reports
  • Gather actual values for the entire project - effort, resource, schedule and cost. Required data to capture will depend on project attributes such as project type, work type and phases undertaken, as summarized in the estimation checkpoint report
  • Determine the actual variance for the project
  • Add the project closing actual to the final estimation checkpoint report
Provide the estimation factor data to Subject Matter Expert (Estimating)
  • Add the project closing actual estimation factor data to the final data point in the committed worksheet of the estimation factors report
  • Consolidate final numbers of the estimation factors used in the estimating approaches undertaken (for example number of function points developed, number of reports implemented), in the estimation factors report
Run a review to confirm the availability of project estimation data
Confirm that the project data is available for the following:
  • Estimate from the estimation reports and estimating considerations checklists
  • Actual data from the estimation checkpoint report
  • Estimation factors from the estimation factors report
  • Actual variance from the estimation checkpoint report
  • Governance data from the evidence of governance reports:
    • independent technical review report
    • independent review report
    • price review report
    • estimation checkpoint review report
    • confirm project data is appropriate review report
  • Check for completeness and, if incomplete, identifies actions to acquire the missing data (if this is not possible, documents why the data is missing, and makes a note to identify this during the project 'lessons learned' workshop)
  • Check the data for accuracy and quality
  • Put in place appropriate actions to address any concerns raised by the data checks

Document the results of the review in the appropriate review report, signed off by an Independent Subject Matter Expert (Estimating)

Organize a review of estimation work products
Organize a review of estimation work products in accordance with the Work Product Inspections Procedure (IMSP530), preferably with an Independent Subject Matter Expert (Estimating) as the Inspector

The criteria are:

  • All previous estimation steps have been completed satisfactorily
  • All project data is available and ready for final reporting, including justification of variance between estimated and actual results achieved
  • The project data and information being reported is a true and accurate reflection of what happened in the project

Prepare input to project lessons learned

Assess and document lessons learned
Assess and document lessons learned:
  • Identify the experience gained and lessons learned from the project, to be passed on to future projects and incorporated into existing processes, standards, procedures, methodology and tools
  • Report the success story to the repositories such as IBM Rational Asset Manager (iRAM), if reusable assets were applied within the project which resulted in higher quality output (faster, cheaper, smarter, better due to the usage of proven assets)

If productivity for the project varies significantly from the established trend, review and document the key contributing factors

File project estimate and actual data
File project estimate and actual data values and information in historical repository
Review and identify intellectual capital candidates
Review and identify work products produced by the project that may be a candidate for inclusion in an intellectual capital repository. Non-deliverables can also be submitted as intellectual capital

Examples of potential intellectual capital includes:

  • Project deliverables, including proposals, work plans, reports, meeting agendas, presentations, designs, code, instructional materials and process maps
  • Tools used to implement a process such as checklists, surveys, questionnaires, models, templates, and automated tool capabilities
  • Software code, class libraries, models, and architectures
  • Project profiles and lessons learned
Identify appropriate intellectual capital repository
Identify appropriate intellectual capital repository
Determine existence of similar work products
Check the relevant intellectual capital repositories to determine if intellectual capital similar to the project proposed asset exists

If so, compare the existing intellectual capital with the proposed asset, to determine whether it will add value to the intellectual capital repository

Request for approval to proceed
Request approval from the Project Manager to proceed with the preparation of the proposed intellectual capital. When requesting this approval, the submitter needs to demonstrate the asset's suitability for inclusion in an intellectual capital repository so as to justify the time and effort required to prepare the asset for review and submission

At a minimum, the Project Manager must perform initial evaluation of the proposal. As appropriate, a Process Improvement Team Member can also be involved

Prepare the proposed intellectual capital for submission
Prepare the asset for submission, in accordance with requirements specified by relevant intellectual capital repository

The submitter may be required to:

  • Remove any confidential information from the asset, prior to submission, such as pricing information and commercially sensitive information to the client or the organization
  • Provide summary information
  • Remove all references to client names and application names
  • Save attachments in a standard operating tool or format
  • Fill in a submission form to accompany the asset

The submitter is responsible for ensuring that the submission can legally and ethically be contributed to the relevant intellectual capital. For example a document cannot be submitted to an intellectual capital if the client is the owner of that document

Organize a review of the proposed intellectual capital
Organize a review of the asset in accordance with the Work Product Inspections Procedure (IMSP530) to confirm that it meets the applicable criteria for submission to the relevant intellectual capital repository

Implement the relevant suggestions originating from the review

Submit the intellectual capital
Submit the intellectual capital and necessary supporting material to the relevant intellectual capital repository

The administrator of the intellectual capital repository will evaluate and publish the intellectual capital as appropriate. This evaluation may involve an additional review of the asset, and will result in the acceptance or rejection of the asset for publishing on the intellectual capital repository

Note: This activity may be performed prior to the end of the project as appropriate
