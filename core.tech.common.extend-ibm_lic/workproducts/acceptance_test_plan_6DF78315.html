Artifact: Acceptance Test Plan (ART 0657)
This work product describes the steps the customer will use to verify that the constructed system satisfies the stated requirements.
Domains: Test
Work Product Kinds: Plan
Extends: Test Plan
Purpose

The purpose of the Acceptance Test Plan is:

  • To gain commitment from the development and the client teams to the set of acceptance tests.
  • To identify and allocate all the resources and test components required to execute the set of acceptance tests and processes.
  • To make explicit the sign-off procedure and forms that will indicate acceptance.
  • To articulate what needs to be demonstrated so that the system can be accepted.
  • To ensure that the Acceptance Testing activities appropriately address the testing required to demonstrate that the system meets the business and technical requirements specified for it, and that it is in an acceptable state for operational use.

The Acceptance Test Plan is an essential communication vehicle to specify the scope and focus of the criteria and the process that will be used to determine the acceptance of the deliverable.

Description
Main DescriptionThe Acceptance Test Plan is one of the detailed testing plans and corresponds to the detailed level of testing for acceptance of the system or solution by, or on behalf of, the client. The plan describes clearly and fully how to set up the test and the acceptable system behaviors in response to the test. These behaviors are stated so that it will be obvious whether or not the system behaved according to specification.
 
The contract with the customer should include a clause stating that the system will be accepted formally if all acceptance tests are successful. If there is no direct customer, the contract and the acceptance test plan will be informal, but their existence is still relevant.
 
Traditional nomenclature for this plan is User Acceptance Test Plan, but this title is not appropriate since the acceptance test may or may not be user driven. Where there is user-based acceptance testing, this plan will be used, but the key to this plan is defining who will officially sign-off the project result, and what criteria and verification/validation process will be used. Operations staff, sub-contractors, end-users, managers, etc., may perform acceptance activities, as agreed to and defined in this plan.
 
A system or solution is validated and verified during its development for two purposes. One is to see that the system was built right and the other is to prove that the right system was built. System development methodology provides many safeguards to ensure these expectations are fulfilled. The key to both is the involvement of the client from the beginning of the project life cycle and continuous management of client expectations throughout the project.
 
Effective client involvement should be seen in early project deliverables (such as plan, requirements, specifications, prototype development, etc.) and the Acceptance Test Plan. It provides the project team with essential communication opportunities to understand what is required, and how the solution will be judged and its success measured. This client involvement also allows the project team to manage the client expectations by early identification of conflicting or unclear requirements, business or technical risk areas, and key success factors. The Acceptance Test Plan, developed in conjunction with the client, is the essence of this understanding and commitment by all parties to the quality level of the solution.
 
The Acceptance Test Plan is an integral part of the requirements documentation and, as such, is agreed upon by both parties early in the project. If requirements change during the project, then the change process should be invoked, and the changes cascade to the requirements statement and Acceptance Test Plan as negotiated.

Key Considerations

Requirements documents are often long. Verifying that a given system satisfies every documented requirement can take a very long time, and can be open to interpretation, particularly if there are any hidden agendas on either side. Furthermore, the burden of proof that the requirements are satisfied often lies solely with the development team.

Proving correctness is notoriously difficult. For example, the requirements document may state that a particular interface should use a particular protocol. Proving that the code correctly generates the protocol is a very hard and costly task. Running tests is the only alternative, but which tests and how many? The scope for disagreement is considerable. Bearing in mind that disagreement might mean the lack of contracted payment, the project team must ensure that this situation does not arise.

The Use Case Model work product is very useful, since it provides a basis for identifying objects, object functionality, interaction, and interfaces. It is also oriented to the emerging object-oriented and e-business models, and therefore facilitates expression of new concepts for both business and technology in a structured way.

The use of structured joint sessions, based on JAD, to develop, test, and approve the plan has proven very effective in developing understanding and buy-in while improving the quality of not only the acceptance process, but also the project outcomes. The discussion around the acceptance often clarifies thinking for both clients and developers. It also gives testers key insights for the overall test efforts.

It is important to cover both types of acceptance criteria in the plan, business functions, and performance/operational criteria, unless there are specific reasons not to; e.g., the test is for performance only. Even bringing up both topics may uncover hidden requirements that have not been articulated or understood.

In addition to this acceptance test plan, there will specifications that describe the design, execution plans that help run the tests, and the test results and test reports that capture the outcome of acceptance testing.

Tailoring
Impact of not having

Higher level plans for testing, such as a master test plan, are not executable, as they do not have detailed level activities or tasks. Without task level details, resources and time schedules to carry out those tasks, it will be difficult, if not impossible, to execute, track, manage and control them. Different levels of tests focus on different areas of functionality and risk, and different organizational units may own responsibility. Thus without detailed test plans organized around your main test cycles or iterations, it will be difficult for different organizational units to plan, prepare, execute and report testing without actually increasing risk. Eliminating detailed test plans, especially for large projects, will virtually guarantee project failure.

The creation of detailed test plans is also the activity that is most often underestimated in projects. It must start as soon as pre-requisites are available to ensure that testing requirements and dependencies are identified early and are addressed appropriately. Delaying the start of this activity often leads to project schedule overruns and increased business or technical risks.

The Acceptance Test Plan is a key client communication artifact. Once the contract has been signed, however, the negotiating power of the development team is considerably reduced, and there is no compelling reason for the customer to commit to the plan at all. Without its negotiated success criteria, there are significantly greater opportunities for misunderstandings. The high priority risks may not be addressed, or may be discovered late in the development process. This situation often leads to reduced project quality and client satisfaction.

If there is no Acceptance Test Plan, there should be some attempt to verbalize, negotiate, document, and communicate the acceptance criteria with key stakeholders as early as possible in the project, and to frequently review them formally or informally throughout the project.

Reasons for not needing

If the main factors driving the granularity of your test plans are test levels and test types, then not all these factors are relevant during the duration of a project and therefore some specialized test plans will not be developed or will be limited to just a few test cycles or iterations. In cases where projects are small, with well-defined scope and structure, and all project members are co-located or work closely together, there may be one joint test plan that covers the high level plan and all detail levels of testing.

In small, low risk, or very automated testing projects, or projects without a direct customer, there may be less need for formal acceptance testing. However, some sort of sign-off document, with acceptance criteria agreed to in the early phases of the project, should not be omitted.
Representation Options

As an alternative to formal documentation, you might choose to record the elements of this artifact as a set of informal planning notes, possibly maintained on an Intranet Web site or whiteboard readily visible to and accessible by, the test team.

Optionally, some aspects of this artifact can be presented appropriately as enclosures within other plans, rather than as separate discrete artifacts.
