Artifact: IT Services Strategy (OPS 308)
The IT Services Strategy work product describes the desired future strategic set of IT services and how they would be provided to satisfy the business' requirements.
Domains: IT Management & Monitoring
Work Product Kinds: Strategy
Purpose

The purpose of the IT Services Strategy is to identify the essential IT services that are required to support the future business model and to describe how they will be provided. It is necessary to understand the services that are to be provided in order to subsequently determine what IT capabilities are required and how those capabilities should be enabled or sourced.   

Therefore this work product is necessary to subsequently identify the enabling processes, organization and technology that must be designed and implemented in order to construct and deliver the required IT services.

From the customer’s perspective, the service definitions provide the service customer with the basis for describing the results they expect and the levels of performance they require. From the corresponding service provider’s perspective, they provide the basis for detailing the conditions under which the service will be provided and the characteristics of the enabling IT processes, organization and tools that need to be in place.

Description
Main Description

The IT Services Strategy work product describes the desired future strategic set or portfolio of IT services that must be provided to satisfy the business' requirements and describes the individual services making up that future service portfolio or service model.

An IT service is a specific IT function or output, performed on behalf of or in support of a consumer, that provides measurable value. Services are a basis for doing business with the consumer of IT services, require an understanding of their needs, and are delivered through a series of implemented activities.

An IT service brings together elements of process, organization and technology. The detailed service definitions show:

  • The activities, from one or more processes, that are executed in delivering the service;
  • The service roles or participants responsible for executing those activities;
  • The tools or information technology used to automate the execution of activities and deliver their results.

The IT services strategy describes the set of services that are to be provided and the way in which those services are grouped or categorized. The scope and granularity of the service model will depend greatly on the perspective being taken and the context within which the service model and service definitions are being created. The level of detail necessary in the service definition will vary considerably depending on the audience for that description and the purpose for which it is being created. For example, the IT Service model and service definitions used by a CIO or business executive to facilitate analysis of outsourcing options will be at a much higher level than the service definitions required by the first line IT manager responsible for defining and implementing service workflows.

Brief Outline

The IT Services Strategy may have several components, each of which may use a different notation.

The IT services portfolio will generally be a textual document with supporting diagrams. It will provide a high level model of the service groupings or categories. It may perhaps suggest a hierarchy of service groups, services and service elements depending on the scope and objectives of the engagement. Typically, a context diagram that shows the relationships between the service groups or the hierarchy of services will accompany the textual description of the model.

For the IT services which are identified as the focus of the project a textual service definition will be produced. There are a series of elements suggested below which may go into the service definition. Depending on the project scope and objectives it may not be necessary to define all these attributes of the required IT services. In subsequent stages of the design and implementation life cycle additional work products will be produced which will decompose the service portfolio and specify the services in greater detail.

There may sometimes be a series a of interim supporting outputs that document the processes and criteria that were used to develop the strategic IT service portfolio and to evaluate and select between alternate solution approaches.

From a consumer's perspective, the definition of a service should include:

  • Name – A noun/verb format is suggested, both to provide focus on the result (the noun) desired by the consumer, and to distinguish it from a functional process (which has a verb/noun format). Where possible, the name should reflect the service scope.
  • Description
  • Scope
    • In scope
    • Out of scope
    • Triggers (begins with)
  • Assumptions
  • Policies
  • Principles
  • Identification of responsibilities
    • Consumers
    • Business Owner
    • Contacts
  • Success Criteria
  • Levels of Delivery
    • Quality Items
    • Timing (when, how often)
    • Availability
    • Performance
    • Security (Integrity and Confidentiality)
    • Business Volumes
  • Deliverable/product

Key Considerations

The IT Services Strategy could potentially lead to significant changes to the set of IT services provided and the way in which they are sourced and delivered. As a consequence it could have far-reaching implications for the IT organization, it's skills, processes and tools.

It is therefore important that the work product be reviewed and accepted by the client sponsor. The recommendations and their implications should be fully discussed, understood and signed off. The “strategic fit” of the recommended services strategy and solution approach can often be validated by:

  • Deriving from the service definitions the characteristics of the processes, organization and tools necessary to enable creation and delivery of the required services.
  • Deriving from the IT Services Assessment an indication of the maturity of the enablers and analyzing the gap between the current and required states.
  • Assessing the ability of the client IT organization to make this required transformation.

If it is suspected that the strategic IT services portfolio will entail a substantial transformation of the client’s current IT capabilities, then all the necessary planning, design and implementation activities should be called out at this stage and reflected in the transition plan.

Defining IT Services

The level of granularity at which the IT services are defined will depend upon the scope and objectives of the engagement.
The perspective one takes when discussing services has a profound impact on the result. Different roles in the discussion provide different perspectives. One can take the provider’s perspective, for example, and get one view. If, however, one takes the customer (or consumer) view, one can get an entirely different perspective on the topic of services. Neither is right or wrong, but definitely yield different results. Further complicating the perspective issue is the fact that it isn't always easy to determine the role of customer/provider, and the roles can change.

This leads into the second important issue, context. The only means to resolve the specific question of customer/provider is to put the discussion in some context. For example, an IT organization can supply a 'service' to a business unit, which then bundles that capability with skills / resources of their own to supply a service to an external consumer. From the perspective of the IT organization, they are supply a complete service to the business unit. From the business unit's perspective, the IT component is simply one element in a service they supply to the consumer. In addition, from the consumer's perspective, the 'service' supplied by the business unit is one element in a complete range of services provided by the enterprise.

The last issue, scale, is just as confusing. It is difficult to develop a consistent means to identify a complete service, versus some fragment of a service - what might be called a service element, or component. The difficulty is that, once again, this changes with perspective and context. In the world of tangible products, one company might manufacture a complete assembly, made up of subassemblies. This complete 'assembly,' however, is viewed by another manufacturer as a 'subassembly' of an even larger product. So, is the original product an assembly or subassembly? That depends on your client and their perspective.


Services provide yet one more viewpoint from which to focus on that, which is important to the successful delivery of IT, capability, in a given context. A service perspective does not replace, or compete, or conflict with other enablers such as functional processes, organization or tools. One can’t implement a service without consideration of the process activities, organization elements and tools required to do so.

What is most important is the view of our customers/clients, WHICH WILL CHANGE BASED ON THEIR CONTEXT AND PERSPECTIVE. While we need to demonstrate some measure of leadership and understanding of the topic, more importantly, we need to be adaptable enough to alter our viewpoint of the subject of services to fit that of our clients.  

Strategy

The term “strategy” can be interpreted in many different ways and frequently the client will have a slightly different view of what constitutes a strategy. There will generally be agreement though that a strategy says something about the client’s desired future strategic position, the way they will operate, and the way they will achieve that position.

In agreeing the scope of “strategy” with the client, it may be useful to think of “the strategy” as a deliverable which may encompass several components according to the wishes of the client. From an IT services perspective these could include:

  • (IT) future state vision
  • (IT) strategic business scenarios
  • IT services strategy
  • principles, policies and guidelines
  • IT infrastructure management model
  • IT services sourcing decisions
  • IT services transition plan  

The IT Services Strategy should also be validated against other related work products to ensure that they are consistent and that the strategy does indeed address the business requirements, moves the client towards their desired strategic vision and is aligned with the client’s governance model. In particular, the IT Services Strategy should be validated against:

  • the client’s future state vision
  • IT principles, policies and guidelines
  • current IT assessment
  • the client’s technology model
Tailoring
Impact of not havingThe IT service definitions provide the basis for the establishing the relationship between IT and the business - the consumer of IT services. Without this work product it will be much more difficult to adequately describe and understand the IT services that the business needs or expects and the levels of performance which are required. Consequently, it will be more difficult to establish an agreement on the conditions under which the services are to be provided and on the responsibilities of the involved parties.
Reasons for not needingThis work product will not be required if the client already has an appropriate service model and definitions or agreements surrounding the IT services within the scope of the engagement.
