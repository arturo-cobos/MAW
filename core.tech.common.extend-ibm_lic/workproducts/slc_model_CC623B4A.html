Artifact: Service Level Characteristic Model (ART 0527)
This artifact documents the analysis, and subsequent conclusions, of a study to assess the ability of an architecture to meet the required service level characteristics.
Domains: Operational
Work Product Kinds: Assessment
Purpose

The purpose of this artifact therefore is to:

  • Validate the configuration of a proposed system to ensure it meets the required service level characterisics.
  • Allow an analysis of a broad range of alternative technical solutions to the same general problem to understand how these affect the systems service level characterisics.
  • Analyze the impact on the systems service level characterisics of alternative workload scenarios on a given configuration.
  • Assess the impact of changes or uncertainty in the technological capabilities of the underlying computer system (e.g. different parametric costs).
  • Provide an understanding of how an existing system will perform when exposed to new conditions or usage and what affect these will have on the system’s service level characterisics.

Description
Main Description

A service level characteristic is a runtime quality exhibited by a system. The main service level characteristics, which are important when defining an IT system architecture, are: performance and capacity, availability and security.

The main service level characteristics of an IT based business solution include:

  • Performance and capacity (e.g. end user response times, business volume throughput rates, system sizing and utilization levels).
  • Availability (e.g. mean time between failure for all, or part, of the system, disaster recovery mechanisms).
  • Security (e.g. response to systematic attempts to break in).

A service level characteristic model consists of:

  • A model for a particular SLC (i.e. performance and capacity, availability or security) based on one or more architecture models or configurations.  A model may be a relatively simple spreadsheet or may use a specialized modeling tool.
  • The results of “running” the model under a range of input conditions.
  • An analysis of the results and recommendations on what architecture model or configuration to use.
Brief OutlineAs well as presenting, discussing and analyzing the results taken from its various input sources, this artifact typically includes a description of the approach taken to the analysis, any assumptions and exclusions, and recommendations on the various system configuration options available.

Key Considerations

The following advice and guidance should be considered when developing this artifact:

  • Maintain a clear distinction between:
    • Model Data – Such as
      • Business volumes (for performance modeling) or “usage profiles (for availability modeling) for all involved users, appropriate business transactions and other events.
      • Technical transactions, events and interactions associated with each business transaction, dependent on system design.
      • Parametric costs , dependent on technology, for each technical transaction, event or interaction.
    • Results - From each “model run” using a specific combination of data.
    • Analysis - Comparing, contrasting and drawing conclusions from multiple sets of results.
  • Developing a service level characteristic model is closely tied to the development of the various architecture models that describe the structure of the system as well as its dynamic behaviour.
  • In a “top down” bespoke application development project, it is likely a service level characteristic model will be initially based on high-level business scenarios, with coarse grained data and inaccurate or imprecise architecture models.  Then, as the project evolves, and in particular as the detail and confidence in the architecture models grows, so the service level characteristic model will become more sophisticated and accurate.
  • The service level characteristic model can also be used as a means of matching business requirements to what is possible and “affordable”– particularly those that relate to behavior (such as response times and availability profiles), by documenting how closely different architectures, with different costs, meet the business requirements and thereby facilitating compromise.
  • Validation of a service level characteristic model should be by peer review, usually with a specialist in the appropriate service level characteristic field (e.g. a Performance Architect or Security Specialist).  This will include a review of the planned analysis approach, as well as a review of the results and conclusions from that analysis.
  • A service level characteristic model can help in the development of any of the views of the architecture models, whether from one view to another, or from “scratch”.  It is probably at its most valuable however when working out the best detailed configuration of hardware and systems software (including systems management), and operational systems management processes.
Tailoring
Impact of not having

Without a careful analysis of the various architectural options potentially available for an IT based business solution, there is an enhanced risk of:

  • Unacceptable system behavior.  If there is insufficient or unreliable data to support infrastructure design decisions, and no service level characteristic analysis is performed, there is a high risk that the service level characteristics of the solution will not be acceptable when in operational use.
  • Over-engineering resources.  In the absence of any analysis (or other sound service level characteristic data), designers are likely to over-engineer resources to compensate for the risk of inadequate operational characteristics.  This is likely to lead to a higher than required cost.
Reasons for not needingThe content of this artifact can never be ignored, although there is not always the need for a separate and distinct artifact.  The specific development of this artifact depends on project risks and the availability of appropriate service level characteristic information.  Circumstances, which may indicate a formal service level characteristic model is not required, would be when there is sufficient information from previous projects, reference sites or vendors to make the correct infrastructure design decisions.
Representation OptionsThis artifact is usually built using a spreadsheet with supporting text and tables.  Alternatively specialized performance or availability modeling tools may be used.
