Artifact: Risk Management Plan (ENG 352)
The collection of risk responses for identified project risks.
Domains: Project Management
Work Product Kinds: Plan
Purpose

The purpose of the Risk Management Plan is to document all the plans associated with a risk or set of related risks.

  • Overall risk management strategy for the project.
  • Risk mitigation plans identify the activities that have been planned to reduce the probability of the occurrence of a particular risk and/or to minimize the adverse impact of the occurrence of a particular risk.
  • Risk contingency plans define the strategies, activities, and budget that have been planned for responding to a particular risk, if and when the risk occurs.
  • Reserve plans define the management and/or contingency reserves set aside to address risks affecting the projectâ€™s costs or schedules.
  • Additional project reserve plans define the management and contingency reserves set aside to address risks yet to be identified or to address unexpected costs associated with existing risk response plans.
  • Insurance plans guarantee coverage of loss due to a risk occurrence should it occur but, at an additional cost to the project.

Description
Main Description

The Risk Management Plan is project's overall strategy for managing its risks, and the collection of the project's mitigation, contingency and reserve plans for dealing with identified risks. Each individual plan addresses a single risk or a class of risks. Because a single risk or a class of risks can have more than one planned response (e.g., a risk can be addressed by both a mitigation and a contingency plan), there is one risk response plan (entry) in the Risk Management Plan for each planned response for each identified risk or a class of risks.

Risk Strategy  
Project's risk management scope Includes:
  • Internal risks.  Those under the control or influence of the project team such as level of quality of the deliverables.
  • External risks.  Those outside the control or influence of the project team such as market conditions and governmental legislation.
  • The types of risk that will be considered such as dependency-related, client-related, hardware, human resources, and so on.

Excludes:
  • Risk not directly related to the project, such as risks against future business opportunities.
Standard or known risk triggers and thresholds Example: if a dependency is not met within 24 hours after its due date, invoke mitigation or containment strategy 
Parameters to be recorded for each identified risk Examples: likelihood, consequences
Methods and tools to be used for managing risks Examples: GS Risk, Rational PM
Risk Identification  
Version control information Provide version control information such as version number, date, author and owner names, summary of changes
Introduction Provide a brief overview or summary of the plan and its contents.
Risk class description Repeat this component for each risk class.
Risk class name Provide a unique identifier and a short name for the group of risks. For a single risk, use the same name as the corresponding risk definition.
Risk identifiers Unique identifier and name assigned to each risk grouped in the risk class, based on conventions established for the project. May contain a single or multiple identifiers if the Risk management plan entry addresses a group of related risks.
Mitigation plan Include:
  • The plan objectives including areas to address and objectives to accomplish.
  • The plan owner responsible for implementing the plan
  • The plan authority responsible for approving the plan execution
  • The planned mitigation activities to reduce the impact of the risk or class of risks and/or the probability of occurrence
  • The risk indicators or measures that will be used to track the effectiveness of planned actions in reducing the risk impact or risk probability.
  • The plan costs that have been included in the project budget for the mitigation activities.
Contingency plan Include:
  • The plan objectives including areas to address and objectives to accomplish.
  • The plan owner responsible for implementing the contingency plan.
  • The plan authority responsible for approving the contingency plan for execution.
  • The contingency actions that have been identified to be executed as part of the plan should the risk occur or be imminent.
  • The risk indicators or measures and specific values which will cause the contingency plan to be executed.
  • The plan cost that have been included in the project budget for the contingency actions.
Reserves Include:
  • The reserve plan authority responsible for approving release of the funds.
  • The justification for using the reserve if the risk occurs and the actions to assess impact on remaining reserve for unanticipated risks.
  • The measures and specific values which will cause the reserves to be used.
  • The reserve plan costs that have been included in the project budget for the reserves.
Additional reserves and insurance  
Additional reserves Specify:
  • The amounts of the contingency and management reserves (if any) that have been set aside to deal with any new risks that might be identified during the course of the project.
Insurance Specify:
  • The authority responsible for negotiating the insurance and collecting should the risk occur.
  • The information regarding the relevant insurance that is planned to be purchased including cost.

Key Considerations

Risk response planning is carried out at the beginning and throughout the project, as new risks are identified, and known risks are reassessed. Risk management is performed at each level in the organizational breakdown structure.

Individual contingency, mitigation, and reserve plans are validated as part of the project management health review conducted near the end of planning. Internal procedural reviews, compliance reviews, and project management health reviews conducted during execution verify the accuracy and currency of these risk response plans.

Risk response plans may also be subject to peer and sponsor review.

Tailoring
Impact of not having

Lack of a Risk Management Plan could result in:

  • The project being faced with risk consequences that could have been avoided or lessened through planned mitigation actions.
  • The project being ill-prepared to react to risks when they do occur.
  • Insufficient funds in the project budget to react to risks when they occur.
Reasons for not needing

The policies of the delivery organization typically define when a Risk Management Plan is not required.

Examples of projects that may not need a Risk Management Plan include:

  • Projects for which minimal risks have been identified
  • Projects below a cost threshold
