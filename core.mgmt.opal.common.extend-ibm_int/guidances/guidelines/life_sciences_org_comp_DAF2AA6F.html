Guideline: Life Sciences Organization Compliance
Delivery Process Use this guideline to identify and understand the following in support of a Life Sciences Organization client: a) Life Sciences Organization requirements, b) Work products in IBM Unified Method Framework (The Method) Delivery Processes that support these requirements.
Relationships
Related Elements
  • Life sciences organization compliance standard
  • Outline the life sciences organization validation plan (IMSP491)
  • Work Classification
Main Description

Purpose

Use this guideline to:

Identify and understand the following in support of a Life Sciences Organization client:

  • Life Sciences Organization requirements
  • Work products in IBM Unified Method Framework (The Method) Delivery Processes that support these requirements

Note: The focus of these Life Sciences requirements is on GxP regulations (GxP = Good Practices, where the x stands for M (Manufacturing), C (Clinical) or L (Laboratory)). 

 For a definition of GxP, our definition of a Life Sciences Organization, and other related information, refer to the Life Sciences Organization Compliance Standard.

Scope

This guideline applies to:

Support of a Life Sciences Organization client

For a complete mapping of the Life Sciences Organization Requirements and how the OPAL and IBM Unified Method Framework (The Method) support them, refer to the Life Sciences Organization Compliance Mapping.  The Life Sciences Mapping Requirement in section 3 of the Details section corresponds to the Compliance Requirement Number on the mapping document.

3.2 Validation, Compliance, and Quality System or Plan

Note: The term 'Quality Systems' is used to cover the quality processes in place to achieve compliance (regulatory and legal regulations and a client's interpretation of them, as appropriate), and validation (prove a system or application meets its requirements and specifications).
Step    Life Sciences Mapping Req.s Description
1 2.1 A life sciences organization validation plan must be created, and must describe all required validation activities together with assigned responsibilities, priorities and timing for applications or systems that are GxP relevant. The validation plan requires the creation of protocols that establish a structured series of formal checks to verify that each stage of the validation process has been properly completed. The protocols that are relevant here are: Design Qualification (DQ), Installation Qualification (IQ), Operational Qualification (OQ) and Performance Qualification (PQ).  Execution of this validation plan will positively prove the application or system exactly matches the design specifications (including compliance requirements).

The life sciences organization validation plan may reference other plans, such as the project quality plan, project management system summary.  All plans must be consistent and complementary in content.  For example, all plans for an application must be consistent with each other and not contain any contradictory requirements.  Quality is a superset of compliance and to achieve full compliance, quality must be built into the development and operation of the application. 

Note: GxP is a collective term for good practices where the x stands for M (Manufacturing), C (Clinical) or L (Laboratory).

2 2.2 The life sciences organization validation plan must ensure that all applicable regulations are noted and followed, and that computer systems activities are in accordance with regulations.  The applicable regulations, including the client's policies and standards for compliance, must be understood and included in the life sciences organization validation plan and project quality plan.
3 2.6.5 Validation work must be prioritized according to the functional risk assessment of the applications.  The prioritization must be supported by evidence, such as a Functional Risk Assessment that indicates severity, probability and potential harm of user, functional and system requirements.
4 2.6.6 A Validation Report is required to summarize the validation activities and their results.  While the OPAL does not contain a template for this report, the elements that it must include are based on the elements included in the organization's life sciences organization validation plan.
5 2.6.7
2.6.8 		Procedures must be in place and must be followed for all aspects of Quality Management.  The life sciences organization validation plan and other Quality Management documents and activities must be supported by, and must have documented links to, procedures and plans that support them.
6 2.6.10 Whenever services or products are provided by external sources, all quality management standards apply to these services or products.  The applicable regulations, including the client's policies and standards for compliance, must also be understood and addressed.
7 2.7 Periodic reviews and evaluations must be performed on solutions delivered to production to ensure the solution maintains its validated state, and the period must be defined.  For example: every 6 months, review a whole system to ensure that with changes and enhancements to its components, it is still compliant with its validation plan.

All in-scope activities and work products associated with these periodic reviews must be identified, planned, and managed.  For example, requirements may be included in the requirements-gathering process for creating documentation that will be used for these reviews from an operational perspective, even though 'operations' may be out-of-scope and handled by another organization or tower (such as a Service Delivery Center (SDC)).

Note: See the related requirement 3.6 under 4.3.3 Management Responsibility step 6.

3.4 Quality Assurance

Step    Life Sciences Mapping Req.s Description
1 2.3
2.4.1
2.4.4
4.1 		Quality Assurance is mandatory, and the role of Quality Assurance (QA) includes but is not limited to:
  • Ensuring compliance to client Computer System Validation (CSV) policies and standards
  • If applicable, the approval and implementation of documents required by regulatory authorities (such as Food and Drug Administration (FDA)), including compliance to GxP related policies, standards and guidelines
  • Ensuring that quality practices are defined and are implemented

QA must also perform periodic reviews, approve documentation and management processes, and assist the Project Team in risk management and quality management activities.

QA MUST be separate (have separate reporting lines) from those who own, operate, or perform work on the system.  The client may play some or all of the roles associated with Quality, and although an IBM SME is preferred to play this QA role, it is desirable for the client to be involved. The IBM QA role is to ensure that the work product IBM is required to produce is of adequate quality and meets any applicable regulatory requirements, including the client's interpretation of the regulations.

If an IBM Quality Assurance Group plays this role, they must incorporate regulatory compliance into their QA activities. Refer to the Quality Assurance section of the OPAL Policy Guideline for additional information on IBM Quality Assurance.

For process assurance activities, the process assurance review report - project and the process assurance review report - organization can be used to cover regulatory compliance.

  For work product inspections, the Work Product Inspections Procedure (IMSP530) or the Quality Planning Procedure (IMSP230) can be used to include any work products that are mandatory for inspection.

All in-scope activities and work products associated with these QA activities must be identified, planned, and managed.  For example, requirements may be included in the requirements-gathering process for creating documentation that will be used to conduct these activities from an operational perspective, even though 'operations' may be out-of-scope and handled by another organization or tower (such as a Service Delivery Center).

2 4.2 Client QA must be involved in all Quality and Regulatory activity, as the client has ultimate responsibility for their applications or systems.  Client QA must sign-off on the quality and regulatory required documents.
3 4.3
4.4 		Requirements must include the appropriate regulatory and legal requirements, including health and safety parameters for products or data, and these must be reviewed with both IBM and client QA teams.
4 4.5
4.6 		IBM QA must ensure that all IBM personnel are appropriately trained and if necessary, further training is planned, executed, and documented.  Client QA is normally involved in staffing of projects and ensures use of appropriate personnel with the correct training and skills.  If further training is required, IBM QA and Client QA to be involved in ensuring that the appropriate training is determined, delivered, and documented.
5 4.7 QA (IBM and client) must review all Quality System documents, such as the life sciences organization validation plan.  Client QA must sign-off on the Quality System documents.
6 4.8 The organization must establish plans and responsibilities for conducting regular inspections and audits of systems, applications, processes, and data (including raw and processed data) that must be performed periodically during the life of the project, and during the life of the system or application.

All in-scope activities and details associated with these inspections and audits must be identified, planned, and managed.  The inspections or audits will occur during production operation, even if 'operations' is out-of-scope and handled by another organization or tower (such as an SDC, in which case this planning is performed in collaboration with that group).  Also reference the requirements under the section 3.24 Internal Quality Audit.

7 4.9 Both IBM's and the client's QA must sign-off on all changes that affect the project (including changes to policies, standards, procedures, and guidelines (including software used)).

Client QA must sign-off to approve a change request, and IBM QA must sign-off when the change/testing/validation documents are done to indicate completion of our activities.

8 4.10 All corrective actions, no matter how small, are managed using an action log.  Corrective or Preventive action requests (CPARs) must reflect progress and approvals, and must be managed according to the Document Management Standards.
9 4.11 QA teams, both client and IBM, must have a checklist of regulatory-required records, and must periodically ensure that these are available and current.  This must be added to every quality review.
10 4.12 QA approval and Validation is required for changes to computer systems or applications before a change is implemented.  This applies to all changes except those that do not affect software, hardware, configuration or operation, such as for example adding toner in a printer.

For Validation, refer to the life sciences organization validation plan.

3.9 Testing

Step      Life Sciences Mapping Req.s Description
1 9.1 The Method Testing assets are required for all Life Sciences applications, and the level of testing to be appropriate, for example, higher risk elements undergo more extensive testing.  All applicable regulations and standards must be adhered to in all phases of testing (including preparation and execution).  This includes documentation of the following: test phases, acceptance criteria, roles and responsibilities, test review and approval processes, error or problem handling, preparation of a test log, and reporting of test findings.
2 9.2 The test strategy must be comprehensive and cover all aspects of the application or system, such as software, hardware, communications, links, and so on.
3 9.3 An explanation of the test strategy employed at each level of development (such as unit testing, integration testing, system acceptance testing or alpha/beta testing) must be documented as part of the test strategy.

Note: For systems or applications with compliance requirements from the FDA, IBM recommends incorporating the risk-based systems testing approach in alignment with the FDA's Vision of the 21st Century.

4 9.4 The test strategy must be translated into test specification.
5 9.5 A formal testing procedure must be documented and used to execute testing.
6 9.6 Test cases must be fully and clearly documented, including unique references, unambiguous descriptions, acceptance criteria/expected results and traceability back to the specifications they support.
7 9.7 Testing must include stress testing (repeat testing under different conditions) and performance testing (adequacy of system performance), as appropriate.
8 9.8, 9.9 Testing must be executed based on appropriate pre-defined and pre-approved test documents, with pre-defined acceptance criteria.
9 9.10 There must be evidence that testing activities are appropriate and conform to the testing procedures.
10 9.13, 9.14 Status of all phases of testing and all associated records are documented and maintained, with reviews, approvals and distribution performed as established by the standards and procedures for testing and quality.
11 9.16 Testers must be independent of application developers.
12 9.19 The procedure for handling test failures, deviations or errors, and escalating them appropriately, must be defined, document, approved, and followed.
13 9.20, 9.21 Use of test software, data and test tools (such as simulators) must be documented and carefully controlled.

3.31 Good Documentation Practices

Step    Life Sciences Mapping Req.s Description
1 9.18
31 		All documents and work products must be controlled through established Document Management standards and procedures, and these documents and work products must conform to the established standards and regulations.

It is recommended that if a tool is used for managing documents, the tool to be reviewed and approved by the appropriate personnel who can ensure that the tool conforms to the appropriate standards and regulations.

For the details of what is required, see the remainder of this section, and section 3.6 Document Control.

The following outlines good documentation practices:

  • Documentation format is defined (layout, style, reference numbering)
  • Documents are subject to version control
  • Document status is clearly identified
  • Document authoring, review, approval, effective cycle is defined and in effect
    Note: The Client organization, not IBM, to be the document's final 'approver'.
  • Documents are formally approved before becoming effective, with appropriate approval details defined (such as signer responsibilities) and documented (signer details such as title)
  • Approvals for documents must also include the reason or type of approval, such as provisional, final, interim, and so on
  • Documents are stored safely and appropriately
  • Documents are indexed and their status and location identified
  • Documents are controlled so that their current and effective versions are in use, and prior or obsolete documents are identified as such and are stored or archived appropriately

Note: It is recommended that a documentation control tool be used, and that the tool has been verified as acceptable for regulatory compliance.
