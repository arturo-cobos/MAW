Glossary: Life Sciences
This document is the life sciences glossary of terms, which defines the terms used in a life sciences organization.
Relationships
Related Elements
  • Life Sciences Organization Compliance
  • Life sciences organization compliance standard
Main Description

The client term section is made available to a life sciences organization for mapping the industry terms to the locally used client specific terms, as appropriate.

Industry Term Client Term Definition
21 CFR Part 11 <Client specific term not applicable>

A regulation set forth in Title 21 of the US Code of Federal Regulations (CFR) that sets rules for: 

  • Governing access, storage, retrieval, control and security of electronic records.
  • Governing security, control and use of electronic signatures.
  • Establishes the basis by which electronic records and signatures may be used as equivalents to paper records and traditional handwritten signatures.
Acceptance Criteria <Client specific term may apply> Specifications and/or conditions those are necessary for making a decision to accept or reject.
Accountability <Client specific term may apply> The stipulation, stated in the 21 CFR Part 11 regulation, that the pharmaceutical (or other Life Sciences Industry) company is ultimately responsible for reaching and maintaining regulatory compliance, and that this responsibility cannot be delegated.
Accuracy <Client specific term may apply> The closeness of agreement between an observed or measured value, and an accepted reference value.
Adulterated <Client specific term may apply> Condition in which a product is unfit to be sold as a result of contamination, mix-ups or errors, or has been manufactured under conditions which were not in a state of control.
Audit <Client specific term may apply> Activity whereby someone (internal personnel, a consultant, or the US Food and Drug Administration (FDA)) checks a manufacturing plant and the processes that occur there for compliance with Current Good Manufacturing Practices (cGMP) regulations.
CFR <Client specific term not applicable> Code of Federal Regulation. US Government publication that contains all regulations.  FDA regulations can all be found in 21 CFR.
Change Control <Client specific term may apply> A formal monitoring system by which qualified representatives review proposed or actual changes that might affect validated status, and take preventive or corrective action to ensure that the system retains its validated state of control.
Computer Network <Client specific term may apply> A system (transmission channels as well as supporting hardware and software) that connects several remotely located computers via telecommunications.  The Network starts and ends at the outlet in the wall where client workstations and servers (including mainframes and midrange systems) are plugged in, and therefore excludes the cable connecting a computer to the network.  All parts between any two such outlets are considered as the components of the network.
Computer System <Client specific term may apply> Computer hardware components assembled to perform in conjunction with a set of software programs, which are collectively designed to perform a specific function or group of functions.
Computerized System <Client specific term may apply> The computer system plus the controlled function it performs.
Computerized System Validation <Client specific term not applicable> Establishing documented evidence that provides a high degree of assurance that a specific computerized system will consistently operate in accordance with predetermined specifications.
Current Good Manufacturing Practices (cGMPs) <Client specific term not applicable> The minimum requirements by law for the manufacture, processing, packaging, holding or distribution of a material as established in Title 21 of the US Code of Federal Regulations.  Also known as GMP, meaning Good Manufacturing Practice.  Examples are Part 211 for Finished Pharmaceuticals, Part 606 for Blood and Blood Components, Part 820 for Medical Devices and Quality System Regulations (QCR).
Design Control <Client specific term may apply> Procedures followed to ensure that the design of a device has been adequately tested and then correctly transferred into production without change.
Design Qualification (DQ) <Client specific term not applicable> The Design Qualification provides documented verification that the system, equipment or process has been designed adequately to ensure proper function and performance.  The Design Qualification protocol evaluates the operational and performance requirements as well as the capability of the design to meet specifications.  Through this review, problems can be identified and solutions proposed before system, equipment, or process commissioning.
Disaster Recovery <Client specific term may apply> This relates to plans and procedures to be followed to allow the business to continue as soon as possible after a loss of computing service, or after a physical disaster.  Also commonly known as business recovery.
Efficacy <Client specific term may apply> Quality of a regulated product indicating that it actually does what it purports to do.
Electronic Record <Client specific term may apply> Any combination of text, graphics, data, audio, pictorial or other information represented in digital form that is created, modified, maintained, archived, retrieved or distributed by a computer system.
Electronic Signature <Client specific term may apply> A computer data compilation of any symbol or series of symbols executed, adopted, or authorized by the individual to be the legally binding equivalent of the individual's handwritten signature.
EU <Client specific term not applicable> European Union (European Community).
FDA <Client specific term not applicable> Food and Drug Administration; Agency of the US Department of Health and Human Services which regulates the safety, purity, and effectiveness of food, drugs, medical devices, biologics, and cosmetics.
FDA 483 <Client specific term not applicable> Statement of a specific area of non compliance or regulatory concern issued by the US Food and Drug Administration.
GAMP <Client specific term not applicable> Good Automated Manufacturing Practices.  The GAMP Forum exists to promote the understanding of the regulation and use of computer and control systems within the pharmaceutical manufacturing industry.
GCP <Client specific term not applicable> Good Clinical Practices.  A group of federal regulations promulgated and administered by the health authorities (such as FDA) as standards by which clinical studies are designed, implemented and reported so that the rights, integrity and confidentiality of the subjects are protected and there is public assurance that the data are credible.
GITP <Client specific term not applicable> Good Information Technology Practice, also short for Global Information Technology Planning.
GLP <Client specific term not applicable> Good Laboratory Practices.  Federal regulations established and administered by the health authorities (such as FDA) for conducting non-clinical laboratory studies that support, or are intended to support applications for research or marketing permits for regulated products. A similar set of regulations has been established by the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency.
GMP <Client specific term not applicable> Good Manufacturing Practice (regulation); GMP (also known as cGMP, meaning "current" Good Manufacturing Practice) is a set of regulations requiring that quality, safety, and effectiveness be built into foods, drugs, medical devices, and biological products. Its goal is consumer protection, and manufacturers must comply with them, as they carry the force of law.
Guidance Documents (US FDA) <Client specific term not applicable> Guidance documents represent the agency's current thinking on a particular subject.  They do not create or confer any rights for or on any person and do not operate to bind FDA or the public.  An alternative approach can be used if such approach satisfies the requirements of the applicable statute, regulations, or both.
GxP <Client specific term not applicable> Collective term for good practices where the x stands for M (Manufacturing), C (Clinical) or L (Laboratory).
Handwritten Signature <Client specific term may apply> The scripted or legal mark of an individual handwritten by that individual, and executed or adopted with the present intention to authenticate writing in a permanent form.  The act of signing with a writing or marking instruments such as a pen or stylus is preserved.  The scripted name or legal mark, while conventionally applied to paper, may also be applied to other devices that capture the name or mark.
Installation Qualification (IQ) <Client specific term not applicable> The Installation Qualification establishes documented evidence that all key aspects of a system or equipment installation adhere to approved design requirements and that all manufacturers' recommendations have been considered.  The protocol contains the documented plans and details of procedures that verify specific static attributes of a facility, utility system, or process equipment.  The executed IQ protocol provides verification that all aspects of the installation adhere to the approved design intentions and that the manufacturer's recommendations have been reviewed and considered.
Life Cycle <Client specific term may apply> The time frame from early stages of development (including identification of user's requirements, analysis, design, integration, qualification, and so on) until commercial use of the product or process is discontinued (Retirement).
Master Plan <Client specific term may apply> Description of the validation of a specific facility and its equipment and processes.  It includes the cGMP requirements for a validation program. Its also provides the outline and scope of such a program.  The Master Plan is also known as the 'Validation Plan'.
Operational Qualification (OQ) <Client specific term not applicable> The documented evidence that the system or equipment performs as intended throughout all anticipated operating ranges.  The Operational Qualification protocol contains the procedures to verify specific dynamic attributes of a system or equipment throughout its operating range, which may include "worst case" conditions.  Applicable Standard Operating Procedures and training procedures are documented in the appropriate protocol section.  The executed operational qualification protocol verifies that the system or equipment performs as intended.
Performance Qualification (PQ) <Client specific term not applicable> The documented evidence that the system, equipment or process is capable of consistently producing a safe product of high quality.  The Performance Qualification protocol describes the procedures that verify the specific capabilities of a process equipment/system through the use of simulation material and/or actual product.
PIC/S <Client specific term not applicable> Pharmaceutical Inspection Cooperation Scheme.
Predicate Rules <Client specific term not applicable> Any requirements set forth in the US Food, Drug and Cosmetic Act, the Public Health Service Act, or any FDA regulation, with the exception of 21 CFR Part 11, are referred to as predicate rules.
Process <Client specific term may apply> A series of steps and/or actions, controlled by a written procedure, which are used to accomplish or produce a desired result.
Process Validation <Client specific term may apply> Establishing documented evidence that provides a high degree of assurance that a specific process will consistently produce a product meeting its pre-determined specifications and quality attributes.
Prospective Validation <Client specific term may apply> Validation conducted prior to the distribution of either a new product, or product made under a revised manufacturing process, where the revisions may have affected the product's characteristics.  It is also to ensure that the finished product meets all release requirements for functionality and safety.
Protocol <Client specific term may apply> A documented set of instructions designed to confirm specific static and/or dynamic attributes of the installation, operation or performance of a utility, system, equipment or process.
Qualification <Client specific term not applicable> Verification that equipment is appropriate and correct for the process(es) being investigated or validated.
Quality <Client specific term may apply> The totality of features and characteristics that bear on the ability of a product such as finished pharmaceutical product or device to satisfy fitness-for-use, including safety and performance.

Quality also means the ability to consistently meet Client needs and expectations as well as regulatory requirements while developing full utilization of resources used in the process.

Quality Assurance <Client specific term may apply> The activity of providing evidence that all the information necessary to determine that the product is fit for the intended use meets cGMP requirements.  The Quality Assurance Department executes this function.

Quality Assurance also means the planned or systematic actions necessary to provide adequate confidence that a product or service will satisfy given requirements for quality.

Quality Control <Client specific term may apply> The activity of measuring process and product parameters for comparison with specified standards.  This activity is to assure that they are within predetermined limits and therefore, the product is acceptable for use.  Operational techniques and the activities used to fulfill the requirements of quality.  The Quality Control Department executes this function.
Quality System Regulation <Client specific term may apply> The regulation that embodies the required good manufacturing and documentation practices required for the design and manufacture of medical devices.
Regulatory Requirements <Client specific term not applicable> Any requirements set forth in the Food, Drug and Cosmetic Act, the Public Health Service Act, or any FDA regulation. The regulations address research, production, and control of FDA regulated articles.  Generally, they are categorized into areas like manufacturing, laboratory, clinical and pre-clinical research, adverse event reporting, product tracking, and pre- and post-marketing submissions and reports.
Retirement <Client specific term may apply> Represents the end of the life of a System.  Productive use of the system ends. It still requires validation control and documentation because the data generated by the system will potentially outlast it.
Retrospective Validation <Client specific term may apply> A validation process for a product already in distribution, based upon accumulated production, testing and control data. This type of validation is showing absence of error based on history data and tests, to prove that the system produces expected results. This validation is unreliable, and therefore, is not recommended. Also see Prospective Validation and Revalidation.
Revalidation <Client specific term may apply> Repeating the validation process or portions of it in order to demonstrate that no changes have occurred which could alter the performance of the original validated equipment or process.
Security <Client specific term may apply> The protection of computer hardware and software from accidental or malicious access, use, modification, destruction, or disclosure.  Security also pertains to personnel, data, communications, and the physical protection of computer installations.
SOP <Client specific term may apply> Standard Operating Procedure.  Written procedures prescribing and describing the steps to be taken in normal and defined conditions which are necessary to assure control of production and processes.
Specifications <Client specific term may apply> Documents which define and set acceptable and/or authorized modifications.
Testing <Client specific term may apply> Testing when related to validation is meant to ensure that the specification was properly assembled and implemented.
Validation <Client specific term may apply> Establishing documented evidence that provides a high degree of assurance that a specific system (an interacting or interdependent group of items that function together to achieve a specific function), process, or facility will consistently produce a product meeting its predetermined specifications and quality attributes. Validation can be subdivided into three activities; installation, operational, and performance qualifications.
Validation Program <Client specific term may apply> The collective activities that are related to validation.
Validation Scope <Client specific term may apply> The boundaries of the equipment use or process application intended to be addressed by the Validation activity.  For example, a protocol's scope might include challenging the capacity of a glassware washer to remove process residuals but not to qualify the washing process for depyrogenation.
Verification <Client specific term may apply> The activity where checks are made to confirm that a deliverable is complete by ensuring that it contains all required information and follows standards.
Warning Letter <Client specific term not applicable> An informal advisory to a firm communicating the agency's position on a matter but does not commit FDA to taking enforcement action.  The agency's policy is that Warning Letters should be issued for violations which are of regulatory significance in that failure to adequately and promptly take corrections may be expected to result in enforcement action should the violation(s) continue.
Worst Case <Client specific term may apply> A set of conditions encompassing upper and lower processing limits and circumstances, including those within standard operating procedures, which pose the greatest chance of process or product failure when compared to ideal conditions.  Such conditions do not necessarily induce product or process failure.
