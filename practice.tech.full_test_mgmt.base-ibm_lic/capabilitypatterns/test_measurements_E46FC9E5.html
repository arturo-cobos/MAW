Main Description
The Test Measurements include a consolidated summary, in a meaningful predefined format, of the metrics and measurements collected during test planning, test management, defect management, static and dynamic testing throughout the testing project, plus metrics and measurements reflecting the total testing project. The Test Measurements should satisfy executive, management and technical requirements for information on the capacity and capability of the development, infrastructure, test and other stakeholder organizations as demonstrated in this project. This information can be used to compare actual against plan and to predict how these groups will meet current and ongoing quality objectives of the overall organization.
 
The Test Measurements must be organized according to the guidelines created by the quality management and test organizations, so that trend analysis can be carried out by test management for improving the quality and test functions based on the heuristics collected over time from testing projects.
 
For the purposes of this document, the term "measurements" and "metrics" are used interchangeably.

Key Considerations

Keep the Test Measurements simple and meaningful.  Measurements that are easily understood, collected, analyzed and presented will be more readily created and accepted.  Many academic publications provide complex statistical algorithms for calculating and reporting Test Measurements.  However unless the Testing organization is highly complex (many heterogeneous technologies, complex applications and external interfaces, sharing of data across numerous lines of business, distributed teams, etc), it is best to maintain a simple set of measurements that are cost-effective to produce, analyze and use.

There exist in the marketplace many Testing tools that provide the facility to gather Test Measurements during dynamic testing.  The choice of tool will depend on the underlying technology of the applications being tested and the ROI expected by the organization on this investment.  It may be preferable when initiating a Test Measurements program, to use a spreadsheet to capture the metrics and to graph them as “prototypes” until Test Management decides what information is required to be gathered.  The findings can then be used as criteria for acquisition of Testing tools to manage the management information most supportive of the Testing organization’s objectives.

It may be beneficial to measure Defect Rate against Development Productivity (metrics from the Application domain), as the inefficiencies may not always lie within the Testing function. 

[Rubin and Ed Yourdon conducted an international study of software development productivity and quality for the federal department industry Canada. The study involved 227 companies in the United States, South America, Europe, South Africa, The Far East, and India. The study found that Canadian software developers were much more productive than the worldwide average. They accounted for 111 function points per developer compared to the worldwide average of 92.5 function points per developer. The results also indicated that Canada had the highest defect rate, 5.12 defects per thousand lines of code. The worldwide average is 3.79 defects per thousand lines of code. The United States had the overall lowest defect rate, 2.3 defects per thousand lines of code.  The study supports the hypothesis that the faster you write software the more defects you make. The United States which had the lowest defect rate creates software at an average (88 function points per developer) slightly lower that the worldwide average.]

Detailed information on how to classify defects (for process improvements and root cause analysis) using a standardized, rigidly defined categorization technique, may be found at http://www.research.ibm.com/softeng/ODC/ODC.HTM.  At present the examples on these pages are geared towards the development of systems software, but the analogy can be adopted for application development.  The IBM Testing Competency is currently investigating the inclusion of this technique into the IBM Global Services Method – Testing Subdomain.

Some additional suggested industry-standard Testing Metrics and Measurements may be found by referring to the ISO standard – ISO-9126, but caution is advised in their adoption as it could become a lengthy and costly program.
