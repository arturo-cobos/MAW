Main Description

The PE strategy work product information includes the following:

  • Introduction: An introduction to the PE strategy work product.  It describes the document, its purpose and, if appropriate, provides project background.
  • Scope: At the PE strategy level, this description should coincide with the scope of the Customer Baseline and the current IT systems which will be affected by the solution implementing these requirements. But PE scope may go beyond project scope if the affected systems interact with others that are thought to be ‘out of scope’ in the sense that they are not receiving changes. 
  • Assumptions: Any underlying assumptions for the PE strategy are clearly stated in this section.
  • PE Goals: Describe the tangible goals of performance engineering.  PE objectives can be at varying levels of detail.  At the PE strategy level, the objectives set the tone and direction for how performance engineering is going to be done.
  • PE Strategy Overview, which will include:
    • PE Focus Areas: PE focus areas are those critical performance requirements of the solution that must be met to provide the expected level of confidence in the application or system.  If there are any such requirements from the RFP, or from discussions with the client, as to what areas the client considers critical to the business or to the technology implementation, these may be captured in this section.
    • Development phases: The phases of development like SRS, BRS etc., which will be used to manage the larger development project, whether it is a traditional “waterfall” project or an iterative development project. The phases correspond to each of the successive System Engineering baselines such as Customer Baseline, System Baseline, Component Baseline, Design Baseline, Test Readiness and Production Readiness.
    • Risk Assessment: PE at a high level is a risk mitigation discipline in and of itself, and performance-related risks are subject to identification, assessment and mitigation activities at any time during the project.  The assessment of risks done in the PE strategy document is not expected to be exhaustive, but rather should focus on known project and technical factors that may increase performance-related risks or hinder the ability to address those risks using PE techniques.
    • PE Themes: Each of the PEMM themes has a number of PE activities associated with it.  A core part of the PE strategy is to select the appropriate PE activities to be employed during each of the SE&A baselines, as well as during development and test. 
    • Organizational Responsibility: Organizational responsibility refers to describing in generic terms the hand-offs and handovers that occur at various points in the development lifecycle, as the product or solution evolves from specification to design to implementation.  This is done at a high level to identify which business or functional areas need to get involved in order to achieve the desired end-state. 
    • PE Methods and Tools: If there are performance standards at the organization, business unit or competency levels that are to be used on this project, this information is included here.  Any techniques specific to the development or technology environment could be mentioned here, too.  It is useful to include any technology or tools strategy that the organization, business unit, or competency may have embarked on (or should embark on), and which would be relevant to the performance aspects of project.  The impact of these on any other strategy components should be noted in their discussion. 
    • PE Decision Checkpoints (DCPs): Decision checkpoints are used at key milestones to determine whether the project is making adequate progress toward meeting its goals.  For example, entry and exit criteria are frequently used to manage the beginning and the end of a test phase respectively.  Similarly, PE DCPs should be used to determine whether the project is making adequate progress toward its performance goals.  For an SE&A-managed project, PE DCPs will typically be aligned with the SE&A baseline reviews, but they may be appropriate at other points as well.
    • Performance Metrics: As with the methods and tool strategy, if the organization, business unit, or competency has any metrics program or measurements strategy relating to performance, the PE strategy is the place to include the information. However, if they don’t have any specific strategy, plan should be made to create one.
    • PE Data Management Strategy: PE data may include performance statistics from test or production systems or estimating models, business and technical volumetrics (quantitative information which has a bearing on the performance or capacity of a given solution). The PE data management strategy should explain at a high level how this data will be gathered, analyzed and managed, whether repositories will be used and change control will be needed.
