Guideline: Performance Engineering Activities Supporting the Production Baseline
The Production Readiness Review (PRR) ensures that the implemented, tested system has been able to meet its requirements and is ready to be deployed.

Main Description

1 Description

In an Enterprise SE&A project, the Test Baseline encompasses the release contents, test plans and implementation for the system described in the preceding baselines.

Figure 1 – Production Baseline in SE Capability Pattern

In the SE Capability Pattern, a number of work products are developed before it is time to review the Production Baseline, as shown below.

The performance engineering team has a vested interest in ensuring that performance testing has confirmed the ability of the system to meet its performance requirements, and that there are no performance- or capacity-related obstacles to the system’s deployment in the production environment. Deliverables which are deemed deficient from a performance and capacity standpoint may lead to the entry of a defect, which is then taken into account during the PRR Scorecard process.

The PE team should also be working with operations and performance/capacity management personnel to ensure that system performance and capacity can be managed successfully, and that performance-related service level attainment can be measured and reported on an ongoing basis.

2 Usage Context

This technique paper should be used as guidance on applying PE activities and techniques in support of the Production Baseline Phase.

3 Content

Organizing the PE Effort during the Production Baseline Phase

During this stage of the project, the PE team is typically concerned with activities such as the following.

  • Test confirmation of solution’s ability to meet performance requirements – The performance test results will generally give the most reliable indication, short of live production, of whether the solution is going to be able to meet performance requirements. 
  • Validation of PE findings – The results of the performance test will provide valuable feedback to the PE team of the accuracy and effectiveness of their estimating, modeling and performance risk management efforts to date. 
  • Deployment recommendation – Once the planned testing is complete, the PE team should have a relatively complete picture of how the solution can be expected to perform. At this point, the PE team may be called upon directly or indirectly to contribute to a recommendation of whether or not the solution should be deployed. 
  • Turnover to delivery / operations – Since the delivery / operations team will be assuming responsibility for the developed solution, the PE team and others should participate in turning over information that will be needed to deploy and manage the solution in the production environment. 
  • Accepted monitoring approach – During test, the performance monitoring approach should be rehearsed to make sure that it will adequately support production. 
  • Confirmed capacity sizing – Performance test results should serve to validate capacity sizing information developed earlier. 
  • Accepted SLAs – The delivery / operations team will be finalizing and instituting the Service Level Agreement(s) for the system defined earlier, using findings from test and from initial production experience to confirm that the SLAs can be supported.

Calibration and validation of earlier models – Results from performance testing can be used to validate the findings of earlier modeling, and make future modeling more accurate by adjusting the model so that its results align more closely with the results of performance testing.

Risk and Performance Management Focus during the Production Baseline Phase

The results of performance testing will serve to validate the findings of performance engineering, and more importantly it will give a clearer, more complete picture of performance risk associated with the system. The PE team must now contribute to the process of deciding whether or not the tested system can be deployed as planned. Ultimately the decision of whether or not the system will be deployed is made by management, but the PE team and the other technical stakeholders will be responsible for advising management of any risks associated with deployment which need to be considered, and making a recommendation of what should be done. For SE&A-managed projects, the Production Readiness Review (PRR) will provide the PE team their primary opportunity to contribute to the deployment decision.

Preparing for Production during the Production Baseline Phase

The primary purpose of the production baseline phase is to prepare to turn the solution over to the delivery / operations team for deployment. Up to now, the PE team may already have been working with the delivery / operations team on matters such as monitoring, capacity sizing and SLAs, which should now be close to a finalized state. The PE team can continue to assist the delivery / operations team by:

  • Participating in peer reviews of performance- and capacity-related deployment work products prior to the PRR, 
  • Ensuring that lessons learned from earlier phases (such as performance defects encountered and recommendations for tuning and other improvements) are communicated to the delivery / operations team, 
  • Seeing that the necessary performance and capacity management tools and procedures are in place to manage the production system.

Estimating Performance of the Production Solution

Even after performance results are available from the test and production environments, performance estimation and modeling can still play a very important role in the future of the system. As previously discussed, the results of testing and production can be used to calibrate earlier performance models, and then the improved models can be reused to make performance and capacity projections for the future, even after deployment. Once developed, performance models can continue to serve as valuable assets for future performance and capacity management if they are well maintained and leveraged.

Interacting with Test Team during the Production Baseline Phase

The performance test may include multiple test scenarios and approaches, depending on what types of applications are being tested, which performance requirements are covered and what areas of performance risk are being examined. For a complex solution with many components, the combination of component-level and end-to-end performance testing may take weeks or even months to complete, between populating databases, executing test scenarios, analyzing test results, getting problems resolved as they are encountered, implementing tuning changes and other performance improvements, and reporting on the results of the test. The performance test will require the use of a variety of skills, including test environment management, performance test scripting and execution, platform-specific analysis and tuning, application-level analysis and project management.

The PE team can assist in this effort by alerting the performance test teams to key performance risk areas in advance, by reviewing the test plans and cases, and by assisting with the performance problem determination and performance results analysis. However this is done, the goal should be to provide the customer not only with the results of the PE and testing efforts, but with analysis, findings and recommendations which can be understood and used to make business decisions.

Participating in the Production Readiness Review (PRR)

In addition to reviewing the results of the performance test, the following excerpt from the Preliminary Review scorecard denotes the PRR criteria which apply to performance matters.



1. Requirements and Architecture (Content)

1.2 Requirements in the baseline versus requirements being delivered - Non functional requirements defined, enumerated, traced

1.5 Gaps or risks identified and mitigated or transferred (Exclusions)

2. Test Results

2.2 Non-functional testing complete or projected

2.4 Gaps or risks identified and mitigated or transferred (Exclusions)

3. Expected Availability, Performance, and Response Times

3.1 Expected end-to-end availability in production

3.2 Expected system response times and other performance criteria (if not covered in performance or stress test results)

3.3 Infrastructure changes made to the production systems to meet requirements

3.4 Gaps or risks identified and mitigated or transferred (Exclusions)

6. Deployment / Production Readiness

6.2 Install Plans defined

6.6 Dependencies, Issues and Risks defined



Below are some examples of questions that the PE team can be asking above and beyond the standard PRR scorecard criteria.

  • Do the performance test results indicate that the solution will meet the performance requirements and SLRs within the constraints of the capacity plan? 
  • Is there (or are there plans to develop) a Service Level Agreement (SLA) for the deployed solution? 
  • Have the tuning recommendations from test been communicated to the deployment team and planned for implementation? 
  • Have all tests been completed and all performance requirements been met within acceptable tolerances? 
  • Is there documented evidence of the performance acceptance criteria having been met? If not, will the stakeholder sign off on any exceptions to allow deployment to continue? 
  • Did the performance test team rehearse the live monitoring and performance/capacity management tools and processes that will be used by the production support team? If so, have the results of this rehearsal been communicated to production support, or were they involved in the rehearsal process? 
  • Has the production support implemented the live monitoring and reporting tools and processes that will be needed to support the deployed solution with respect to performance and capacity? 
  • Has the performance risk been reassessed since the TRR and are those risks being adequately addressed?
    Are there plans that will allow for a backout, staged implementation or extra capacity should unforeseen critical performance or capacity issues occur?
