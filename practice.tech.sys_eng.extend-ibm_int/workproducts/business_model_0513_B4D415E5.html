Deliverable: Business Model (DEL 0513)
The Business Model deliverable describes, in business terms, the operating model of the business enterprise.
Purpose

The Business Model establishes the basis for aligning the business architecture, business processes and resources in an enterprise. It is a representation of the business architecture comprising business processes, business events, business roles, and the functional and operational characteristics of the business architecture.

This deliverable is used as the basis to:

  • Define the scope of the IT engagement implementations
  • Provide a useful vehicle for communication and discussion
  • Assist in gaining executive agreement to the scope and boundaries
  • Provide the basis to identify detailed functions, business processes, and requirements (use cases)
  • Provide a basis for aligning process and data for automation of package implementation processes
  • Provide the basis to define the future environment for architectural design
  • Represent a detailed view of how users will execute the client’s business process using the packaged application
  • Assist Development Teams in configuring the system and begin development work once the model is completed

Main Description

This deliverable identifies, depicts, and describes the business architecture of an enterprise. An ordered snapshot (traditionally called a baseline) of the business model can be used to define the scope of a transformation or IT engagement implementation.

This deliverable serves the following specific purposes:

Establish an ordered snapshot of the business model of an enterprise.
While business processes map the work activities in an enterprise, we often find that business process models are lacking in detail or are incomplete. Work activities are often codified in terms of the workers’ job titles instead of their roles in the enterprise. Work activities are not rationalized with similar activities elsewhere in the enterprise. Business events are recognized in terms of their symptomatic occurrence rather than their business significance. The net result is often a large collection of processes that are ultimately unable to articulate the business functions of the enterprise.

This activity aims to collect the various work products and induce thought rationalization into them. User roles and business events must be systematically and categorically rationalized across processes. Business events must be rolled up to the meaningful abstraction that stimulates change in a business enterprise. Finally, the baseline collects the processes and all their supporting artifacts and establishes a time-coded snapshot of the business functions in the enterprise.

Establish the concept of change management.
From the previous point, it is clear that changes to the business model should follow a well-defined change management process. Change management ensures that: process changes do not affect other upstream or downstream processes, or the overall continuity of the business; process roles are clarified and rationalized as processes are split or joined across organizations; and as a consequence, speeds up future process changes. For IT engagements, change management ensures that the IT provider receives a complete specification of the business functions at the outset of the engagement.

Foster partnership and collaboration across an organization.
Enterprises are often challenged by rapidly changing market conditions. These conditions can affect the processes (and the people) in dynamic or unpredictable ways. Establishing change management makes it possible for executive management to plan process changes and thus makes it possible for the business managers to roll out changes effectively without interrupting the business. It also ensures that the right people at the right levels in the enterprise are notified and informed about changes to their processes.

Packaging Instructions

The final document will be delivered to the customer in a reproducible format; use of proprietary file formats that may not be supported on the client’s platform should be avoided. The actual models may have originally been created using any diagramming or modeling tool, but should be included as a finished portion of the report for final delivery. All accompanying text should included and clearly identify the relevant portions of the model it supports. The final report may also be delivered in hard copy format printed in a single, bound document.

Tailoring
Impact of not having

This deliverable is critical to the success of any IT transformation or implementation engagement. Without this deliverable, the project carries the following risks:

  • No comprehensive view of the client’s business processes. This is a direct cause for open scope which, in turn, leads to lost revenue and declining profitability on the engagement. In SOMA engagements, this deliverable is a key requirement to perform domain decomposition to identify services.
  • No qualitative or quantitative basis for identifying problem areas in existing processes or gaps in IT systems to drive new processes. No metrics to determine where the engagement team should focus their attention during project delivery.
  • Lack of communication and understanding between the client, IBM and any vendors or partners involved in the engagement.
