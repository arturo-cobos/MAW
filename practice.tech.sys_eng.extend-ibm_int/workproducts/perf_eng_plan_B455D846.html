Artifact: Performance Engineering Plan (ARC 403)
The Performance Engineering Plan documents the expectations of the various stakeholders regarding performance engineering.
Domains: Architecture
Work Product Kinds: Plan
Purpose
The purpose of the PE plan work product is to:
  • Address performance engineering from a high-level system viewpoint.
  • Provide a big picture view, or a view of the forest, whereas the detailed plans provide views of the trees in the forest.
  • Provide an early definition of the test environment requirements that include requirement for tools, metrics and development standards.
  • Ensure that the detailed plans are built systematically and completely and there is no more and no less overlap between detailed plans than is necessary with respect to addressing performance.

A PE plan provides planning information (ahead of time) to alert the organizations to address performance requirements by allocating the right set of resources. Providing high level requirements for hardware, software, and people resources as early in the lifecycle as possible, increases the likelihood that the resources will be planned, prepared, acquired, and trained in a timely manner.

Description
Main DescriptionThis artifact documents the “what” (scope and objectives), the “why” (purpose), the “when” (key milestones), and the “who” (organizational roles and responsibilities) for performance engineering across the project phases. This also includes how well the current system performance is understood and monitored at present.
Brief Outline

The table of contents of a Performance Engineering Plan includes the following sections, but does not include detailed testing tasks or tracking of them or test schedules:

  • Introduction
  • Scope
  • Assumptions
  • PE Goals
  • PE Strategy Overview
  • PE Overall Plan
    • Roles and Responsibilities
    • Schedule of PE Activities
    • Major PE milestones: Lists major PE schedule targets.
    • Resource requirements
    • Contingencies
  • PE Management and Reporting
    • Reporting Procedure: Describe how PE progress will be reported (E.g. charts, presentations etc.).

Key Considerations

The development of the Performance Engineering Plan is started early in the development lifecycle, and is refined as more information becomes available.  It ties together all of the detailed plans which address performance and points to them.

A PE Plan evolves from the PE strategy, and is ultimately driven by the performance-related items contained in the project’s non-functional requirements (NFRs). It uses the organization level standards (if exist) and applies them to the project to identify specific PE focus areas, themes and activities as they apply to the project. For example, while there are typically detailed plans for development phases and testing levels, a given project may require a performance modeling effort that needs to have a detailed plan of its own. Thus a PE Pan needs to be scalable and tailorable, and must be flexible enough to be used for various project sizes and organizations.

As the PE Plan is developed it is possible that the approach initially envisioned in the PE strategy may need to be modified, based on the criticality of the performance requirements, or the availability of resources to address performance.

If the recommended process for developing the PE strategy was followed, a PE project definition workshop (PE PDW) will have been held prior to the creation of the PE strategy. (See Performance Engineering Strategy WPD for details.)  In this case, the lead Performance Architect may choose to conduct a PE planning meeting to flesh out the details of the PE Overall Plan section mentioned above before creating the PE plan document and circulating it for review.

If it is necessary to develop a PE Plan shortly after the engagement of the performance engineering team, the lead Performance Architect may choose to expand the PE PDW to define both the PE strategy and the PE plan elements, and then publish a PE plan document which encompasses both.

Tailoring
Impact of not having

Without a Performance Engineering Plan, the various stakeholders may be working independently and addressing performance in an incomplete, overlapping or inconsistent manner. A PE plan brings the stakeholders and their representatives together, collectively and collaboratively as a team, to agree and work toward a common set of goals. Absence of a PE Plan can lead to incomplete or duplicate effort and result in missed plan components. Therefore you need a plan to address these issues comprehensively and PE provides a mechanism to address these.

If this work product is not produced, then PE will not happen at all and the overall performance may not meet the needs of business. The detailed plans will then each have to address PE independently, and each detailed plan will have to go through the PE focus areas and identify the PE activities for their part of the project.

Reasons for not needingIf the project is simple and self-contained, low risk, and all team members are co-located, then instead of a separate PE plan, the PE plan elements may be incorporated into another plan, such as the Systems Engineering Management Plan or another project-wide plan. Plans for addressing PE should be documented whether or not there is a standalone PE plan document.
