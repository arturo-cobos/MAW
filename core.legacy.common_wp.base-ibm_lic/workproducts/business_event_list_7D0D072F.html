Artifact: Business Event List (BUS 101)
The Business Event List work product documents the events that are the initial stimuli causing the business to act.
Domains: Business Process
Work Product Kinds: Model
Purpose

This work product is produced to:

  • Identify the different business transactions that the business must be able to handle
  • Assist in identifying the goods and information flowing between the external involved parties and the business (service encounters)
  • Assist in identifying the business processes/sub-processes
  • Assist in identifying the process triggers
  • Verify completeness of the business process model
  • Assist in verifying the scope of the new system
  • Organize use cases according to a high level grouping, i.e. the business events
  • Provide the foundation for metrics for the proposed system, e.g. transaction rates

Description
Main Description

Events can be either external or internal to the business, external events are performed by external involved parties (i.e. external entitites) that "touch" the business, while internal events occur within the target organization's business.  Some types of internal events are:

  • Dates (e.g. today is the 15th of the month)
  • Conditions (e.g. 20 is the low stock level point)
  • Incidents (e.g. a laptop is stolen)
  • Life cycle events (e.g. employee leave)
  • Decisions (e.g. an early retirement package is offered)
  • Opportunities (e.g. another company is acquired)

Triggers actually cause the business to act. They represent the state change indicating that the event has taken place (uses past tense). For example:

  • Commercial customer complaint received (from the event "commercial customer registers a complaint")
  • New business partner payment request received (from the event "new business partner requests payment")
  • Date reached (from the event "today is the 15th of the month")
  • Threat managed ( from the event "threat agent attempts to access" )

From a security perspective, important Business Events that impact security could include the following:

  • Computer virus outbreak
  • Environmental disaster that disables or disrupts operations (such as a flood)
  • Theft of company data
  • Hacking of a company’s network or website
  • Acquiring and releasing employees
  • Entering business agreements with new or untrusted business partners
  • Modifying or extending the IT architecture
  • Developing or acquiring important business applications
These lists are not meant to be exhaustive but to provide insight into the myriad of business events that can impact security.
Brief Outline

Business events are text-based and kept as a numbered list, with each business event being an item on the list. 

  • Event Number: Sequentially-allocated number to aid the future referencing of the event
  • Event Name: Name the event.  For external events, use the name of the external entity and indicate what they do that requires the enterprise to respond.  For internal events, indicate what triggering condition has occurred.  For temporal events, simply state what has happened, (in many cases, using the word "due" is appropriate).
  • Event Description: A full definition of the event, including who or what the source is, an explanation of what they have done that requires a response and an indication of what information they send as a stimulus.  For temporal events, define what conditions apply (e.g. the 5th April every Year, the 2nd Friday of every month, when 21 days have elapsed after confirmation of delivery).  Describe the current physical triggers and any potential future ones.
  • Event Frequency/Volume: Provide some indication on how often the event takes place.

 It is useful to structure an item in a noun-verb-noun structure, where the subject is the external entity or internal process that initiates the triggering condition.  Qualifying the nouns is particularly important, as the business must be able to handle various types of transactions:

  • From different customer segments e.g. commercial customers, residential consumers
  • For different product / service offerings e.g. loan accounts, deposit accounts
  • For different contractual terms e.g. fixed rate mortgage, variable rate mortgage.

Key Considerations
  • Involve customers, domain experts, and end users in the formulation and review of the business events.
  • A potentially useful means of identifying list items is to walk though a typical "day in the life" of a user.
  • Review the life cycle of a client or employee identifying the enrollment, role changes and removal.
  • Looking at users’ training materials can also be useful.
  • Consider the ‘special’ business events including ‘anti-‘business event such as a ‘non-customer’ or threat.
  • If the list grows large, it is useful to group business events into categories.
  • Review the list of business events with other users and experts to get other opinions.
  • Watch for redundancy.  Some events may be subsumed by others.  In such cases, you may choose to represent subsumed events as part of an aggregation of a larger-grained event.
  • Consistent level of granularity may be difficult to achieve.  This isn’t a serious problem, as it will be dealt with in later, more formal work products, such as a use case model.
  • Clarify which events are in or out of scope.
Tailoring
Impact of not having

If this work product is not produced, then:

  • There will not be an understanding of all the business transactions that need to be addressed by the business.
  • Business processes/sub-processes may be inadvertently omitted from the other work products.
  • Business process models may be developed that are too narrowly focused (by organization or functional area) rather than looking at the end-to-end process path (customer or other external involved party view).
  • Use cases may cover multiple events, thus becoming complex and causing higher design costs and a higher probability of errors.
  • There may be design defects in the new system, based on all of the above.
Reasons for not needing

This work product will not be required when:

  • Business process models already exist which show external interfaces, events and triggers.
  • The business is simple and straightforward and the business events and transactions are self-evident.
