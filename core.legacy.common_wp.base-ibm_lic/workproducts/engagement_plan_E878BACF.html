Artifact: Engagement Plan (ENG 003)
An Engagement Plan defines and documents the objectives, scope, level of detail, deliverables, responsibilities (key activities and tasks) and resourcing for a small projects.

(As opposed to a more lengthy integration projects where more formal work products would be used)
Domains: Project Management
Work Product Kinds: Plan
Purpose

An Engagement Plan should be used to:

  • Document the activities, decisions and agreements taken during any planning discussions or workshops
  • Brief the core project team to ensure all participants have a common understanding of what, how and when deliverables will be produced
  • Provide input to the definition of an information-gathering plan

Additionally the Enagement Plan is used to:

  • Provide an input to the sizing, scoping, resourcing and pricing of a client engagement
  • Provide a framework within which a client engagement can be effectively managed
  • Provide the basis for negotiating any changes to a project, managing expectations and client satisfaction
  • Provide the basis for measuring success / helping to ensure client satisfaction with the work done

Description
Main DescriptionAn Engagement Plan will normally be defined and developed during joint discussions and workshops between the parties concerned (IBM, client, plus potential 3rd parties).
Brief Outline

The overall contents of an Engagement Plan should include:

  • List of project benefits, objectives, and issues to be addressed by the prject and an agreed-to project scope and level of detail. Areas to explore should include:
    • Organizational boundaries
    • Functional boundaries
    • Agreed list of Work Products
    • Use of packages
    • Availability of key inputs (e.g. Strategies, Data & Process models)
    • Relevant platforms and environments; for example, business areas, database, networks, Office/MIS

Systems, Application Development and Service Management

  • Recommended next steps - for example, a list of information to be gathered, interviews, task assignments, reviewers, further definition of work product requirements and contents an overview of the agreed project approach, to include:
    • Responsibilities
    • Project style, for example use and timing of workshops
    • Timing, ownership and content of reviews
    • Project management
    • Documentation tools and techniques
    • Project logistics (locations, resourcing, working environment)
  • Documented assumptions, givens and constraints.
  • A high-level project plan for the proposed engagement - a very detailed project plan would normally be documented separately.
  • An information gathering plan - this plan would be used as an input to the detailed project plan.
  • Customized table of contents for final engagement deliverable, or for each work product, whichever is applicable.

Key Considerations

Engagement Planning (via Planning Workshop) is a key activity as this is where the IBM team lays down the foundations for the whole future success of an engagement. These planning workshops are often run as a separate chargeable engagement in their own right. There are many reasons and benefits (both to IBM and the client) for doing this:

  • Two half-day workshops may be run in a week allowing time for existing documentation to be collected.
  • The workshops allow the consultant time to review and assess the applicability/usefulness of existing documentation.
  • The consultant can more sensibly predict the number and type of interviews/data gathering needed.
  • More effective estimating, resourcing, costing and pricing of the main engagement can be performed.
  • Provides the opportunity for new IBM clients to see and experience the caliber and professionalism of IBM Consultants.
  • The client has a full engagement plan as a deliverable, which can then be used as appropriate. The client may undertake the main definition activities, with IBM Consultants acting as facilitators or reviewers.


The following are some basic hints and tips:

  • Where a single engagement spans work products that are produced by more than one method or discipline (e.g. IT Strategy, Enterprise Architecture and IT Process Model), make sure that you have involved consultants and resource from each of these areas in your planning activities. You will also need to use peer reviewers from multiple disciplines.
  • Choose a single method or approach to provide the overall framework for the engagement - it will probably be the one the engagement manager is most familiar with.
  • Run an internal IBM consultant team workshop(s) to agree the overall approach you will use before running any planning workshops and / or reviewing any draft plan with the client.
  • To meet client deadlines, you may have to run activities and develop work products in parallel. This requires careful planning, timing and consideration of dependencies and potential overlaps.
  • The work products produced during strategy, architecture and design projects can be to varying levels of detail. During the engagement planning activities, you should be discussing and agreeing these levels of detail with the client
  • Choosing your sponsor will be key. The sponsor should be the person in the client organization who has the most to gain if the project is successful and the most to lose if it is a failure.
  • The time between workshops provides an opportunity to assess the amount and level of information available as input to the Engagement Plan, the level of resource required, and the potential cost.
Tailoring
Impact of not having

Without this work product the project could fail for a number of reasons and the client will more than likely be dissatisfied. These include:

  • No shared understanding between IBM and the Client on the work to be performed
  • No shared understanding regarding the responsibilities of each party as the engagement proceeds
  • No vision of the tasks necessary to complete the required work
  • No effective basis for potential change management
  • A lack of definition of the Deliverables to be given to the Client
Reasons for not needingThe Engagement Plan is constructed only for smaller engagements. For those projects that last more than a few weeks, more formal work products should be used.
