Artifact: Change Cases (ARC 118)
The Change Cases work product documents future changes to the system or it's environment. Changes are relevant and deserve to be included if they affect the architecture and design now.
Domains: Requirements
Work Product Kinds: Requirement
Purpose

As with other requirements documents Change Cases are used to clarify scope and direction with the sponsor. Extra architecture and design work will be needed now to make sure the change cases can be met in the future at reasonable cost. The sponsor must be prepared to pay the extra cost. If not, the Change Cases should be reduced to what the sponsor is prepared to pay for.   

Change Cases are also used to evaluate the architecture. They influence the development process enabling the design of appropriate architectural features to minimize the impact of future changes.

Description
Main Description

The artifact focuses on potential changes to:

  • the system capabilities and properties
  • the way the system is used
  • the system operating and support environments

This artifact clarifies properties of the system described by the phrases, "easy to extend," "easy to port," "easy to maintain," "robust in the face of change," and "quick to develop."  Change Cases focuses on what is important and likely rather than what is possible. Change Cases tries to predict changes. Such predictions may not turn out to be exactly true.

Changes can arise from many sources, for example:

  • Business drivers: new and modified business processes and goals
  • Technology drivers: adaptation of the system to new platforms, integration with new components
  • Changes in the profile of the average user
  • Changes in the integration needs with other systems
  • Scope changes arising from the migration of functionality from external systems

Key Considerations

Availability of Goal Information: Clear goal statements from users’ sponsors often are not readily available. In such cases, the goals can be inferred by the people documenting the requirements. This approach may be expedient, but a more satisfactory approach is for the appropriate sponsors to take the time to formulate clear system goals.

Sources of Change Cases: Change Cases do not only come from the future needs of the users. The point of view of the sponsors should include all of the systems that may change as a result of the interaction with the target system and the new capabilities deployed. Also consider Change Cases evolving from anticipated technology drivers.

Kinds of Change Cases: Change Cases may be obtained from:

  • Future use cases for the target system or from related systems.
  • Development increments for the target system.
  • Changes in system users.
  • Changes in the hardware platform or peripherals.
  • Integration or interoperability required with other systems.
  • Alternate configurations of system functionality to serve future users.
  • Routine changes to the system made at build time (such as changes to tax laws in tax preparation programs).
  • Reuse of components from the target system in other systems.

Change Cases as a Product Plan or Product Line Plan: The Change Cases is effectively the same as a product plan or a product line plan. Such a plan contains the projected instances of growth, change, reconfiguration, and reuse for the immediate future of the product. If a product plan is not currently in place, the Change Cases could be offered as a starting point.

Exchanges between Run Time and Build Time Requirements: A major source of Change Cases is desirable requirements not within the scope of the current project. During the early stages of a project, there may be several adjustments in the scope of the project. Each change of project scope will result in an increase or decrease in the number of Change Cases that must be considered.

Tailoring
Impact of not having

Not documenting Change Cases makes it less likely the delivered system will be extensible, robust, and reusable. Merely documenting the requirements does not guarantee that the delivered system will be extensible, but it is a useful prerequisite.

Without clear and agreed Change Cases the developers may do work which is not recognized, or even for which they are not fully paid.

Without the influence of Change Cases on the architecture and design of the system, the following situations may arise in the lifetime of the system:

  • Changing requirements will cause excessive amounts of rework
  • New capabilities will be harder and costlier to provide
  • Robustness of the system will degrade, as new capabilities are "grafted-on" in expedient ways
  • System performance may get worse
  • Because of inappropriate implementation changes and extensions, defects may occur in other parts of the system
Reasons for not needingChange Cases are unnecessary if the sponsor and the development team have no incentive to think beyond the next release.
