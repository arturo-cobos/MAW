Artifact: Request for Proposal (BUS 063)
The Request for Proposal (or Request for Solution) provides an overview of the business objectives, company background, the project scope, its desired future business processes, and a brief overview of the current technical environment.
Domains: Selection
Work Product Kinds: Record
Purpose
The purpose of the Request for Proposal is to gather enough detailed information about the vendors in order to compare vendor solutions in detail. It solicits vendors, identified through the vendor analysis task, to assist the client in reaching a viable solution to their future business needs. Unlike the RFS, it includes more specific requirement and environment formats and often includes legal terms and conditions the vendors must accept before they go forward in the selection process.

Normally, this document is used in conjunction with the Requirements Matrix to evaluate each vendor’s functional "fit" to the client’s needs, but some engagements may require that the requirements be embedded in the RFP document.

Description
Main DescriptionThe Request for Proposal (RFP) is a formal procurement document that solicits responses from vendors to detail their software solution.  It presents a clear understanding of the software selection goals and objectives to the vendors and solicits the vendors to provide their software’s solution to address those business needs. It focuses on the major business processes, business performance goals and the essential system requirements associated to the desired business processes for the future.

Some clients may choose to use a more informal procurement document, the Request for Solution (RFS) instead of the RFP. The client’s procurement organization and guidelines will make this choice. The RFS also presents a clear understanding of the client’s software selection goals and objectives to the vendors and solicits the vendors to provide their software’s solution to address those business needs.

Brief Outline

The RFP (or RFS) is usually a word processing document that typically includes the following sections:

  • Title Page - A title page for the RFP should be created to display the organization name, logo and project name as well as the date of release to the vendors.
  • Table of Contents - A table of contents should be developed to assist ease of use for the vendor.
  • Procedures and Rules - A brief description of the RFP, its purpose and the selection process should be included.  This section should outline the RFP schedule, how to respond to the RFP and who should be contacted.  In addition, a standard statement of confidentiality appears in this section for the vendor to sign upon their response.
  • Legal Terms and Conditions (normally not required for the RFS) - A detailed overview of the terms and conditions required to participate in this software selection bid process. It includes:
    • Contract type
    • Rules for modification
    • Terms
    • Settlement of disputes
    • Confidentiality
    • Termination criteria
    • Penalty and Bonus clauses
    • Payment Schedule
  • Background - The background section should detail the organization, their current environment; any key issues or problems as well as their future business strategy.
  • Vendor Questionnaire - The vendor questionnaire section should consist of specific questions addressing the vendor’s company background and financial performance, their warranty policies, a technology overview as well as soliciting references for the reference check task.

Key Considerations

The RFP should ask the vendor to use a creative approach and the strengths of their product to offer progressive solutions to the client’s business needs.  It should include as much base information as possible to help clarify what the client is looking to facilitate with the software solution.

The RFP should be confined to the scope of the project Statement of Work (SOW).

Tailoring
Impact of not having

The Request for Proposal is required for software selection to determine the vendor short list for demonstrations. Without the Request for Proposal, the project team may demonstrate vendor software that is not a good match to the future business processes and they may also eliminate other vendors who would better satisfy those future business processes.

The Request for Proposal is also used to gather other vendor evaluation criteria beyond the software and support requirements. By eliminating the RFP, this information will not be available for evaluation.

Reasons for not needing

This work product would be not be required in the following scenarios:

  • The project team is conducting a software validation project, where the software vendor has already been selected
  • The short list of vendors has already been determined for functional demonstrations
