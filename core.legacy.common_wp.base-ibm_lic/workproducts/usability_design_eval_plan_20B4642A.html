Artifact: Usability Design and Evaluation Plan (APP 144)
This work product specifies the activities, deliverables, schedules, dependencies, process, team members, and other aspects of how user-centered design will be implemented throughout the course of the design and development of a solution.
Domains: User Experience
Work Product Kinds: Plan
Purpose

The Usability Design and Evaluation Plan ensures the proper level of usability attention is specified for a project and is communicated to the developers and stakeholders.  It allows the team to define, from the inception of a project, how user-centered design will be implemented. The purpose of the Usability Design and Evaluation Plan is to:

  • Ensure adequate user-centered design resources are allocated and scheduled
  • Ensure a clear and shared understanding of how user interface analysis, design, and evaluation will be addressed
  • Describe how the proper user-centered design activities will be staged to ensure that the implemented system meets stated usability requirements
  • Describe how the implemented system will meet the usability requirements for all users, including those with disabilities
  • Estimate how much a focus on usability will affect the project cost, project duration, and required skills and resources

Description
Main Description

The first stage of an evaluation is to develop an evaluation plan. Without a comprehensive evaluation plan, you will not be able to be thorough or organized in your evaluation and will have a much harder time gathering and interpreting data.

You should address most of the basic issues as follows:

  • Introduction and Background--Introduce the general plan and provide any necessary background information on the project or learners.
  • Purpose--What do you want to get out of this evaluation? What are you goals? You can have multiple purposes, but make sure that they are each clearly defined and that you’re not trying to gather too much information at one time.
  • Limitations--What are you going to exclude in this evaluation process? Will you come back to some of these issues later?
  • Audience--Who are the people participating in the evaluation? Have they already agreed to be involved or do you need to recruit them? How willing or unwilling are they? Do you need to provide any incentives? Is the accessibility audience included?
  • Decisions--What kinds of decisions will be affected by the evaluation? Are there any decisions that you will not be changing, no matter what the result of the evaluation?
  • Methods--Specify the evaluation design and procedure. Who will be recording data and how will they do it? How will you use the feedback you receive? Develop the questions you will be asking during the evaluation, as well as any forms, tests, tasks, or surveys that you want the participants to complete.
  • Logistics--Which of your team members will be participating? Do you need to bring in any outside assistance? Do you have the proper space for the evaluation (including computers, projector and screen, or any other technical equipment you may need)? Are there accessibility needs (such as large print documents or sign language interpretation) that must be met? This is the time to order any supplies you need, make sure everyone on your team can attend, and go through all the logistical elements of running the evaluation.
  • Timeline--If possible, evaluate early and often. Provide a schedule for each evaluation you want to complete, allowing sufficient time for analyzing the data you receive and making changes to the project.
  • Budget--How much will this evaluation cost? Consider supplies, participant incentives, room and equipment rentals, etc.

After you’ve created your evaluation plan, you should be well prepared to successfully complete your evaluation.

Brief Outline

This artifact consists primarily of sections of text with some tables based on the following standard headings:

  • Introduction: This section provides a description and purpose for the Usability Design and Evaluation Plan and describes why the work product is produced.
  • Engagement Overview: This section describes the client company in a few paragraphs and describes the specific organization IBM has engaged.  This section also typically describes the application and its intended benefit, particularly from an end user perspective.  The reader may be referred to supporting information such as the client’s Web site and the applicable contract under which the work is being performed.
  • User-Centered Design Process: This section provides a brief description of user-centered design, its benefits, and a general overview of how the process is achieved.  In addition, high-level usability goals and features are described.  Usability goals are refined into usability requirements later in the design process.  Usability features are refined into detailed usability design requirements later in the design process. Usability goals, design requirements, and later refined design requirements need to address the usability of the accessibility features of the project.  
  • Problem “Fix” Rate Model: This section identifies the problem fix rate model for the solution.  Problem severity is characterized in a three-level severity rating, which is consistent with that used in the industry.  There are three fix rate levels that are typically considered.  The selection of a particular fix rate level is negotiated with the client and guides the sizing of the usability design and evaluation effort.
    • Model 1: “Market Leader’” Fix Rate: This is appropriate for projects that need to claim a market leadership position in ease-of-use   This model targets fixing 100% of Level 1, 100% of Level 2, and 90% of Level 3 problems.
    • Model 2: “Meets Competition” Fix Rate: This is appropriate for projects that need to focus on meeting competitive, industry levels of ease-of-use  This model targets fixing 100% of Level 1, 90% of Level 2, and 65% of Level 3 problems. 
    • Model 3: “Maintain Status” Fix Rate: This is appropriate for projects where competitive, industry levels of ease-of-use are not judged to be important, but where a base level of ease-of-use is required.  This model targets fixing 100% of Level 1, 80% of Level 2, and 50% of Level 3 problems.
    • The levels of user problems are as follows:
      • Severity Level 1. Users are unable to continue with a task or series of tasks due to the problem.
      • Severity Level 2. Users have considerable difficulty completing a task or series of tasks but are eventually able to continue.  Users may fail to complete a task correctly.
      • Severity Level 3. Users have minor difficulty completing a task or series of tasks.  
  • Sizing Estimates, Activities, Deliverables, and Schedule: This section provides a high-level summary of the sizing estimates for the usability design and evaluation effort, the activities/tasks that will occur, and their associated hours, timeframe, deliverables, additional hardware requirements (to meet accessibility and resource requirements), and delivery schedule.  Any additional sizing estimates for accessibility support are included for the project.
  • Multidisciplinary Team: This section describes the members of the user-centered design multidisciplinary team that are responsible for the analysis, design, development, implementation, and evaluation of all components of the user interface.  This section also identifies whether the team member is a full- or part-time participant. A team member with accessibility skills should be considered.
  • Assumptions and Dependencies: This section describes assumptions required for any given deliverable or task, such as dependencies between user interface and application development tasks and other development tasks.  To meet accessibility requirements, there will be additional dependencies to provide assistive technology and perhaps recruit users with disabilities as part of the evaluation process. When users with disabilities participate in the evaluation, ensure the facility and orientation materials accommodate their special needs (large print, sign language interpreter, wheelchair ramps, etc.) by asking them for their special needs.
  • Change Control Process: This section describes the process for making changes to the usability design and evaluation effort, including the:
    • Assumptions
    • Design
    • Personnel
    • Other defined components of the project that serve as the basis for the selected activities and related plan

Key Considerations

The Usability Design and Evaluation Plan is typically 10 to 20 pages in length, depending on the size of the engagement.  It should provide a high-level overview of all of the user-centered design activities, but the details of each effort should reside in each of the detailed work products.

Often a design notebook is set up to track the user-centered design efforts.  A design notebook is a repository for the Usability Design and Evaluation Plan, and for all artifacts of the design process, including user feedback data and reports, code, design objectives, design rationales, prototypes of the user interface, visual design, and the design specification, among others.  The design notebook is a “living document” that changes and grows as the design progresses.  A record of the design activities of the team aids intra-team communication.  In the case of turnover of key team members, a new team member has the benefit of being able to review the evolution of the design.

Usability Design and Evaluation Plan should not be expanded to verify accessibility - that's not the purpose of this work product.  An Accessibility Design and Evaluation Plan should verify technical accessibility - for example that all images have alt text.  The Usability plan should however verify that when a blind user listens to the product the alt text is meaningful and useful.  It should verify that someone with a disability can use the product just as easily as someone without a disability.  See References for information on doing usability testing with users who have disabilities.

Additional advice includes:

  • Engage all key team members in creating the plan.
  • Be very specific with regard to team member roles, deliverables, schedules, etc.
  • Ensure special needs accommodations (large print, sign language translation, etc.) are considered when engaging the sponsor, stakeholders, and team members.
  • Secure necessary approvals for the Usability Design and Evaluation Plan prior to kicking off the project.
  • Secure training for team member and the team as a whole if necessary.
  • Discuss and document extent of team empowerment versus the need for management approval of various checkpoints.

Projects that need to claim compliance with Accessibility Standards should target fixing 100% of all accessibility defects.  Products deployed with known accessibility defects may meet the standards required by federal (U.S. EU, Australia, Japan, etc.), state and local government, education, and enterprises.

Tailoring
Impact of not havingIf this work product is omitted, developers will not have specifics about the needs and resources needed to perform the required usability activities. In the absence of the plan, the team will use some judgement and possibly omit some or all of these resources. An engagement team that operates without a Usability Design and Evaluation Plan risks delivery of a difficult to use application with low user and customer satisfaction, where user interface design was an ad hoc process resulting in missed schedules, unmet usability requirements, and possible budget overruns.
Reasons for not needingIn any engagement where an application user interface is proposed, a Usability Design and Evaluation Plan is required. If the work is primarily an internal code release no Usability Design and Evaluation Plan may be required.
