Artifact: Business Drivers (BUS 314)
The Business Drivers work product identifies the top business issues, challenges or priorities for the enterprise. They can be thought of as high level immutable requirements, which the strategy and its infrastructure (business and IT) must support.
Domains: Business Strategy
Work Product Kinds: Requirement
Purpose
Business Drivers are used to assist in assessing the focus and viability of business goals, strategies, strategic capabilities, architectures and the allocation of resources. Additionally, they assist in focusing efforts related to lower level capabilities and infrastructure. At the end of an engagement, consultants can use them to gain executive "buy-in" and help them answer the question as to why they should execute recommendations.

Description
Main DescriptionBusiness drivers could be external in nature (regulatory mandates) or internal (e.g., we must transform our business from product centric to customer-centric). While they are not as specific as business capabilities, they provide the context from which these capabilities are developed. Business drivers are an output of the business environment analysis. In this context they are sometimes called "industry" drivers. They are defined as major imperatives or motivations for a company to position itself in a particular way or for some business transformation.
 
All opportunities and threats identified during business environmental analysis can be external business drivers. In this case, they are used to begin developing strategic options and making strategic choices as well as determining what capabilities are needed. During capability modeling, external drivers become "internal" business drivers that "drive" the key actions of a company, whether it be the set of statements that focus the development of a particular infrastructure, or a set of statements that focus the actions related to a specific key process, organization, knowledge or technology.
Brief Outline

Business Drivers are expressed as a textual document, containing the following:

  • A statement or paragraph describing the rationale of why the driver is important or a major influence on the business, the sources of information and how they will subsequently be used.
  • A heading statement, which captures the essence of the driver - useful as a bullet on a foil
  • One or more paragraphs providing a more detailed definition of the driver, plus any related benefit statements
  • Key business drivers are usually three to five, and not more than ten.
  • A matrix of Business Goals, Critical Success Factors, Business Initiatives to Business Drivers, is often captured in a  spreadsheet, although some clients have successfully used graphical representations.
  • The data in a 'Business Drivers Impact Chart' is normally captured in a spreadsheet and translated into a graphical representation for presentation to executives.

Key Considerations

Care should be taken to not confuse drivers with goals, objectives and/or critical success factors or capabilities.  Items as these in the strategic direction are usually "driven" from the external Business Drivers or strength and weakness analysis of internal required capabilities.  Normally, drivers are fairly broad statements of the business needs, which must be satisfied by an appropriate Enterprise Architecture.

If the source of a driver cannot easily be identified, then the importance of the driver or its validity can be questioned.

Allow time for drivers to be reviewed, both by the team and by other influential business users - this is a key point to gaining acceptance and support for the many follow-on projects or activities.

Tailoring
Impact of not havingA business will find it difficult to focus on the most important aspects influencing it without an analysis of key business drivers. The business is therefore likely to inefficiently and ineffectively allocate resources and may lose competitive position. The lack of well-articulated business drivers may also indicate a lack of consensus or clarity on the business strategy.
 
It would also be difficult to assess the alignment of mission, vision, goals and critical success factors without this work product.
Reasons for not needing

This work product is not an essential work product.  Similar results can be gained by the simple prioritization of threats and opportunities found during environmental analysis activities and strength and weakness analysis during capability modeling activities.  Drivers are often used to create links to items that are further specified (e.g. goals, CSFs, capability enablers).

This work product may not be needed if this the client has already performed this analysis.
