Artifact: Business Case (BUS 082)
The Business Case work product documents and quantifies the benefits, costs, schedules and assumptions required to justify the implementation of the Transformation project recommendations.
Domains: Business Value & Financials
Work Product Kinds: Assessment
Purpose
The purpose of the Business Case is to justify investments, measure progress, secure resources, comply with legal requirements, communicate benefits, measure strategic fit, convince skeptics, and make go/no go decisions.
 
The purpose of the Business Case is to provide detailed identification, quantification and timing of expected financial and other business benefits (justifications) derived from implementing the transformation project, and to formally document those. The Business Case also identifies costs and assumptions used in the calculations, so as to provide the true understanding of the benefits. The Business Case can provide the record and road map enabling personnel to claim all of the identified benefits and for the management team to track actual versus planned benefits. The Business Case can also be the source for the final presentation when gaining senior management or Board of Directors approval to proceed with the transformation project.

Description
Main Description

The scope of the Business Case work product addresses economic considerations associated with project recommendations:

  • All identified "hard" benefits to the business, using an accepted, balanced and consistent financial evaluation approach
  • "Soft" benefits to the business, selected from among those identified
  • Selected "Quick Hit" benefits to the client that can be identified and claimed based on the project, which may or may not be solely due to the implementation of the recommendation
  • The timing and order of recognizing benefits, based on a high-level implementation plan
  • The costs of the recommendation, including (but not limited to) hardware, software and services, based on best available information
  • All assumptions used in calculating the costs, benefits and schedule

The Business Case development process is iterative and continuous throughout the project. The Business Case outlines how actual benefits or results can be measured, compared to existing metrics and benefits claimed, as recommendations associated with each business process or function are implemented.

Brief Outline

The Business Case Table of Contents may have the following sections:

Executive Overview

  • Describes project background, including primary business drivers, vision, goals, high-level requirements for new processes/focus, etc.
  • Defines scope by identifying the list of business processes included in the analysis, and the timing and status of the project
  • Describes approach such as team(s) size and composition, and summary of detailed project activities
  • Describes recommendation with summary of other solutions considered
  • Includes high-level summary of "hard" or quantifiable benefits with a presentation of measurements, such as total project benefits ($), total project expenditures ($), internal rate of return (%), net present value ($), discounted payback period (years) and/or other meaningful selected measurements.  May also include high-level cash flow analysis for 5-10 years ($/year)
  • Describes high-level summary of suggested "soft" or difficult to quantify benefits

Primary recommendations and rationale for each (Value Proposition)

  • Describe the recommendation concisely but in enough detail to enable business to understand clearly what is being proposed.
  • Indicate the specific Business Processes and Functional Areas involved in each recommendation
  • Explain the value drivers; e. g., Time Savings, Higher Quality, More Timely Offerings, Time Savings in Delivering offering Better utilization of Assets, Higher Value Offerings, etc.
  • Describe the Intermediate Outcomes & Performance Indicators (i. e., the mechanism through which the  value drivers will create value for the business and indicators that this mechanism is performing as expected): More Sales Calls, Higher Sales Closure Rate, Improved Customer Satisfaction, Greater Ability to Realize Premium Price based on Value, etc.
  • Economic Value Outcomes (The change in financial results as the results of acting on the recommendation): Revenue Growth, Higher Gross Margin, Lower Costs

Benefits

  • Describes the process of deriving benefits and the approach used
  • Describes the summary of benefits ($) by subgroup (team/functional/area/division/location/etc.)
  • Describes breakdown of benefits by each subgroup (team/functional/area/division/location/etc.), utilizing the same format to aid in comparison, understanding and roll up of costs.  Each subgroup should describe their particular benefits with a suggested format of:
    • What improvements are identified
    • How the new system will help achieve the benefits
    • How much benefits ($) are estimated
    • A summary of benefits ($) by year by subgroup (team/functional/area/division/location/etc.)

Costs

Documents best available Total Life Cycle Costs by year, typically consisting of costs/expenses for labor, consulting, training, hardware and infrastructure, hardware maintenance, software licensing, software maintenance, and post implementation components

Cash Flow Analysis

  • Documents Cost vs. Benefits, graphically showing non-discounted, post tax project costs in cost, benefit and net components by year for the project duration.  May want to show Net Present Value calculation also.
  • Documents Internal Rate of Return (IRR), graphically showing true and +/- 15% benefits sensitivity (85%, 100% and 115%)
  • Documents Break Even Analysis, graphically showing true and +/- 15% benefits sensitivity (85%, 100% and 115%) 
  • Team may want to also show the Discounted, post tax pay-back (years)

High-Level Implementation Plan

  • Documents criteria for selecting a pilot site
  • Defines Implementation and roll-out strategy
  • Documents entire implementation plan on quarterly basis by location by phase/module
  • Describes Resource Requirements at each site, showing number of people, total training (days), total data preparation (days), and total interface building (days)
  • Describes Benefit Performance tracking needs and reasons, including measurements (metrics tracked) and reporting start date and frequency

Next Steps

Describes the series of activities/tasks following the sign-off on the implementation.  Typically this could include internal tasks such as:

  • Approval of recommendations
  • Required changes (refinements) in the Business Case (if any)
  • Internal funding paperwork and approval
  • Approval for recommended process and organizational changes
  • Official announcement company wide of the project approval
  • Formation of the implementation team
  • This often includes external client tasks also such as:
    • Selection of integration partner (for I/T projects)
    • Final contract negotiations for software, hardware and services

Appendices

Documents in greater detail an analysis of Business Case financial calculations or other applicable Work Products developed during the project that contributed to the Business Case

Typical financial calculations would be:

  • Cash Flow Analysis details
  • Hard benefit details
  • Soft benefit details
  • Specific detailed financial assumptions that should not be included in the assumptions area (if any)
  • Any organization-specific details

Typical assumptions used in generating the Business Case related to:

  • Financial
  • Operational
  • Organizational
  • Schedule
  • Implementation and deployment: sequence and locations

Key Considerations

Management of the Business Case is extremely important. The Business Case is usually the primary documentation for the justification of the project’s recommendations and the method the client will use to measure the ultimate benefits of adopting those recommendations versus the current baseline. As such, it is essential to involve the client in all aspects of Business Case. Early in the Business Case task, the Project Manager should meet with the Client Team Manager and appropriate “client financial personnel”, to identify accepted client financial measurements and calculation techniques; appropriate areas for consideration, as well as areas for non-consideration and proper client personnel contacts and procedures. One of the main areas for non-consideration may be any claimed labor-savings if there is no reduction in headcount. This must be discussed with the client management before allocating resources to the calculations. Many times this type of labor cost saving is included as a “soft” benefit in the Business Case.

The Business Case must be validated by the project team, as well as by client management and financial personnel.  Attention should be paid to the Business Case being consistent with the design and implementation of the future ("Future") business processes.  The Project Manager must gain client agreement on the validation process early in the Business Case task.  Any claimed benefits should have appropriate client management sign-off as to the amount, timing and beneficiary area.  Lacking a true sign-off, the client management team must agree that the benefits are reasonable and obtainable, and this agreement must be detailed in the report.  Sometimes the facts outlined in the assumptions will change, causing the benefits to vary; however the client still expects the benefits to remain constant.  Only by accurately capturing and maintaining current assumptions, consistent with the final calculations, can client expectations be managed.

Tailoring
Impact of not havingA Business Case is usually required, depending on the needs and desires of the client, and therefore will usually be developed. Without a documented Business Case, the risk is great that decisions, identified benefits and assumptions, which were once very clear and well understood, will be lost, forgotten or distorted by the time actual benefits can be claimed. Typically, after the decision to proceed with the implementation of the transformation project is made, all thoughts of benefits are forgotten until after the system is up and running, the personnel trained, the multitude of procedures resolved, and the hectic pace of implementation normalized. Many of the justifications are based on tough business decisions on how to restructure an organization or function, at times causing displacement of people. Without the Business Case to solidly identify these decisions and agreements, client personnel may not act and benefits may not accrue to the project. Many times, the earlier knowledge is lost, and the benefits, cost and schedule need to be redone, involving additional cost, time and effort so that the client can calculate the benefits. A well-documented Business Case eliminates this effort by preserving all of the original thoughts, plans, calculations, assumptions and agreements.
 
The Business Case can also identify "Quick Hit" opportunities and claim their contribution to the benefits of the project. Sometimes client personnel know what should be done to improve their organization and processes, however the momentum of daily business, the threat of unpopular decisions, the lack of people to put it into action, the inertia of the organization, needed simultaneous improvements in other processes, a lack of vision, or any number of other reasons can cause the client organization not to act upon it. By incorporating these improvements into the Business Case, the benefits can be claimed by the project, the various processes can be improved and the client personnel can gain recognition as strong contributors. A plus from the project perspective is that a "quick hit" can usually be accomplished very early in the project, many times before implementation, thus improving greatly the attractiveness of cash flow calculations.
Reasons for not needing

A Business Case, while of primary importance in most projects, may be of minimal importance in others. Examples of these instances of minor importance could be:

  • The client must replace a system or reengineer a process for non-financial business reasons, and does not feel the need for additional expenditure to justify the decision (e.g., the existing does not meet regulatory requirements and must be replaced, changing business requirements forces the replacing of the existing system to meet customer needs, etc.).
  • The client has already allocated funds for the project and chooses not to spend additional funds for "unnecessary" justification.
  • The client chooses not to cause employee or organizational unrest by highlighting benefits that would cause efficiency-driven layoffs of personnel (e.g., the identification of labor related savings in a union environment, etc.).

In many of these situations, the Business Case may still be used as a management summary and reporting mechanism, but lack the strong emphasis on financial justification. All of this must be discussed and agreed upon with the client prior to the definition and costing of the project.

Representation Options

The Business Case is a presentation or word-processing document with inserts of spreadsheet templates, charts and graphs for certain components of data presentation.
