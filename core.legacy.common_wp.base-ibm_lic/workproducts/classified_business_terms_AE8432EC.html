Artifact: Classified Business Terms (APP 301)
The Classified Business Terms work product consists of two things: a classification structure of generic business concepts and a set of terms that are actually used by the business people in the particular business entity or organization under consideration.
Domains: Business Strategy
Work Product Kinds: Model
Purpose

This work product provides the foundation and much of the material for many other business modeling and system development work products.  Additional reasons for creating this work product include:

  • Anticipating information systems requirements. Experience has demonstrated that the more application functionality is provided, the more users demand. We can get out in front of this requirements gap by anticipating user needs before they materialize by understanding common patterns of semantic structure
  • Avoiding a focus on technological plumbing at the expense of the business content of the system being built
  • Removing the limitations of programming-based and engineering-based analysis paradigms. The Classified Business Terms word product supports a rigorous understanding of the meaning of business terms without clouding the issue with object-oriented or database design concepts
  • Expressing other work products (requirements, business processes and events, business rules, business object models, use cases, user interface and database designs) in terms understandable to the customer

The overarching goal of business language analysis is to ensure that the right thing is built for the customer in a way that supports the customer’s current and future information needs. Information system solutions should be built with a fundamental appreciation for the meaning and intent of business communications. Creating a set of Classified Business Terms forces the development team to listen to the customer effectively. It is an intensive effort to understand how the business people think and what they are concerned with. This work product resolves conflicting, ambiguous business language used by different work groups and organizations that need to reach a common understanding in their use of automated information systems.

Description
Main DescriptionThe terms are classified and grouped according to the more generic business concepts.  At the highest level, the types of classification include things like resources, results or products, locations, processes, events, time, roles, organizations, etc.  Resources can generally be subclassified as physical resources, monetary resources, information resources, energy, and capabilities.  The generic subclassification can continue, with information resources being classified as measurements, identifiers, history, etc.

The following is a useful starting set of concepts that apply quite generally to almost any business in the process of classifying the language used in that domain:

  • Organization
  • Role

  • Capability

Brief Outline

The Classified Business Terms work product consists of words and phrases that are actually used in the business area under study, classified and grouped by generic concepts that apply across businesses.

The basic form is:

  • Classification
  • Term
  • Another term
  • Still another term

As the set of terms grows and the knowledge of the business area increases, there are two refinements that can be made to the basic notation.  One is that more refined and specific classifications can be introduced within the high-level generic classifications.

  • Classification
    • A subclassification
  • Term
    • Another term
    • Still another term
    • Another subclassification
  • Term
    • Another term
    • Still another term

The other refinement is to recognize variations on the terms themselves, which focuses attention on specific distinctions of meaning in the business (and points to possible classes and subclasses of business objects).

  • Classification
    • A subclassification
  • Term
    • Modified term
    • Another modified term
    • Another term
    • Still another term

Key Considerations

It is important to create Classified Business Terms as early as possible in the project, before designers have locked themselves into a set of abstractions invented without the benefit of real business terminology.

Involve client personnel in analyzing the business language to ensure that the benefits of increased mutual understanding are fully realized.  It is imperative that this includes business owners and systems users, not just client IS people.

Before beginning a business language analysis, exercise the engagement team should conduct a thorough search of IBM’s intellectual capital databases for any existing Classified Business Terms that may apply, either because they are in the same industry domain or are related to a similar functional or application area.

Business language analysis is intrinsically an activity of validation and verification.  It firmly grounds the whole business model and resulting solutions in the business domain.  Some of the linkages and traceabilities that build on business language analysis include:

  • Completion Criteria: A key strength of the business terms approach is that it is relatively deterministic.  There are some straightforward tests that can be applied to determine that the Classified Business Terms list is complete and accurate for the business domain. These tests include:
    • All key client personnel have validated completeness of the sources used to build business language models
    • Terminology is converging–fewer and fewer new business terms are discovered in either written sources or conversations with domain experts
    • Terms have been completely classified by business concepts
    • New concepts have been incorporated into the classification scheme
    • Quality Criteria - A business person should be able to validate that the terms are meaningful to the business and are properly classified.
Tailoring
Impact of not havingWithout the kind of business understanding that begins with Classified Business Terms, the system developers run the risk of missing fundamental information requirements of the business. There is a heavy cost in moving forward with projects where the information requirements are not well understood. There is cost in time expended reworking inadequate requirements. There is cost in credibility when delivered systems do match the needs of the business.
Reasons for not needingThe only time this work product is not needed is when the work being done is purely technical in nature. When a team is simply re-architecting a middleware layer or swapping a persistence mechanism, there may be no business content actually addressed. In such a case there is no need to do any business modeling, including creating a list of Classified Business Terms.
