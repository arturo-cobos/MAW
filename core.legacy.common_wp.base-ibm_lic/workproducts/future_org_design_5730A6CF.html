Artifact: Future Organization Design (ORG 006)
The Future Organization Design work product details the new structure, performance enablers, and behaviors required to achieve the business vision.
Domains: Organization Design
Work Product Kinds: Specification
Purpose

A clear, simple description of the future organization is needed in order to:

  • Define and agree on the proposed organization
  • Understand the gap between the current and future organization
  • Define the change that has to be managed (e.g., introduction of new roles, responsibilities, and measures)
Relationships
Parent Deliverables
  • Architecture Management
  • Organization Assessment Deliverable
  • Transition Management Strategy Deliverable
Description
Main DescriptionThe design must support the requirements of the organization (such as new technology and business processes). It includes organizational groupings, team design, high-level jobs, roles and responsibilities, competencies and performance measurements, as well as organization culture.
Notation

Use standard organization chart formats that show work groups and reporting relationships. They should include any changes to department descriptions, major responsibilities, and any new customer interfaces. Both textual notation and graphical representation may be used to further explain the new designs. This includes an explanation of how the redesign meets key design points at the business unit, department and individual level; descriptions of how management systems and human resource programs need to be altered; and contrasts of the current culture with the culture desired in the future.

Key Considerations

Ensure that design points act as guidelines for designing the organization.

Do not expect to fulfill all the design points--seek compromises on the most critical areas. Be prepared to be flexible in how you achieve the design points--do not be dogmatic. As you design, capture the trade-offs you are making and articulate them explicitly in your documentation.

This is an iterative, developmental process. It is more like unraveling a mystery than solving a math problem. You will not achieve this in one attempt. It is very important to spread the work over several days so that the team can reflect on what they’ve done and generate new ideas for the next round. Record each iteration--good documentation of the evolution of the design can be very helpful during the review and approval step.

Keep focused on what is needed to implement and sustain the initiative that is driving the redesign. Stay in scope. If the new design has an effect on out-of-scope groups or elements of the management system, point out the implications but don’t attempt to redesign them.

Tailoring
Impact of not havingThe sponsor and the project team will not fully understand the organization-wide impact of the proposed transition. This will lead to difficulties in the implementation of initiatives in other domains (i.e., technology and process transitions).

Without this work product, the client will not see how to adapt the interrelationships between departments to get maximum value out of their transition and achieve the company vision and goals.
Reasons for not needing

The work product is not needed if an organization assessment has concluded that redesign is not needed

If an organization assessment has not been done, the need for this work product depends upon whether there is a need for redesign. If the proposed transition involves using new technology within the existing organization and its processes, then there will be no need for redesign. However, most initiatives do have a ripple effect on the organization. Even if the changes are incremental rather than radical, make sure all of the following conditions are met before eliminating this work product from the work plan:

  • Few major leaps in skills are required, and existing skills will generalize easily to new requirements with minor training
  • Few new job roles or jobs are required
  • With a little fine-tuning, any new work can be performed well within the existing units, teams, or work groups
  • Overall size of the organization does not change
  • Factors which influence compensation do not change
  • Individual performance expectations undergo little change

Even if there is no organization redesign, it is still advisable to carry out some form of cultural analysis to understand the difficulties of introducing any degree of change in the existing organization.

If the organization redesign is being done by the client and is therefore out of the engagement's scope, this work product is not needed
