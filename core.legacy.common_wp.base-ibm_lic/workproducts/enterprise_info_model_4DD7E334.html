Artifact: Enterprise Information Model (ARC 307)
The Enterprise Information Model work product represents the strategic information requirements of the enterprise.
Domains: Enterprise
Work Product Kinds: Model
Purpose

The development of an Enterprise Information Model has several purposes with the exact emphasis and priority depending on the nature and objectives of the specific project. The main reasons for developing an Enterprise Information Model are:

  • To provide a strategic overview and understanding of the major high-level groups of information needed to manage the business and support the processes in the process definitions.
  • To provide architectural parameters and boundaries for subsequent data analysis and data design activities. It sets the baseline.
  • To provide high-level planning constructs with respect to the data / information needs of the enterprise.
  • To enable and support a proper alignment between the key information requirements of the business & its partners, with the goals and objectives of the enterprise.
  • To enable consultants and senior management to explore the constraints on the business and opportunities for, and implications of change.
  • To provide a useful vehicle for communication and ownership.
Description
Notation

Enterprise Information Model depicts, in both graphical and textual form, the structure and content of the key categories, or "subject areas" of persistent data that need to be managed by the enterprise.

The Enterprise Information Model will usually comprise several separate, but related, components:

  • An Entity Relationship Diagram (ERD): showing the network of major entities or groupings of entities (information subject areas) and the relationships between them
  • Entity, or information subject area, definitions
  • Relationship Definitions

The Entity Type and Relationship Definitions add detail to the Entity Relationship Diagram by defining all of the objects on the diagram.

Key Considerations

The Enterprise Information Model has many similarities to a "logical data model". Both are interested in identifying the information / data that the business manages and both will use a similar graphical notation accompanied with text to represent this. The Enterprise Information Model, however, will be at a significantly higher level of abstraction and is more concerned with gaining an understanding of the scope and scale of the information that is managed by the enterprise. Rather than defining the detailed specifics of any one-subject area, the Enterprise Information Model is more focused on knowing that a class of information exists and that it is important.

The Enterprise Information Model, once developed, is highly reusable and will provide the architectural basis for all subsequent analysis and requirements projects. The development of logical data models will use the Enterprise Information Model as input.

There is a very strong relationship (or "synergy") between the development of the Enterprise Information Model and the business process definition. Many of the activities in the process definition exist primarily to manage the business information and each piece of information owes its existence to an activity that creates and manages it. Each of these models can be used as input into the development of the other and in most cases they will be developed in parallel and by the same people. More detail on how these are developed in parallel is provided in the "Enterprise Information Modeling" Technique Paper.

When related to the conceptual framework for Enterprise Information Architectures developed by John Zachman, (see references in Reference Section), the Enterprise Information Model represents the "Ballpark" & "Owners" view of the data architecture.

While the Enterprise Information Model should cover the full scope of the enterprise, in some engagements this may not be possible/feasible, given time constraints, etc. If this is the case a 'limited scope' Enterprise Information Model can still be constructed and used as a starting point for further modeling work. (i.e., while the enterprise model can be built in pieces, this must be done by extension of a single model and not by the uncoordinated development of several separate models).

The Enterprise Information Model can be used in the early stages of software package selection. The high-level subject areas can be mapped to the data model of the potential packages to determine, at a high level, the "fit" of the package to the enterprise information requirements.

The model must be a good cognitive representation of the strategic information requirements. The business stakeholders, including those involved in the development of the model should validate and approve the contents.

Tailoring
Impact of not having

Without this work product, it is not possiible to demonstrate an understanding of the structure and content of the information requirements. This means:

  • There is no basis for partitioning the organization’s data and scoping subsequent projects.
  • There is a danger of missing important information needs and MIS implications.
  • Some key functional requirements may be missed.
  • An assessment of information sharing requirements, across the business units within the enterprise, is not possible.
  • An assessment of the impact of requirements and design decisions on future options is not possible.
  • There is no foundation to develop a coherent database strategy.
  • There is no assurance that business goals and objectives will be properly supported.
  • The accuracy of the estimation of subsequent projects may be affected.
  • There is a potential for delay in follow-on SI projects.
Reasons for not needingThere are two scenarios where this work product would not be developed as part of the engagement:
  1. Work Product already exists: Many customer organisations may already have developed this work product, (or something very similar to it). This will usually be at a variable level of detail and may exist in fragments resulting from separate projects. In any situation, the existing model(s) should be reviewed and its currency checked. Where detailed project-specific models exist, they will require to be generalised and "leveled up".
  2. Limited Engagement Scope: Some enterprise-wide based architecture engagements may have a limited scope and focus primarily on the IT Architecture levels. In such engagements there may be no requirement to identify the major types of business information that the enterprise manages.
Representation OptionsThe recommended notation for this work product is that used for Entity Relationship modeling. There are several "accepted" variations of this notation, all recognizing the same basic constructs. The most common are those adopting the Information Engineering approach or the "Integration Definition for Information Modeling (IDEF1X)" approach.
