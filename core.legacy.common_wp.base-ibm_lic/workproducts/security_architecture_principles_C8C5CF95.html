Purpose

The Security Architecture Principles work product provides:

  • the framework for a consistent approach in decision making about the security of corporate information; guidance for how to balance risk and investment;
  • linkage to business objectives; for the definition of a security architecture, policies, standards, products etc;
  • relates the value of security of information back to the needs of the business.

Security Architecture Principles, combined with corporate security policies, provide the direction for development of technology/product-specific security standards and related processes/procedures, as well as the business justification for the architecture.

Description
Main Description

The Security Architecture Principles indicate the direction, policies define how the principles are implemented and standards define the rules that enforce the policies. The work product may cover the entire enterprise (Generic) or be limited in scope (Design) to address a particular subset of the enterprise such as:

  • Identification and Authentication.
  • Authorization and Access Control
  • Message Protection.
  • Detection and Monitoring
  • Auditing/ Logging
  • Resilience
  • Security Management

Key Considerations

The Security Architecture Principles should be validated against other related work products to ensure that they are consistent, the strategy does indeed address the business requirements, they move the organization towards their desired strategic vision and they are aligned with the enterprise's business and IT models.  In particular, Security Architecture Principles should be validated against the:

  • Security Strategy
  • Security & Privacy Policies
  • Security & Privacy Requirements
  • Information Asset Profile

This work should only be performed by Methodology-trained Security professionals.Security related Principles and workshop guidance are available in the IT Security ICAP Database.
Tailoring
Impact of not havingLack of Security Architecture Principles will result in decisions that may be inconsistent across the enterprise, may not support business and IT strategies and may result in a lack of standards and processes to support those strategies. A lack of Principles will mean decisions are made in isolation and cause inconsistent investments in risk mitigation and result in over-investment or under-investment in the risk controls. When there are no agreed security architecture principles to guide management decisions and solution development, risks increase dramatically that human errors and poor controls will enable a organization's critical information to be compromised in terms of confidentiality, integrity, privacy or availability.
Reasons for not needing

If the client has or is in the process of developing an Enterprise (IT) Architecture, then a separate Security Architecture Principles work product would normally not be developed. Instead, security architecture principles would be integrated in the Enterprise Architecture. Also, if the client has a current, comprehensive, enterprise security policy and technology-independent security standards that contain security architecture principles, there is no need to develop this separate work product.

Client indicates that security and privacy considerations are out of the engagement scope (e.g., when engagement is a proof of concept or rapid development of a prototype). In this case, consider adding the approved Security Disclaimer to the SOW (see References below).

On engagements with a very narrow focus (as opposed to an enterprise level), rather than including this work product, consider proposing a separate engagement to develop an Enterprise Security Architecture for the client.
