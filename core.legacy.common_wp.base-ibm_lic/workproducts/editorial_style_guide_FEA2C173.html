Artifact: Editorial Style Guide (ENG 426)
This artifact provides a baseline for editorial quality control and serves as a reference for editing. The Style Guide provides information about everything starting from basic style and pattern and layout etc.
Domains: Environment
Work Product Kinds: Standard
Purpose
A style guide is a time-tested reference tool for any editorial endeavor. This artifact helps to maintain editorial consistency of the content presentation throughout a Web site or interactive media application, streamlining the editing process for content strategists/editors.
 
Editorial consistency is also extremely important for users accessing content through certain assistive devices such as screen readers, and for users who have cognitive disabilities. Style guides provide advice on presentation and interaction such as “when a user has to make a choice from a set of options, use radio buttons.”

Description
Main DescriptionThe style guide acts as the basis for style, tone, voice, grammar, accessibility (editing language) requirements, and usage, and contains rules applied to terms specific to the interactive media application, including Web-based or e-business types of applications. The style guide provides information about structure, page layouts and formatting specific to the presentation of the content; it should also contain accessible (editing) requirements and rules to make resulting documents accessible.

This work product triggers checks for accessibility compliance. Therefore, when the Style Guide is created, take care to incorporate metrics to evaluate this requirement.
Brief Outline

A style guide sets standards for text-based content usage. Examples of style guide standards may include, but are not restricted to:

  • When and how to reference company-specific verbiage
  • Glossary of terminology and acronyms (description of acronyms is especially important for screen reader users and people who have cognitive impairments.)
  • Use and placement of punctuation and grammar (The correct usage of punctuation is especially important for deaf and cognitively-impaired users for conveying the correct meaning.  Deaf people, for example, sometimes have difficulty perceiving the “pauses” that must be presented when writing dialog.)
  • Web terminology usage
  • Terms that include trademarks and/or copyrights
  • Language us addressing disabled users
  • Advice on interaction scenarios
  • Words that can and cannot be used (for example, are foreign language words acceptable?)
  • Sentence length and style

Basic editorial guidelines are just that – guidelines that everyone who is creating editorial content should know in order to create clear, well-written content. A style guide is not intended to be a comprehensive guide to Web or interactive media design or style, nor will it be able to address all terms and concepts. Alternative style guidelines can also be used to help govern editorial standards. The style guide helps us to make thoughts clear and make the writing in reports, policies, etc. easier to understand.  Whenever possible, rely on customer style guidelines.  Some customers have different guidelines for editorial style (written) and visual style (appearance) and the two may be closely related or different.  Some customers even have different style guides depending on the media, for example, a different look for PowerPoint presentations and Web deliverables. Highlight the aspects of the style that are best suited for your environment. 

Guidelines are often applied to a “class” of users in an enterprise rather than the entire enterprise.  For example, data entry users may have different requirements than data analysts.  Mobile and desktop users may have different style requirements from each other.

Key Considerations

A style guide should never be static. This document is meant to change and grow over time. It will change as trends evolve, as new corporate policies are adopted, and as issues of style are debated and decided upon within an organization. A document such as this must be flexible, adaptable, and accessible. It will never be fully useful if it is not available for reference by anyone who is creating content for that organization.

The style guide should be short and flexible, steering people to write in “the spirit” of the guidelines. Nitpicking over language in, or style of a deliverable delays project completion!

A new style guide should be validated through internal proofreading checkpoints and scheduled client review sessions.

Tailoring
Impact of not having

The danger in not having an Editorial Style Guide would be a lack of consistency. If the tone, style, and usage of language and terminology are not uniform, users can become confused and even question the credibility of the website or interactive media application. Furthermore, the following factors can increase difficulty in understanding for people who have disabilities and those who do not:

  • Lack in clarity in the language
  • Lack of consistency in linking or the presentation of content,
  • Inconsistent format and visual placement.
 
An Editorial Style Guide can reduce the risk of such inconsistencies, especially when multiple contributors in different locations are responsible for creating and editing content.
Reasons for not needingThe application uses very little or no content, or an existing style guide is already in regular use.
