Artifact: Test Measurements (APP 316)
The Test Measurements provide a management perspective on all the metrics and measurements gathered and collated during and at the closure of the testing project.
Domains: Test
Work Product Kinds: Measurement
Purpose

The Test Measurements are used:

  • By the originating testing team to evaluate their performance in the context of their testing project, by comparing the Test Measurements with the initial planning estimates and defect management objectives.
  • By the originating testing team to evaluate their performance in the context of the enterprise capabilities in testing.
  • By the quality and test organization management to monitor trends in the performance of the testing teams in order to identify and improve the effectiveness and capacity, across the organization.
  • By the quality and test organization management to benchmark their performance against best practice in a similar industry and sized organization, with comparable complexity of applications and underlying technology, and resources available.
  • By the application development function, in the same fashion as above to improve their capability and capacity to achieve the quality objectives of the organization and move towards a best practice focused on a satisfactory ratio of quality to cost.

The Test Measurements are used:

  • By the originating testing team to evaluate their performance in the context of their testing project, by comparing the Test Measurements with the initial planning estimates and defect management objectives. This facilitates identification of strengths and weakness of the application development and testing teams, plus those of the test management and testing process followed during the project.
  • By the originating testing team to evaluate their performance in the context of the enterprise capabilities in testing. By comparing the Test Measurements from their own team and those of across the organization, highly effective areas and areas requiring improvements will be identified. If the measurements show that the team was better than the enterprise current best practice, then the project should be examined to explain how this performance was achieved. The findings should be shared with the testing organization and incorporated into standard practice where applicable.
  • By the quality and test organization management to monitor trends in the performance of the testing teams in order to identify and improve the effectiveness and capacity, across the organization, of:
    • the testing process and techniques
    • the test management process
    • the skills and resource deployment (from the testing, development, technology and user functions)
    • the number of hours invested in the project
    • the technology (e.g. testing tools) deployed; and
    • the return on investment of all of the above.
  • These management decisions could be based upon the analysis of,
    • the accuracy of the test planning estimates
    • the practicality of the defect management objectives
    • the profile/project mix/experience of the roles/responsibilities in the development, test and user teams
    • the complexity of the applications and the underlying technology
    • the number of change requests to the scope of the project
    • the actual time utilized in each phase of the Testing project
    • the successful mitigating of identified enterprise and project risks
    • etc.
  • By the quality and test organization management to benchmark their performance against best practice in a similar industry and sized organization, with comparable complexity of applications and underlying technology, and resources available. This may prompt the enterprise (through understanding their areas of vulnerability and conducting external research) to embark on efforts to
    • acquire better or different development, testing or project management skills
    • re-engineer their testing, development or project management processes
    • become stricter in enforcing quality and test standards
    • invest in new testing tools and platforms
    • educate current employees; and
    • other initiatives to bring the organization competitive advantage in the quality and integrity of its systems, processes and data.
  • By the application development function, in the same fashion as 1, 2 and 3 above to improve their capability and capacity to achieve the quality objectives of the organization and move towards a best practice focused on a satisfactory ratio of quality to cost.

Description
Main DescriptionThe Test Measurements include a consolidated summary, in a meaningful predefined format, of the metrics and measurements collected during test planning, test management, defect management, static and dynamic testing throughout the testing project, plus metrics and measurements reflecting the total testing project. The Test Measurements should satisfy executive, management and technical requirements for information on the capacity and capability of the development, infrastructure, test and other stakeholder organizations as demonstrated in this project. This information can be used to compare actual against plan and to predict how these groups will meet current and ongoing quality objectives of the overall organization.
 
The Test Measurements must be organized according to the guidelines created by the quality management and test organizations, so that trend analysis can be carried out by test management for improving the quality and test functions based on the heuristics collected over time from testing projects.
 
For the purposes of this document, the term "measurements" and "metrics" are used interchangeably.
Brief Outline

Below is a recommended Test Measurements table of contents:

  • Project Name and Description - Describe the testing project in terms of size, objectives and functionality, giving details of the testing approach (and Engagement Model if adopted) followed for the project.
  • Measurements and Metrics Produced - Describe the metrics that were produced by this project categorized by all or a combination of (1) Testing phase (2) Test Focus Area (3) Test Types by level: Operability, Acceptability, Systems Integration, Systems, Integration, Unit Tests (4) Dynamic Tests (5) Static Tests (6) Testing Project and how (in what order and format) they are presented in this report.  Refer to the sample Table of Contents below if your organization has no pre-defined format for reporting Test Measurements.
  • Measurements Section - Test Measurements summarized to a meaningful level of detail are presented here, preferably in a high-level table/spreadsheet format, accompanied by (if possible) a graphical representation to emphasize important areas of focus.  Lower levels of detailed metrics and measurements should be put into the appendix of the report for further analysis if required. They should be organized in such a way that the testing project itself and another section could use a contiguous section by the testing organization.
  • Conclusions and Recommendations - From the information presented in the Measurements Section and the accompanying findings, document the conclusions and recommendations for the testing team and the testing Organization.  Ensure that these recommendations enable these functions to meet the objectives:
    • described in – Purpose of Work Product above;  and
    • specifically to address the CSFs for successful Quality and Test Management and areas of weakness in the organization.
  • Contacts - A list of contact names, listing the areas of responsibility for the Test Measurements, should be provided for future reference in the event that assistance or clarification is required by interested parties.

Key Considerations

Keep the Test Measurements simple and meaningful.  Measurements that are easily understood, collected, analyzed and presented will be more readily created and accepted.  Many academic publications provide complex statistical algorithms for calculating and reporting Test Measurements.  However unless the Testing organization is highly complex (many heterogeneous technologies, complex applications and external interfaces, sharing of data across numerous lines of business, distributed teams, etc), it is best to maintain a simple set of measurements that are cost-effective to produce, analyze and use.

There exist in the marketplace many Testing tools that provide the facility to gather Test Measurements during dynamic testing.  The choice of tool will depend on the underlying technology of the applications being tested and the ROI expected by the organization on this investment.  It may be preferable when initiating a Test Measurements program, to use a spreadsheet to capture the metrics and to graph them as “prototypes” until Test Management decides what information is required to be gathered.  The findings can then be used as criteria for acquisition of Testing tools to manage the management information most supportive of the Testing organization’s objectives.

It may be beneficial to measure Defect Rate against Development Productivity (metrics from the Application domain), as the inefficiencies may not always lie within the Testing function. 

[Rubin and Ed Yourdon conducted an international study of software development productivity and quality for the federal department industry Canada. The study involved 227 companies in the United States, South America, Europe, South Africa, The Far East, and India. The study found that Canadian software developers were much more productive than the worldwide average. They accounted for 111 function points per developer compared to the worldwide average of 92.5 function points per developer. The results also indicated that Canada had the highest defect rate, 5.12 defects per thousand lines of code. The worldwide average is 3.79 defects per thousand lines of code. The United States had the overall lowest defect rate, 2.3 defects per thousand lines of code.  The study supports the hypothesis that the faster you write software the more defects you make. The United States which had the lowest defect rate creates software at an average (88 function points per developer) slightly lower that the worldwide average.]

Detailed information on how to classify defects (for process improvements and root cause analysis) using a standardized, rigidly defined categorization technique, may be found at http://www.research.ibm.com/softeng/ODC/ODC.HTM.  At present the examples on these pages are geared towards the development of systems software, but the analogy can be adopted for application development.  The IBM Testing Competency is currently investigating the inclusion of this technique into the IBM Global Services Method – Testing Subdomain.

Some additional suggested industry-standard Testing Metrics and Measurements may be found by referring to the ISO standard – ISO-9126, but caution is advised in their adoption as it could become a lengthy and costly program.
