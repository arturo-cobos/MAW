Artifact: Business Roles and Locations (ARC 303)
The Business Roles and Locations work product documents the roles and locations that are pertinent to the enterprise's business.
Domains: Enterprise
Work Product Kinds: Inventory
Purpose

Business Roles and Locations are used to:

  • Understand how business activities and information should be deployed relative to the enterprise business structure.
  • Provide input on the requirements and possible constraints on the placement of application function, data and technology.
  • Provide a framework for the placement / deployment of applications, data & technology.
  • Understand and define the user groups.
  • Scope future development and migration efforts, taking into account the total set of processes that a role is responsible for.
  • Understand the potential impact of any proposed new architectures on the current organizational and business structure.

Description
Main Description

Business roles are logical, categories of jobs that perform the business activities of the enterprise, (e.g. Underwriter, Claims Assessor, Regulator, Loan Recipient, Sales Person). As such, they combine business activities into a recognizable relationship with the enterprise. Although these roles will very often appear to be the same as positions in the organizational structure, this is not the case and organizational positions will usually perform several business roles. Business Roles are strongly related to the functions and activities of the enterprise and are defined by the primary function/activity that they perform. So, for example, the "Claims Assessor" role is defined by the activity of assessing claims, even though individuals who perform that activity may perform other activities as well.

Business locations identify both the physical and logical places, or sites, where the activities of the business are carried out. The physical locations are simply the geographical places where the business has a presence; the logical locations are classifications, or categories, of these physical sites, based on the types of business activities that are performed at them. Examples of logical locations are Head Office, Satellite Branch, Warehouse, Claims Unit, Document Reception Center, etc.

Having identified the Roles and Locations, the relationship between these and Business Activities is documented in the following matrices: Roles Vs Locations, Roles vs Activities, Locations vs Activities.

Key Considerations

Although logical roles and organizational positions should be considered as different, it is inevitable that there will be cases where the two are identical.

If the Business Structure work product has been produced, the "Logical Locations" identified will provide a starter list of locations and may suggest roles.

Roles, job titles, and organizational positions all may exist in many-to-many relationships to each other. Roles can be even more granular, such as "opportunity noticer" or "call recorder" - be aware of going to too low a level of detail at this stage. You will probably end up with between 10 - 40 roles depending on the scope of the engagement and the complexity of the enterprise.

Physical locations correspond very closely to the ‘location’ concept in the IBM Application Description Standard (ADS) for IT systems.

Although this work product defines logical locations, these will usually reflect some aspects of the organizational structure and the physical locations.

Various levels of validation are possible:

  • walkthrough of the business processes
  • walkthrough of the various organization charts
  • review and verification with client sponsor and executives
Tailoring
Impact of not having

Without this work product:

  • It would be difficult to identify and define user groups, (Note; user groups can be "brainstormed" in a single session, however this still requires an understanding of the major roles and locations of the enterprise)
  • There will not be a sound basis for understanding where and how business activities and information are used in an enterprise
  • It will be impossible to make informed decisions on the deployment of applications, data and technology, even at a high-level.
Reasons for not needingProject scope does not cover/require the definition or understanding of business roles and locations or these already exist.
Representation OptionsThe Business Roles and Locations are defined using text, matrices and diagrams. They can be represented graphically, i.e., in a "rich" picture. At a minimum they are captured in a table or spreadsheet that would include the role/location name, a brief description and some quantitative data, e.g., numbers of staff, volumes of business processed, etc.
