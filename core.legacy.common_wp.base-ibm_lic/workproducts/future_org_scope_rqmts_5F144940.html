Description
Main Description

This work product describes and gains agreement to:

  • The geographies, divisions, and levels to be addressed in redesign
  • The elements (structure, behaviors, and enablers) of the organization to be addressed in redesign
  • Organization design points that outline the principal changes to be made
  • The cost, risks, and expected value of meeting the proposed design points

The report should be discussed and reviewed extensively with the client, and agreement with it forms the basis for all future organization design work.

Notation

The scope should be produced as an annotated version of a nine-cell organization design matrix. The matrix is annotated with explicit references to the target organization units and management system elements (e.g., the jobs, functions, and geographies that will be affected or HR programs that need to be redesigned).

The items in the cells can show in-scope elements in a different font or color than out-of-scope elements; if whole cells are in or out of scope this can also be shown by shading or color-coding.

The design points can be presented in text form and organized according to a variety of factors, such as priority, impact to project goals or future state vision, customer or employee-value add, or columns and rows in the nine-cell matrix. The narrative should articulate the linkage of the design points with project goals, future state vision, assessment results, and best practices.

Key Considerations

Experience shows that it is important to get this scope right and to get it in focus at an early stage in the project while the other domains of redesign (process and IT) are also evolving. This allows the integration of redesign and implementation activities into the overall transition plan.

Most transformation projects require, as a minimum, the redesign of:

  • Key job roles and responsibilities (in terms of activities, behaviors, and measurements) particularly those of process owners and key users
  • Cross-functional working patterns, team working, etc.
  • The performance management system that motivates the new behaviors

IBM and the client need to be very clear on what redesign will entail--the scope, the direction, and the risk. Anything that has been eliminated from the scope should be explicitly documented. If the client wants to carry out some redesign activities internally, this is the time to set clear expectations and devise an issue management approach to avoid resource conflicts. For multi-site or -function projects, there may be a need to conduct multiple reviews to gain agreement at the local level as well as the project level.

Review the analysis and recommendations with the client and probe for concerns, objections, and signs of resistance or faltering commitment. Test for their understanding of the possible implications of redesign. Ensure that you have their agreement to both scope and requirements before proceeding with redesign.

Tailoring
Impact of not having

Without this work product:

  • the organization redesign may neglect elements that will then be out of alignment
  • effort could be wasted on elements that do not need redesign or that are already being addressed through other initiatives

Failure to produce this work product may result in "scope creep", allowing IBMâ€™s work plan and budget to lose integrity.

Absence of agreed-upon design points increases two risks: unmet client expectations; and designing different organizational elements to different criteria, thereby producing a misaligned design.

Without a risk/value analysis, the project may create a redesign whose implementation involves greater risk than the client is prepared for, or that will deliver less benefit than the risk warrants. Undue risk in one area can taint the clientâ€™s perspective about the broader project and cause it to pull back on other implementations necessary for overall project success.

Reasons for not needing

This work product is not needed if an organization assessment has concluded that redesign is not needed

If an organization assessment has not been done, the need for this work product depends upon whether there is a need for redesign. Most initiatives do have a ripple effect on the organization and create the need for redesign. Even if the changes are incremental rather than radical, redesign should be eliminated only if all of the following conditions are met:

  • Few major leaps in skills are required, and existing skills will generalize easily to new requirements with minor training
  • Few new job roles or jobs are required
  • With a little fine-tuning, any new work can be performed well within the existing units, teams, or work groups
  • Overall size of the organization does not change
  • Factors that influence compensation do not change
  • Individual performance expectations undergo little change

If the organization redesign is being done by the client and is therefore out of the engagement's scope, this work product is not needed
