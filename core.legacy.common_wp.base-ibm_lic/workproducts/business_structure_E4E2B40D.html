Artifact: Business Structure (BUS 334)
The Business Structure work product is a model that is used to identify sub-processes and their details.
Domains: Business Strategy
Work Product Kinds: Model
Purpose
The Business Structure analysis will identify the requirements for a business architecture that is aligned to the strategy. This business architecture will identify the sub-processes and the business capabilities that the company needs to implement to support its strategic configuration.
 
By checking whether those sub-processes may be shared or replicated by several business units of the enterprise, potential economies of scope and scale will be identified. By capturing the data and information technology requirements of the sub-processes, this analysis will provide a direct input into the development of supporting architectures such as the to-be data model and IT architecture.
Description
Main Description

This work product identifies the following:

  • The sub-processes that are unique and fully self contained.
  • The deployment architecture (centralized / dispersed, shared / replicated) of those sub-processes.
  • The linkages between sub-processes across the enterprise (degree of linkage - loose / tight ) and between the sub-processes and external entities - customers, suppliers.
  • The opportunities to achieve economies of scope, scale and experience from those sub-processes.
Key Considerations


 

  • The goal of modeling the enterprise is to develop a "to-be" model. However, an "as-is" process model must be developed to understand the organization's current environment, challenges, and deficiencies. Additionally modeling of the "as-is" does not need to be as extensive as the "to-be" modeling effort.  "As is" and "to be" information can be gathered simultaneously in the interviews and workshops. The questions are phrased to capture the "as-is" and "to-be" models. However, use only those questions that are appropriate.
  • Workshops should have cross-functional participation of client executives, 8 to 10 executives with knowledge of various aspects of the business.
  • Special Case: In working with companies that play in an industry with great uncertainty, this work product should be applied to the strategic business scenarios that are identified.  This will enable an organization to more readily adapt its structure as the attributes of the scenario begin to play out.  Obviously in this case, estimations of how long it takes to create this work product should be extended.
Tailoring
Impact of not havingThis work product provides key inputs to the development of the IT architecture. Without this work product, there will not be a clear understanding of how the business needs to structure itself in the future and thus there will be no guarantee that the subsequent IT architecture will support the business process sharing requirements.
 
The Business Structure incorporates the organizational dimension for business requirements. It analyzes how sub-processes should be deployed within the organization, and whether business capabilities have common or specific requirements. Not performing this analysis would lead to the gathering of generic requirements, ignoring organizational specifics. Implementing those requirements would lead to scalability problems.
Reasons for not needingThis work product is not needed when the client may have already performed an analysis of the business processes and in cases where the client is a small business with just one or two business units and relatively low complexity, a detailed business design may not be required. A simple enterprise model showing the sub-processes and their interrelationship will suffice.
Representation Options

A graphical model or matrix which illustrates the relationships of logical work or business units and the processes or capabilities that are common, replicable or unique across the business units.
