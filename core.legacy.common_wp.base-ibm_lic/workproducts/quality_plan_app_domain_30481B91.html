Description
Main Description

The Quality Plan for Application Domain describes the organization, activities, and project factors that have been put in place to achieve the planned level of quality for all work products in the application domain.  It provides a framework for continuous improvement through quality reviews.  Through the use of the project quality plan, if one exists, it defines the approach to be taken when planning and tracking the quality of the application development work products to ensure conformance to specified requirements and that expectations are being met.  However, the scope does not include the quality assurance of the development process.

The Quality Plan document includes the following information:

  • Quality criteria: The characteristics that must be considered as the deliverables are being built.
  • Quality contributors: Project factors that, when applied, will assist in achieving the quality objectives.
  • Quality strategy:
    • Static testing: testing before any code is written.
    • Dynamic testing: testing by exercising the code.
  • Quality roles and responsibilities: Definition of roles and organizations responsible for performing those roles.
  • Quality standards and methods: Mapping of quality contributors and project procedures to required quality standards.

The Quality Plan is developed at project startup and is updated on an ongoing basis.

Brief Outline

The Quality Plan for Application Domain is text-based (see attached template).  The Quality Plan Table of Contents includes:

Introduction

  • Project overview and background: brief description of the project to set the context.
  • Quality objectives and scope: The scope should describe what the project does and does not cover.  The quality objectives for the project drive the test objectives, described in the testing strategies and plans.
  • Risk assessment: Risk assessment, in this context, examines the risks that impact the development of the product.  This assessment includes the development capability, impact of technology used for development, complexity of the project environment, application/business knowledge of the development team, etc. 

Quality Strategy

  • Defect prevention strategy: Describes methods used for preventing defects.  Causal analysis is a frequently used method of defect prevention.
  • Defect detection/removal strategy:
    • Static Testing: Scope includes development and testing work products.
    • Dynamic Testing: All dynamic testing levels traditionally referred to as “testing.”

  • Testing standards and methods: Describes the standards and methods used for static and dynamic testing.  It is a mapping of quality contributors and project procedures to required quality standards (e.g., ISO). 
  • Entry and exit criteria: Any standard definitions within the organization that define the entry/exit criteria for the different levels of testing should be included here.
  • Roles and Responsibilities: Definition of quality roles and the project organizational units responsible for performing those roles. 

Quality Model

  • Quality criteria: Defined and measurable characteristics of work products that must be considered to ensure that acceptance criteria for the deliverables will be met.
  • Quality measurements and metrics: Metrics and measurements by which the quality criteria will be measured.
  • Quality contributors: Project factors, such as skills, standards, tools, methods and procedures, that, when applied, will contribute to achieving specified quality criteria.
  • Quality targets: Targets for metrics above.

Quality Tracking and Reporting

  • Quality tracking: Methods or means that will be used for tracking the quality of the work products.
  • Quality reporting: Reporting requirements at milestone checkpoints.  Generally, the test reports will be used to assess the quality achieved against plan before the application or system is promoted to the next level of testing.

Key Considerations

The Quality Plan for Application Domain should be kept simple, realistic, and achievable. It is likely that the sponsor will want to approve the project quality plan. The project quality plan should be used as the main driver for the Quality Plan for Application Domain. Discussing the content of the Quality Plan with the sponsor would help to increase his confidence in the project.

The objective of the Quality Plan for AD is to ensure that the deliverables meet the acceptance criteria specified in the Agreement. If too little quality activity is performed, the deliverables will be unacceptable. If too much quality activity is performed, unnecessary and unplanned costs will be incurred.

Generally speaking, test focus areas referenced in the test strategy and master test plan are the quality criteria for testing. These are driven by the business and technical requirements and are based on the risks and costs as defined in the brief outline section. The test objectives are based on the test focus areas (quality criteria). Quality contributors are project factors that, when applied, will assist in achieving the quality objectives for testing. Testing types are logical tests that are used as tools during the physical levels of testing to ensure that the test objectives are met.

Quality planning for testing must consider the cost/benefit trade-off. The primary benefit of performing quality activities is less re-work, which means higher productivity, lower costs, and increased satisfaction. There is a cost associated with performing those quality/testing activities, however. Therefore, the test manager must continually weigh the trade-off between the benefits realized and the costs incurred.

The testing procedures are documented through the various testing work products. The Quality Plan for Application Domain also shows how Quality Strategies for testing are implemented.

The detailed plans for testing document the activities relating to each detailed level of testing.

Where applicable, the quality-plans must conform to ISO9000 and any other relevant standards that have been adopted by the delivery organization or requested by the sponsor.

The level of quality built into the plan must match the requirements in the Agreement – Statement of Work. Falling short may result in an unacceptable level of solution quality. Providing quality above what is required may increase the cost beyond what was agreed to with the sponsor and jeopardize schedule commitments.

Tailoring
Impact of not having

Without a Quality Plan, neither the sponsor nor the application development team can be confident that the work products/deliverables will meet the agreed-upon acceptance criteria.  It will be difficult to verify and validate that the deliverables will meet the quality expectations for the product.

Failure to focus on the quality of the work products/deliverables may result in:

  • The client not accepting the deliverables.
  • Client dissatisfaction.

In extreme situations this could result in IBM not receiving payment for work performed, and/or legal ramifications.

If a separate quality plan for application domain is not created for an engagement, then the quality expectations of the client, together with the methods and a plan to achieve them, must be documented and implemented as part of the overall project quality plan.

Reasons for not needing

It is not always necessary to produce a formal Quality Plan for Application Domain.

  • In a small project, the manager can often simply provide direction to the team about how the project will identify and meet its quality objectives.
  • In a project with a familiar context, where all team members involved may be well acquainted with how the work will be carried out, a separate quality plan may not be required.
