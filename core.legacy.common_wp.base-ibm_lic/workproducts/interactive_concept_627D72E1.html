Artifact: Interactive Concept (APP 159)
An Interactive Concept is the representation of design ideas for an application, presented as a visualization of the interactive processes the user follows.
Domains: User Experience
Work Product Kinds: Concept
Purpose

During application design, Interactive Concepts are used to communicate the initial look and feel of the application in a narrative, visual, and conceptual format that can be easily understood by people who have little or no design expertise.  Communication of design ideas among team members and with the sponsor is the primary purpose of Interactive Concepts.  Different Interactive Concepts can be used to communicate with a wide variety of audiences: end users, sponsors, project teams, etc.  They may be tailored to communicate specific aspects of the design such as aesthetics or functional design elements. This storytelling complements the use case scenarios by suggesting a design solution that meets the requirements of the scenarios.

In addition, Interactive Concepts provide tangible, visual indications of the scope and functionality of the application, helping to set expectations and keep the whole team in sync. Often Interactive Concepts are the best way to gain approval for the design approach. Detailed instances of Interactive Concepts can be used to provide representation of design specifications.

Key Considerations

Different audiences require different Interactive Concept implementation styles that take into account the audience’s base knowledge and the formality of the situation.  Encourage participation from the whole team.  

The Interactive Concept can also be executed at varying levels of detail appropriate for the relevant stage of development.  Create informal, hand-drawn Interactive Concepts early in the design process to communicate macro design ideas with the entire design team. 

Show only refined Interactive Concepts to clients, making sure to set their expectations for what they will see, and set clear guidelines for the feedback you need in order to move forward.  Design the client’s Interactive Concepts to demonstrate only those aspects of the design for which input is needed.  Make sure the Interactive Concepts use language and imagery the client will understand.  The same criteria apply for Interactive Concepts presented to end users for validation or testing.
Tailoring
Impact of not havingWithout Interactive Concepts, design ideas are frequently communicated through discipline-specific design documents that contain narrative design descriptions, which, by their very nature, are more abstract work products. Abstraction of the design inhibits the review process, both with project teams and sponsors, creating risk that the final product may be different than what the teams or the sposors expect. Being able to actually see the design, even in an iterative state, will usually communicate the design solution better than a written description of the design concept.
Reasons for not needingThe design of the application is already specified by some other means. The sponsor and development teams have a joint understanding of the graphical user interface specifications of the application.
Representation Options

Interactive Concepts are descriptions in the form of text, images, audio, video motion graphics, or some combination of the five.  Particularly in the early stages of design, Interactive Concepts often consist of sketches, developed in short iteration.  Usually they are mounted on presentation boards but can also be presented electronically.

The medium used depends on the ideas the Interactive Concepts are intended to communicate and the purpose for which they were created.  For example, an Interactive Concept that demonstrates an application’s aesthetic design would most likely consist of pictures representing the most relevant views a user would encounter in a particular scenario.
