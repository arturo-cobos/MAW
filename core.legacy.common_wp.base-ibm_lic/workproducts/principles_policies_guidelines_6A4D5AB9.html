Artifact: Principles - Policies and Guidelines (ARC 309)
The Principles, Policies and Guidelines work product defines the underlying general rules and guidelines that an organization will use to utilize and deploy all business and IT resources and assets, across the enterprise.
Domains: Architecture
Work Product Kinds: Standard
Purpose

Policies are used to prescribe management direction, to guide an organization to meet their stated business needs and objectives, to ensure the business confirms to prescribed legislation, to demonstrate management commitment and to clearly define responsibilities.

Principles are used to capture the fundamental, underlying truths about how the organization will utilize and deploy both business and information technology assets and resources. The principles are used in a number of different ways:

  • They provide an effective framework within which the business can start to make conscious decisions about the business, its management style and structure and how it uses/implements Information Technology
  • As a guide to establishing relevant evaluation criteria, and thus exert a strong influence on the selection of suppliers, business partners, components, products or product architectures
  • As drivers for defining the functional requirements of an enterprise-wide architecture
  • As an input to assessing both the existing environment, including IS/IT systems, organization, management practices and the future environment, e.g., a strategic project portfolio, for compliance with the future desired state. These assessments will provide valuable insights into the transition activities needed to implement the principles, in support of the business goals and priorities
  • The benefit statements provide a basis for justifying all proposed decisions and related activities
  • The implication statements provide an outline of the key tasks, resources and potential costs to the business of implementing the principle. They also provide valuable inputs to future transition initiative and planning activities.

Guidelines are used to gain consensus by agreement, in situations where mandated policies or principles would not be acceptable.

Description
Main DescriptionPolicy - a high level statement of how things will be managed or organized, including management goals, objectives, beliefs and responsibilities. Policies are normally defined at an overall strategy level and can be related to a specific area, for example, Security Policies, Management Policies. In many instances, policies reflect the law and givens, which must be adhered to - this is especially true in the case of Security and Privacy policies.

Principle - defines the underlying general rules which an organization will use to utilize and deploy all business and IT resources and assets, across the enterprise e.g. "IT systems will be designed to be capable of rapid expansion in line with unpredicted business growth."

Principles should always include defined motivation and implication statements:

  • The benefit statements highlight the value to the business of implementing the principle - and therefore provide a basis for justifying all related activities, for example, the implementation of an Enterprise Architecture, or the implementation of a Security Framework
  • The implication statements provide an outline of the key tasks, resources and potential costs to the business of implementing the principle. They also provide valuable inputs to future transition initiative and planning activities.

Many different types and levels of Principles can be defined:

  • Guiding principles (normally 6 - 12) which provide an overall framework for decision making
  • Business principles
  • IT principles (applications, data, technology, security, network, user interface, system management, development)
  • Management and organizational principles
  • Principles for mergers and acquisitions
  • Principles for outsourcing

Guideline - a general statement of direction, a desired future state which is not necessarily mandated. Guideline statements are similar in content to principle statements, but without the associated motivation and benefit statements.

To be effective, all principles, policies and guidelines should be defined and agreed by both senior business and IT executives.

Once they have been agreed and published, the principles and policies provide a framework which can be used to explain and justify why certain business and IT decisions are being made, or need to be made.

Brief Outline

Policies are defined as a textual document containing, at a minimum:

  • A definition of the area covered and its importance as an enabling factor for the business and IT.
  • A statement of management support.
  • An explanation of any specific requirements, which may include compliance with legislative and contractual requirements, education, planning.
  • Definition of general and specific roles and responsibilities.
  • Explanation of key processes.

Principles are defined as a textual document containing:

  • The principle statement itself.
  • Motivation (business benefit) statements.
  • Implication statements.

Key Considerations

Having the right client participation is crucial to obtaining buy-in and thus the success of the entire effort. If you must delay the workshops to ensure the participation of the enterprise's thought leaders, then it is well worth it.

After the initial workshops, both the principles and policies will undergo a widening circle of distribution. While this validation process must be tailored to individual client needs, it usually involves getting concurrence from all workshop participants, presentation to executives, and finally publication to the organization. A mechanism for updating the newly defined principles and/or policies should be devised as part of the overall management framework.

The definition of principles and policies should only be undertaken by suitable skilled practitioners, who have been trained in one of the IBM Methods which specifically relates to this work product, for example, IT Strategy, Enterprise Architecture, Security and IT Management - each of these disciplines has detailed guidance, technique papers and samples/IC.
Tailoring
Impact of not havingWithout agreed policies and principles and the level of consensus which defining them brings to the organization, there will be no firm basis for moving forward. The absence of clearly defined policies and principles indicates a lack of management support for the directions that they embody, making conscious, informed decision-making difficult.
Reasons for not needingThere is no excuse for not having a defined set of Policies and/or Principles as a major deliverable in any strategy or architecture engagement. If the client already has a set of policies and principles, which seem reasonable, these should be reviewed for completeness. The engagement team may be able to add further value by assisting with the definition of associated motivation and implication statements.
