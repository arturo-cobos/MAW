Artifact: Leading Practices (BUS 208)
The Leading Practices work product describes highly attractive, best-in-class, or best-of-breed attributes or characteristics for a particular Focal Area, as recognized or measured by superior performance.
Domains: Business Strategy
Work Product Kinds: Inventory
Purpose

Gaining an understanding of what leading companies are doing to achieve breakthrough performance provides valuable input to a company exploring a change of strategy, and/or embarking on a transformation or improvement effort.

The Leading Practices work product is used:

  • To communicate awareness of leading practices within the company, the industry, and other industries to executives and project team members
  • For design points and attributes to consider when exploring creative approaches and specifying target business and IT capabilities, and future process characteristics and capabilities
  • To facilitate change management by exposing key executives and employees to external success stories, (this fosters a greater understanding of their situation relative to other change efforts being undertaken in the industry)

Description
Main Description

The Focal Area to which the leading practices conform can be a specific business or IT capability that supports a desired outcome or value proposition (i.e., capture customer information, match products and services to customer needs, etc.), or a process area (i.e., select markets, fulfill products and services, service customers, etc).

Leading practices are identified by the way a company:

  • Structures its business model to achieve business goals and objectives
  • Interacts, transacts, and exchanges value with suppliers, complementors, competitors/substitutes and other external parties
  • Performs a process via its management policies and procedures
  • Organizes its people around a process
  • Uses technology to support/enable the process
Brief Outline

If the analysis is being done at the strategy level, the following criteria and detail should be considered:

  • Business Design - how the business is structured and organized
  • Key Value Propositions - the value outcomes the business delivers to its customers and other constituents with whom it does business
  • Key Capabilities - what the company does that allows it to create, capture and sustain value
  • Sources of Value Capture - how the company makes a profit and recoups other forms of value through exchanges with others with whom it does business
  • Sources of Strategic Control - how competitive advantage is achieved and maintained

If the analysis is being done for major processes or process areas, the following criteria and detail should be considered:

Each practice has a textual description, which may be structured using the following sections:

  • Process Name - Identifies process classification using the Universal Process Classification Scheme developed by the International Benchmarking Clearinghouse (IBC)
  • Industry Classification - Identifies internal, industry, and cross-industry usage of the “leading practice”
  • Description - Phrases describing the “leading practice” process
  • Process Outcomes - A description of the various outputs of a process which are representative of a “leading practice.” A brief description of how the company accomplished their outcomes
  • Leading Practices/Enablers - The things built into a process, which enable a capability. These are categorized into three groups: management policies and procedures, technology and organization.
  • Metrics - Actual metrics are included when such data is available.
  • Importance to company - Provides insight to the current or potential usage and high level assessment of potential benefits
  • Company Information - Identifies referenced company performing the leading practice (including time frames of implemented leading practice)
  • Sources - Indicates the sources of the process information

Key Considerations
  • It is extremely important to clearly understand what is needed and to correctly target the Leading Practices research.  This will ensure that client expectations are appropriately met, that the research and deliverables provide the appropriate information and value, and that time spent doing data collection is minimized versus spending inordinate amounts of time and reaching overload.
  • If primary research or benchmarking will be done, it is important for the client project team to document and understand the leading practices as thoroughly as possible.  Where feasible, it may be helpful to contact the company performing the leading practice directly to ask more specific questions. 
  • During the Strategy Development work efforts, Executive Visioning sessions, and other key work sessions, executives often ask many questions about the ways other companies are performing certain activities and the benefits those companies are receiving.  The client project team gains strong credibility when they can immediately answer leading practice-related questions from the executives.
  • Leading practices should be oriented around industry (and when possible, should be prepared in advance around key issue or impact areas based upon industry knowledge or experience).
  • Consultants should also refer to AdvisoryWare, an IBM tool that expresses some best practices in terms of particular metrics, such as the "speed to process a loan", but mostly describes leading practices in terms of characteristics or enablers of a process or capability, such as the use of bar codes, wireless devices, dynamic order guides etc. for managing inventory and orders.
  • It is important to note that in multi-divisional companies, sometimes the scope of the engagement is to determine "leading practices" within the company.  Consultants should use basic process related work products for benchmarking within a company.  They can try to facilitate the client as to which may be a better practice using ICAP.
  • Ensure the sources of all collected information are documented and maintained.

Note: The Leading Practices work product uses the generic process classification scheme which is based on the Universal Process Classification Scheme developed by the International Benchmarking Clearinghouse (IBM is a participating corporate member of the International Benchmarking Clearinghouse). This helps to identify potential processes.
Tailoring
Impact of not having

If the Leading Practices work product is not produced:

  • The company runs the risk of not capitalizing on the potential learning and experience of others
  • The process to derive initiatives, approaches and solutions may potentially take longer and not result in what is needed
  • The executive decision and buy-in cycle will potentially be slower, due to lack of experiential proof of concept
Reasons for not needing

The Leading Practices work product is not needed (though it may still be of value) if:

  • The business strategy or area being pursued is so unique or innovative that no comparisons are available
  • The client has chosen to implement a solution with a pre-defined configuration or "template" and will accept whatever results the package can deliver
  • The client will perform benchmarking against other companies, or has availability of current practices or benchmarking information
