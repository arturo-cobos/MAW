Artifact: Analysis and Design Guidelines (APP 100)
This work product contains project-specific guidance on the analysis and design work products and the process by which they are produced.
Domains: Environment
Work Product Kinds: Standard
Purpose
Analysis and Design Guidelines focus on the needs of a particular project and improve the efficiency of development work. They explain how the chosen analysis and design tools will be used and define any variations from common terms. The Guidelines provide a basis for consistency of product and approach. Likewise, documentation templates speed development. To ensure uniformity, it is essential that all producers of Architecture and Analysis and Design work products follow the same process model.

Description
Main Description

The Analysis and Design Guidelines describe extensions, modifications and interpretations of the standard work products and processes.  The Analysis and Design Guidelines work product might detail things such as notational changes (possibly required by a modeling tool) or a different approach to reviewing work products (possibly including some third-party reviewer performing an audit or product assurance role).

Brief Outline

The Analysis and Design Guidelines usually consist of lists of guidelines, grouped by work product. Notations are free format text augmented by work product templates or examples.

Items to include in these guidelines include:

  • Naming conventions
  • Diagramming conventions, especially variations on the standard
  • Specific interpretations of the semantics of notations
  • Guidance on when to produce analysis and design work products
  • Guidance on the completeness and formality of the work products
  • How the chosen tools will be used
  • Specific review guidelines


Process guidelines suggest how the analysis and design should be done. These guidelines may be a list of suggested techniques or may be more prescriptive. The guidelines might also include tools advice and recommendations for the use of the tools.

The guidelines should be used to document any definitions that may be ambiguous that are required to do analysis and design for this engagement.

Key Considerations
  • The guidelines should be in place before doing any analysis or design.
  • Any analysis and design education must be consistent with the guidelines.
  • The guidelines should be minimal but sufficient to ensure that the analysis and design work products are understandable, usable, and consistent.
  • Use work product templates where relevant. Ensure that each team member can access the templates.
  • Use standard guidelines where they are available and appropriate. Modify these only as necessary. Use standard notation guidelines where they are available and appropriate. Modify these only as necessary.
  • If your guidelines are novel, publish them for others to comment upon and to use.
  • Harvest good templates from the workbooks of other projects.
  • Use the guidelines as entry criteria to analysis and design reviews. Adherence to the guidelines should be part of any review. Unjustified deviation should be identified and brought to the attention of the appropriate team leader.
  • If the project team consists largely of novices, more prescriptive guidelines might be appropriate.
  • Briefly review the guidelines at the end of each phase to check their adequacy and completeness.
  • Remember that the guidelines should be a living document that is updated over the course of the engagement. Change or add to the guidelines during the project if necessary. Keep guidelines under change control, and disallow amendments through a memo, oral directive, or other informal process.
  • Include statements about what is the expected completion criterion for the analysis and design work products for each phase.
  • Provide rules only if the lack of rules will lead to confusion or inefficiency.
  • Be sure that writing guidelines does not become a distraction from the real work.
  • Do not be over-prescriptive.
Tailoring
Impact of not havingAnalysts can waste time in long, open-ended discussions. Differences between notations supported by a tool and that described by the method may never get properly resolved. Different analysts may do their work to different levels of detail. Some may not add enough detailed description. Others may add more than is necessary for subsequent development. If you do not have and enforce design guidelines, you will most likely produce a design with varying styles and inconsistencies.
Reasons for not needingIf you do not deviate from the method or are able to reuse existing guidelines, you need not produce a new version of this work product. An extremely small team may also not require this work product, although even in this case, the guidelines should be recorded for maintenance, which may be done by others.
