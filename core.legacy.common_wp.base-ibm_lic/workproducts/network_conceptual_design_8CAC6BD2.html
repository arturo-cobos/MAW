Artifact: Network Conceptual Design (ARC 322)
A Network Conceptual Design describes the high-level view of required network services, major user communities, and service connections.
Domains: Operational
Work Product Kinds: Specification
Purpose
The purpose of the Network Conceptual Design is to establish a common understanding between the stakeholders and the people architecting and designing the network by describing the network that will support the business requirements. It is especially critical since it is typically the very first time during a network design project that the sponsor will actually see the first ‘picture’ of the future network design. The conceptual design is used to help the executive sponsor understand how the network design addresses the stated business goals, objectives, and requirements. The conceptual design is also used as the basis for the Network Specified Design.

Description
Main DescriptionThe services described should include access, backbone, security, and external providers. The Network Conceptual Design is broken into two levels. The Level 0 Network Conceptual Design depicts the relationship of the required internal/external services and user communities. The Level 1 Network Conceptual Design adds the next level of detail depicting access nodes, backbone nodes, security nodes, connections, user communities, and service connections for the basic network infrastructure using client terminology (locations, buildings, sites, etc.).
Notation

The notation used in defining a Level 0 Network Conceptual Design is in the form of a diagram such as a barometric chart that shows the relationship between the user communities, required services, and the network nodes (access, backbone, security, etc).

The notation used in defining a Level 1 Network Conceptual Design uses standard block and line topology diagrams to represent conceptual nodes and connections (non vendor specific symbols).  Since this is the first occurrence of expressing the network in ‘pictures’, and this is still considered to be a ‘high level’ effort rather than a ‘detailed’ effort, the diagrams used to express a Network Conceptual Design typically contain non-descriptor or generic devices used to represent nodes and lines or arrows are used to represent network connections.  Generic devices are used since at this point of the overall network design effort NO vendor products have yet been identified (nor should they be identified at this early step).

It is important that the diagrams produced by this effort are a reflection of the nodes, components, locations, buildings, sites, etc. showing the recommended connections needed to support the business requirements (as expressed in the network strategy and architecture).  This is important since it demonstrates to the client our understanding of their environment and gives them a feeling for where the ultimate network design is headed.

Key Considerations

The terminology used when selling a network design engagement MUST be clearly articulated and defined - initially to the client and then in the proposal (and to the engagement team).  These terms can very easily be misrepresented, misunderstood, or misconstrued, and it is very difficult to complete a successful engagement if the client thinks they are getting a ‘Specified Design’, when in the network consultants’ minds they are only planning to do a ‘Conceptual Design’ with no intent of getting to a deeper level of design.  In particular, be very careful when using terms such as ‘high level design’ or ‘low level design’ without doing an additional level of qualification as to what deliverables the client actually will be getting.  An effective technique to use here is to actually show the client sample sections of prior engagement deliverables that help to distinguish between the terms ‘high level’ and ‘low level.’  Or better yet, use the definitions and examples found in the Networking Methodology and in the work product descriptions for conceptual, specified, and node designs.  An additional reference source is the Architecture Description Standard (ADS) document that clearly specifies the vocabulary and distinction in levels of design.

It is important that the diagrams produced by this effort are a reflection of the client nodes, components, locations, buildings, sites, etc. showing the recommended connections needed to support the business requirements (as expressed in the network strategy and architecture).  This is important since it demonstrates to the client our understanding of their environment and gives them a feeling for where the ultimate network design is headed.

It is key to include an experienced Network Architect during the development of this work product. The networking industry is changing at such a rapid rate that to attempt to do any Network Design without this level of experience and skill in networking standards, protocols, products, industry directions, etc. will surely result in a less than satisfactory engagement.

Tailoring
Impact of not havingThe impact of not having this work product is that the linkage between business goals, objectives, and requirements and the network design are lost. This lack of clear linkage can result in a failure to obtain executive level buy in to the final design recommendations, reduced funding and or the loss of the implementation engagement.
Reasons for not needingThe Network Conceptual Design Work Product description would not be needed if a client wanted an assessment of their network or if the engagement was constructed to only require a network strategy. In these situations, this level of design would not be needed.
