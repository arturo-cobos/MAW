Artifact: Critical Issues Opportunities and Recommendations (BUS 005)
The Critical Issues, Opportunities and Recommendations work product aggregates the results of the ongoing assessment and analysis activities associated with a variety of types of consulting projects.
Domains: Business Value & Financials
Work Product Kinds: Assessment
Purpose

This work product is produced to:

  • Clarify and communicate a finite number of specific problems and/or opportunities, within the scope of this project, for which effective solutions will be sought
  • Focus the energy of the sponsor and the team on a few critical areas, helping avoid time lost in "boiling the ocean" and the ever-present danger of "scope creep"
  • Provide an enduring reference point throughout the project for justifying the investment of resources to design, develop and implement solutions
  • Provide critical triggers for the creative design activities
  • Identify where issues and opportunities vary across different geographic locations, divisions or subsidiaries as a basis for harmonization in the future process design (subject to constraints such as country unique legal and taxation requirements)
  • Support the generation of hypotheses, when an issue-based consulting approach is being used
  • Ensure focus on developing recommendations to cover all the critical issues and opportunities

Description
Main DescriptionThe Critical Issues, Opportunities and Recommendations work product identifies important problems and improvement opportunities for which effective solutions must be provided. It also suggests actions to be taken to rectify the situation and the benefits to be derived.

It results in a customer-oriented document, which presents a comprehensive view of issues, concerns, potential problems and their solutions and allows the customer to prioritize efforts to address the issues and opportunities.
Brief Outline

This work product produces three fundamental items: 

  1. A list or chart of the critical issues that fall within the stated scope of this project.  The issues may be expressed in terms of business direction/strategy, competitive market/product positioning, unacceptable business performance, insufficient capabilities, customer dissatisfaction, breakthrough opportunities etc.
  2. A list or chart of "opportunity areas" for the re-design of selected business processes to address the critical issues identified above.  Opportunity areas are often discovered as the result of root cause analysis of known problems/defects or by investigations into the current external environment and the performance of the current business processes/systems.  They may relate to the processes, the current and potential use of technology, the organization, the use of knowledge and/or customer needs and wants. 
  3. A set of charts defining, in graphics and text, the recommendations and how they relate to the critical issues and opportunities.

Key Considerations
  • The processes being analyzed must have already been defined, documented and mapped, showing key activities.
  • Use subject matter experts to minimize lost time and credibility.
  • Use the simple tools and techniques to help identify root causes; brainstorming, cause and effect (fishbone) diagrams, scatter diagrams, pareto diagrams.
  • Process flow diagrams are excellent vehicles to enable process analysis - they can be appended with cycle times and symbols to indicated problem areas or opportunities for improvement.
  • Try to do as much analysis at the activity versus the task level.  Note: an activity can be manual or automated and completes a unit of work.  A series of activities are linked together into a workflow to accomplish a sub-process that provides a discernible output.  Several sub-processes (e.g. provide product / service information, provide quotation, etc.) make up a process (e.g. agree contract).
  • There are often many problem areas, so it is key to prioritize these and only undertake root cause analysis on the top priority ones.
  • There are usually many root causes, so it is key to understand how severe / to what extent these contribute to the problem areas.Then only brainstorm ideas to solve the top priority root causes.
  • Avoid getting bogged down in "analysis paralysis".

  • e-business is providing an opportunity to conduct business differently.  It is important to understand the client's focus –
  • improving current processes with web technology,
  • re-engineering their business to take advantage of web technology, or
  • reinventing the supply chain and how they deal with their customers and suppliers. 

This affects how much analysis should be done based on the current environment versus focussing on the future design.

Tailoring
Impact of not having

If this work products is not produced, then:

  • Issues and problems might not clearly be identified and their degree of importance not clearly understood, which could lead to the wrong issues being prioritized
  • Justification for proceeding with changes could not conclusively be presented. The imperative for effecting changes is not created, this might lead to the sponsor not being clear as to the problem/issue they are addressing causing the stoppage or redirection of the effort
  • The linkages between recommendations might not clearly be understood and the interdependencies might not be addressed appropriately
  • The sponsor might not understand issues/problems being addressed by the project, which could lead to lack of focus by the team, possible scope creep and failure to meet expectations
Reasons for not needing

This work product is not required when:

  • The client is responding to a management decision to implement a specific "enterprise-wide solution" and is mentally "coping" with the change rather than trying to achieve focused results
  • The project is driven primarily by a systems initiative (i.e. technology driven change); the client sponsor (likely the CIO) may wish to focus primarily or solely on that issue
