Artifact: Data Stores (ARC 306)
The Data Stores work product identifies, describes and groups business data in terms of its characteristics.
Domains: Enterprise
Work Product Kinds: Specification
Purpose

The Data Stores work product provides a framework for capturing the characteristics of the business data. The work product is used to ensure:

  • Future data bases are effectively defined
  • Future systems meet business performance, availability, security and manageability requirements
  • There is a clear understanding of business data characteristics - key to the successful implementation of IT based business solutions.

At the enterprise level, the work product is used to help define the rules, policies and guidelines on data access, sharing, placement and management.

At the system level, it is used to drive the shape and style of specific solutions; without it, users could end up with poor response times to data that is out of date.

Description
Main Description

Data Stores are an extrapolation of the Information Classes defined in a high-level business information model, grouped to reflect data characteristics. The way in which data is grouped will guide many later design and implementation decisions, and is driven by business requirements (not technology), such as:

  • "One customer database or ten?" A business may require a single view of customer data, irrespective of organization, while another, for business and or legal reasons, may insist that customer data is owned at a divisional level, and no single view of customer data is allowed.
  • "How big is the data store, and how is it used?" A data store which is large (and probably growing), which also needs to support a high rate of read/write activity will pose a very different design challenge to one which, although very large, is relatively stable and used only as reference (read only) data.

This Data Stores work product can be defined at different levels, dependent on the scope of an engagement and the time available for these activities:

  • Conceptual data stores level - where the focus is on defining the main data stores and their overall characteristics. This level is used as input to the definition of enterprise-wide rules, polices and guidelines on data access, sharing, placement and management
  • Logical data stores level - where all required data stores and their detailed characteristics and operational considerations are defined. This level is used as a direct input to system design activities, driving the shape and style of specific solutions.

A data store represents a logical grouping of data. It does not imply any decisions regarding data replication, segmentation, copy management, or any specific database technology. Rather, it captures the requirements that will drive decisions on replication, segmentation and copy management, using defined enterprise-wide data access and placement guidelines. Further on, in the development of an enterprise architecture model and during system design, these requirements will be used to provide guidance on the selection of standards and components to support the physical implementation of data stores (databases) to meet business usage, availability and management objectives. In addition, data stores identified at an enterprise level may decompose into many, more detailed data stores when designing a specific single business system.

Notation

The notation is largely tabular, often with supporting descriptive text. Such text will explain the nature of the characteristic (table columns) as well as the nature of the logical data store (table rows).

It is useful to link the data stores defined here to the data entities (or data subject areas, or information classes) in the enterprise information model. This can be shown in a matrix form (and is sometimes referred to as a data store/entity cross-reference).

Each data store represents a subset of business data with different characteristics (i.e., types, based on volume, volatility, form), different usage, access and management requirements. The characteristics used will generally be chosen based on the identified needs of the enterprise or the target business system.

For example:

  •  Size: How big is the data group - large volumes of static data, vs. transactional data?
  •  Rates of Growth: 10% per annum, vs. 400% (by acquisition) over the next 2 years
  • Volatility: What is the rate of change of the data in the data group - 50% of the data changes daily, vs. 2% of the data changes monthly?
  • Currency: How up to date does the data have to be - maximum of one minute vs. value as at close of monthly trading?
  • Form: Is it text, image, structured, etc.?
  • Access: Is the data mostly accessed "read only," or does it require update?
  • Service Characteristics: Performance, availability, etc.
  • Partitioning Requirements
  • Sharing Requirements
  • Management Requirements

Deciding which characteristics are important, and what values they take, normally depends on the context of the data's usage. For example, currency requirements may be dependent on who the users of the data are, and what they require of the data; or the volatility of a data store may depend on the time of day or year.

Key Considerations
Ensure the information captured in this work product is validated by business representatives. Much of the information is "non functional," e.g., volumes, volatility, etc, and it is likely that much will have been estimated or assumed.

Remember, with this work product, the objective is to identify what the data stores are (might be), not how they are to be implemented, how they are to be managed within a particular database technology, or how they are to be distributed across the enterprise.

Do not, therefore, allow yourself to take too much account of technical or implementation considerations. This work product is very much focused on requirements, and finding a way of describing the business data of the enterprise, or business system, in a manner which allows it to be carved up into manageable and distinctly different chunks which will make the business of systems implementation more straight forward.

In order to ensure a robust work product, it is important to make use of experience in the deployment and management of business data within the enterprise, as well as people who understand the business requirements - although the focus is on requirements, there is a great benefit in ensuring the "art of the possible" is equally understood.

Tailoring
Impact of not havingWithout a clear knowledge of the total framework of data required by the business, including its characteristics, the ability to access and share common data is limited and the task of data management becomes very costly, ineffective and time consuming. In the converse situation, where an organization requires a clear separation of data for sound business reasons, the Data Stores work product provides a sound basis for rules and guidelines for the separation and subsequent access and management of data. In either instance, without this work product, subsequent design decisions, at the system level, will not have a sound basis to be made from.
Reasons for not needingIf the data requirements are not considered to be challenging, or data access is 'unsophisticated'. For example, where a major portion of the data is 'read only' and stable - customer facing information systems such as railway timetables or on-line shopping catalogues.
