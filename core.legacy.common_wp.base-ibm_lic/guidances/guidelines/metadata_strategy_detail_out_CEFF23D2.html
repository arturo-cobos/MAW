Related Elements
  • Metadata Strategy
Main Description

A Metadata Strategy artifact contains information, which documents and describes (at a summary level):

  • The capabilities currently in place to provide information in order to understand access and use the organization’s data resources.
  • The current metadata management organization framework—what policies and practices already exist.
  • The types of metadata currently captured, maintained, managed or made available for use. These types typically fall under the following broad categories:
    • Data: information about the organizations data resources (e.g. definitions of files, data bases, objects, records, data elements, data relationships, business terms, schemas, etc).
    • Business Process: information relating to the organization’s business functions; information which defines and describes the organization’s business rules, processes, practices and methods to conduct and manage the business.
    • Technical (systems) Process: information related to defining or describing technical (systems) processes that automates, performs or supports a business process or business function. Included under this category are items such as program specifications, algorithms, processing logic, transformational logic, summarization/aggregation rules, etc.
    • Organization: information about the client’s organizational structure, reporting relationships, etc.
    • Technology: information related to systems technical infrastructure, application systems, networks, etc.
    • Administrative/Operational: information related to the support, monitoring, administration or management of metadata or systems facilities, performance, etc.
  • How well the various categories of information needed by the organization to conduct and manage its business are documented and made available to the various management, business and technical constituencies within the company.
  • The extent to which business professionals—either the organization's or the organization’s customers—have access to and use metadata in conducting their business activities.
  • The organizational units that have accountability for metadata.
  • What standards, naming conventions or other data administration practices exist.
  • The vision, aims and goals regarding metadata. This artifact clearly delineates client plans and intentions regarding metadata management and use. It is used to document and describe the steps the organization desires to undertake to reach their ultimate goals. For example, this artifact could be used to describe an incremental strategy where the metadata capabilities are implemented in a series of progressive, incremental steps starting with a Business Information Directory and proceeding toward a full repository that embraces the entire information environment.
  • The scope and boundary factors of the envisioned (target) metadata solution. Describe in this artifact:
    • The type of metadata management facility intended, for example:
      • Business Information Directory (BID) – a facility that contains business-related metadata (business terms, business term definitions, business term to system data element cross-references, etc).
      • Metadata Repository(Business and Technical Use) – a comprehensive broad based facility shared and used by both the technical (systems) and business constituencies.
      • Metadata Repository (Technical only) – used as a tool for storing information about software components, their interfaces and how the relate to other components. This type of facility is used in a technical systems context only. Business professionals are normally unaware that this type of facility exists.
      • Hybrid facility -- Utilize the capabilities and features of data modeling tools, data capture/cleansing/transformation tools and data analysis/presentation/reporting tools to manage metadata.
    • Organizational or geographic boundaries that would constrain the services, availability or access to a metadata facility.
    • Desired functionality/capabilities.
    • The extent of integration and sharing of metadata across various tools, facilities and components. Discuss in this artifact the considerations for sharing metadata across various tools and components.
  • The imminent activities and plans for metadata management. This artifact should describe the immediate plans, scope and parameters of a metadata management facility (i.e., what are the next steps for metadata in relation to the overall metadata vision).
    • The work product should specify if the organization intends to establish a metadata facility that is intended to:
      • only support a specific “one-off” solution – for example a data mart or a single application system,
      • support an enterprise (multi-subject area/cross business function) data warehouse,
      • support the client’s operational systems environment only,
      • support both the client’s operational systems environment and their informational (business intelligence) environment.
    • It is possible that the organization intends to do nothing—that is, the organization does not intend to have a metadata facility of any sort. This kind of decision, its implications and the rationale should be documented in this work product.
  • The intended high-level functional tasks that a metadata management facility would be used to perform—describe at a high-level what the metadata facility is envisioned to do. Typically these capabilities are categorized as business tasks, operational/administrative tasks or technical tasks. Examples of technical tasks which a metadata management facility would be used for include: designing BI solution data stores (ODS, DW, Data marts, etc.), designing/developing data capture/conversion/transformation systems and facilities, or managing the definition and description of the contents of the BI solution data stores. Examples of operational/administrative tasks include creating data warehouse access/usage statistics, defining production system environment schedules and configurations. Examples of business tasks where a metadata management facility would be used include: discovering what data exists, determining how to use the data, creating decision support queries or reporting objects.
  • The critical success factors and implications of introducing a metadata management facility—define what organizational, business process, policy or practice changes are likely to be needed given a metadata management facility is established.
  • Typically the most significant consequence of a metadata management facility is its impact on the way the business organization manages the gathering, definition, maintenance and sharing of its metadata. With the introduction of a metadata management facility comes the creation of a metadata management organization framework. This framework is used to establish the context for collecting metadata, maintaining its accuracy and integrity and making it available to authorized technical and business users. Collecting metadata is an on-going activity. Metadata is captured during all stages of systems development.
    • A metadata management organization framework consists of the following elements:
      • The description of the organizational responsibilities for metadata; the lines of responsibility, accountability and authority.
      • The purpose and role of data administration.
      • The purpose and role of data owners (prime authorities), data custodians /stewards.
      • Common procedures for the collection, maintenance and dissemination of metadata.
      • Common procedures for controlling access to metadata—what access control mechanisms will be used to ensure authorized accessibility and availability of metadata.
      • Major constraints and issues that could potentially affect, impede or prevent the design or development of a metadata management facility.
