Main Description

The step-by-step approach for producing this work product is as follows:

  1. Identify potential target users of the new system, including those who have disabilities, as described in the user profile.
  2. Classify users according to their roles and responsibilities.  Different classifications of users have different requirements.
  3. Conduct requirements gathering sessions, surveys, observations, etc. with target users of the new system,  including target users who have disabilities. Ask participants their accessibility needs (such as large print, sign language interpretation, wheelchair access, etc.) prior to conducting sessions.
  4. Identify high-level usability wants and needs (e.g., easy to use).
  5. Categorize usability and accessibility wants and needs to reduce redundancy.
  6. Identify why the usability wants and needs are important (e.g., need to complete a banking transaction without assistance, because there will be no help available after the 1 week of training has been completed).
  7. Prioritize the usability wants and needs (e.g., completing a transaction successfully is high priority, while the ability to print three copies vs. one copy of a record successfully without assistance is low priority).
  8. Define the usability wants and needs more specifically, from the perspective of the target users  (e.g., able to complete a transaction without assistance in 60 seconds or lessâ€¦transaction defined as XXXX).
  9. Validate Usability Requirements with new groups of target end users, including those who have different types of disabilities.
  10. Repeat the above steps if there are multiple groups of users, as wants and needs for a new solution may vary among these subgroups of users.
  11. Review requirements with users and stakeholders (management, marketing, etc.) to ensure there are no contradictory requirements (this includes all requirements, not just usability) and address workforce turnover and forecast.  Usually, the stakeholders have to review or approve the usability requirements.  They are the people who have an interest in the product:  business sponsor, content owner, marketing department, customer service representative, development or analysis department, creative or visual designer.
  12. Employ participatory methods (e.g., Joint Application Design (JAD), Customer Requirements and Task Specifications (CRTS), User Groups, etc.) with users to review and refine these requirements.
  13. Write requirements so they can be observed, measured, and understood by all engagement team members (e.g., there may be several implementations available to fulfill one Usability Requirement).
  14. Document requirements in the form of a matrix with preconditions, who, conditions, and criteria (see example above). Ensure the requirements document itself is accessible (see References).
