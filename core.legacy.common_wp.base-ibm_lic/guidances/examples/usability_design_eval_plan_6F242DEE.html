Main Description

Introduction

The ABC Kiosk Project Usability Design and Evaluation Plan is a document that specifies the activities, deliverables, schedules, dependencies, process, team members, and other aspects of how user-centered design will be implemented throughout the course of the design and development of the solution. Usability of the Accessibility design is included as part of the plan since the customer wants to be able to sell the Kiosk design to the US federal government which requires compliance with Section 508 accessibility standards. Its purpose is to ensure that ABC and its affiliates will provide their customers and end users (citizens) with a system that meets their needs from the standpoint of usability, accessibility, performance, and satisfaction.

The Usability Design and Evaluation Plan is identified as an early deliverable in of the ABC Kiosk Project. The intent of this document is to create and maintain a high level of focus on the overall usability of the kiosk for all users, including those with disabilities. For the solution to succeed, it is important to establish and intensify the level of the usability focus for all developers and to institute the proper tasks and procedures to ensure that the usability efforts are maintained throughout the project.

The Usability Design and Evaluation Plan is designed as a “living document” to be reviewed, updated, and expanded during the course of solution development.

Engagement Overview

The ABC Company is a leader in the communication business. They wish to expand their marketing to alternate channels, such as using kiosks in supermarkets and retail stores. This project is an attempt to move in that direction. The company also plans to expand their customer base to the US federal government by using similar kiosks in Post Offices across the country.

The new ABC Kiosk is based on requirements that examined the current system and future needs. As a result, a system is defined that will move users from the current, human-assisted solution to an interactive, computer-based solution. The current solution is a manual method of purchasing ABC products and services at a kiosk-like booth located at a handful of BigStore Super Stores.

The proposed solution encourages consumers to purchase services at automated, self-service kiosks to be located in 2,400 BigStore Super Stores throughout the U.S. To perform this task, the consumer must be able to identify, communicate, and select the desired service; enter sufficient personal and preferential information to be able to purchase the service and have it installed; and place the order and indicate payment option. Other functions of the system include marketing the ABC services in a manner that will provide the user with the experience of:

  • Seeing and hearing promos
  • Sampling services through a mix of live and canned samples
  • Listening to CD-quality audio, that also include captions and volume considerations
Interacting in all modes (keyboard, touch screen, perhaps voice command, etc.) with the display

User-Centered Design Process

User-centered design is an industry leading approach for designing competitive ease-of-use into the total user experience with applications. It ensures that products are easy to set up, easy to learn, easy to use, easy to upgrade, engaging, intuitive, and integrated. It involves having a multidisciplinary team design the solution, starting with the externals, everything the user sees, hears and touches. Continuous user input is gathered via user-centered design user feedback methods that address understanding users, evaluating design, and assessing competitiveness. For User-Centered Design, you should describe the audience using the product. After the target audience is described, finding the solution for the following questions is important.

  1. What do you want the product to do for you?
  2. In what sort of environment will you be using the product?
  3. What are your priorities in using this software? For example, what functions will you be using most often?

Usability Goals

The usability goals of the system are:

  • To provide an engaging self-service kiosk experience to the consumer that meets or exceeds the needs of ABC/affiliates customers
  • To allow ABC/affiliates to meet or exceed customers’ ordering needs through use of a kiosk system that:
    • Allows consumers to “walk up and use” the system without prior training and without the need for human assistance
    • Is easy to use
    • Is satisfying to the user

To test the design so far, the team puts together a preliminary version called a prototype. Prototypes can be as simple as pieces of paper with proposed screen designs sketched on them, or as developed as finished products. A prototype may not have all the function that will be in the product, but it has enough to test some part of the design. Test participants recruited from the target audience try out the prototype, and their task performance, reactions, and comments help the designers decide what to keep and what to change about the design. The design goes into a cycle of modification and re-testing until it meets functional and usability criteria.
Area usability goal that addressed the usability of the accessibility features of a system would:

  • Allow people with a variety of disabilities (including hearing, vision and mobility disabilities) to use the system as effectively as people without disabilities and limiting the need for any special assistive technologies. For example, personal ear phones may still need to be provided by a blind user, but would not require a complete screen reader.
  • To allow people with disabilities to “walk up and use’ the system without the need for assistance

Usability Features

“Walk-up-and-use”

In the current human-assisted, non-automated solution, consumers may not know what questions to ask to gain sufficient information to order their services at the kiosk. In other words, they may not know what they need to know in this new technological arena. Furthermore, users may feel intimidated by being forced to interact with a human in this domain, especially since they may know very little about the services being offered. This can be embarrassing for many consumers. By providing a “walk-up-and-use” interactive computer experience, users are free to explore the options at their own pace and not feel pressured to order until they are ready. They will be likely to approach the kiosk several times without the fear of being embarrassed in public by their lack of knowledge about the services. They may gather information on one visit and return to order the services on their next visit to BigStore.

By providing users with a simple solution that can be easily navigated without the use of a manual and without the need of prior domain or kiosk experience, the user can order wireless services successfully, in private, and relatively fast while completing other shopping tasks at the same time. In other words, the user drives the system rather than having the system drive the user.

Some user interface design techniques that will be used to assist the consumer in using the system without training or prior knowledge include:

  • Visual, tactile and auditory feedback to confirm actions completed, including ordering
  • The ability to go back and edit user-supplied information
  • Having buttons that are clear, easy to find and accessible for persons of varying abilities. (e.g. low vision or blind, height flexibility). For example, all buttons should include Braille labels, be reachable, and should be simple to operate with one finger by not requiring tight grasping, pinching, or twisting of the wrist.
  • Having displays that are easy to see for someone in a wheelchair. The displays need to provide auditory feedback for users who are blind.
  • Simple navigation paths and methods
  • Assisting the user by means of visual and auditory help or a computerized “assistant”
  • Displaying and explaining up front what the user can do with the system
Easy to use

The same features that make the new ABC system “walk-up-and-use” also make it easier to use. Up front overview information and an interactive computer assistant or intelligent agent emphasizes to the consumer that no training is necessary, thus putting the user at ease early in the shopping experience. 

By putting the user in charge, instead of the system or the human booth attendant, users are able to more easily perform tasks when they want to perform them. In addition, they have the capability to handle multiple tasks simultaneously without losing data or having to back all the way out of an existing task. For example, a user can start entering initial information, get more detailed information about a service, and continue with the ordering process with no effort and no lost information.

Other features of a graphical and interactive user interface that improve ease of use include consistently used icons, graphics, and information organization. Icons are symbolic representations of objects and information. They allow for quick and easy identification of the objects they represent without taking up much screen space. When combined with text, users find them much easier to find and work with than text alone.

The ability to display graphics and audible descriptions not only allows the user interface to be more aesthetically pleasing, but also provides the ability to represent large quantities of data without having to show numbers. For example, pictures and audible descriptions of equipment can be displayed and spoken to provide a better understanding to consumers of what will be attached to their house or on their property in order to activate a service.

Good user interface technique also allows designers to better organize information using real-life metaphors. Similar and familiar information can be easily identified when seen in logical groupings and by real-world objects. This allows users to find desired information more easily when they need it because they can tap into things they already know about.

Problem “Fix” Rate Model

Because ABC views ease of use as a market differentiator of public kiosk sites, ABC wishes to establish a market leadership in ease-of-use for its kiosks; therefore, 100% of the Severity 1 and 2 usability problems and 90% of the Severity 3 usability problems will be fixed
These are defined as follows:

  • Severity Level 1. Users are unable to continue with a task or series of tasks due to the problem.
  • Severity Level 2. Users have considerable difficulty completing a task or series of tasks but are eventually able to continue. Users may fail to complete a task correctly.
  • Severity Level 3. Users have minor difficulty completing a task or series of tasks.

Multidisciplinary Team

The multidisciplinary team will be staffed with the following positions (if a person has been identified, their initials are shown in parentheses):

  • User Interface Architect (JB) – Overall responsibility for user interface design and development (full-time)
  • User Interface Designers (BS, AD) – Provides high-level and low-level user interface design specification (both full-time)
  • Visual Designer (MD) – Provides visuals (bitmaps and icons) for user interface design (part-time)
  • Usability Evaluation Specialist (DP) – Plans and conducts usability evaluations of user interface. Works with Accessibility Specialist to ensure scenarios needs of users with disabilities and that people with disabilities are included in the evaluations whenever possible. Identifies any new hardware required to enhance accessibility. (part-time)
  • Accessibility Specialist - Provides input to high-level and low-level user interface design specification to ensure accessibility requirements are met. (part time)
  • Documentation Specialist (EA) – Writes application user’s manuals and help information. Works with Accessibility Specialist to ensure manuals and help information are accessible and address alternatives of interacting with the system. Writes any special instructions with respect to the usage of unique hardware required to enhance accessibility.(part-time)
  • Electronic Performance Support Specialists/Performance Analyst (JM) – Assesses training needs and skills gaps and designs and develops electronic performance support systems (full-time)
  • Lead Use Case Modeler - Business Analyst or Representative – Analyzes requirements and writes use cases (part-time)
  • Object Architect – Assesses potential for reuse of business objects, patterns, and frameworks. Often considered responsible for business object model (part-time)
  • Lead User Interface Developer (DB) – Designs and develops user interface objects and views (part-time)

Assumptions and Dependencies

  • Availability of skilled resource to staff the usability design and evaluation effort from both client and IBM
  • Access to a large sample of users of varying abilities who match user profiles and can readily serve as design evaluation participants
  • Timely access to use case models so that design activities can be launched and conducted properly
  • Access to business analysts who can provide explanation of unclear use cases
  • Access to properly equipped system units that meet the memory, hard disk space, input device, and display requirements of the eventual intended system
  • Availability of stable software during usability evaluation of interim application builds. Software may also include assistive technology software that is being updated to better work with the new application software or system.

Change Control Process

The following provides a detailed process to follow if a change to this Document of Understanding (DOU) is required.

  • A Project Change Request (PCR) will be the vehicle for communicating change. The PCR must describe the change, the rationale for the change, and the effect the change will have on the project.
  • The designated Project Manager or authorized representative of the requesting party will review the proposed change and determine whether to submit the request to the other party.
  • Project Managers or authorized representatives will review the proposed change and approve it for further investigation or reject it. XYZ will specify any charges for such investigation. If the investigation is authorized, the Project Managers or authorized representatives will sign the PCR, which will constitute approval for the investigation charges. XYZ will invoice GOV for any such charges as part of the monthly debit memo process. The investigation will determine the effect the implementation of the PCR will have on price, schedule, and other terms and conditions of the DOU.
  • A written Change Authorization and/or PCR must be signed by both parties to authorize implementation of the investigated changes.

Accessibility Considerations 

When considering usability for users with special needs, consideration of their characteristics can be useful. For example, physically impaired individuals face their primary difficulty in using the computer's input devices, or in handling storage media. Individuals in this group include individuals with congenital disabilities, spinal cord injuries, and progressive diseases, as well as individuals who are without the use of just a hand or arm. Some individuals who can use only one arm or hand (temporarily or permanently) or who use a headstick or mouthstick cannot activate multiple buttons or keys at the same time. Adding some options to the keyboard handling routines would allow many individuals to use the keyboard. Providing means to connect "alternate keyboards" would provide access for individuals who have more severe disabilities.

Visually impaired individuals have their primary difficulties with the output display, although newer display-based input systems (e.g., mice, touchscreens) may also pose problems. This group includes individuals who have failing vision and individuals with partial vision, as well as those who are blind. The primary solution strategies involve providing a mechanism to connect alternate display or display translator devices to the computer, and providing alternatives to display-based input
