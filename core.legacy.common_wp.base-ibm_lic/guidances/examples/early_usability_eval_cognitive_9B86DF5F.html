Cognitive Walkthrough

Executive Summary
A cognitive walkthrough evaluation of the preliminary ABC product design was performed from October 7 to October 16, 19xx. The focus was on the Order Entry function of Sales as the central function of the application. Most of the other functions support Sales or exploit the information gathered during the order entry process.

Overall, the preliminary design of ABC is very good. The users did not have any major difficulties performing the Order Entry task. The layout of the Order Entry window was obvious to them and easy for them to follow. The terminology used for labeling field prompts and choices caused most of the problems. Many of these were resolved using terms agreed to by the users in a follow-on session and are given in the results in this report.

ABC was found to have 135 usability issues or questions and 4 accessibility issues or questions. The following is a summary of the impact of these issues:
Twenty-six were rated high-impact issues or questions. Action must be taken prior to deployment to enable task completion and avoid extreme user dissatisfaction. Many of these are due to the early state of the prototype. They include ensuring that processes are well-defined and easy for users to do, avoiding serious errors caused by incorrect default values, and the like.

There is a cumulative effect of many low- and moderate-impact issues that may have caused significant user dissatisfaction. Take this into consideration when allocating resources to enhance the design of the application.

It is recommended that these usability and accessibility issues be corrected, that the prototype be enhanced starting with the Sales functions and other high usage functions, and that additional evaluations and usability tests with the intended users be done before the first installation of the application (see “Next Steps” on page 32).

Introduction
Solution Overview
The ABC application is an integrated florist shop application that supports taking orders for wire delivery, local delivery, and carryout. It is designed to integrate with the common florist delivery networks, the florist shop’s accounting software, and a credit card verification service. It also supports a variety of business analysis reports and marketing support functions.

Method
Overview
The cognitive walkthrough evaluation involves observing user representatives performing selected tasks using a prototype or printed copy of the available windows for an application. A follow-on design session with user and development representatives occurred to resolve some of the issues that arose during the evaluation.

Participants
Ten DEF employees representing the users participated in the evaluation. These participants had an average tenure with DEF of 8.7 years and had been performing the tasks tested in this evaluation for an average of 4.3 years. Eight of the ten participants had a high school education; one participant had 2 years of college. One participant was visually impaired and uses a screen reader to access the application.

Tasks
Four scenarios were performed:

1. Entering a customer order.
2. Completing the initial processing of the order that would send it over the network.
3. Making a change to the order.
4. Checking the status of the order.

Usability Impact Ratings (Measures)
Usability issues or questions are rated according to the level of impact they have on successful task completion and user satisfaction. There are three levels of impact:

High: Prevents the user from successfully completing the task. Will cause extreme user dissatisfaction or make it easy for the user to make errors. Examples include:

- Lack of a clearly defined process.
- Difficult-to-use widgets, forms.
- No initial focus set.
- Keyboard equivalents are not available for all mouse-centric tasks; all important visual widgets do not have meaningful alternative text.
- Extremely confusing navigation.
- No audio equivalents for significant visual material.
- No visual equivalents for significant audio material.
- High contrast system settings not supported.

Action: Must be corrected or resolved prior to deployment to enable task completion and avoid extreme user dissatisfaction.

Moderate: Causes the user difficulty but task can be completed. Will cause user dissatisfaction. Examples include:

- Confusing or inconsistent placement of controls.
- Somewhat confusing navigation.
- Confusing tabbing order.
- Nonstandard use of widgets and terminology.
- Information presented by the speech interface is confusing or misleading.
- Text generated through standard API’s.

Action: Should be corrected or resolved prior to deployment to facilitate task completion and avoid user dissatisfaction.

Low: Minor problems that do not significantly affect task completion. May cause some user dissatisfaction, especially when considered in aggregate. Examples include:

- Unappealing visual design.
- Unclear language.
- Punctuation.
- Redundant or useless information is presented by the speech interface.
- No standard or robust set of short-cut keys.

Action: Should be corrected or resolved prior to deployment to avoid user dissatisfaction.

In addition, participants completed questionnaires to assess their satisfaction with the software across a number of attributes.

Procedure
The participants took part in the evaluation individually. When brought into the evaluation setting, the participant was introduced to the concept of usability and accessibility evaluation and its goals. The participant was informed that the evaluation was a test of the software design, not the participant, and the participant’s individual performance would not be shared with his or her management. The participant was informed that he or she would take part in four scenarios to evaluate a software design. The participant was encouraged to think aloud as he or she walked through the scenarios and was given a short demonstration showing how the think-aloud method might be used when setting the time on a watch.

The participant was then asked whether there were any questions. All questions were answered except those dealing with specifics of completing the four scenarios. The participant was shown how to signal the beginning of each task and how to signal when he or she thought the scenario was over. At the end of each scenario, the participant completed a written questionnaire to assess his or her perceptions of the application. After completing the questionnaire, the evaluation conductor answered questions from the participant and clarified the participant’s survey answers if they were not clear.

Before leaving, the participant was reminded not to share the specifics of the study with his or her coworkers. The participant was also reminded that the software was in early design, so significant changes could be expected by the time the software arrived on the production floor many months later.

Recommendations for Improvement
Order Entry Menu Bar and Pull-Down Menus
This is a preliminary attempt to organize some of the actions available from the Order Entry window on the menu bar pull-down menus. These actions are taken from the list provided in the requirements document (page OP-16). Some of the actions from the list were not included because they are not used during order entry, were duplicates, or belonged in other windows such as the Method of Payment window.

Note that any choices that are never available to users because the users do not have the authority to perform that action are removed. For example, if shop clerks are not allowed to void an order, then the Void action does not appear on their Order pull-down menu.

Next Steps
The user interface design for ABC has made an excellent start. It is recommended that the following additional usability and accessibility activities be performed:

  • Conduct additional design sessions to lay out and review the windows for the various functions. This can save rework on the prototype, not to mention the developed product.
  • Add an accessibility activity. Use the appropriate Accessibility checklist to verify that all accessibility issues have been addressed during the design phase. Alternately, use Home Page Reader or similar technology to review the accessibility of the interface.
  • Develop a more complete design prototype of the key Sales functions: Order Entry (including the Payment Method window), Cash Register, Wire In, and Ticket Entry, along with key supporting functions such as Message Console and Customer Maintenance. Prototype these functions first to support early testing.
  • Add the additional functions to the prototype so these functions can also be tested with the appropriate users.
  • Conduct additional usability and accessibility tests with the new prototypes. Select users from outside DEF as test subjects. Ensure that the user’s background matches the scenarios being tested. For example, use flower shop owners/managers to test the Business Analysis and Reporting functions. Use shop owners/mangers or bookkeepers to test the accounting functions. Use store clerks to test the Sales functions.
