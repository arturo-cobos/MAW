Main Description

Introduction The introduction section focuses on establishing a context for the current and future states of the applications or systems, within scope.

Overview Brief overview of the project or release.  This section creates a context and scope for the master test plan. 

Current system environment A short description of the current technical and operational environment is included here. 

Project Goals This sub-section describes the project goals.  These goals include the broad business objectives and any underlying business process changes.

Release Objectives This section focuses on the system development objectives for each release, and these objectives, in turn, drive the overall test objectives described in the next section.

Test Objectives Test objectives describe the tangible goals of testing.  Test objectives can be at varying levels of detail.  At the master test plan level, they are driven by the solution objectives and describe the overall objectives for testing.  They include the high level objectives to test the business functions and the technical requirements, and help to reduce the risks and costs of failure. 

Test Scope The scope defines the boundaries of testing.  This section describes or lists applications and systems within scope of the master test plan.  The master test plan covers only dynamic testing.  Static test plan covers static testing of the development and testing work products.  If any of the applications or systems is known to be out of scope for testing, it is important to mention the exclusions here.

Assumptions Any underlying assumptions on which this master test plan is based are clearly stated in this section.  This section is key to successful testing because, should any of the assumptions turn out to be untrue, this master test plan and the detailed test plans it drives will require adjustment.  Documenting them here allows them to be tested for validity throughout the project lifecycle. 

Test Strategy

Business Functions The business functions or use cases from the use case model are included here.

Structural Functions Also known as technical requirements, the non-functional requirements, such as availability, performance, and security that are within scope for testing are listed in here.

Risk Assessment Risk assessment at this level is driven by the project risk assessment and expands on risks specific to the test domain.  For example one of the objectives for testing is to mitigate the project risks through testing (i.e., within the scope of test domain), and to manage the testing risks.  A detailed risk assessment is done here, driven by the business functions and technical requirements of the solution under development; e.g., new technology, limited application knowledge, etc.

Test Focus Areas The generic set of test focus areas from the test strategy are examined and applied to the project to determine the specific ones that are applicable to the project.  Again, the basic set may be expanded or a subset used, based on the project needs.  At this time the quality goals would also determine the needs for test focus areas. 

Levels of Testing As indicated earlier, the basic set of test levels from the standards may be used or expanded to accommodate project specific needs.  Each stage of development represents a known level of physical integration and quality.  These stages, known as testing levels, would have been defined at the time of testing strategy using the testing standards of the organization, if they exist, or using testing practices.  The build procedure from the solution development helps to confirm the appropriate levels of testing and the contents of each.


Entry/Exit Criteria The prerequisites (entry criteria) and post conditions (exit criteria) are defined for each of the detailed levels of testing within the project scope.  The entry and exit criteria are defined with the participation of the stakeholder representatives so that the handing-over organization fully understands what the receiving organization expects, and any gaps can be clarified and negotiated to a common agreement and understanding. 

Sometimes the Statement of Work and contracts will have dealt with these questions and issues in extensive detail, and at other times they may have been ambiguous or incomplete, and clear definition of criteria will be needed.  This definition helps to reduce problems later during detailed planning, and also to better plan whatever is within scope for each detailed level of test.  Any organization change management activities or business process testing issues can also be identified at this time. 

Functional and Structural Test Types Testing types relate to testing of business functions (Functional Types) or to structural functions (Structural Types).  Using the testing types that were outlined in the test strategy, and taking into consideration the goals of the build cycle, the business and non-functional requirements and the operational model, the functional and structural testing types will be selected that are appropriate to the specific needs of the project/solution.

Test Plan

Test Planning Workshops During the master test plan development activity (especially for large projects), joint planning sessions of all stakeholder representatives are held where they collectively agree on a common set of priorities for all levels of testing.  The details of any joint facilitated planning workshops held are documented here.  The list of stakeholder representatives who participated in the session and their roles on the project are also documented. 

Roles and Responsibilities Organizational responsibility refers to describing in generic terms the hand-offs and hand-overs that occur at various points in the development lifecycle, as the product or solution evolves from specification to design to implementation. 

During the master test plan development activity, all stakeholder representatives come to a consensus on, among other things, areas of organizational responsibility.  This is done at a high level to identify which business or functional areas need to get involved in order to move the overall test planning into detailed levels of test planning, and to designing test cases, preparing to execute the test cases and to reporting the results.  At the master test plan level, it is a good practice to identify primary as well as secondary responsibility for the major testing activities.

Contingencies Standard contingencies as well as any specific ones suggested by the project and test domain risk assessments should be identified here.

Test Environment Build Strategy With the overall test planning activities and responsibilities defined, it is time to define test environment requirements so that the necessary hardware, software, tools, and other infrastructure components can be assembled to create the test environment.  Especially with changing and evolving technology, it is imperative to not only create the test environment early in the project, but also to consider the ways and means to test the environment and to put standards and procedures in place to operate the test environment.  This strategy includes backup and recovery of the test data, and regression testing of software and hardware upgrades.  The master test plan initiates the planning for building and maintaining the technical test environment.  This includes the test tools as well.

Test Data Strategy By the time the master test plan is developed, enough development planning would have occurred to begin to delineate a test data strategy and an approach for test data creation, setup, and maintenance.  It is also appropriate to consider how test data can be shared amongst the different levels of testing.  No details are included here; they are the subjects of the detailed test specification work products relating to each detailed level of testing.

Test Tools Build/Buy Using the technology or tools strategy described during the test strategy, tools specifications, evaluation, selection, acquisition, and implementation planning steps are initiated at this time.  The actual selection and implementation processes are the subject of the test environment work product and are out of scope for the master test plan.

Test Management and Reporting Procedures

Test Management Test Management refers to the management systems that govern the testing activities.  These systems refer to the procedures for problem tracking/management, change management, and progress tracking during testing.  It is best to define the test management systems once, as part of the master test plan, and to let the detailed test plans refer to them and use them during testing.  Problem tracking/management and change management processes are the subjects of defect management procedures.

  • Problem Tracking/Management Procedures: Only references may be included here, not the actual procedures.
  • Change Management Procedures: Only references may be included here, not the actual procedures.
  • Progress Tracking Procedures: Only references may be included here, not the actual procedures.

Test Reporting Status and other periodic test reports for ongoing progress tracking, as well as hand-offs from one level of testing to another, are described here. 

  • Test Reports: Frequency and format may be described here.
