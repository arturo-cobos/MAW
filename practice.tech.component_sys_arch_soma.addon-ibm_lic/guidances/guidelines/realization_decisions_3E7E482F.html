Guideline: Realization Decisions
Describes the realization decisions in SOMA.
Main Description

Realization decisions are related to mapping of conceptual and logical architectural elements to their physical implementations. These decisions comprise different aspect of services and service component realization such as the technologies, the standards, the legacy system functionality that may be leveraged, etc. Existing decisions are consolidated into realization groups to simplify the future implementation. In addition all decisions are validated constantly.

The following table contains key architectural decisions which are usually needed to be made. It is important to mention the scope and context of these decisions are dynamic and constantly changing.

There are a number of alternatives to be considered relative to deciding how to realize services:

  • Build vs. buy
  • Transformation uses a combination of techniques including business rules extraction to pull out a segment of functionality to be used independently for the realization of the component's service.
  • Integration creates wrappers for legacy applications to access required functionality
  • Subscription is based on the availability of a publish-subscribe model (in a message-oriented middleware context) in which a consumer subscribes to the services provided by the provide.

Tooling: The process of establishing realization decisions as well as architectural decisions overall always has been more art than science. It heavily depends on someone's technical knowledge and experience more than objective approach. In addition, many such decisions are project-specific and usually documented retrospectively. Hence, it is hard to find tools or methodology for establishing realization decisions.

One of the known techniques is called QOC (Questions, Options, and Criteria). According to QOC we map each problem statement (question) to a set of options each of which can provide a solution to the problem in question. Decision criteria are associated with the options. Selecting an option can lead to a follow up question and process repeats until a proper solution will be defined.

QOC helps to organize architectural decision process; however, it does not eliminate subjectivity nor does it offer a list of questions to be asked. However, a fully-automated tool in this case probably won't be very effective since an architecture decision will always have a project-specific part as well as will always be subjective.
