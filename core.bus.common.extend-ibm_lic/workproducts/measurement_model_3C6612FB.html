Artifact: Measurement Model (BUS 360)
The Measurement Model work product describes the measurements that have been identified and developed as part of an project, either as a standalone unit or in the context of an implementation of a measurement program.
Domains: Business Value & Financials
Work Product Kinds: Model, Requirement
Purpose
The purpose of the Measurement Model work product is to formally define measurements that will reflect an organization’s performance and to describe empirical methods for collecting, analyzing, and using those measurements. It can provide a basis for contract negotiations, as well as for product and process improvement commitments. In addition, the Measurement Model becomes the "requirements" feeding into the measurement program implementation being implemented for the organization.

Main DescriptionThe Measurement model describes various attributes about the measurements defined in the model including:

  • Primitive Metrics

  • Frequency of Data Collection
  • Reporting Frequency
  • Traditional Reporting Process(es)

The term “measurement” as used in this document reflects the sorts of entities that must be elicited when defining and developing the measurement program and system for an organization.  Generally, the term “measurement” can refer to either a computed metric, which involves a calculation among primitive metrics, or a primitive metric that reflects a directly observable or countable event or data point.

Key Considerations

Industry-accepted techniques for building the Measurement Model work product include the Balanced Scorecard approach, the Goal-Question-Metric (GQM) approach, and similar modern analytical methods for objectifying organizational performance.  These techniques are documented in published materials found in References. 

There are several methods for validating a Measurement Model work product:

  • Peer Review

An independent consultant with expertise in measurements should participate in a review of the developed Measurement Model.  An independent consultant’s expertise and exposure to various organizations equips him or her with the experience and insights necessary to validate this critical work product.  In addition, the work product should be reviewed by a technical member of the project team and by a member of the target organization.

[Release 2 of the Measurements Catalog provides function for specifying organization-specific policies in the association between a measurement entity and the organization, resolving a significant problem with reusing measurement entities presented by Release 1 of the Measurements Catalog.]

  • Measurements Catalog Correlations

Definitions in the proposed Measurement Model should be compared to definitions for existing entities in the formally governed publications such as the Global AMS Delivery Measurements Catalog.  Measurements that map to entities in the catalog should be named precisely as they appear in the catalog.  New measurements defined in the measurement model should be added to the catalog and associated with the contributing organization. 

  • Measurement Model Success Factors

A well-defined Measurement Model adheres to the following principles, which assist in ensuring its success in implementation:

  • “Less is more.” Many measurements in a model do not necessarily make a good model.  Through the prioritization and balancing process of the IBM approach, an optimal minimum number of measurements persist into the final model.  As a result, those measurements should naturally provide a tight coupling to the target organizations identified business goals.
  • Alignment to the defined organizational behaviors.  Measurements that do not line up with the defined organizations behaviors will not persist in the final Measurement Model, unless such retention is required by specific legal mandates or business policies firmly rooted in the charter (such as a contract) for the organization.
Tailoring
Impact of not havingWithout this work product, no formally agreed-to methodology for demonstrating performance achievement in managing an organization would exist. As such, the ability to implement a successful measurements program for the organization is greatly hindered if not completely blocked.
Reasons for not needingThere exists no valid rationale for not building the Measurement Model work product as part of a measurement engagement unless a substitute work product describing the measurement requirements in sufficient depth is available.
