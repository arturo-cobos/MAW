Artifact: Measurement Framework Requirements (ART 0596)
This artifact defines the policies and guidelines for identifying and designing performance measurements that reflect the business goals of the enterprise.
Domains: Organization Design
Work Product Kinds: Measurement
Purpose

Documenting this artifact ensures that performance metrics for both the operational business process, that has been either deployed or outsourced, to deliver business capability and the process organization that performs the business process are synchronized to deliver business goals.

Description
Main Description

This artifact defines the method in which metrics will be identified and applied to measure business results for end-to-end processes. It translates the high-level business performance objectives of an end-to-end process into an integrated set of lower-level metrics and targets for each of the component processes that may be performed in different organizations to ensure that individual process performance contributes optimally to the achievement of the end-to-end process objectives.

This document covers:

Notation

A Measurement Framework should address the policies, standards, and guidelines for the following components:

  • Process Measurements
  • Organizational Measurements
  • Service Level Agreements (SLA) for IT and 3rd party providers

Process Measurement

A growing number of enterprises are adopting some form of a balanced scorecard to communicate strategic plans and to identify both leading and lagging measurements critical to realizing those plans.

A basic principle of the balanced scorecard is that measurement targets are identified for different perspectives. These are:

  • Financial Perspective – the top perspective that focuses on measuring whether an organization is achieving its financial goal
  • Customer Perspective – focuses on what must be done, what’s most important, from the customer’s perspective to achieve the Financial Perspective
  • Internal Process Perspective – focuses on what an organization must be doing well to meet the customer needs defined in the Customer Perspective
  • Learning & Growth Perspective – focuses on how an organization is improving its ability to innovate, improve, and learn in order to support success with the critical operations and processes defined in the Internal Process Perspective

Developing a balanced scorecard can take several months and involve a number of levels of management. To achieve the measurements identified in the scorecard will more than likely require initiating new business initiatives and numerous projects. This is outside the scope of this work product and should be viewed as a management process focused on strategic planning. However, developing metrics for an end-to-end process may be driven by measurement targets identified in a balanced scorecard or other well define measurement program within the enterprise. It is extremely important to leverage these when defining a Performance Framework.

Whether a balanced scorecard exists or not, it is extremely important to identify the metrics for core strategic drivers and then decompose those into operational metrics that can be allocated to end-to-end business processes.

For business processes, the Measurement Framework defines the method in which metrics will be applied to measure business results for end-to-end processes. It translates the high-level business performance objectives of an end-to-end process into an integrated set of lower-level metrics and targets for each of the component processes that may be performed in different organizations to ensure that individual process performance contributes optimally to the achievement of the end-to-end process objectives.


Organizational Measurement

When considering performance measurements you should also consider developing measurements for the organizations that support processes. The idiom "people do what they’re paid to do" can result in negative process performance results if organizational measurements are not aligned with process measurements. Therefore, it is important that organizational (people) measurements, with appropriate incentives and rewards, motivate process performers in a way that supports the goals and objectives of the end-to-end processes.

Since process performers may have a functional responsibilities unrelated to their process responsibilities, you may need to consider proportioning measurements between more than one perspective, e.g. process results and function unit results.

An enterprise typically sets a structure of incentives and rewards to motivate employees to behave in a way that helps the enterprise achieve certain business goals or objectives. However, this structure is often focused on behaviors that are associated with the objectives of specific organizations within the enterprise that may have little or nothing to do with end-to-end process performance.

Once processes have been restructured and integrated to deliver end-to-end business results, the incentives and rewards of the people performing those processes should be examined, and, where appropriate, realigned with the end-to-end results. This realignment should encourage the types of behavior that helps the enterprise achieve its end-to-end process performance targets.

For example, a customer support center may decide that it’s important to handle as many customer calls per day as possible and recognize and reward those individuals with the highest call volume (even if the same customer has to call multiple times to resolve a problem). However, if the goal of the end-to-end customer relationship management process is to achieve high levels of customer satisfaction, the customer support process may contribute more effectively by focusing on the number of customer problems resolved on the first call. This might lead to longer calls and fewer calls by customer service representative, but a higher level of customer satisfaction.

Rewarding the right behavior will, in many cases, mean redefining what the right behavior is in light of the higher level business objectives and measures.

Service Level Agreements

Each process has certain performance and nonfunctional requirements that have to be met by the organizations that execute or enable the process (e.g., IT, 3rd party service providers, etc.). These requirements need to be defined, documented, communicated, accepted and then managed by those involved in order to maintain satisfactory performance of the processes.

Service Level Agreements (SLAs) serve as a clear and unambiguous definition of the responsibilities of all the participants in a process and are used to manage ongoing performance and respond to process execution problems and issues.

Internally, within the enterprise, process managers depends on others to meet their performance commitments in order to maintain the effective operation and performance of the processes under his or her control. These commitments may define the required performance of other process areas that provide services to one process area or they may define the required performance of the technology that enables the process. In either case, the explicit definition, communication and commitment to performance requirements and objectives is critical to the operation of an enterprise.

When dealing with 3rd party providers (e.g., payroll service providers), the SLAs define the relationship and the responsibilities of the parties and serve as the basis for assessing and managing the performance of the 3rd party providers.

Key Considerations
  • Process performer measurements identify the individual performance metrics that align with the end-to-end process and, consequently, to the enterprise objectives and targets. As part of the implementation of the end-to-end process, examine the existing incentive and reward structure related to the processes operating within the specific business units and decide if:
    • they reward the behaviors required by the integrated process (keep them),
    • if they reward behaviors that are counterproductive (replace them),
    • if they have no impact at all on end-to-end performance (justify them), or
    • if desirable behaviors are not recognized (add them).
  • SLAs need to be as complete and explicit as possible. They need to define what is expected in terms of throughput, turnaround, accuracy and content for all normal and reasonable exception conditions. They should focus on what the parties are expected to do, not necessarily how it will be done (unless the how is critical to specific performance requirements). They should also specify what will be done when requirements and commitments are not met.
Tailoring
Impact of not having

Without this artifact:

  • Individual processes that are performed and managed by different functional units will likely adopt metrics to measure their performance that will focus on local priorities and objectives, but will likely sub-optimize end-to-end performance. Starting with the end-to-end business performance objectives will help to identify the individual process metrics that contribute to those objectives. The resulting integrated set of KPIs will allow organizational process managers to focus on measuring and managing the right behaviors, activities and performance for optimal business results.
  • The corollary to "you can only manage what you can measure" is that "you get what you measure." Even with the same process design, results may be significantly different when different outcomes are valued. To ensure that the outcomes of the process contribute to the achievement of business performance goals, the incentives and reward structure process performers has to be aligned with the end-to-end process goals. If it is not, process and business performance may be less than desired.
  • The lack of clearly defined SLAs for service providers will have a negative on internal business processes that have dependencies on the services.
Reasons for not needing

This artifact is not required if a robust measurement already exists.
