Task: Plan the Project
A collaborative task that outlines an initial agreement on how the project will achieve its goals. The resulting project plan provides a summary-level overview of the project.
Purpose
Get stakeholder buy-in for starting the project and team commitment to move forward with it. This plan can be updated as the project progresses based on feedback and changes in the environment.

      Main Description

      On Agile With Discipline projects, the project is planned at two levels:

      Level one project planning

      • Is first conducted at the end of the open phase, before starting the solution definition iteration (during an IBM / Client sales cycle or as part of the clientâ€™s governance process) to the level required to acquire and assign key staff to facilitate and conduct the definition iteration. If enough information is provided, or some level of assumptions can be made, multiple development iterations can also be estimated by creating an agile tops-down estimate, which involves estimating the development staff that will be needed for n iterations of a fixed time. The leads estimate how much scope can be completed in each iteration to set a range of estimate about how much scope can be completed in each iteration, so that some level of expectation can be set with the client.
      • Is conducted throughout the solution definition iteration, as the requirements, architecture and planning elements are elaborated on. This allows the team to set stakeholder expectations (Time, Cost, and Scope), develop teaming scenarios to meet the business needs, and then gain commitment for multiple development iterations.
      • Includes the initial allocation of functionality to iterations when there is enough information available, or reasonable assumptions can be made. For instance, how many iterations, how much functionality is allocated to each and how many (parallel?) teams are needed based on the estimated amount of work.

      Level two project planning:

      • Is conducted at the start of each iteration and considers reflections from the past iteration (definition or development). These details are addressed in the iterative development practice and tasks.
      • Validate the functionality allocated to the iteration.  Refer to "Define and estimate the iterations and scenarios " in steps section.
      Steps
      Identify, evaluate and plan for risks

      The team identifies project risks, performs a qualitative risk analysis to assess their order of magnitude, and updates the PM log.

      The project manager facilitates works with the team and stakeholders to address and develop mitigation plans.

      Responses may include avoiding or mitigating risks, exploring opportunities, or increasing the probability and positive impacts of the risk. Depending on the case, work items may have to be added or removed from iteration to make sure that responses will be prioritized and handled by the team along with other project work. Because it is not feasible to plan responses for all identified risks, the team may decide to accept some of them.

      Plan and estimate the definition iteration

      The team uses the information gathered to date to estimate the number of weeks of facilitated sessions required to gather enough breadth of requirements, architecture and planning information to start a first iteration of development of one or more empowered agile teams. This time will vary from project to project, but, for most IBM services project, three to five weeks is a good starting range. Then, at that point, for a larger initiative, based on business priorities, a second iteration of definition work can begin, in parallel with the first iteration of development.

      The team uses the workshop plan template to develop a rough plan, considering facilitated sessions, dedicated team members, and assuming a high degree of stakeholder participation. Staff is estimated using AWD solution generation assets.

      Plan for accelerator usage

      The team uses the AWD Accelerator Plan to explicitly plan the use of high-return AWD Accelerators / Best Practices.

      AWD Best practices should be consistently planned for and used across agile projects to support consistency, reuse and scalability across agile projects.

      Indicate the level of usage planned for each accelerator.

      Enter a distinct description of how the team will use each accelerator.

      If reuse opportunities are extensive, extend the reuse accelerator by using the candidate assets list.

      Develop the Agile Project Management System Summary

      The Project Management System Summary provides a brief summary of the project management aspects that should be considered for any project, and provides the structure that allows the team to be fully empowered.

      As content is defined through facilitated sessions and follow-up efforts, document the summary plans.

      The Project Schedule and Resource Plan sections are typically created at a high level, using a scheduling tool. They contain:

      • Overall Calendar â€“ Specifies business days and holidays.
      • Work Breakdown Structure (WBS) â€“ This is a hierarchical decomposition of the phases, activities and tasks that the AWD Team must complete, and aligns with the standard AWD process or is copied from another like-project.
      • Shows timeboxed iterations and key milestones that are key to the project team including any demands on external teams that are not part of the core agile team.

      Review and finalize scheduling information and ensure the critical path is clear. Ensure start and end dates and resource levels are reasonable and in alignment with the AWD Charter, and ensure resource weekly hours are level. Ensure the schedule is realistic and achievable.

      Expand areas that require additional detail (e.g. deployment checklist).

      Define and estimate the iterations and scenarios

      Develop iteration goals, completion criteria and a timebox end date, and document them in the accelerator plan.

      Based on the priorities, and estimates developed during Story Driven Development activities, use the iteration column of the AWD Accelerator Plan to group prioritized-requirements into iterations that align to the iteration goals, or if there is not a theme that is aligned with each iteration, break them into equal-sized iterations of two to six weeks (a guideline of four week iteration time boxes is recommended for new applications).

      The team forecasts the number of iterations required to complete the project, using the known or anticipated velocity of the team to estimate roughly how much work can be done in the given time-frame. Based on the constraints of time (key business milestones), cost (resources) and scope (prioritized requirements), the team can develop scenarios of how the stories iterations can be decomposed into multiple parallel agile teams to deliver more scope in a shorter period of time. Additional resources may be needed to provide guidance and integrate the efforts of the two or more agile teams.

      Whenever possible, the team should consider deploying small releases (release cycles of three to four months at most). Releasing software into production frequently is a good way to get early feedback from the users, in order to enhance the overall product quality. Frequent releases may cause more churn in the application than some stakeholders may want, so the main driver should be to release based on key business value milestones, which are specific to each project.

      The end-to-end integrated iteration plans become the release plan.

      Estimate resource and other project costs
      Develop a cost of fixed, dedicated team to support the iteration plans. A simplified capacity based project costing model can be applied by multiplying the approximate cost for a dedicated agile team (or teams) for a fixed duration of time. This first round of planning should keep things very rough and flexible. The goal is just to articulate value against the budget constraints of the project, and to help stakeholders decide whether it is worth moving forward with the project or not. If necessary, propose options to decrease costs, such as removing low-value and high-cost work items from the scope. Some stakeholders may be willing to be more flexible than others, but the key argument to make is it is better to quickly arrive at a ballpark number, begin work, then validate those assumptions through execution.
      Obtain approvals and baseline the plan

      Review the PMSS with stakeholders and update it to reflect their feedback.

      Obtain all approvals, including Sponsors and other key stakeholders.

      Baseline the Plan.

      Key Considerations
      • Gain agreement with stakeholders and the rest of the project team regarding the order of objectives and the duration of the project. Make adjustments as necessary.
      • When there are multiple teams and multiple iterations, consider the dependencies among components, data, or requirements which impact the ability to schedule parallel work among teams. When dependencies exist, the Agile team needs to analyze different scenarios (e.g., specialized teams, re-allocate functionality) to determine the best staggered delivery model to minimize the impact of the dependencies and integration effort between teams. The idea is to empower each team and minimize cross-team communication links while assuring communication occurs. Multiple streams of agile teams require a small, dedicated team of specialists to manage cross-team communication, integration and standards.
