Main Description
The course materials are validated by the subject matter experts and again by the staff to be trained.  After delivery of each course, a course evaluation is distributed, completed by the end user, and then analyzed.  The final validation is the Summative Evaluation.

1. Review the Training Strategy and Curriculum
2. Prepare Training Schedule and Logistics
3. Manage the Training Instance
4. Train the Trainer
5. Deliver Training
6. Evaluate Training and produce final report

Review the Training Strategy and Curriculum

Prior to creating the training schedule, the IBM consultant and the client review the detailed training strategy and curriculum paying special attention to the training delivery methods, number of classes needed per course, facilities, and other constraints.
If not previously documented, it is important to take the time to gather as many constraints as possible prior to creating the first draft of the training schedule. For example, if a client has 10 people in the customer service department and no more than 4 people can be gone at time, it is important that the constraint be identified so the proper number of classes are scheduled.

Prepare Training Schedule and Logistics

Determine and document the approval process for the training schedule. It is important that the client’s management support the approval process to reduce the number of revisions to the schedule and to increase ownership of training delivery.
The client, with the help of the IBM consultant, prepares a preliminary training schedule. Depending on the size of the project, the constraints, and the approval process the training schedule may go through many iterations.
Based on the training schedule, determine the number of instructors needed. Secure additional resources as needed. Effective trainers have specific skills and characteristics. It is beneficial to use the Instructor Qualifications document as a guide. (See References)
Map the trainers to the courses and prepare a back-up plan for unforeseen absences.
Prepare the training facilities. Refer to the Training Facilities Document as a guide. (See References)
When the schedule is solidified, notify the participants. During the notification, it would be beneficial to communicate expectations/policies such as being on time and committing to the entire class period.

Manage the Training Instance

The technical team creates the training instance, normally just prior to courseware development. It is a representative subset of the future production environment.

Train the Trainer

Prior to delivering training, trainers must be identified and trained. They must understand how to train adults, how to use the training course materials and tools such as on-line help, and how to perform the system related transactions.

Deliver Training

Based on the training strategy and the need assessment, all or some of the training scenarios below will be delivered.

  • Prerequisite Training - for a user to be successful in functional training, they must be computer literate. Windows training is an example of prerequisite training.
  • Business Process Training - when a company reengineers their business processes, they may decide to conduct training on the new processes prior to the functional training.
  • Pilot - the first teaching of a class. Normally a technical editor, client, process owners, and consultant subject matter experts attend and review the class as it is delivered. Updates are made to the course.
  • Conference Room Pilot - a review of the training materials that consists of reading through the materials together with the client and performing a subset of the corresponding exercises.
  • Navigation Training - used to teach the end user to feel comfortable with the specific software application menu paths and selection options, without process or functionality specifics. Navigation training is often delivered with a Computer Based Training (CBT) or facilitated CBT.
  • Functional Training - the actual training on the new system. It teaches end users how to perform their job-related tasks in the system.
  • Case Studies - a description of a process is presented to the end user. This case study provides minimal guidance and is used to determine/re-enforce the end users ability to determine which transactions must be completed and to actually complete the transactions in a training strategy.
  • Dress Rehearsals - a company wide or department wide test of the end user’s logons, security, and ability to perform transactions. This exercise is often completed 2 weeks prior to production start and at the end of the functional training schedule.
  • Refresher Training - depending on the elapsed length of time from end users training to production start, exercises or short sessions may be provided to the end user to help them maintain their skills.
  • Remedial Training - some end users may not completely grasp the functional training. A process should be established to identify these people and to provide them with additional training or support.

Evaluate Training and produce final report

Training material development, delivery, and effectiveness can be measured by conducting a training evaluation. Normally the evaluation strategy is divided into two components, the formative evaluation and the summative evaluation. During the Initial Training Strategy workshop, it is important to educate the client on the suggested evaluations, their purposes, and how conducting the evaluation will benefit the client.
The formative evaluation is used to improve training course effectiveness and is conducted during courseware development. The summative evaluation is conducted after the courses have been delivered and is used to evaluate the effectiveness of training.
Refer to the Training Evaluation Strategy document for detailed information regarding the components of each evaluation and the method for conducting the evaluations.
Class evaluations and post-production evaluations make up the summative evaluation.
