Guideline: Systems Engineer Support for Change Management
Main Description

1 Description

After the Systems Engineer and Architecture team has defined the technical baselines (including the requirements deliverables and architecture deliverables, the team will support the development and test teams for the rest of the project life cycle.

Throughout the project, the Systems Engineer must control changes to the technical baselines. The technical baselines include the requirements documentation and the design/architecture documentation.
 
The change management process defined by the Project Manager (PM) for the project must be followed for all changes to the technical baselines
 
At the Business Requirements Review, the customer baseline is set. At the System Requirements Review, the system baseline is set. At the Preliminary Design Review, the architecture and component baseline is set.
 
The systems engineer must manage these baselines until the project completes. He/she must control all changes to these technical baselines to ensure that both the customer and IGS agree on any changes. All changes must be sized by the design, development, test, and Service Delivery teams to determine the impact of the change. All changes must be approved by both the customer and IGS. The systems engineering team must control the project's technical baseline to ensure that unwanted changes are not introduced.
 
For any change is that approved to the baseline, the systems engineering team must update the appropriate project baseline documentation and ensure that all stakeholders are aware of the change.

As changes are defined and approved through the development process, the systems engineer must prioritize the changes with respect to the current baselines.

2 Details

Support to the Component Development/Test teams

The systems engineer will support the development teams throughout the development cycle - originally through the development of the system and component requirements.

During the Develop phase of the project (after the component requirements have been defined for the development teams), the systems engineer with support the development teams in two ways:

  1. Controlling baseline change requests;
  2. Resolving requirements questions/conflicts from the development teams.

Baseline changes requests are changes/additions/deletions to the current baseline documents. All changes must be approved by the customer and IGS. The development team will assess any changes before they are approved.

Requirement questions/conflicts will also be resolved by the systems engineer. The systems engineer will review the requirement(s) with the questioning parties, and work with the parties to clarify the requirements. If changes to the requirements are needed, then the baseline change request will be written and the process described above will be performed.

Support to the Verification and Validated teams

The systems engineer will support the verification and validation test teams throughout the development cycle - originally through the development of the system and component requirements and through the creation of the Requirements Traceability and Verification Matrix (RTVM). The RTVM describes the acceptance criteria for each requirement and the test method / test phase in which the requirement will be tested.
During verification and validation, while tests are being run, the systems engineer will support the development and test teams in a ‘Requirements Analysis’ role:

‘Requirements Analysis’ Scenario:

  • As testers work through test cases, they generate test incident reports (TIRs) to document where they believe the application under test has failed a given test step. These TIRs are typically routed into a development process to address as defects. 
  • In some number of cases, however, development team may object that a TIR is not a defect; they will typically state that the system is “working as specified” (WAS). This means that they believe that the reviewed and approved design has been implemented and is functioning as expected. It may turn out that the test case is not valid, or that the tester was not aware of a changed process, or some other misunderstanding that would allow the TIR to be closed without changes to the application.
  • In some subset of these “WAS” cases, however, the tester and/or the customer may disagree and insist that the agreed-to functionality or performance has not been delivered. In these cases, where development and the tester are at a standoff, the TIR may be placed into ‘a Requirements Analysis queue’.

‘Requirements Analysis’  role:

  • The Systems Engineer must take the TIRs placed in the Requirements Analysis queue and determine whether the TIR is a defect or not.
    • The Systems Engineer must review the requirements and the design information, plus any relevant supporting materials, to determine if the TIR is a defect or not. This requires, at a minimum, understanding why development thinks the function is WAS and why the tester thinks it is a defect.
    • If it is determined to be a defect, then the development team fixes it as such.
    • If the system is determined to be working as specified, then a project change request must be created.
    • Most of the TIRs in the Requirements Analysis queue need to be fixed whether they are a defect or not. The most common non-defect situation is a missed requirement: the requirements or the design did not adequately cover the function or, worse, incorrectly specified it (perhaps based on an incorrect but unchallenged underlying assumption). For the situation of a missed requirement, the Systems Engineer must work with the stakeholders to ensure that a project change request is developed that correctly documents the requirement. If the change request cannot be immediately supported, it may also be necessary to discuss interim or stop-gap measures. In some cases, mini-design reviews may be needed.

Requirements Analysis’ effort:

  • Unlike the Concept Phase requirements definition and review activities, the testing Requirements Analysis workload does not follow a fixed timeline, with milestones and checkpoints. The TIRs flow into the Requirements Analysis queue as they are encountered. And if there are concurrent test activities / phases, there will be multiple Requirements Analysis queues. Usually, by the time the TIR enters the Requirements Analysis queue, it has already been discussed between the development and test teams; as a result, the Systems Engineer may receive it with a week already elapsed since it was opened and focus (possibly executive) already on it. The result is that this is an “interrupt based” process; the Systems Engineer will typically have to focus on any severity 1 and 2 TIRs in their queue until there is a resolution, to the exclusion of other responsibilities.
  • The Systems Engineering effort is completely dependent on the size of the project and the test effort, and the quality of the requirements and design documentation. A rough estimate would be full-time during first third of the verification and validation phases and quarter time for the remainder of the phases.
