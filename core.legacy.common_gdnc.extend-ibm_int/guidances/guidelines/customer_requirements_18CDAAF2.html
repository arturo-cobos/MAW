Guideline: Customer Requirements
Main Description

1 Description

The approach is divided into the following five steps. The aim is to provide the information needed by the business process redesign teams.

  1. Review the client customer requirements internally
  2. Review existing customer evaluations of the client performance to specific requirements
  3. Review customers’ plans and expected requirements for the future
  4. Link requirements and the client’s performance with specific business processes
  5. Organize and integrate customer requirements into the critical issues and entry point deliverable and ultimately the process redesign effort

The following section summarizes the approach for each of the above steps:

2 Steps

Step 1 - Review the client customer requirements internally

Review of the client’s customer requirements will consist of the collection and processing of existing documentation from within various the client sources.  It is expected that management will direct the team to the best available sources of this data and provide additional the client support in the collection process as required.

In addition to reviewing existing documentation, it is also expected that the team will interview key the client personnel close to the customer requirements definition process.  Management will assist the team in the identification of these individuals who it is expected will include personnel such as sales representatives and account executives.

Step 2 - Review existing customer evaluations of the client performance to specific requirements

As with the previous steps, it is assumed that the client has information pertaining to customer evaluation of its performance in satisfying key requirements.  This information is important to the business process redesign effort in that it provides two major points of information.  First, it establishes a baseline against which future state performance will be measured and second, it indicates where the largest gaps between the client performance and customer expectation are, highlighting the areas of concentration for business process redesign within the processes.

Step 3 - Review customers’ plans and expected requirements for the future

A critical step in the customer requirements effort is to determine what the customer’s future requirements will be and how that affects the various processes within the client. The value of this information in the business process redesign effort is obvious in that it provides for future state targets to be designed to for each process.  We recommend that the customers’ future requirements are investigated twofold:

  1. Through interviews with the client representatives. This can be combined with step 1 ("Review the client customer requirements").
  2. Through interviews with selected customers. The  team needs to choose a sample of large and   medium sized customers, that will be representative of most customers. The interviews might be held via phone, videoconference or the team might need to visit customers sites. We recommend to perform approximately 5 - 10 customer interviews.

Although all customers may not have identified all of their To-Be requirements, it is likely that they will have at least identified the direction they foresee their business heading.  Based on this direction, it is expected that the client has then translated this vision into the subsequent requirements, which will be required to support it.  

Step 4 - Link requirements and the client’s performance with specific business processes

A critical step in the customer requirements effort is the consolidation and linkage of various customer requirements into the business process areas which they affect.  For instance, a customer requirement that the client provide EDI for order entry would be categorized into the Order Management business process as this is the area to which the requirement pertains.

The reason for the linkage of requirements according to their business process is that the customer requirements will be incorporated into the reengineered processes to ensure that the To-Be process satisfies the customer’s wants and needs.  Without this linkage it will be difficult to ensure that the business process redesign effort is being driven by customer requirements and that the goal of increasing customer satisfaction is being met.

Actual work in this area will consist of the decision into which specific process a requirement should be grouped (possibly more than one).

Step 5 - Organize and integrate customer requirements into the critical issues and entry points deliverable and ultimately the process redesign effort

The final step in the customer requirements effort is the integration of the information collected in steps one through four into the critical issues and entry points deliverable. This will be accomplished through the distribution of customer requirements to the respective business process redesign teams.  In cases where requirements cross or affect multiple processes, the requirement will be distributed to all those teams affected.

The above requirements information will be utilized initially by the teams through the workshops in which the To-Be process will be designed.  The requirements will be introduced as primary drivers and continuously revisited as a validation for the developing designs.  This structured verification process is designed to ensure that processes provide value to the customer.

Examples of Requirements and Customer Information

The following sections give examples of the type of information which will be required during the customer requirements effort.  The examples given here are divided into two major sections.  The first section illustrates high level information requirements, while the second section provides more detailed examples of the information needed.  The second section also divides the requirements into several process examples which, as stated previously, would be a necessary step in the overall requirements effort.

As stated previously, this is not intended to be a comprehensive list, but merely examples of the types of information to be collected. 
High Level Information Requirements

  • Specific points of contact between the customer and the client
  • Ranked list of points of contact in order of importance to the customer
  • Evaluation of the client’s performance/service in each of these points of contact
  • Evaluation of competition’s performance in points of contact with relation to the client
  • Customer viewpoint on the future of their business (i.e. where is the industry heading, what major initiatives do you see launching, etc.) and where does the client fit in
  • Customer input on single issues the client could address to improve service/satisfaction today
  • Customer viewpoint on what the client will have to do in the future to remain/become supplier of choice (e.g. vendor managed inventory, or increased emphasis on category management)
  • More detailed examples of the kind of information relating to its customer requirements is given below.  These requirements are broken down by major process, a necessary step in the overall customer requirements effort.

Detailed Information Requirements

  • Order Entry/Order Fulfillment (By Customer)
  • What method of order entry is currently utilized
  • What is the cycle time and volume (by customer) for the order entry process
  • What other methods of order entry are being used by suppliers other than the client
  • Is a Vendor Managed Replenishment system currently being utilized
  • How are orders for the client currently being tracked 
  • Are you (as the customer) satisfied with the client’s Order Entry/Fulfillment process
  • What areas in the client’s order entry/fulfillment service require the most improvement
  • What would you like to see the client provide in this area of service
  • How does the client’s performance in this area compare to other major suppliers (how would you rate it in relation to the competition)
  • What major changes/initiatives do you see launching in the next few years which might influence your requirements in this area
  • What would you expect the client to do in the next few years to improve service in this area

Product Delivery

  • What are your current requirements for product delivery in terms of time from order?
  • How do you rate the client’s performance vs. these requirements?
  • How does the client’s performance in on-time delivery compare to other significant vendors you deal with?
  • What method is most commonly used for shipment of the client products to your facilities (the client trucks, your carrier, third party)?
  • How are rush orders handled (i.e. air, Fedex, etc.)?
  • Are your client orders currently delivered to individual stores or to a common warehouse/distribution center?
  • Do you require partial shipment/continuous replenishment?  If so, does the client provide satisfactory service in this area?
  • Who (if anyone) provides better product delivery service than the client?  What is it that differentiates this vendor’s service?
  • Do you measure order accuracy by vendor?  If so, how does the client compare?
