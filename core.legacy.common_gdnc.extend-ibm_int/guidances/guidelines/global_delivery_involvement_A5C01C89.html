Guideline: Global Delivery Involvement
Main Description

This Global Delivery Technique Paper is a brief guidance for project managers and project management assistants on how to handle the most relevant issues of using Global Delivery (GD) capacity within a development project. It describes the minimal must-do activities and must-have work products to be created. It covers the phases from project acquisition to delivery phase. It gives advice to the main pitfalls and the most common risks within GD Projects.

Definitions

GD: Global Delivery Resources perform project work offshore from the customer location. GD can either be a subsidiary of the service provider (IBM) or a service provider independent company (e.g. IBA Minsk).

GD-Entity: The Global Delivery Resources / Global Delivery Partner. This document uses ‘GD-Entity’ when talking about contracting, legal interaction and agreeing commitments e.g. during acquisition.

Service Provider (SP): In this document ‘service provider’ is defined as the legal entity which takes on engagement responsibility of the customer – here: IBM. This could be a combination of on/offshore resources. IBM assigns a lead partner who is responsible for coordinating the GD Team as well as customer communication. During the acquisition cycle, the Service Provider will determine if they are going to leverage a GD Team or a locally based team.

Usage Context

When delivering a Global Delivery Project, certain work is outsourced to be performed by a GD-Entity, e.g. implementation, design or even analysis. Involving GD requires that the project manager define the work the GD-Entity will perform then communicate expectations clearly and measure progress frequently.

In this context the following issues are seen as critical success factors:

Critical Success Factor Description Risk
Availability of GD Resources Skilled resources are limited in number.. Prior to committing to global delivery make sure the needed are available. Low prices are committed but delivery via global resources is not possible
Customer buy in and confidence The customer supports the idea of GD and trusts the SP to handle the complexity of the engagement successfully. Customer does not trust the SP in the use of GD and does not support the model of engagement.
On- and offshore team agreement The SP and GD-Entity have the same engagement objectives, feasibility, effort and risks and agree on project success criteria. SP and GD have different understanding of the project objectives, feasibility, etc. and therefore do not work as one team.
Development lifecycle excellence Production of deliverables is effective, efficient, and transparent and guarantees project success. The obstacles of remote development are not overcome and delivery within time and budget is unlikely.
Steady delivery Demonstration of successfully meeting deliverables from the beginning of the project. Customer trust is maintained. Project results are not delivered in adequate timeframes. Progress is not visible and or the project is delivered over budget. Customer looses trust in project success.
