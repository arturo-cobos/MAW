Guideline: Performing Performance Gap Analysis
The Technique Paper will offer background information on the Performance Gap Analysis (PGA), and will outline its benefits. Upon completion, you will understand the purpose of the PGA, and will recognize several resources that are available to you for reference.
Main Description

1 Description

Who Should Use This Technique Paper

IBM professionals involved in performing a PGA include the following:

  • Performance Consultants
  • Learning Consultants
  • Instructional Designers

What is a PGA?

The Performance Gap Analysis (PGA) identifies and documents discrepancies between an organization’s current outcomes and its desired outcomes, and determines how this gap may be closed. An organization’s current and desired outcomes are measured by examining the skills/knowledge/attitudes (S/K/A) of the organization’s employees and the S/K/A required to meet business goals. The PGA establishes whether training will close this gap, or whether it would be premature or inadequate, in light of the identified business needs.

2 Context

Why Perform a PGA?

The primary benefits of a PGA include the following:

  • Identify specific areas in which training may help an organization meet its business goals, by helping to close s/k/a gaps.
  • Identify the job role(s) in which the most severe s/k/a gaps exist.
  • Identify situations in which training will not solve the immediate business needs of a client, but other interventions may be required (for example, through reorganization or by a change in process).

When Should You Perform a PGA?

A PGA is typically performed under the following circumstances:

  • It is unclear whether a learning solution will meet and solve the business needs of a particular client.
  • It is unclear what job role(s) will require a learning intervention.
  • It is unclear where performance gaps exist in an organization.

3 Steps

The following steps are meant as guidance only. There are many methods for conducting a PGA. Please look to the Learning Services Intellectual Capital Management (ICM) database for future PGA resources.

  1. Identify the job role(s) which will be the target of the PGA. Typically, this information is gathered from the client sponsor (or from someone identified by the client sponsor).
  2. Identify the desired state of the job role(s) identified in step one. The “desired state” is based on the defined role(s) and responsibilities for a particular job role.
  3. Identify the current state of the job role(s) identified in step one. The “current state” is a description of the current characteristics of the individual(s) currently performing the job role(s), as compared with the desired state identified in step two.
  4. Identify the gaps which exist between the current and desired states for each job role identified in step one.
  5. Establish whether gap(s) may be attributed to one or more of the following:
    • Skills, Knowledge, Attitude (s/k/a),
    • Role definition,
    • Performance tools, or
    • Business processes and procedures.
  7. Evaluate whether those issues that may be attributed to s/k/a gaps might be resolved through a learning solution.
