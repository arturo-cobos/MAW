Guideline: Strategic Knowledge Surplus and Shortfall Analysis
Main Description

1 Description

The objective of this technique is to assist in discovering knowledge surpluses and shortfalls within an organization given the business strategy set forth by senior management.  The purpose of this analysis presumes a belief that any and all initiatives to manage knowledge should focus on the knowledge needed to fulfill the business strategy as well as any excess knowledge within an organization that has the greatest chance of enhancing organizational value.  More specifically, only through analyzing gaps between and among existing, required, competitor, partner, and customer knowledge can a knowledge management program be recommended that will in fact prioritize and then manage the most strategic knowledge to the organization.

2 Steps

Step 1 - Determine Client's Knowledge Requirements

Review the client's knowledge requirements.  Through interviews with senior executives as well as through compilations of self directed and company directed sources, determine the knowledge that is required to formulate and implement the corporate, business, and functional strategy.  The knowledge in question should be rated using a scale consisting of attributes such as "unique, rare, exploitable, valuable, and defensible" in order to determine the degree to which the knowledge in question provides a strategic advantage.  Moreover, the knowledge should be evaluated by degree of dynamism given that knowledge is rarely static; competitive environments that are dynamic often require faster and more extensive learning to keep knowledge current.  Organizations’ learning capabilities must match the learning requirements of their competitive environment.  Therefore, each area of knowledge should be evaluated as to the extent and intensity of learning required. 

Step 2 - Determine Client's Existing Knowledge

Review the client's existing knowledge.  Through interviews with senior executives, company directed sources, and other content analysis techniques (see technique paper for Assessing Knowledge Content) determine what the company actually knows.  Once this knowledge is determined, separate that knowledge into knowledge that is required to formulate and implement the business strategy as well as knowledge that exists, however is currently not being exploited to a degree that may provide further strategic advantage for the company.  As with the knowledge requirement analysis in step one, existing knowledge should be rated on both a strategicness and dynamism scale.  Moreover, it would be beneficial at this point to detect if and how the company measures the knowledge that they have.  Lack or existence of measurement initiatives helps to shed light on how serious the company is about competing on and managing their knowledge.

Step 3 - Determine Client's Competitor, Customer, and Partner Knowledge

Review the client's competitor, customer, and partner knowledge. Even though firms may posses the necessary knowledge and capabilities to secure their strategy, companies must evaluate the extent to which other firms are competing on the same knowledge.  For instance, if the knowledge in question is basic and thus common to many firms, the knowledge is probably not very strategic and easily duplicated.  This analysis aids in creating a complete picture of the strategic value of a firm’s knowledge.  Through interviews with client employees, emphasizing marketing or business intelligence camps, as well as company directed sources and other market analysis techniques, determine what the client's competitors, customers, and partners know.  Although this may seem to be a daunting task, concentrate on that knowledge that would represent an opportunity or threat to the client.  The knowledge should once again be ranked by degree of strategicness and dynamism so as to aid in determining which knowledge would be most strategic, relevant, and efficient to manage.

Steps 1-3, determine the possible surpluses and shortfalls between…

  • The firm's existing knowledge and their required knowledge.
  • The firm's existing knowledge and their competitors', customers', and partners' knowledge.
  • The firm's existing learning capabilities and their required learning capabilities.
  • The firm's existing learning capabilities and their competitors', customers', and partners' learning capabilities.

Step 5 - Determine Client's Knowledge Management & Learning Program Alignment

Determine how well the client's knowledge management and learning capabilities address their strategic knowledge requirements.  After conducting a comprehensive scan of the client's knowledge management and learning programs determine if a strategic fit exists between the current knowledge management and learning programs and the knowledge surpluses and shortfalls. Consider those surpluses and shortfalls within the company that are related to the client's corporate, functional, and business strategy, goals, mission, and vision.  Misalignment will represent an opportunity for the client to utilize knowledge management and learning techniques that properly address the existing chasms.  Alignment will also represent an opportunity for the client to enhance existing knowledge management and learning programs by way of making them more time efficient, less costly, etc.
