Guideline: Engineering Learning Solutions
The Technique Paper will offer general guidance on engineering courseware for Technology-Based, Instructor-Led, and Blended learning solutions. Upon completion, you will understand the basic differences between each type of solution, and some of the implications for engineering each.
Main Description

1 Description

Who Should Use This Technique Paper
 
IBM professionals involved in engineering a learning solution include the following:
  • Instructional Designers
  • Information Developers
  • Application Developers

2 Context

Within Custom Learning Content Development engagement, the Engineer Solution task results in the valuable product of the engagement: the learning solution itself. Typically, the solution will take the form of one of the following:

  • A Technology-Based Solution, such as Web-Based Training (WBT), Computer-Based Training (CBT) delivered via CD-ROM, or an Enhanced Performance Support System (EPSS).
  • An Instructor-Led solution, such as a classroom course.
  • A Blended solution that includes some combination of the above.

Depending on the type of learning solution that is to be delivered to the client, “engineering” may take on one of several meanings. However, the net result is the same, regardless of the chosen delivery strategy: a complete learning solution, including all components needed to begin delivering the solution to the target audience.

3 Steps

The following is meant as guidance only. There are many methods for engineering a learning solution. Here, three possible approaches are outlined, one for Technology-Based Solutions, one for Instructor-Led Solutions, and one for Blended Solutions.

3.1 Technology-Based Solutions

Once the required elements have been created (learning solution script and supporting media), the following general steps are taken to engineer the final solution:

  1. Ensure that all needed elements are available, as outlined in the Detailed Design Document. These may include (but are not limited to) any or all of the following:
    •  Scripted text
    • Audio narration
    • Audio music
    • Audio sound effects
    • Video
    • Animations
    • Complex technology-based interactions (such as Flash elements)
    • Photographs, illustrations, diagrams, or other graphic elements
  2. Depending on the development tool being used, at this point text and supporting media are either “dropped into” the solution, or the solution is coded and media is placed appropriately.
  3. The solution is “published,” creating an executable file from which the solution may be launched.
    In the case of Technology-Based solutions, the solution engineer typically requires some programming or other technical skill.

3.2 Instructor-Led Solutions

  1. Ensure that all needed elements are available, as outlined in the Detailed Design Document. These may include (but are not limited to) any or all of the following:
    • Scripted text
    • Photographs, illustrations, diagrams, or other graphic elements
    • Video
    • Simulations, games, or other materials needed for classroom activities
  2. Assemble the elements into the components of the final deliverable. These components may include (but are not limited to) any of the following:
    • Student notebooks
    • Student exercise guides
    • Instructor guides
    • Instructor exercise guides
    • Presentation materials
    • Lab setup guide
    • Materials in support of classroom activities or games
  4. The solution is “published” as a package of all solution-related elements.
    In the case of an Instructor-Led solution, the solution engineer need not possess programming or other technical skills, but will typically require familiarity with at least one technology-based development tool or system.

3.3 Blended Solutions

The steps taken to engineer a blended solution are much the same as those taken to engineer a Technology-Based or Instructor-Led solution, with any needed adaptations. In the case of a Blended solution, at least one of the solution engineers will typically require some skills in the area of programming.

4 Tools

The following are among the tools used to develop and engineer learning solutions (Please note that this does not include those applications used to create supporting materials, such as audio and graphics files):

4.1 Technology-Based Solutions

  • IBM Knowledge Producer – Template-based tool used for Internet, Intranet, and CD-ROM delivered courseware. Information Developers script directly into the tool, and media elements may simply be “dropped in.”
  • IBM QuickSim – Development tool used to capture application functions and generate simulations that may be delivered as a “Show Me,” a “Guide Me,” or a “Let Me Try.”
  • Macromedia Authorware – Development tool for creating interactive, rich-media learning applications for delivery over the Web, LANs, and CD-ROM.
  • Macromedia Dreamweaver – Development tool used to create HTML-based solutions and sites.
  • Macromedia Flash – Development tool used to create animations and complex interactions.
  • Lotus Freelance Graphics and Microsoft PowerPoint – Tools used to create presentations (often for delivery as Web Lectures).
  • Lotus LearningSpace – An e-Learning technology platform that provides a flexible learning experience and broad support for curriculum planning, instruction, and learning administration.
  • Lotus Sametime – Real-time collaboration environment that features a whiteboard, application sharing, and chat.
