Guideline: Rapid Infrastructure Sizing Offering
This paper describes a task by which the Rapid Infrastructure Design (RID) Team will collect specific information from the client about their needs and current systems, and relay it to another IBM that specializes in web-based application capacity planning.
Main Description

1 Description

The results of the rapid infrastructure sizing assessment (arriving between 5 and 10 days) will be used as a guide to the deployment of the RID engagement. The point of the task is not to assess the sizing strategy of the client for yourself, but to gather correct and sufficient information for the Capacity Planning Team to make a rapid assessment.

2 Context

Capacity planning is more cost-effective and efficient if done prior to deployment.  Performance problems resulting from a lack of capacity are more complex and costly to resolve after deployment.  Capacity planning provides the information needed to strategically assess future IT requirements for new equipment, additional network capacity, and new, underlying IT architectures.  Engaging an independent organization experienced in Web-application, system and, network architectures helps ensure an unbiased analysis of application resource requirements.  This in turn helps the deployment team to adequately prepare systems and networks for new traffic loads.

2.1  Best Approach

There are two developed methodologies that can help IBM assess resource requirements for new Web-based applications.

Architectural sizings employ architectural design points and benchmark data to identify needed resources. Organizations must size the project and estimate resources before the application is developed to ensure that the development team is given accurate feasibility input. If the ROI is unattractive, the application may need to be redesigned, or management may decide to drop the development project altogether.

Test-based sizings use testing results to simulate user-access scenarios and running production-level code. These results, along with base user information, validate architectural sizings and finalize deployment system resource requirements. If there is any discrepancy in the sizings, incorrect input from the development application team may be the cause.

Ideally, both types of sizings should be performed.  However, development activities may cause one or the other to be bypassed.  If an application is delayed in development but must be in production by a predefined date, test-based sizing can be performed while the application is being deployed.  The Capacity Planning Team will help you decide which approach is most appropriate for the engagement.

3 Steps

3.1  Gather Preliminary Data

Prior to contacting the Capacity Planning Team, try to gather as much information as possible about the client’s architectural plan.  Recommended documents and information to have available include:  diagram of client infrastructure and components (including backend architecture, servers, routers, etc.); list of planned or installed applications, middleware; statistics about the total and potential volume of transactions, number of end users, possible concurrent usage, and usage patterns (daily/seasonal peaks, anticipated specific use patterns); transaction volumes over periods of time; hardware platforms; size and scope of the engagement and infrastructure.

Having all of this information ready and available before your initial call to the Sizing Team is rare and not mandatory, but it may help produce more speedy results.  The Sizing Team prefers soft copies whenever possible, but will also accept faxes of key documents.  (A sample questionnaire is included in Appendix A to help organize the preliminary data.)  Remember, all of the questions need not be answered completely, but should be addressed if the information is already easily available to you.

3.2  Contact Capacity Planning Team

Call the e-business Sizing Control Desk to set up a conference call to discuss components and sizing.  The initial call will serve 2 purposes:

  • Inform the team leader of the size of the project so that he/she may be better able to staff the Sizing Team to accommodate the RID Team’s needs.
  • To plan a 2-hour conference call within one business day at the earliest to determine strategy, requirements, and estimated date of completion.

The Sizing Team leader will request CLAIM codes for the project on this initial call or on the conference call, so that work may begin as soon as the data is submitted.  During the conference call be sure to bring up any questions or additional requirement regarding the delivery of the solution. 

Because each engagement and each client’s infrastructure will vary widely from previous ones, there is no standardized template by which a practitioner may gather needed information from the client.  By the end of the conference call, however, the team will be able to supply the RID Team with a tailored template that will waste little time and effort on the data collection effort.

3.3  Gather and Submit Infrastructure Data

To properly determine resource requirements for an application, the capacity planning team requires architectural information, along with a functional description of anticipated usage.  The completeness and accuracy of the sizing depends on the quality of the information received.  When portions of information are unknown or missing, the risk factor for incorrect sizing increases.  (See Appendix B for a list of the types of information the Sizing Team uses to perform an assessment.  This list is not exhaustive.)

The practitioner should expect to gather the bulk of the requested information from the client after the conference call described above.  This can be done by person-to-person interview or by phone with client representative.  Experience shows that the best individuals from which to gather the data are a combination of the client’s Infrastructure Architect, and a person who acts as an end user representative (usually the owner of the solution).  Combined, these 2 people can provide the most accurate data in the least amount of time.

Once the requested information has been gathered from the client, submit it as soon as possible vi email to the Capacity Planning Team so that they may begin analyzing and assessing the sizing.  Most rapid sizings take about 3 weeks or less, but may last as long as 6 weeks, depending on the quality and quantity of information provided, and the size of the client’s infrastructure.

3.4  Receiving Sizing Assessment

Once the capacity team has the information for the sizing, it can apply standard calculations to produce results.  The capacity assessment report will include a recommendation and risk factor associated with the recommendation. The RID team will receive the results of the assessment in document format, unless otherwise specified on the first conference call.  Tabular or spreadsheet formats are available upon request.

The confidentiality level of the resulting assessment is Class 2, and can be shared with the client.  The RID team is advised that the assessment should inherit the confidentiality level of other documents exchanged between the IBM Team and Client Team.

Should the RID Team need further clarification of the sizing assessment, they are encouraged to contact the Capacity Planning Team leader if needed.

5 Appendix B – Partial List of Requested Client Data

The Capacity Planning Team uses the following type of information to drive the capacity planning analysis:

  • Percentage of new function supplied by solution
  • Percentage of new data elements created
  • Business-use scenarios
  • Data transferred to or from an information warehouse
  • Solution architecture definition:
    -  Business function, scenarios and supporting models
    -  Data architecture, solution architecture—business and deployment architecture diagrams
    -  Technical architecture and schematic (client, server, network, Web)
    -  Build/buy assessment
  • Operating systems
  • Type of Web server
  • Network protocol: TCP/IP, SNA, IP Dial, etc.
  • Subnetwork protocol: FDDI, ISDN, ATM
  • Data access services: DB2®, Lotus® Approach
  • Programming languages: JavaTM, Perl, SAP, LotusScript
  • Presentation services: Netscape, SAP GUI
  • Application/work roup services: SAP, CICS®
  • Object management services: SOM®/DSOM
  • Communication services: DCE RPC, MQSeries®, SAP ALE
  • Distribution services: NFS, DFS, Kerberos
  • Systems management: SNMP, AntiVirus, ADSM, TME
  • Common Gateway Interface (CGI): Total number, languages, number of times called
  • Application interface with legacy data/systems
  • Definition of performance exit criteria: end-user response and data throughput
