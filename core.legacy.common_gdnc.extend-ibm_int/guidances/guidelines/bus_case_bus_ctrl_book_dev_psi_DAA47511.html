Guideline: Business Case and Business Control Book Development (PSI)
This guideline describes the steps involved in developing and benefits of developing a Business Case. A Business Case is a financial justification of a course of action and serves as the vehicle to justify a change in the business
Main Description

1. Description

A Business Case is the result of detailed analysis focusing on:

  • opportunities for improvement in the organization
  • potential benefits, costs and associated risks
  • the effect on organizational stakeholders
  • the effect on organizational profitability

Thus, the Business Case documents and quantifies the benefits, costs, schedules, risks and assumptions to justify the acquisition and implementation of a business solution utilizing future state, cross-functional business improvements.

This technique paper provides guidance to practitioners in the Business Case development process. It is intended to be concise, yet cover theory, practical application and linkage of Business Case development.

The Business Case has multiple sections and purposes, among them are:

  1. Executive Overview: To outline project background; define scope, timing and status; describe approach; summarize project activities; describe recommended solution and other solutions considered; summarize high level costs and benefits and supporting financial calculations
  2. Primary Recommendations and Rational: To identify and describe each Opportunity Value Proposition with the corresponding business process and improvement; and how the improvements will derive value to the client.
  3. Benefits: To describe the process of deriving benefits; detail the benefits; and quantify the benefits
  4. Costs: To document the ‘best available’ Total Life Cycle Costs over multiple years in all cost categories.
  5. Financial Analysis: To document cost vs. benefits over the life of the project using multiple financial tools and techniques to indicate value to the client.
  6. High-Level Implementation Plan: To document the method; locations; timing; client involvement; resources; and operational date.
  7. Project Risks: To describe and compare various courses of actions and their associated risks.
  8. Next Steps: To outline the various activities to gain final approval, make announcements; acquire internal funding, form teams; start implementation; etc.
  9. Appendices: To document detailed financial calculations and assumptions.

The Business Case is typically the document Executive Management utilizes to justify the expenditure of funds, especially for major projects.

2. Context

As a diversified consulting firm, IBM Global Service provides a broad range of services to our customers. Regardless of whether we’re implementing hardware, software, or re-engineering their business one thing remains constant, clients ask IBM to enable changes in their business processes. These changes typically require a significant investment in capital, labor, and change in corporate culture. This is where the Business Case becomes a major enabler.

A Business Case, as defined by IBM, contains a:

  • Text description of the reasoning behind taking a course of action
  • Detailed quantification of expected benefits
  • Detailed profitability analysis
  • Management controls and constraints identified

All elements have a high level of analysis depth for both tangible and intangible benefit quantification and focus on buy-in from Executive, Middle, and Line levels of management

The Business Case development draws from information identified and gathered for developing the Case for Change. That information is contained in the Business Strategy Assessment, Critical Issues, Opportunities and Recommendations, Preliminary Cost Benefit Estimate, and Principals for Harmonization

The opportunities identified and subjectively evaluated during the Case for Change, are further expanded and developed with more detail for cost, benefits, schedule, assumptions, responsibility, etc. as during the engagement and are documented in Opportunity Description. These will constitute a major portion of the input for the Business Case. Other major inputs for the Business Case are the High Level Gap Analysis and Estimated Preliminary Costs. This information will be combined with information from Risk Analysis and High Level Implementation Plan to define the information and operating parameters for the Business Case Development.

3. Steps

3.1 Plan the Business Case

The approach of the Business Case must be determined prior to the actual kickoff activities. The options are,

  • Top Down – Where the focus is primarily financial, with little involvement from middle or line management and staff. This results in a faster solution that directly affects the bottom line, but does not have the organizational buy-in from those necessary to affect the changes.
  • Bottom Up – Where the focus is primarily operational, with great involvement from middle or line management and staff. This results in a solution with significant organizational buy-in, but that lacks focus and consensus, and has a much longer development time.
  • Hybrid – Where the focus is operational with a clearly defined financial linkage to the bottom line, with involvement from a representation of executive, middle or line management, and the results are validated by a staff, where needed. This results in solution with strong local buy-in due to the middle or line managers acting as change managers.

The Hybrid approach is the recommended approach. It offers the best balance of development cycle time, operational depth, and organizational buy-in.

Also, the appropriate IBM role must be discussed with the client prior to completion of the Statement of Work (SOW). Depending on the IBM role, our staffing and budgeted hours will change dramatically. The total client and IBM hours for Business Case development tends to stay the same within a project. The decision needs to be made who is responsible for gathering information and producing the final product, with appropriate hours assigned.

There are two distinctive roles that IBM can play in the Business Case development process.

  • Directive – Where IBM performs most of the work and the client involvement is limited to being a data provider. This results in a shorter Business Case development cycle, but the client has little understanding of the process or how to use the information to control change when IBM leaves. There is no transfer of knowledge to the client and only limited organizational buy-in.
  • Coaching – Where IBM performs lead development and the client staff is involved in the researching and development of the final product. This results in significant organizational buy-in and knowledge transfer to the client team, however it also increased the Business Case development cycle time.

The Coaching role is recommended. Although it increases the Business Case development time, it adds significantly to the organizational buy-in for our clients. This aspect has major appeal to the Client Management team and should always be recommended in planning the Business Case.

As stated above, the total Business Case development hours tend to stay the same within a project, however, using the directive role, the responsibility and hours are transferred back to IBM, thus raising the cost and at the same time lowering the organizational buy-in.

Once the approach and roles are defined, the principal/project manager will then be able to complete the Planning Timeline / Activity Decomposition forms. This will be used in the completion of the statement of work and the project budget

3.2 Determine Client Business Strategy

The client’s business strategy is a major element in determining value for projects. There must be an alignment to the business strategy, or at least recognition of how the project relates to the business strategy, before the valuation process begins. Not providing the relationship of the project to the business strategy will always raise questions and increase uncertainty with the client management team

A full discussion concerning the intricacies of business strategy is outside the scope of this technique paper. However, it is appropriate to briefly review the business strategy’s relationship with the business case development.

Business strategy is the mechanism that guides the organization into the future. It includes the corporate Vision and Mission.

  • The Vision describes the values and long term direction of a company.
  • The Mission describes what business the corporation participates. In other words, what markets and products will the organization utilize to achieve its Vision?

3.3 Determine Client Business Structure

The business structure has three elements:

  • Process – that provides the value that customers buy
  • People – to enable the process
  • Technology – to enable the process

A strong relationship and balance exists between these elements for a successful organization

Business processes translate strategy into action. These processes are executed, enabled, and controlled by people and technology. The balance between these elements is critical to organization’s ability to achieve the stated mission.

3.4 Gather Opportunity Description for Packaged Solution Integration Information

Over time, gaps develop between the corporate strategy and the ability to effect it through the business structure. These are called opportunities for improvement. Through the Business Case development process, IBM assists customers in identifying these opportunities, analyzing their potential contribution to the organization and determining which should be implemented.

The goal of the business case is to determine which steps the client should take to eliminate the gaps between the business strategy and the business structure. It also quantifies the potential impact (costs and savings) of these changes on the organization.

Opportunities for improvement alone are not enough to determine the best use of an organization’s limited resources. In fact, when researched further, many of these opportunities may lie in non-critical areas or have negative returns. This is where Opportunity Value Propositions have an impact.

An Opportunity Value Proposition starts with an opportunity for improvement. The high level opportunity was probably first identified in the Case for Change. This opportunity is further researched and developed as an Opportunity Value Proposition containing:

  • Potential benefit
  • Potential cost
  • Potential risk
  • Probability of success (organization’s ability to make the change)

The Best Practice Based Visioning Workshop is the primary technique for producing the Opportunity Value Proposition. Reference: Conducting a Best Practice Based Visioning Workshop Technique Paper.

3.5 Develop the Business Case

The Business Case can now be developed that:

  1. summarizes quantifiable benefits of all Opportunity Value Propositions
  2. defines the organizational enablers that needed to support the change
  3. analyzes their financial and organizational impact
  4. defines which Opportunity Value Propositionswill be implemented
  5. groups and summarizes all of the information from the applicable Opportunity Value Propositions by organization/location/etc.
  6. calculates financial performance indicators

3.6 Assemble the Business Control Book

The Business Control Book can now be assembled that:

  1. contains the detailed benefits documented from the Opportunity Value Propositions
  2. expected benefits are detailed by process area, gives the process owner responsible and documents the expected dollarized benefits per year over a 5 year horizon,
  3. the metrics to be used and how it will be measured to determine the realized benefits are agreed upon by the client and documented
  4. current and target measurements are documented
  5. best practices driving the expected benefit are also documented where applicable

4. Detailed Steps by Phase

The development of a Business Case involves the use of several skills. These include:

  • issue based consulting
  • process transformation / improvement
  • financial analysis
  • operational analysis
  • vertical industry knowledge

The theory behind of each of these skill areas is outside the scope of this document.

4.1 Business Assessment Activities

Early on, cross-functional teams are set up for each process within scope. This is the time to organize the business case team and identify key resources as well. The business case team should have:

  1. a designated team leader, preferably from management
  2. one member from each of the process teams (team leaders desirable)
  3. extended team (not full time, line managers and staff) members, where possible, for input and validation

Before process workshops begin, the Business Case team will meet to discuss the team’s role. During this meeting they should:

  1. be educated on the IBM Business Case approach
  2. discuss how each of the processes will impact one another during the Business Case development process
  3. review the timeline and management expectations
  4. define baseline business case assumptions

As workshops are being run to map the current state, opportunities for improvement are being identified for each of the process areas. These opportunities end up in the Case for Change work product, which is the first step toward achieving the Business Case. The consultant needs to leverage process transformation and vertical industry knowledge skills to generate the opportunities.

These defined opportunities are inputs into the Opportunity Value Proposition identification process and are identified and cataloged on the Opportunity Value Propositions Data Collection Form.Throughout this phase the Business Case team should meet at least weekly to discuss progress.

Issue based consulting skills are used to identify each opportunity as a hypothesis and pursue analysis into each opportunity’s:

  • Potential benefit
  • Potential cost
  • Potential risk
  • Probability of success (organization’s ability to make the change)

Later, the Case for Change is expanded further to define a client’s best practice based vision. The Best Practiced Based Visioning workshop is the technique used to expand the opportunities for improvement. The Opportunity Value Propositions are expanded further. This means identifying Best Practices that might resolve the issues in each opportunity for improvement area. And identifying specific metrics needed to measure best practice "enhanced" processes. The client’s current and target measures for each metric are identified. The best practice based vision bounds the future state definition and enables the development of:

  • target future business capabilities
  • future process attributes

Process transformation / improvement and vertical industry knowledge skills are imperative here.

Information obtained here provides input needed to complete the Opportunity Value Proposition definition. It is here that detailed analysis is performed to identify:

  • potential tangible and intangible benefits
  • potential tangible and intangible costs
  • potential risks
  • probability of organization to affect the change

Potential Risks

There are several methods available for performing detailed risk analysis. The theory behind these methods is out of the scope of this document. However, the risk analysis used in this step of the Business Case development process is simple. For each opportunity for improvement identified, an IBM consultant should help the client identify risks (internal / external) that could be posed if that opportunity is or isn’t adopted. Risk areas for analysis include:

  1. financial risk (e.g. cash flow, capital investment)
  2. operational risk (e.g. late delivery, discontinuity of operations, etc.)
  3. cultural/organizational risk (ability of culture to change)
  4. market risk (impairment to market competitiveness)

Probability of Success (organization’s ability to make the change)

The IBM consultant should take the information gathered in the Opportunity Value Proposition and assist the customer in determining each one’s probability of successful implementation in their organization. Based on the potential net benefit and the probability of successful implementation in the organization, the Opportunity Value Propositions can be prioritized.

The result is the development of the Case for Change deliverable as well as a draft set of detailed Opportunity Value Propositions. This Opportunity Value Proposition information is approved by the Business Case team and passed to line managers and staff on the extended team for validation. This process is reiterated several times until the organization buys-in and agrees to take responsibility for the change and its associated benefits.

4.2 Technical Assessment Activities

This is where potential technological enablers are reviewed for their ability to support the organizations best practice based vision. The primary focus is on standard software enterprise packages and potential bolt on’s. Once a technology is selected, the future state design will be developed within the bounds of this technology using the best practice based vision as input. Process transformation / improvement and operational analysis skills play a key role here.

Note: Often the package is already known by the client and no formal Package Selection is required; however, a formal business case may still be within the scope of the project. Once the technology is known, the detailed project costs can be calculated and incorporated into the Business Case.

These costs must be broken-out into the same time buckets defined in the Opportunity Value Propositions. This will enable detailed project profitability analysis within a defined Business Case time horizon.

The key financial measures utilized in profitability analysis should be, at a minimum:

Measure

Description

Total Return on Investment

ROI measures the potential profitability of a proposed project. The calculation considers the total net project benefit compared to the total project cost, excluding the effects of the time value of money.

Net Present Value

NPV is used to evaluate an investment by comparing the present value of the total benefits to the initial investment. If the NPV (present value of benefits – present value of costs) is greater than zero, the project is generally considered acceptable. However, the greater the NPV between alternate investments, the more desirable the project.

Discounted Payback Period

DPP is the point (duration in years) in which a project’s discounted cash inflows equal the initial investment. These cash flows take into account the time value of money.

Internal Rate of Return

IRR is the discount rate that equates the present value of the expected cash inflows with a project’s initial investment (PV inflow – initial investment = 0). The general rule is that a project is viable if it’s IRR meets or exceeds the company’s desired hurdle rate (Cost of Capital + Premium %).

The above are standard measures used to analyzing capital projects and are easily understood by executive management. Formulas to calculate these measures are available in most spreadsheet applications (including Lotus 1-2-3 and Microsoft Excel).

Once the Business Case development model has been created (e.g. spreadsheet, Advisory Tool, etc.) sensitivity analysis can be performed to see how robust the Business Case really is. This is done by changing the discount rate, annual benefits, annual costs, and value proposition mix. The goal is to find the "decision point". The Decision Point is where the Business Case team has balanced project profitability with their personal comfort level and can commit to and support the numbers.

At this stage of Business Case development, it is common to have the client representatives reduce their potential benefits as they realize that these numbers will become firm management commitments. The IBM consultant must be able to calm the client through coaching. Being too conservative may incorrectly show that a project is not profitable. Being too liberal may show the project is profitable, when it is not. Either way, the client should be able to find a relevant range where they buy-in to stretch goals while maintaining a healthy amount of conservatism.

Finalizing the Business Case is the equivalent to an annual financial budgeting process. It will be the approved plan by which executive management will judge the success of the project. If needed, a summary of the business case can be used in the development of a Board of Directors presentation.

4.3 Design Phase and Beyond

Once approved by executive management, the Business Case and Business Control Book is the plan by which executive management will judge the success of the project. It will also serve as input into the future state design to ensure that the best practice based vision is implemented. Of great importance to the project and organization is the ability of the future state design to track and calculate the Metrics defined in the business case. These provide the key to process control and improvement during and after implementation.

As with any projection, Business Case numbers should be refined throughout the life of the project to reflect environmental changes. This will ensure the most accurate picture is being presented to executive management. The Business Case serves to build a level of trust between the implementation team and executive management through tight control and effective communication that they understand.

Excessive deviation from the business case indicates that the best practice based vision isn’t being implemented or the business environment has changed dramatically and the project should be reviewed to assess its viability.

5. Other Concerns

5.1 The Business Case and Advisory Tool

The Business Case development procedures defined in this document apply equally for manual and automated delivery techniques. The Advisory Tool application, however, offers significant advantages.

Advisory Tool:

  1. provides pre-configured industry models to start with
  2. provides the direct links between Strategy, Process, Best Practices, Metrics, Benchmarks and Business Case
  3. captures new capital in the field
  4. captures historical engagement data (including business case) for future analysis

Advisory Tool supports the process of creating the Best Practice Based Vision and drives this through Business Case development. Advisory Tool is one of the key tools available to the PEBT community will collect and deliver reusable assets within IBM.

5.2 Business Case Scalability, SMB Implications

Scalability Options

Not all customers need or want a full business case. Instances where this might occur include:

  1. limited capital to invest in external consulting for a project
  2. small, single site organization with active owner involvement
  3. typical "theory X" directive management style (top down approach)
  4. project already approved, but pending financial validation

Small and Medium Business is typically an area where this applies.

Regardless of the client environment, a full Business Case development is preferred by PEBT over any other approach. This approach minimizes the impact of change during implementation and increases the probability of success tremendously.

Where limited scope projects are delivered, the term Business Case should not be used!

In the instance that the client does not want to pursue full Business Case development, there are options that make this approach scalable.

5.3 Project Justification vs. Business Case

A Project Justification includes the following elements of a Business Case:

  1. potential benefits (tangible / intangible)
  2. potential costs (tangible / intangible)
  3. profitability analysis

In these areas, standard Business Case development procedures apply.

A Project Justification does not include:

  1. risk analysis
  2. probability of success (ability of organization to change)
  3. detailed control book as a guide to process design and management to the best practice based vision

It’s sole intent is to provide a simple justification for a project at the executive level only.

Because of its financial nature, the Project Justification is developed using the top down approach with a directive role in IBM (see Supporting Documents: Project Justification Outline.doc). This approach will minimize the cost and cycle time of its development, while meeting its intended purpose.

The Opportunity Value Proposition concept does not exist in a Project Justification. Analysis is simplified so that the minimum amount of work can be performed to justify the project to executive management. Given this, there is limited cultural buy in.

The following best practice based visioning elements must be available before the Project Justification can be developed:

  1. critical business processes
  2. opportunities for improvement
  3. potential process changes
  4. metrics to measure the process

In developing the Project Justification, the consultant should focus on:

  1. critical processes only
  2. major benefits and costs associated with a process change (less detailed than a Business Case)
  3. limiting analysis to the minimum needed for project approval (e.g. don’t calculate IRR if management does not require it for approval)

Opportunities and Risks

IBM’s goal is provide the proper services to our clients in terms of quality and risk. This requires professional skepticism going into any project. Each project should be reviewed prior to completing the Statement of Work to ensure that the proper work has been proposed prior to IBM’s engagement so as to ensure success.

This is where the Project Justification can help. Although it can’t take the place of a solid business case, it can be used as a vehicle to "get in the door" and demonstrate IBM’s capabilities. Once with the client, a fair analysis can be performed to ensure that the proper pre-implementation activities have occurred. If not, this should be brought to client management’s attention and a Project Change Request pursued.

In general, the Project Justification is useful for clients who:

  • have chosen a package and want to move straight to implementation, but the process was halted until executive management could validate the investment
  • are small, have only enough capital available to implement a package, and need only simplified executive management justification
  • have been working with another consulting firm in a selection, validation or implementation project and want an independent opinion

As mentioned before in this document, the Project Justification does not include:

  1. risk analysis
  2. probability of success (ability of the organization to change)
  3. detailed control book as a guide to process design and management to the best practice based vision

This will open IBM and the client to the following risks:

  1. IBM performance will be judged using this document, yet, the analysis scope is limited
  2. Organizational change (buy-in) aspects included in a Business Case development effort are not present, limiting its value and effectiveness to the client
  3. It has limited use after project approval, since it provides a weak link between financial targets, enablers and risks that must be managed to meet them

The bottom line is "Be careful when providing this service to our customers". Make absolutely sure that it is the right fit. Regardless, discuss and document the limitations of this approach throughout the engagement.

5.4 Summary

Business Case Development provides significant value to IBM’s customers. It increases the probability of success in an engagement by:

  1. quantifying expected benefits from process changes
  2. analyzing organizational impacts
  3. balancing benefits and risks with organizations capabilities
  4. laying out a plan to manage the change

In developing the Business Case, we suggest the Hybrid approach. It offers the best balance of development cycle time, operational depth, and cultural buy in.

The suggested IBM role is coaching. Although it increases the Business Case development cycle time, it adds significantly to cultural/organizational buy-in of our clients. This buy-in is key in increasing the probability of implementation success.

Don’t underestimate the value of intangible benefits. They often help push a marginal project through approval.

Where appropriate, a project justification can be used. Although this does not replace the Business Case, it provides IBM opportunities to compete in limited scope engagements.
