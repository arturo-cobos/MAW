Guideline: Building a Scorecard
The Building a Scorecard technique paper provides a framework for the definition of a report that can be used to summarize the measures (whole or in part) from a measurement model.
Main Description

1 Description

This summarization focuses on the assessment of a set of measures against specific performance expectations and provides a methodology for deriving an overall assessment score for that measurement set.

2 Context

The “scorecard” as a reporting mechanism is widely used within the business and IT community given its earliest introductions through the work of Robert Kaplan and David Norton in their Harvard Business Review article entitled, “The Balanced Scorecard – Measures that Drive Performance”.

This technique paper does not intend to replace or duplicate an organizations desire to apply the methodologies devised by Kaplan and Norton as a way to identify and define a set of measures appropriate for a scorecard. 

Instead, it seeks to compliment the approaches and techniques described by a measurement model, with a corollary approach for deriving an appropriate scorecard from a measurement model.

As part of laying the groundwork for further discussion in the development of the scorecard, it is important that a thorough understanding of the stakeholders associated with the measurement program be performed.  In the IBM Global Services environment, those stakeholder could include both IBM internal organizations such as a Competency / Competency Segment and IBM’s Customers. 

To further this discussion, it is not uncommon for IBM’s Services Customers to require the implementation of a scorecard to give them appropriate visibility into the achievements that IBM is delivering to them.

Before defining an approach for building a scorecard in section 3, it is important to first define the scorecard in a generic sense as a framework for applying the steps for development and to define the necessary terms used to support the scorecards development.

What is a scorecard?

In a very generic sense, a scorecard could be analogously looked at in the context of a student’s grade point average (GPA) – the net result of a series of assessments across different courses, ultimately resulting in a singular assessment of a student’s achievement.
The scorecard takes this analogy one step further by organizing the various courses into appropriate categories and applying a weight to each of the defined categories.

Following through with the GPA example, consider the idea of weighting the effect of various courses on the GPA based on the area of study and relationship to your chosen field (or major of study).  For instance, if you are computer science major, your classes in computer related courses were given a higher weighting than non-major courses that every student was required to take.

To re-phrase the analogy into terms associated with the measurement model, each measurement is placed into an appropriate measurement category.  A target is established for each measure to be used to evaluate performance.  Each of the measurement categories has an assigned weighting factor determined by the overall importance of the category.  The scorecard is then simply a computation, first at the category level to determine the categories score and than across each category to arrive at an overall score for the scorecard.

The following picture describes this concept:


It is important to note that in step 1, the sum of the weighting for each category must be equal to 100%.  You can see that in category D, where there are two measurements, that the total is indeed 100%. 

In addition, in step 2, the sum of all of the weights for the categories must also sum 100%.

Finally, at both the category level in step 1 and the final score from step 2; the numeric assessment result could be translated into an alphabetic grade given an appropriate scale definition.

For instance, an A could be defined as 90 to 100, a B as 80 to 90 and so on.  The grade letter result for category A for by a “B” and for category C would receive an “A”.

Finally, the scorecard can be built at multiple levels of the organization.  In addition, the assessment of a single scorecard can be done at different levels of the organization.  For instance, a single scorecard could be assessed at the CIO level of the organization and then individually assessed at the business units and various application areas.

In this level of context, several critical success factors should be understood before moving into the steps for building a scorecard:

  • A reasonable number of measures should be included in the scorecard.  If the measurement model has dozens of measures, they should not all be included in the scorecard.
  • The number of categories should be kept small.  You could use the same categories described in the measurement model, or map the measurements to a smaller set of categories, such as the classic four from Kaplan and Norton.  They are described below:

 

  • The appropriate processes must be implemented and in place to handle a number of operational requirements of a scorecard:
    • Define / refine the measurements included in the scorecard (might be done yearly for example)
    • Identify the targets for the measurements (done in conjunction with the refinements to the scorecard measures)
    • Define the weighting for each of the scorecard categories
    • Define the weighting for each of the measures included in each of the categories of the scorecard 

3 Steps

3.1  Define the categories for the scorecard

In deciding what categorization schema to use for the scorecard, it is suggested that this categorization schema be the same, or map closely to the categories used in building the measurement model.  This is important given that the balancing and prioritization processes performed in building the measurement model can be carried over into the processes for developing the scorecard.

3.2  Identify the measures to include in each category

Once the categories are defined, the measures to be included in each category should be identified.  There are several ways to accomplish this:

  1. A facilitated session that includes the various stakeholders of the scorecard to identify the measures
    • The relative importance of the goals and behaviors that these measures drive.  If there are specific goals that are vitally important to both the account and the customer’s success, those should be included in the measurement model
  2. Include the highest priority measures from the measurement model – for instance the top 2 or 3.

It is not uncommon at this point to also include contractually obligated measures since the scorecard can provide an additional level of visibility for those measures.

3.3  Define the weighting for each measure

Now that the measures are defined, the weight of each measure can be considered.  Again, there are several ways to accomplish this:

  1. A facilitated session such as the one used in Step 2 could also be used to weight the measures.
  2. An equal split could be done for each of the measures (for instance 2 measures could be split 50-50, 4 – 25-25-25-25).
  3. A more specific weight could be determined by the importance of each measure as determined by the measurement model.

3.4  Define the weighting for each category

The final step in building the scorecard is to determine the weight for each category. This can be done by identifying the relative weighting of the various measurement categories by looking at the prioritization that was performed in the measurement model.  Since the prioritization is typically done at the measurement category level in addition to the measurement level, the category with the highest overall prioritization could get the highest weighting, second highest, etc.

A second way could be done based on the relative important of each category as defined by the scorecard stakeholders.  This can be done in a facilitated session during the identification of the measurements to be included in the model and the weighting of each of the measures.

3.5  Establish a baseline and/or targets for each measure

In order for the scorecard to provide feedback on the performance of the organization, several attributes must be considered for each measure:

  • A baseline helps to establish the “where am I today?” context and can be used to help establish the targets for each measure
    • For example, a target might be a 5% year over year improvement in productivity.
  • Targets need to be established for each measure in order to determine the performance attainment for each measure.  These help to establish the “where do I want to be?” context for each measure.
    • For example, a target might be a turnover rate of no greater than 8% by 2002.

3.6  Define the scoring criteria for each measure and category

If the requirement(s) to assign an equivalent grading is needed for the scorecard at the measurement level, the category level or both, the scoring criteria need to be established. 

  • These could follow traditional educational scoring:
    •  90% and greater Attainment = A
    •  80% to 90% = B
    • 70% to 80% = C
    •  etc.
  • Or the scoring could be defined in relationship to the targets established:
    • + / - 5% of the target = A
    • + / - 5 to 10% = B
    • etc.

Scorecard Hint’s and Tip’s

  • Although already stressed in the context section, it is important to remember to try to keep the number of measures included in the scorecard to a small set. 
  • In addition to the approach described in the technique paper, a variation of the methodology described in the various publications that describe developing Balanced Scorecard could also be applied on the defined measurement model to help identify and weight the set of measures for the scorecard.

4 Tools

There are a number of effective tools that can be used to produce a scorecard report.  These include the following:

  • Spreadsheets: Static reports that are updated on a monthly basis to show overall performance.
  • Real-Time Graphs: A number of tools, such as Wired for OLAP and BRIO, allow the end-user to view performance results in a graphical manner.  These tools support analysis through drill-downs and other analytical techniques.  These techniques enable root-cause analysis to be done on those organizations (or on the organization as a whole) that are performing at a less-than-desirable level.
  • In addition, there are many good Scorecard tools that are available that deal specifically with reporting and analysis around a scorecard and can compliment any number of database architectures to source the data:
    • Hyperion Performance Scorecard - http://www.hyperion.com/epm_pm.cfm
    • Gentia Balanced Scorecard - http://www.gentia.com/products/gbsc/gbsc_intro.htm
    • Dialog Software Balanced Scorecard - http://www.dialogsoftware.com/balancedscorecard/balanced_scorecard.htm
    • Panorama Business Views PB Views - http://www.pbviews.com/pbviews/index.html
    • SAS Strategic Vision - http://www.sas.com/products/strategic_vision/index.html

Real-time graphical reports are the recommended alternative because of their dynamic access and for the speed with which they provide unambiguous feedback concerning the performance of an organization.
