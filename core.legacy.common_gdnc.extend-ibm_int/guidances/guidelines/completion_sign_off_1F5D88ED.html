Guideline: Completion Sign Off
This Technique Paper covers the definition and documentation of the Completion Sign-off process for a rollout implementation that includes asset management services.
Main Description

1 Description

Every effort has been made to list the essential activities for a smooth transition of product ownership to the customer. This strategy addresses steps to take as the implementation approaches and arrives at conclusion. The main focus is the handling of issues that arise when concluding a project. There may be other implementations that could utilize this document, as some activities may be relevant beyond the specific application started.

There are two kinds of evaluations that occur in projects. Formative Evaluation occurs throughout the project life cycle and provides information to guide corrective action. This type of evaluation is the building block for customer relationships, environmental considerations, setting expectations of all involved parties, and maybe most importantly, the project information. Though completion sign-off relies heavily upon information from this type of evaluation, this topic is more concerned about Summary Evaluation. Here we focus on the end of the project, when the customer accepts responsibility of ownership of the product that was produced for them. It is here that a positive lasting impression can be made on the customer, thus inviting opportunity for future engagements.
 
Completion Sign-off is a post mortem of performance and deliverables of the project implementation. It can be the most crucial stage of bringing about additional business, as well as retaining the customer as a reference. It also provides for an opportunity to improve upon the delivery of future projects at other sites. Most generally it is the Project Manager (PM) who oversees the Completion Sign-off. The PM must insure that all work is closed out, all issues are resolved and/or handed over to responsible people on the customer team for follow-up, and all commitments have been met or compensated for.

It is important that all appropriate information be communicated to the customer and that all documentation be delivered in a timely and orderly fashion. This is the final stage of an engagement and all efforts should be made to keep it smooth and impressive as possible.

2 Context

The purpose of the Completion Sign-off is to enable successful transition of ownership (or responsibility) to the customer team by:

  • Identifying a standard methodology for implementation of an asset management product
  • Recommend the most effective approach to implementing the desired business product ownership transition
  • Reduce the depth and duration of disruption to the project transition process by imposing consistency and direction
  • Facilitating acceptance by ensuring that the transition is well thought out by both the client and the implementation team, and that critical success factors are addressed and in place

Key components of Completion Sign-off are:

  • Document Package
  • Knowledge Transfer
  • Transition Team Management
  • Product Demonstration
  • Sign-off

3 Steps

3.1  Document Package

Introduction

The document package includes the collecting of all existing documentation, status reports and “How-to” documents from all practitioners, consultants and analysts who assisted on the project.  This should have been an ongoing process throughout the life of the project, as documentation should have been collected from each team member as they completed their tasks.  In addition, all parties on the project team should have submitted weekly status reports.  This documentation can be used for transition and skill transfer purposes.  As such, these documents should provide all the necessary information for each completed task.  Documentation should be precise, and include machine names, scripts, userIDs and passwords, configurations, IP addresses, and all other relevant information whenever possible.  There should also be step-by-step documentation of how each task was completed. Often this type of documentation resembles a user guide.

It is highly recommended to have all documentation complete and packaged at the time of sign-off.

Deliverables

  • Status reports should be organized alphabetically by task and in chronological order
  • How-to documents should be organized alphabetically by task
  • Configuration spreadsheet that lists installed modules, patches, hardware
  • Transition team roles
  • Outstanding issues document
  • Remaining documentation should be organized alphabetically by task and in chronological order

Scheme

Status reports will show what was accomplished during the project.  Normally, the members of the project team, on a weekly basis, will submit these to the PM or team lead (TL).  The TL and/or PM will then review these with the customer during weekly status meetings.  On occasion, a practitioner who completed their assigned deliverable may be asked to attend this meeting before departure from the project.  The weekly review will assist in controlling issues as they arise, thus ensuring a smooth transition at the time of Completion Sign-off.  These status meetings will assist in keeping the customer, TL and/or PM current with the project status.  This will allow progress to stay in alignment with the completion date.  This will also provide a mechanism to ensure the transition team is in place and prepared to take ownership of the completed product.  In addition, these meetings will allow the PM, TL and customer to preview what the finished product will resemble before the time of transition.  This will assure that all expectations will be met at project termination. 

How-to:  The customer will use the ‘how-to’ documents once they have taken ownership and the project team has departed.  This documentation will allow the customer to recreate the product in the event that any piece of it fails or needs to be duplicated in day-to-day operations.  Granularity is important with these documents, they will be the tools used to prevent the customer from relying on support for day-to-day tasks.  The focus of this documentation is to supplement the owner’s manual or installation instructions provided with the product, they are not a re-write of them.  Because each environment is unique, they offer different challenges and issues that must be overcome.  So, these documents should include:

  • Lessons that were learned while implementing the product in the customer environment
  • Configurations that are unique to the customer environment
  • Unique tasks associated with day-to-day operations should be covered
  • Anything not covered in the product documentation

Configuration spreadsheet workbook document will illustrate the customer environment in relation to hardware and software configuration.  The workbook will consist of two (2) spreadsheets.  The first will be a Patch/Module listing and the second will list configuration information. 

Patch/Module listing:  Format of this spreadsheet will be by populating the left most column with the titles:

  • Name – for Machine name
  • Role – the specific role the machine performs within the product
  • Hardware -- make and model of each machine
  • OS -- installed operating system (OS)

The following rows in the left most column will be populated individually with the names (or aliases) of each:

  • Installed OS patches
  • Product modules
  • Product patches

The intersecting cells for each module/patch listed in the left most column and machine name listed in the top row will be populated with the installation date of the patch/module.  If the patch/module is not required on a specific machine an n/a for ‘not applicable’ will be entered in the appropriate intersecting cell. 

Configuration listing information will include the top four (4) rows of the Patch/Module listing discussed above.  The following rows in the left most column will include, but not be limited to, the following:

  • UserIDs -- and associated rights/privileges. 
    o Include permanent IDs used by the application(s) for access to databases, etc.
    o Administrator IDs used for access to the product
    o Temporary IDs used by project personnel for access to the customer environment
  • Passwords -- that were changed by the IBM project personnel.  These may be included in another document for security reasons.  The method to change these passwords should also be communicated to the customer before departure of project personnel, as they will most likely elect to change them.
  • Ownership -- of these machines (if other than the customer)
  • Location – country, city, state, building and floor as applicable.  
  • Location access -- requirements and contacts
  • IP -- addresses and network configuration of each of these machines  
  • OS local support -- individual(s) and department, contact information
  • Local product/module -- support individual(s) and department, contact information
  • Hardware configuration -- and inventory information of these machines
  • Local hardware support -- individual(s) and department, contact information

Transition Team Roles:  A directory of product administration roles and responsibilities should be compiled.  This document will list and define the various roles that are necessary for the product to function seamlessly.  At this juncture in the project, all the roles should be filled with qualified personnel.  Many of them may already be functioning within these roles.  This document will also act as a confirmation of management’s approval of the customer team responsibilities.  At times, individuals and/or supervisors may not have fully joined the project effort.  This document may serve to illuminate partial buy-in by customer team member and/or their respective supervisor.  The intended time allowance for the customer team member may not be sufficient for satisfactory execution of the newly assumed responsibilities.  So, this document can also serve to remind the customer of a mismatch between a proposed role of a team member and role expectations and/or abilities.  The name, department contact information, and backup resource details of each role should be included in this document.

Outstanding Issues Document:  Any unresolved issues should be listed in an outstanding issues document.  These are issues that cannot be resolved prior to exit.  At a minimum, the information should include:

  • Module name
  • Brief problem description
  • Description narrative giving detailed information of the issue
  • Symptom(s)
  • Effect on product and/or other modules/applications
  • Support vendor problem identification number
  • Vendor name, technical lead, contact information
  • Notes indicating progress and actions taken thus far,  listed by date
  • Team member responsible for resolution and contact information

Any remaining pertinent documentation not mentioned above should also be included in the project documentation package.  These may include timelines and plans, etc. 

3.2  Knowledge Transfer

Introduction

Many projects fail because of a lack of knowledge transfer.  If no one is prepared to use the product, the blame will usually reside with the implementing team.  If a product is neither fully utilized nor supported with local personnel, the blame will most likely reside on the implementers.  It could be argued who is at fault.  Regardless, the resulting view is that a considerable amount of money has been wasted.  In addition, no further opportunities for project engagements with this customer would be likely in such a scenario.  Knowledge transfer comes in various flavors. 

Objectives

  • Hands-on
  • Informal training sessions
  • Documentation review

Scheme

Hands-on:  The ideal environment during the project would allow the customer to work directly with the implementor during each phase of the project.  This would allow the customer to become more familiar with the product, as well as to understand the various issues and remedies.  Because the customer is seldom available to assist with the implementation, the combination of the product and the project documentation provide a means for the customer to achieve the required level of knowledge.  This point should highlight the need for granularity in all aspects of the documentation that will be given to the customer.  The how-to documents are extremely important and may be used as a tool in any knowledge transfer that occurs.  As the conclusion of the project approaches, a walk through of the modules would assist the customer team to once again become familiar with the GUI’s, methods, and policies of the product.  Often this will be the only time various members of the customer team will be made available to familiarize themselves with their responsibilities.  Time should be set aside for a high level demonstration of the product offerings and abilities.  This also allows for an opportunity to show that the product is working as promised. 

Informal Training Sessions:  Some organizations prefer, or even demand, to have training sessions.  These training sessions should augment rather than replace the formal classroom training offered by various vendors.  Most generally time does not permit for such training, though if it is provided it should be communicated to the customer that the training is meant to augment the formal training.  Because the customer should be familiar with the information in the user’s guides and manuals at this point, the how-to docs may be valuable here.  Again, these will be the documents that the customer will use once the project has been completed.  It should be communicated that the final version of the docs will be in the document package. 

Documentation Review:  At a minimum, the how-to documents should be reviewed with the individual(s) responsible for the associated piece of the product so that they are familiar with, and have a copy of this documentation.  Once the module is complete, the respective how-to document should be completed and submitted to the responsible individuals for that module.  A second copy should be delivered to the PM for inclusion in the final document package.  It should be communicated to the customer team that, in the event of any changes, the final version of the document will be included in the document package at the conclusion of the project.  Effort should be made to provide the final versions of these documents to each appropriate member of the customer team at the conclusion of the project to ensure they have an updated version. 

3.3  Transition Team Management

Introduction

It is extremely important that each member of the customer team knows their respective responsibilities and how they relate to the product roles.  Many of the individuals will have already assumed their respective roles once the project has neared completion.  It is important that everyone is aware of his or her roles with this new product before the rollout team exits the site.  This will contribute greatly towards the success of the project. 

Deliverables/Objectives

  • Spreadsheet illustrating roles
  • Transition Meeting with customer

Scheme

Spreadsheet:  The purpose of the spreadsheet listing is to illustrate the customer transition team.  It will indicate the various job functions required by the product.  These functions will need to be defined therewith thus indicating the required technical and/or administrative trait for that role or function.  This will serve as a high-level indicator of the requirements for each position/role.  The primary and secondary individuals for each role will also be listed, along with their respective contact information.  This document will serve to illustrate the individual, role, description and requirement, thus possibly suggesting to the customer the quality of fit.  Another function of this document will be for contact purposes, as well as backfilling the positions in the future. 

Transition Meeting:  During an arranged Transition Meeting, this spreadsheet will be presented to the customer to show that the transition team is in place and aware of their respective responsibilities.  Any concerns of mismatches may be discussed at this time.  The purpose of the transition meeting is an informative one and not for reassignment of responsibilities.  This is an opportunity to encourage success of the project after the implementation team has exited by ensuring a superior customer team is in place and prepared to assume ownership of the product. 

3.4  Product Demonstration

Introduction

The purpose of the product demonstration is three-fold:  (1) to demonstrate that the product functions as specified and meets all objectives; (2) to point out that any subsequent changes made in the customer environment, or in the product itself, may result in the successfully installed hardware and/or software to be partially or fully disabled; and (3) to use the demonstration as an opportunity to discuss areas for future engagements. 

Objectives

  • Demonstrate a working product
  • Discussion of pending issues
  • Explore opportunity for future engagements

Scheme

Demonstrate the product:  Because changes in the environment may render the product inactive, we want to show the customer a working product before departure.  An effort should be made to perform a somewhat high-level demonstration of each piece of equipment or module individually.  It will be the responsibility of the PM or a designee to perform each demonstration.  It is suggested that the implementing member of the team conduct the demonstration.  This person has the knowledge of the hardware or module as well as the uniqueness of the customer environment that the hardware or module resides in.  The objective is to show the customer that the implementation is complete and successful.

Ways in which the specific product has satisfied the needs of the customer should be highlighted.  These results are defined as requisite and bonus.  Requisite results are those for which the product was purchased.  Lists of these results were compiled at the beginning of the project and should be brought into the demonstration.  It is important to show how each of them has satisfactorily addressed the need that was identified at the beginning of the project.  Somewhat more importantly are the bonus results.  These are needs we have discovered while working in the customer environment that the customer did not anticipate.  Bonus results were exposed in status reports, other documentation, and through discussions with team members.  The implementer will have detailed knowledge of the bonus results.  

Discussion of Pending Issues:  Discussion of outstanding issues for each product should take place at this time.  If agreeable, the customer will need to accept responsibility to arrive at a solution, and sign-off on the product as-is.  The responsible customer team member for this issue should already have been made aware of the problem and have all the information from the Outstanding Issues Document (see step 3.1 above).  A copy of this document will be included in the document package.  The name of the individual responsible for this issue will have been included in the outstanding issues document. 

Explore Opportunities:  High-level discussion for potential future engagements should be expanded during this meeting.  While demonstrating successful completion of the current project, there is opportunity to study future engagements.  The demonstration method should show the customer areas of opportunity, thus giving rise to other contract opportunities.  Methods to resolve other needs discovered in the customer environment should be mentioned and expanded upon as well.  These needs should have been communicated to the PM or TL while the project was executed. 

3.5  Sign-off

Introduction

This is the final step of the transition.  It is important that the customer sign-off on each piece or module of the product.  At this juncture, the customer understands that each piece is working and free from defects.  The customer will also understand and accept any outstanding issues.  Once accepted, the customer assumes responsibility for support and modifications of that module. 

Objectives

  • Customer sign-off and acceptance of each module
  • End of Engagement document

Schema

The product demonstration steps above should be concurred with the sign-off list.  As each piece of the tool is demonstrated, customer acceptance should be obtained.  It is at this point the customer will sign-off and accept the implementation.  Support and modification will be the responsibility of the customer transition team from this point forward.  This process will be exercised for each component of the implementation until the entire product has been turned over to the customer. 

The End of Engagement document should be discussed and submitted to the customer for completion.
