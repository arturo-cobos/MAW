Example: Business Case and Business Control Book Development (PSI)
Here, operational and financial analysis skills are used to drive out tangible and intangible benefits. Tangible benefits are determined using the metrics identified in the best practice based vision.

Example 1:

The metric identified for measuring the inventory management process is inventory turns. It was determined that implementing Just In Time inventory techniques could drive inventory levels down 20%. Linking the metric (financial) with operation improvement would work as follows:

Inventory turns = Cost of Goods Sold / Average Operating Inventory

Currency = $ US

Current client measure (inventory turns): 8

Improvement: average operating inventory reduction 20%

Cost of Goods Sold (COGS) current: $ 180,000,000

Annual Inventory Opening Balance current: $ 20,000,000

Annual Inventory Closing Balance current: $ 25,000,000

Calculation:

Average Operating Inventory Reduction:

[(20,000,000 + 25,000,000)/2] X .2 = $ 4,500,000 US

Target Inventory Turns:

180,000,000 / [22,500,000 – 4,500,000] = 10

Example 2:

The metric identified to measure the success of the Human Resource benefits administration process is HR Full Time Equivalent Support Staff to Total Employees Ratio. It was determined that automating HR support through employee self service would reduce the need for 60% of the support staff needed to support employee benefits. Linking the metric (financial) with operation improvement would work as follows:

HR FTE Support Staff to Total Employee Ratio = total full time support staff in benefits administration / total number of employees

Currency: BEF (Belgian francs)

Current client measure:

Current Support Staff FTE’s: 125

Current Total # Employees: 25,000

Average Support Staff Salary Expense: 960,000 BEF

Current Client Measure (metric): .005

Calculation:

Reduction in FTE Savings:

(125 X .6) X 960,000 BEF = 72,000,000

Target HR FTE Support Staff / Total Employees:

(125 X .4) / 25,000 = .002

Where defined metrics exist, tangible benefits are easily calculated. Here one only needs the metric calculation, current client values and industry benchmarks. The monetary benefits are not necessary.

Sometimes, Benefits are not easily quantified. This happens when:

  1. no metric has a direct relationship with the proposed process
  2. the client doesn’t have operating numbers readily available (they don’t capture the data)
  3. a proposed process is new to a company and no historical operating data exists
  4. a proposed change is strategic and the affect on external markets, customers, etc. can not be quantified through empirical means

Unfortunately, these difficulties are quite common in Business Case development efforts.

Intangible benefits can be just as effective as tangible benefits in justifying a project. The key is to show solid analysis through developing and researching Opportunity Value Propositions, so that management maintains their confidence in the numbers.

It is important that all benefits and costs be broken-out over a series of years. This time frame will become the Business Case Time Horizon used for profitability analysis.

The ultimate goal, whether identifying tangible or intangible elements, is to have the client buy-in and take responsibility for achieving the benefits outlined.

Calculating Potential Tangible and Intangible Costs

Potential tangible and intangible costs at this point are all process related. This means that they are attributed to the impact of process changes only.

Example 3:

Example 2 discussed the savings that could be achieved for a customer implementing HR Employee self-service. In making this change, a severance package with retraining benefits was being offered as an incentive. The average cost of this package was 480,000 BEF.

Potential process change related costs must be defined as a reduction to the proposed benefit to calculate the net potential benefit.

In the early phases, the potential technology costs have not been identified yet. Only costs related to process changes can be researched. Therefore, any costs related to implementation of a new system (e.g. software purchase, system training, financing, etc.) are not taken into account. This is one of the key reasons that Opportunity Value Propositions only include process change related benefits and costs. System related costs will be identified and analyzed in later phases.
