Use this guideline to obtain additional information on implementing continuous risk management.

Main Description

Purpose

Use this guideline to:

Obtain additional information on implementing continuous risk management.





Scope

This guideline applies to:

Continuous risk management at the project or organizational level.

Continuous risk management provides a disciplined environment for proactive decision making to:

  • Continuously assess what can go wrong (risks)
  • Identify critical risks
  • Define and implement strategies and plans to deal with those risks

Overview

This guideline provides information on categorizing, ranking, analyzing, and tracking risks. It also provides guidance for preparing the risk mitigation plan, and for identifying risk sources at the Organizational level.





Details

1. Risk Sources - Organization Level


The Risk Sources must contain identified environmental conditions pertaining to the Contract, Client, or business environment that may create a risk.
Items to be documented by the organization at contract start up include the following:

  • Contractual Clauses.
  • Annual Program Risk Review Session.
  • Organizational Units planning or strategic planning sessions.
  • Contract Amendments.
  • Key milestones or events such as end of Transition.
  • Key changes in the client environment such as change in leadership.
  • Key technical changes.
  • A change in the major stakeholders.
  • Change in competition.
  • Reviews, audits, self-assessments, controls reviews, and incident reports.
    Quality assurance may not be customized by a local organization and is conducted in accordance with the organization's quality assurance business rules, as appropriate.
  • Environmental or political changes.

2. Risk Management Preparation Guide

Risk is a measure of the potential inability to achieve overall project objectives within defined cost, schedule, and technical constraints. Risk management is a proactive event-based approach to continuously assess "what might go wrong" and set activities into action to control, minimize or even eliminate the risk. Risks are typically defined with parameters such as the probability and likelihood of failing to achieve a particular outcome, the consequences and impacts of failing to achieve that outcome, and thresholds to determine if the status of a risk requires action.

2.1 Define risk parameters


When determining risk parameters consider the following:

  • Define consistent criteria for evaluating and quantifying risk likelihood and severity levels.
  • Define the thresholds to trigger management activities. Examples of thresholds include the following:
    • Project-wide threshold could be established to involve senior management when product costs exceed 10% of the target cost or when Cost Performance Indexes (CPIs) fall below 0.95.
    • Schedule threshold could be established to involve senior management when Schedule Performance Indexes (SPIs) fall below 0.95.
    • Performance thresholds could be set to involve senior management when specified key design items (such as processor utilization) exceed 125% of the intended design.
    • Key tasks, project phase-end, or cutover dates are in jeopardy; planned Gross Profit is in jeopardy.
  • Define boundaries on the extent to which thresholds are applied against or within a category. These boundaries can be used to assist in scoping risk management effort and avoiding excessive resource expenditures.

2.2 Define risk management strategies

Risk Management Strategies are high level plans for how various risks will be addressed. Typical strategies include:

  • Accepting the risk: the "do nothing" option. Assume the risk without engaging in any special efforts to control it.
  • Research the risk: conduct an in-depth investigation to determine the potential causes of the risk. (This strategy is typically followed by one of the other strategies based on research results).
  • Reduce the risk: make changes to the project to reduce the risk to an acceptable level while still meeting project objectives or minimizing the impact on the project.
  • Transfer the risk: Reallocate project requirements to other aspects of the project (for example, add funding, add schedule or reduce requirements).

When determining risk management strategies consider the following:

  • Scope of the risk management effort.
  • Methods and tools to be used for risk identification, risk analysis, risk mitigation, risk monitoring, and communication.
  • Project specific sources of risk.
  • How the risks are to be organized, categorized, compared, and consolidated.
  • Parameters, including likelihood, consequences, and thresholds, for taking action on identified risks.
  • Risk mitigation techniques to be used, such as prototyping, simulation, alternative designs, or evolutionary development.
  • Definition of risk measures to monitor the status of the risks.
  • Time intervals for risk monitoring or reassessment.

3. Risk Analysis Guide

3.1 Classify risk

Structures for classifying risks are often based on risk factors or risk consequences, and risks are classified by applying appropriate criteria, such as:
  • Risk factor-based. Group risks by source. For example all risks relating to "unstable requirements" are grouped together.
  • Consequence-based. Group risks that lead to the same general consequence. For example, all risks that might impact the "quality of specifications" could be grouped together.
It is important to clearly partition risks based on the scheme selected. Classification based on risk factors may result in a risk list such as the following:
Group 1 - Risks resulting from "unstable requirements"
  • Risk 1
    • Consequence 1
    • Consequence 2
  • Risk 2
    • Consequence 3
Group 2 - Risks resulting from the "project technical environment"
  • Risk 3
    • Consequence 3
    • Consequence 4
  • Risk 4
    • Consequence 4
  • Risk 5
    • Consequence 5
Grouping risks in this manner leads to creating separate mitigation plans for risks related to "unstable requirements" and risks related to the "project technical environment".

3.2 Impact categories

When determining risk impact categories consider the following:

  • Determine if there is a sponsor or delivery organization policy that prescribes the framework to be used for impact categorization.
  • Determine if the prescribed framework fits the current needs of the project and if it will help the project effectively plan and manage risk.
  • Adapt the prescribed framework or, in the absence of a prescribed framework, create a framework that best meets needs of the project.
Risk impact categories include the following:
  • Financial loss. Impacts to project costs resulting from budget overruns or penalties.
  • Time loss. Impacts to project schedule.
  • Quality loss. Impacts to acceptance criteria.
  • Functionality loss. Impacts to some property that the delivered solution possesses.
  • Resource loss. Impacts to staff, skills, equipment.
  • Sponsor satisfaction loss. Impacts to measured sponsor satisfaction.
  • Benefit loss. Impacts to business benefits that are attained.
Note that the first three risk impact categories (financial, time, and quality) are the most frequently used.

3.3 Analyze risk

Risk analysis is carried out at a level of detail sufficient to determine the relative importance for planning cost-effective risk responses.
In analyzing risks, it is important to remember a number of complicating issues, such as:
  • Risk factors and consequences can interact in unanticipated ways (for example, schedule delays may force consideration of a new strategy that reduces overall project duration but increases project costs).
  • A single occurrence of a risk can have multiple impacts (for example, when late delivery of a key component produces cost overruns, schedule delays, penalty payments, and a lower-quality product).
  • Common techniques involving mathematical manipulation of values assigned to impact, probability, and time frame, often creates a false impression of the precision and reliability of risk analysis.
Evaluating the probability and impact of risks essentially requires a subjective judgment. Describing the probability and impact of risks with numbers can easily give a false impression of precision and reliability. IBM's Worldwide Project Management Method (WWPMM) recommends the use of qualitative descriptions when evaluating risks. If desired, ordinal numbers that rank the properties of risks can be associated with the qualitative descriptions.

3.4 Ranking criteria

Ranking may be based on risk exposure (probability and impact), or based on individual considerations related to probability, impact or time frame. For example, it may be decided that it is important to work first on:

  • All high-probability risks regardless of impact.
  • High-impact risks with up to medium probability.
  • All risks expected to occur within an immediate time frame.

These decisions are based on the expert judgment and experience of the decision maker (such as the project manager and team at a project level, or project executive at an organizational level).

If dealing with a large number of critical risks, it may be necessary to categorize the results of the ranking to assist in monitoring and reporting.
Additional questions that are considered when prioritizing risks include:

  • What is currently important to the sponsor, the project, and/or corporate management?
  • Are there critical milestones or dependencies faced by the project that may be impacted by risks that have been identified?
  • What resources are available for risk planning?
  • What risks may have the greatest "downstream" or secondary impact?

In prioritizing critical risks, it is important to think about containment (mitigation and contingency) plans as well as their cost and probable effectiveness. Risks for which there are no mitigation actions, or for which mitigation costs are prohibitive, may have a low priority. These are generally risks that the project is willing to accept with no further action except to continue to observe for further likelihood of occurrence. In contrast, risks that have low containment costs with a high probability of being contained may have a high priority.

The time frame can also affect the priority of a risk. The same risk, occurring at different points in time, can have different priorities. Usually the later a risk consequence is expected to affect the project, the more time there is to deal with the risk and the lower the priority. In contrast, when the time frame is very short, the priority typically must be increased.

4. Mitigation plan guide

4.1 Transfer and assign risk responsibility

When transferring and assigning risk responsibility consider the following options:

  • To the sponsor or supplier through terms in the Agreement (original or amended).
  • To another internal organization not connected with the project through formal written documentation.
  • To another organization in the same business unit as the delivery team through informal written documentation.

When transferring and assigning risk responsibility consider the following questions:

  • Who could solve the risk?
  • Who have the power and authority to allocate resources?
  • Who is accountable or can be held accountable for the risk?
  • Who has the time to manage the risk?
  • Who has the ability to take action?

In determining risk assignment, it is important to consider the concepts of accountability, responsibility, and authority:

  • Control refers to the ability to influence the outcome due to the boundaries that exist around the situation.
  • Accountability defines who is ultimately held "accountable" for determining the risk approach, as well as the success or failure of mitigation and/or use of contingency plans.
  • Responsibility defines who is charged with developing and implementing (or overseeing) risk mitigation actions and/or use of contingency plans.
  • Authority refers to the right and ability to assign resources for mitigation and/or contingency.

4.2 Risk mitigation plan

The purpose of risk mitigation planning is to identify, define, plan, and staff activities that will lead to reducing the probability of a risk occurrence or the impact of a risk that might occur. These countermeasures aim to change aspects of the risk factors leading to the risk and/or to attack the consequence of the risk itself.

The Risk Mitigation Plan is sometimes referred to as the Contingency Plan.

Examples include:

  • Risk factor: Design skills of the team are considered to be too low
    • Mitigation: Plan a training program in required skills.
  • Risk consequence: Design will not meet the performance criteria
    • Mitigation: Use a subject matter expert during the design phase.
    • Mitigation: Conduct design reviews throughout the design phase.

When creating risk mitigation plans include the following actions:

  • Modifying or adopting new strategies and project approaches (for example, planning a workaround if a dependency is not met on time).
  • Modifying or adding activities to project plans (for example, reviews, testing).
  • Modifying or adopting procedures.
  • Modifying human resource component of the project (for example, add skills, train, use subject matter experts).
  • Modifying the organization component of the project (for example, adding project office, increasing involvement of management).
  • Modifying the culture in which the project operates (for example, improving communications).
  • Modifying the technological component of the project (for example, using proven technology).
  • Modifying the scope of the project (for example, removing less important components).

Risks identified during project definition can often be mitigated by modifying the approach to the project. For example, risks associated with software development projects that are highly complex can be reduced by adopting an incremental approach. Similarly, risks associated with projects that have a high level of uncertainty can be reduced by adopting an evolutionary approach.

In planning risk mitigation, the cost of the mitigation is compared to the benefit or reduction in risk exposure expected from implementing the mitigation plan. If the value of the impact of the risk can be estimated, it can be compared to the cost of mitigation.

In many projects where risk is exceptional or high, a small number of factors such as new technology, an inexperienced team, or a first-time project manager contribute to much of the overall risk. Plans are developed and implemented to manage and contain those risk factors (for example, education in the new technology, adding experienced people to the team, or close involvement on the part of middle management).

5. Risk Tracking Guide

5.1 Continuously assess risk


Tracking is the process in which risk data is acquired, compiled, and analyzed for trends and effectiveness of risk management plan(s).

When collecting and reporting risk tracking data consider the following:

  • Status information is only as good as its accuracy and timeliness.
  • Out of date data is more dangerous to decision makers than no data at all; a wrong decision can be made on false assumptions.
  • When a group of indicators is required to assess a risk occurrence, all the data must be from the same time period.
  • The analysis of risk data is the responsibility of the person assigned to the risk and/or responsible for dealing with the risk.
