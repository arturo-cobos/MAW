Guideline: Work Classification
This document provides guidance on work classification determined based on work type and the associated project management effort.
Main Description

Purpose

Use this guideline to:
Provide work classification guidelines which will be used to determine the appropriate level of project management to apply to the execution of projects (large and small) and work efforts (small and very small).

Scope

This guideline applies to: All projects and work efforts.

Overview

The following documents the types of work and levels of project management that may be applied based on the type of work.

Details

1. Identify Type of Work

The Project Manager classifies the service request or project, based on the type of work. The following are examples of types of work:

·         Development - includes work that leads to the creation of a new application.

  • Typical Service Lines:  AIS, AMS, BAO

·         Enhancement -  include all new requirements to an existing application. This may entail adding, changing, or deleting function of the existing application.

·         Typical Service Lines:  AMS, BAO, Oracle, SAP

There are two types of Enhancements:

1.       Major Enhancement – work  will usually cause an impact to the business, organization or architecture, and may require significant cost, effort, and time to complete. Examples may include migration to a new application platform, adding new interfaces or redesigning a database.

2.       Minor Enhancement - work that includes some new requirements against an existing solution and involve code modification, but has minimal impact on the business, organization or architecture.

·         Maintenance - is focused on supporting or fixing defective business functions. It includes – Corrective Maintenance, Emergency Fix, Perfective Maintenance, Application Support and Ad-Hoc requests that do not change the code function (such as table updates or additional report running)

Note 1: Maintenance is NOT appropriate if the service request, or incident report requires changes to business functionality, business data, application architecture or non-functional requirements, or it requires additional training.

Note 2:  If the work involves changed business function it is an Enhancement.

·         Typical Service Lines:  AMS, AMS SAP, AMS Oracle, BAO

There are several types of Maintenance:

·         Corrective Maintenance -  work that is initiated by a problem incident report and is generally referred to as a permanent fix. Involves changes made to application code in support of new or changed system software. Cost and effort involved are relatively low. This work may be initiated to provide a complete fix after an emergency fix was performed.

·         Emergency Fix - work that is primarily aimed at restoring service. Changes must occur immediately, and the fix may be temporary, requiring follow-on corrective maintenance work to complete the permanent fix. Examples of defects that would initiate an Emergency Fix are a bends, incorrect or missing data, incorrect calculations, and incorrect processing sequence.

·         Perfective Maintenance - work that is initiated in order to avert foreseeable problems, improves performance, quality, reliability, efficiency, usability, or maintainability of an installed solution.

·         Ad-hoc Extracts - Tracked separately but all within Maintenance and support. Also includes:  security administration and monitoring across all in-scope applications; quality assurance through maintenance and use of test environments; Documentation and Training Materials.

·         Applications Support - work that is requested to provide general support and upkeep of an installed application that does not add function to the application.

·         Testing - Projects where the scope is exclusively testing.  This is defined as work that is focused on the process of exercising or evaluating a program, product, or system, by manual or automated means, to verify that it satisfies specified functional and non-functional requirements, to identify differences between expected and actual results.  Artifacts include the testing requirements, test specifications, testing plans, test cases, test scripts, test results etc.

·         Typical Service Lines:  AMS, AMS SAP, AMS Oracle

·         Transition & Transformation - Projects that are done at the beginning of an outsourcing contract focused on either transition or transformation.   

  • Typical Service Lines:  AMS

1)       Transition: That part of the delivery where we manage the smooth change over of the application support and the associated processes from the Client resources / personnel or incumbent vendor resources to IBM.

2)       AMS Organizational Transformational: This is the effort that IBM put towards optimizing the processes and show value to the client in terms of improved productivity and also where required and agreed to bring up the Client application support to a particular CMMI level.

·         Other - Work efforts that do not fit in any other category, this includes consulting, configuration, BluePrint (SAP), rollout design (with no development), business process modeling, business analysis, installation activities, deployment projects and activities that are not related to developing, maintaining or testing software.

Note:  No tailoring guidance will be provided as these are not applicable to application development and maintenance.

  • Typical Service Lines: Any

2. Determine level of Project Management

Determine the level of project management to apply to different work classifications as per the classification table below.

·         Complex/Large/Standard Project: Has the increased planning and controls required to manage change, issues, problems and risk. There is also a greater level of Quality Assurance due to its size and complexity.

Note: Complex System Integration projects should use the highest level of Project Management, regardless of project size

·         Small Project/Minimal Project: This type of project may be managed within a Project Management Structure (PM Structure) or as a standalone project, and both are managed with a full project management discipline. In a PM Structure, the Small Project/Minimal Project is managed partially at the project level and partially at the PM structure level. For a Small Project/Minimal Project-standalone or a Small Project/Minimal Project-within a PM Structure, the same project management disciplines apply as they do to a Complex/Large/Standard Project, but on a smaller scale in terms of the formality, granularity, frequency or scope of activities, often because a Small Project/Minimal Project involves less effort and typically has fewer risks, issues, and so on. The difference may also lie in the reduced frequency of Quality Assurance reviews, the role that performs those reviews, and reduced levels of authority for the release of and acceptance of some deliverables.

·         Small Work or Very Small Work Effort: Will obtain minimal value from formal planning and management activity. Such a small effort usually involves little or no risk, and the requirement for project change management is almost non-existent. Work of this size requires no formal planning or management outside of standard management activities. When a small work effort is part of a Project Management Structure, the Structure's plans will describe any project management activities required at the small work effort level. This effort is not managed like a standalone project, but is managed within a Project Management Structure. A lower level of project management disciplines apply to a small work effort, as compared with a project, noting that a Project Management Structure is managed with the full project management discipline.

Here is an example of a level of project management applied to a work classification:
Project Management (PM) Structure: A single conceptual structure used to manage multiple Small Project/Minimal Projects, small work efforts, or both, with a full project management discipline. This structure may also be applied to managing any grouping of projects or efforts. Typically, a grouping is managed this way under certain circumstances, including the following:

·         Requirements that affect the same application

·         Routine Maintenance of an application

·         A group of one or more applications in a business area

·         An application with multiple releases in a time period

·         A combination of applications and releases

·         Related efforts over a defined period of time

Similar services to a client or range of clients provided by an organization such as a delivery center (or part of a delivery center)

For IBM related Organizations, there are two more examples of types of work:

  • Legacy Transformation, work delivered through the following offerings:
  • Consolidation and Migration work that reduces complexity and enables more cost-effective application operations through code conversion; code and data cleansing, to eliminate errors and promote rationalization of data to reduce redundancies and confusion; data migration, from non-relational to relational databases or from one relational database to another, as well as migration from one data schema to another and porting data between operating systems; and complete documentation of code and data, to make application maintenance and modification easier and to provide a knowledge foundation for better understanding of embedded business processes.
  • Application Integration work that integrates legacy applications with other enterprise software to deliver greater availability of business data and services to clients and suppliers through portal implementations, to provide web access to legacy applications and data using portlets; and services that enable the integration of legacy applications using messaging as well as data transformation and message routing based on business rules.
  • Web Enablement work that focuses on extending the reach of legacy applications both within and outside of the enterprise, to provide for optimized data exchanges and improved collaboration across the enterprise value chain.
  • Application Renovation work that documents, extracts and centrally manages the business rules held in legacy applications; turning these assets into reusable, network-based processes that can be leveraged over time, through a standardized architecture. This work includes the documentation and extraction of business rules; redesign of extracted business rules for improved workflow and componentization; componentization and transformation of legacy code into web services; and implementation of workflow for process improvement and flexibility.
  • Application Portfolio Management, which enables companies to strategically transform their IT environment to align more directly with business priorities. It allows clients to align their portfolio of applications with their business, strategic and technology priorities. It entails the process of continuously assessing, transforming and managing an organization's portfolio to meet specific business objectives. Through a thorough interview process and by direct analysis, it can assist firms in gaining better insight into their portfolio inventory. There are two components, as follows:
  • Portfolio Assessment provides a comprehensive assessment of the full spectrum of an organization's applications and identifies redundancies, areas of inefficiencies, and applications that don't conform to the company's current business practices and processes. Included in the evaluation is the client environment (business strategies, major changes planned); business capabilities and gaps (for example, are the business needs being met by IT or business process capabilities); how to close any identified gaps; business IT priorities; IT strategy; and enterprise architecture. From this assessment, a plan is created that prioritizes the steps necessary for optimization and transformation of the portfolio to the next generation of IT including e-business on demand. Proposed actions might include retiring, reducing, consolidating or upgrading applications.
  • Portfolio Transformation Management includes the implementation of the portfolio assessment plan with ongoing transformation management and governance of the application portfolio in accordance with the plan. The results of the portfolio transformation component include detailed requirements for skills, people, schedules, budget and fit in client environment; program management for planning and managing portfolio management projects; functional and nonfunctional requirements at the next level of detail; change management, configuration management and risk management plans; portfolio management to manage execution of mandated configuration changes, work orders/plans for porting, software level upgrades, etc.; and issue resolution and prioritization management. A key inclusion is the periodic reassessment of the portfolio. Major business changes, for example, mergers or acquisitions, can cause the need for a new assessment. In addition, an assessment will need to be done at predetermined intervals to ensure the application portfolio stays current with changing business priorities.
