Guideline: Communications Management
Use this guideline to define the communications required in managing a project and to create a project communications management plan.
Relationships
Related Elements
  • Define regular meetings, reporting and other communications (P491)
  • Update other project plans (IMSP391)
Main Description

Purpose

Use this guideline to:
Define the communications required in managing a project and to create a project communications management plan.

Scope

This guideline applies to: The communications, orientations, meetings and reports that can be used for all projects.
It includes information on how to develop the communications plan. The intent of the communications plan is to provide the basis for communicating information regarding the project and its activities to the client, project manager, project team, and others.

Overview

This guideline is for use by Project Managers and any Stakeholders who are involved in communications management. It includes planning and delivering information to all project Stakeholders, including:
  • Communicating
  • Meetings
  • Reporting
  • Orientations

Details

The following documents the communications, orientations, meetings and reports that occurs for all projects. Depending on the type and size of projects, combinations may be used.

1. Communicating

1.1 Develop the Communications Management Plan

The communications management plan defines the regular meetings, reporting, and other communications that take place during the project. It identifies the type of information, the medium for communicating, and the audience to whom it is directed. The communications management plan is developed with the involvement of appropriate stakeholders. It is typically approved by the project manager and validated by the sponsor and/or the management.
  1. Reviews memos from the sponsor, the agreement, the project decision structure and the organizational breakdown structure to:
    • Determine the requirements for reporting and meetings imposed by the sponsor
    • Determine the requirements for reporting and meetings specified in the agreement (and any amendments) or in the project definition
    • Determine the requirements for reporting and meetings imposed by the project decision structure and the organizational breakdown structure
  2. Reviews Supplier Agreement and any amendments.
    The agreement or an equivalent can be used for the supplier or sponsor.
  3. Reviews project procedures description to understand the specific project policies and procedures regarding status reporting and status meetings. Policies and procedures of the delivery organization often define the format and procedures for status meeting and reporting.
  4. Performs Stakeholder analysis and:
    • Establishes communications objectives for each of the stakeholders and documents in the communications management plan
    • Determines the information needs of the stakeholder constituencies involved in the project
  5. Determines if there are any external media communications requirements. If there are media communications requirements, the project manager consults with the organization's public relations prior to committing to provide any information.
  6. Determines:
    • Who communicates
    • Who signs the communications and gets their approval
    • The best group to send the communications and request their assistance. The typical options are from the requester directly, through the communications office (if applicable) or through the team leads.
  7. Determines how the information needs of each stakeholder are satisfied, by:
    • Reviewing the technical environment plan to identify communications technology channels available within the project infrastructure
    • Defining the project meetings that occurs to satisfy stakeholder requirements
  8. Defines the project meetings, which occurs to satisfy stakeholder requirements and documents in the communications management plan.
  9. Defines the information retrieval and distribution strategy that are used to share information among team members and with the sponsor, including technical and project management work products within and across project organizational units.
  10. Determines the delivery schedule.
  11. Finalizes the communications management plan.
  12. Reviews with the sponsor and the management.
  13. Adheres to the documentation control procedures as defined for the project and ensures that the communications management plan is reviewed and approved as required.

1.2 Communicate with the sponsor

When communicating with the sponsor, checks that the right people are receiving the appropriate messages. Different messages are often directed at different levels within the organizations. Often there is a single individual or group that acts as the communications link to the rest of the sponsor's organization. For most communications this is sufficient. There may be specific messages, however, that require that traditional links be by-passed or supplemented with additional communications within the sponsor's organization. Considers these communications carefully and seeks the advice of the management team in these instances. Maintains a correspondence log with the sponsor.

The output correspondence log can be in the form of e-mails or any other correspondence with the sponsor. There is no standard template for correspondence.

1.2.1 Event Driven Communications with the sponsor

  1. Assesses the need to communicate with the sponsor based on the event and the urgency surrounding it. Responding to sponsor complaints received in writing requires special attention. It is important to:
    • Respond in writing to each element in the complaint
    • Acknowledge that the element is legitimate (if it is)
    • Propose to initiate actions to analyze the situation
  2. Plans communications by performing the following actions:
    • Provide an objective appraisal of the situation, including a recommended approach for dealing with the problem
    • Do not minimize the problem, but do not overstate it either
    • Be prepared to explore alternatives with the sponsor
      Prior to communicating with the sponsor:
    • Makes sure that the full impact of the event is understood
      Decides how the event is addressed.
    • Develops strategies with the delivery organization's management to:
      • Determine who in the sponsor's organization needs to receive communications
      • Determine who has to deliver communications
      • Determine who has to be in attendance when news is delivered
      • Determine method of communications
  3. Delivers communications to the sponsor.
  4. Documents communications and sponsor response.
  5. Develops correspondence log and enters sponsor responses.

1.2.2 Informal Communications with the sponsor

  1. Meets with the sponsor on a regular basis (daily, weekly, bimonthly) to informally discuss the project.
  2. Seeks feedback and regularly checks the level of the sponsor's satisfaction with the project. This can be accomplished informally or as part of the business process specific to the business area.
  3. Uses electronic mail to keep the sponsor informed of project activities in which they might have a special interest.
  4. Posts items of interest to the sponsor to a shared web site, if available.

1.2.3 Formal communications with the sponsor

  1. Creates the sponsor review report according to the sponsor agreement and using the project status report, and:
    • Summarizes accomplishments for the reporting period and planned accomplishments for next period at the milestone and deliverables level to ensure appropriateness for the sponsor audience and modifies if needed.
    • Summarizes the benefits of postponing planned accomplishments (in those instances where milestones are at risk) to ensure appropriateness for sponsor audience and modify as needed.
    • Determines the cost, effort, and schedule information to be reported to the sponsor. On fixed price projects, hours and costs are typically not reported to the sponsor.
    • Reviews the reported changes, issues, compliance incidents, and risks for the appropriate level of detail.
  2. Reviews contents of the review report with the sponsor per the communications management plan.
  3. Discusses sponsor concerns, by:
    • Reviewing corrective actions that may be taken to address variances and issues
    • Identifying opportunities and threats related to actions requested by the sponsor

For projects managed in the context of a program, or an organization with a defined sponsor review process, refer to the P3 - Process, Performance and People Critical Thread Review Procedure (IMSP435) and its associated plan, the communications management plan (organizational) for P3 CTRs, for additional organizational level guidance.

1.3 Communicate With Project Team

Communications with the project team focuses on establishing and maintaining a common vocabulary, keeping the team informed, and creating and maintaining an environment in which there is a free flow of information within the project team.

1.3.1 Event Driven Communications with the project team

  1. Assesses the need to communicate with the project team based on the event and the urgency surrounding it.
  2. Plans communications by performing the following actions:
    • Providing an objective appraisal of the situation
    • Not minimizing the problem, but not overstating it either
    • Being prepared to explore alternatives with the project team
  3. Delivers communications to the project team. Encourages an open and honest dialogue if this communication is delivered during a team meeting.
  4. Discusses concerns based on communications and addresses immediately or determines a date for responding.
  5. Documents communications and project team response.

1.3.2 Informal Communications with the project team

  1. Meets with the project team (face-to-face or electronically) on a regular basis (daily, weekly, bimonthly) to informally discuss the project as well as non-project related subjects that are of interest to the team.
  2. Uses electronic mail and other groupware technology to keep the team informed of project activities of a general interest.

1.4 Communicate with the subcontractor

1.4.1 Event-driven communications with the subcontractor

  1. Refers to the subcontractor agreement (and any amendments) to understand the types of formal and informal communications allowed with the subcontractor.
  2. Assesses the need to communicate with the subcontractor based on the event and the urgency surrounding it.
  3. Plans communications by performing the following actions:
    • Providing an objective appraisal of the situation
    • Not minimizing the problem, but not overstating it either
    • Being prepared to explore alternatives with the subcontractor team
      Prior to communicating with the subcontractor:
    • Makes sure that the full impact of the event is understood or that the impact can be determined with the subcontractor
    • Decides how the event is addressed, or the way in which the subcontractor decides how to address the problem
    • Strategize with the delivery organization's management
    • Determines method of communication
  4. Delivers communications to the subcontractor.
  5. Determines actions and issues that need to be raised based on communications.
  6. Documents communications and subcontractor response.

1.4.2 Informal communications with the subcontractor

  1. Meets with the subcontractor management and team on a regular basis (weekly, monthly) to informally discuss the project.
  2. Uses electronic mail to keep the subcontractor informed of project activities of a general interest.
    Communications with the subcontractor focuses on maintaining a free flow of information between the subcontractor and the project team so that surprises can be avoided.

2. Orientations

The project manager ensures that the following orientation meetings are scheduled. The minutes for the orientation meetings are captured in the meeting documentation, or an equivalent form that, at a minimum, includes the date held, the attendees and the topics covered.

2.1 Project Manager Orientation

Orientation of the Project Manager occurs at the turnover of the project to the Project Manager. The presenter is the Program Manager, Contract Owner and Stakeholder. The audience may include the project team members assigned at that point in time and any relevant stakeholders. The orientation can be face-to-face or by teleconference. The results are captured on the meeting documentation.

2.2 Project Team Orientation

Project team orientation includes the following topics:
  • Project objectives, communications, facilities, schedule, project approach
  • Project logistics and team travel
  • Procedures to be followed by the project
  • Methodology to be followed by the project
  • Tools to be used on the project and how to use them
  • Standards to be applied on the project
  • Roles and Responsibilities for the project team members
  • Technical aspects of the project
  • Required training (Quality assurance, Peer Reviews)
The presenter of project team orientations is the Project Manager. The audience includes project team members, project stakeholders and if applicable Process Owners. Process owners, also can include Quality owners and/or Configurations Management teams. The orientations takes place by the end of the planning phase and can be a face-to-face meeting or teleconference. There may be multiple meetings to schedule depending upon the project. The results are captured on the meeting documentation.

2.3 Management Orientation

Management orientation includes briefing management on technical aspects of the project. The Presenter is the project manager and the audience includes project team members assigned at that point in time and relevant stakeholders. The orientation takes place at project turnover and can be a face- to-face meeting or a teleconference. The results of the orientations are captured on the meeting documentation.

3. Meetings

General meeting guidelines of interest to project managers are as follows:
  • Only call a meeting if there is a real need. Ask if the meeting is really necessary or if there is a better way to meet the objective. Examples of situations where a meeting is appropriate includes those, where:
    • Interaction and expression of opinions by participants improves the idea, plan, or issue resolution or its acceptance
    • Group dynamics are critical to accomplishing the goal
    • Time constraints indicate that a meeting is the most efficient method
    • Complexity of the subject matter requires interaction and explanation
  • Examples of situations where another form of communication might be more appropriate than a meeting include:
    • Factual data needs to be disseminated to a large group of people, some of whom are in far - distant time zone
    • Distribution of change orders after decisions have been reached
    • Publication of "human interest profiles" or similar public relations information
  • Makes the purpose of the meeting very clear. Prepare an agenda and distribute it in advance, when possible, to allow adequate preparation
  • Follows the agenda
  • Encourages participation
  • Issues minutes, where needed, within one day of the meeting
  • Follows-up on actions or issues generated by the meeting

3.1 Prepare for the Meeting

  1. Reviews the communications management plan to help define the objective and intent of the meeting, which can be:
    • Informational
    • Problem-solving
    • Decision-oriented
    • Interpersonal
  2. Asks some key questions, such as:
    • Is the meeting really necessary?
    • Is there a better way to achieve the objective?
    • What consequences results from not holding the meeting?
  3. Reviews the communications management plan to help determine meeting type and participation, to:
    • Determine the meeting level: one-on-one, team, sub-project, or project-level
    • Identify specific meeting participants
  4. Assigns meeting roles, such as facilitator, scribe, and decision makers.
  5. Prepares a meeting agenda that includes topics and estimated durations.
  6. Arranges the meeting location.
  7. Prepares and distributes invitations and other materials needed prior to the meeting.
  8. Creates or assembles presentation materials that are required during the meeting.
    There are several different types of meetings and Section 4.4 describes them.

3.2 Conduct Meeting

  1. Begins the meeting on time without waiting for people, as this can unintentionally reinforce the notion that arriving late is acceptable and punishes those who arrive on time.
  2. Introduces participants and the roles they are intended to play at the meeting, if necessary.
  3. Reviews the intent, purpose, and subject of the meeting, as well as the meeting agenda.
    For each Agenda item:
    • Presents, collects, discusses, and analyzes information for each agenda item
    • As appropriate, reaches consensus, raises issues or actions, or, if consensus cannot be reached, makes the unilateral decision, communicates the decision, and ensures that team progress continues
  4. Reviews actions and issues raised during the meeting, and:
    • Establishes a date for completion or follow-up
    • Assigns responsibility for each action or issued raised
  5. Keeps meeting notes of discussions and results.

The specific conduct of the meeting depends on the type of meeting, its size and the participants. WWPMM Domain Communications Management, sub-domain of Conduct meetings describes the recommended style to use to conduct different types of project meetings.

4. Types of Meetings

There are several types of meetings that are held during the life of the project.

4.1 Periodic Meetings

Periodic meetings are those meetings that occur throughout the project on a pre-defined schedule (such as weekly, monthly, quarterly).

  • Notification - Since invitations to periodic meetings typically occur well in advance, a notification can be sent, one to two days prior to the scheduled meeting, as a reminder of the meeting and for distribution of the meeting agenda.
  • Meeting Agenda - Periodic meetings have predetermined items that need to be discussed at each occurrence as well as general items for discussion.
  • Meeting Minutes - Meeting minutes for each meeting need to be documented and distributed to the attendees.
Refer to meeting documentation to capture the meeting agenda and minutes.

4.2 Examples of Periodic Meetings

4.2.1 Client Status Review Meeting

The objectives of the client review are to:

  • Update the client on project status
  • Agree upon responsibility for new project issues, dependencies and risks
  • Approve changes to the project in accordance with the Change Management Procedure (IMSP610)

Reviews the following items with the client:

  • Requirements status and issues - requirements document, mapping of requirements to project plans and deliverables
  • Change requests status and issues
  • Project status - project plans, issues, risks, dependencies
  • Deliverables status
  • Quality assurance review status as requested
For projects managed in the context of a program, or an organization with a defined sponsor (or client) review process, refer to the P3 - Process, Performance and People Critical Thread Review Procedure (IMSP435) and its associated plan, the communications management plan (organizational) for P3 CTRs, for additional organizational level guidance.

4.2.2 Senior Management Review Meeting

The objectives of the Senior Management reviews are to monitor status across the projects on a periodic basis and ensure that activities at all management levels are managed consistently throughout the organization. With management agreement, Sr. Management reviews may be done using e-mail notes, with return receipt requested by the Project Manager when the e-mail note is sent.
Management may then schedule a face-to-face meeting or teleconference to address only specific items of concern identified in the e-mail note.

Refer to the P3 - Process, Performance and People CTRs Procedure (IMSP435) and its associated plan, the communications management plan (organizational) for P3 CTRs, for additional organizational level guidance on Senior Management Review Meetings.

4.2.3 Project Management Review Meeting

The objectives of the project management review are to:

  • Understand progress of work against overall plan
  • Review and optimize short term plans
  • Discuss exception areas and raise issues or change
  • Discuss requirements for other reviews

Reviews the following items:

  • Action items from previous meetings
  • Status of the overall project and sub-projects against the plan and outlook for the next period
  • Any outstanding risks, issues and dependencies
  • Outstanding and newly approved changes to the project - depending on the size of the project, it may be necessary to have a separate meeting with the Project Change Control Board

Obtains status of the following items:

  • Requirements Management - requirements status and issues - requirements document, mapping of requirements to project plans and deliverables
  • Project Planning and Tracking - project status - project plans, issues, risks, dependencies - project commitments to individuals and external groups
  • Subcontract Management - software subcontract process, deliverables, and activities (if applicable)
  • Quality Assurance - Quality assurance review status and deviations status
  • Software Configuration Management - SCM status, issues, risks, dependencies
  • Peer Reviews - Peer Review plans/status and defects status
  • Other managerial issues, as required
For projects managed in the context of a delivery organization, program, or a matrix managed organization with a defined project review process, refer to the P3 - Process, Performance and People Critical Thread Review Procedure (IMSP435) and its associated plan, the communications management plan (organizational) for P3 CTRs, for additional organizational level guidance.

4.2.4 Project Team Review Meeting

The objectives of the project team review are to understand and agree to the project plan and its consequences, such as:

  • Being aware of decisions required in the near future and who needs to be consulted
  • Confirming general planning assumptions about the business environment
  • Formally accepting deliverables
  • Addressing variances and address corrective actions
  • Resolving conflicts
  • Providing general direction to the project team

The Project Manager ensures that the following topics are discussed:

  • Review of action items from previous meetings
  • Current status of outstanding issues, risks, changes and dependencies
  • Potential issues, risks, changes and dependencies
  • Accomplishments since the last project review meeting
  • Progress - determine if meeting requirements and operational constraints
  • Plans for the next week (or specified period)
  • Key decisions made since the last review meeting

4.2.5 Configuration Control Board (CCB) Meeting

The objectives of the CCB review are to identify software baselines and review and approve changes to software baselines. The communicator ensures that any version of a work product that has been placed under Configuration Management (CM) control can be re-created at any time to define and organize the elements of a system. The participants of the meeting are defined in the Software Configuration Management Plan.
Refer to the Configuration Management Procedure (IMSP585) for additional information.

4.2.6 Subcontract Review Meeting (if required)

The objectives of the Subcontract review meeting are to check whether the subcontracted work is on target and that there is no unforeseen impact to the overall project.
Reviews the following items with the subcontractor:

  • agreement - commitments and changes to commitments
  • Subcontract status - plans (and performance against plans), issues, risks, critical dependencies, critical resources, action items
  • Subcontractor's visibility needs to end users/clients
  • Review of non-conformance issues
  • Technical reviews to ensure commitments are being met

4.3 Event-Driven Meetings

Event-driven meetings are those meetings that occur because a certain incident requires the meeting to take place. Event-driven meetings can be planned or unplanned.
  • Planned Event-Driven Meetings. Planned event-driven meetings include items like Peer Review meetings, where the meeting is scheduled based on the completion of the work product being reviewed.
    • Invitation and Notification. Invitation to planned event-driven meetings occurs far enough in advance to allow for maximum intended participation and prep time, if required. The invitation includes the dates, start and end times of each day of the meeting, the reason for calling the meeting and, at a minimum, a high-level agenda. After meeting invitation acceptance and if there is time between the original invitation and the meeting, a notification is sent one to two days prior to the scheduled meeting, to remind the invited attendees and distribute the final meeting agenda.
  • Unplanned Event-Driven Meetings. Unplanned event-driven meetings include items like resolution of an issue that occurred during test.
    • Invitation and Notification. Unplanned event-driven meetings occurs typically at the spur of the moment with the intent to group together as many people as are available to discuss a particular topic. Additionally, conversations between team members may evolve to a discussion level where they might be considered an event-driven meeting.
  • meeting documentation for each meeting are documented and distributed to the attendees.

4.3.1 Examples of Event Driven Meetings/Reviews

  • Milestone Reviews - The Project Manager reviews Project milestone status, and:
    • Conducts reviews with relevant stakeholders at meaningful points in the project's schedule, such as the completion of selected stages or other significant milestones, as determined by the stakeholders or project manager
    • Reviews the commitments, plan, status, and risks of the project
    • Identifies and document significant issues and their impacts
    • Documents the results of the review, action items, and decisions
    • Tracks action items to closure
  • QA Reviews - Multiple reviews are scheduled based on the type of project, and are recorded in the Quality plan. The Project's Quality Assuror:
    • Reviews project health, process and work product compliance dependent on the specific review scheduled - specify review.
      Refer to the Quality Planning Procedure (IMSP230) for additional information.
  • Peer Reviews - Multiple reviews are scheduled based on the work products defined for the project and the type of project and are included in the quality plan. The Moderator:
    • Reviews work product (one for each specific work product) dependent on the specific review being scheduled - specify work product.
      Reviews are conducted in accordance with the Work Product Inspections Procedure (IMSP530).
  • Software Baseline Audit - The Software Configuration Management representative:
    • Reviews Software Baseline for integrity of software baselines, structure and facilities of software baseline library contents, and compliance with applicable Software Configuration Management Standards and Procedures.
      Refer to Configuration Management Procedure (IMSP585) and Baseline Audits Guideline for SCM details.
Additional types of meetings can be found in WWPMM Domain Communications, sub-domain Meetings.

5. Reporting

Reporting includes:

  • Preparing and reviewing reports
  • Reporting of sub-project, team, or project status to the delivery organization's management
  • Reporting of individual status to the project manager

5.1 Project Status Report

The project status report is a scheduled report that is created by the top-level project manager for the project. The Project status report includes:
  • Status that describes the state of the project vs. the project plans
  • Progress that describes what the project has accomplished
  • Forecasts that predict future project status and progress based on current project data
The project status report is created by the project manager who has the responsibility for the overall project. The project manager follows the procedures and any business unit policies as described in the project management system summary in preparing the report. The report is created by aggregating sub-project/team and subcontractor status reports into the project status report. Augmentation with graphics is helpful in showing progress or potential problems.

5.2 Prepare and present project or sub project team status

The sub-project team status report is a scheduled report that is created by the project manager or team leader for each sub-project or team within the project organizational unit that they manage. The sub-project team status report provides information about the progress that the sub-project or team has made relative to its project management schedule during the reporting period. The purpose of the report is to alert the next-level project manager to any variances or problems so that corrective actions can be initiated. It is also used by the next level project manager to create his or her own Sub-Project/Team Status Report. Where the next level manager is the top-level project manager, it is used to create the overall project status report that is presented to the line management and the sponsor.

The steps are generic and can be used to prepare sub-project team status report for any level of management within the delivery organization including the sponsor when the sponsor is internal. Reports needs, however, to be tailored to the specific audience to which they are directed. A sub-project team status report may address a single sub-project, aggregating data from individual status reports or may address multiple sub-projects owned by a single project organizational unit. Team status reports are generally used to report status on related tasks, such as a line item. A one-page management summary is usually sufficient for senior management and to prepare this:

  1. Reviews the communications management plan to identify the report(s) to prepare as well as audience, content, and format.
  2. If the project procedures description has been used to establish a status reporting procedure, uses this procedure to identify and understand the specific instructions for preparing status reports
  3. Reviews previous status reports so trends and issues are easier to recall.
  4. Summarizes the financial status:
    • Reports budgeted and actual cost to date, and where appropriate, budgeted and actual revenue and profit
    • Reports cost variances, and where appropriate, revenue and profit variances
    • Reports the forecast of project costs at completion and, where appropriate, revenue and profit
    • Reviews the schedule variances
    • Reports the forecast effort and completion date for the project
  5. Summarizes earned value indicators if appropriate to the project.
  6. Identifies accomplishments for current reporting period using the project management schedule and lower level status reports.
    • Identifies and reports the achievements since the last reporting period
    • Identifies and reports achievements planned but not completed during the reporting period
    • Identifies and reports the work products completed since the last reporting period
    • Identifies and reports the products planned but not completed during the reporting period
  7. Identifies accomplishments planned for the next reporting period using the project management schedule and other available data.
  8. Summarizes the status of changes, issues, compliance incidents, and risks by reviewing them.
  9. Summarizes the status of quality both in terms of project deliverables and the effectiveness of project processes contributing to quality.
  10. Develops the project status summary as follows:
    • Provides an overall project status rating by summarizing the overall status of the sub-project or project being reported using the above information.
    • Overall status may be reported using a "Project grade" or "Three-level color" scale:
      • A / Green = the project is well controlled. Some issues have been identified, but effective actions are planned for solving them.
      • B / Yellow = the project has problems that require change in plans. The cost may exceed the estimate or budget, and/or sponsor satisfaction is at risk. Additional control is essential to bring the project back under control.
      • C / Red = the project has serious problem(s). For example, cost overruns or rejection by the sponsor are unavoidable. Project management needs to take drastic steps to turn the project around.
    • Documents each of the project milestones showing their status (met/not met), and planned and forecast completion dates. Provides any explanatory comments necessary.
    • If the overall project status indicates "problems" or "serious problems", reviews the report with the project management team to ensure that remedial actions have been identified and included in the report, and determines how best to communicate the status to the sponsor.

5.3 Reporting to Senior Management

After the project status report has been completed, the communications management plan may require a separate, one-page, "management summary" for senior management of the organization. Follows these steps, if required:
  1. Determines if the organization has guidelines for producing a management summary.
  2. Focuses on providing the information in which senior management is interested; this includes health issues and concerns on which they must act.
  3. Avoids describing technical issues for which the project team is responsible.
  4. Determines corrective actions to be taken to address variances and issues. Focuses on communicating to management those actions that management needs to be aware of because of their potential impact or those actions that must be escalated to management.
  5. Reports status at the project level (or in some cases the sub-project level) and no lower.
  6. Reports on milestones and deliverables, no lower.
  7. Reports on project health. Health information is obtained from the results of prior health reviews.
  8. Reviews the contents of the management summary with the senior management of the delivery organization per the communications management plan. For internal projects, senior management may also be the project sponsor.
    • Pays particular attention to such matters as delayed milestones, missed deliverables, open issues, pending risks, exceptions to policies and procedures, negative trends, and sponsor satisfaction issues
    • Reviews those corrective actions that require senior management assistance
  9. Identifies opportunities and threats related to any selected corrective actions. The efforts to improve the schedule may have a negative result on costs, and efforts to reduce cost may prolong the schedule.
    Refer to the P3 - Process, Performance and People CTRs Procedure (IMSP435) for further information on Management Reviews.

For projects managed in the context of a delivery organization, program, or a matrix managed organization with a defined project review process, refer to the P3 - Process, Performance and People Critical Thread Review Procedure (IMSP435) and its associated plan, the communications management plan (organizational) for P3 CTRs, for additional organizational level guidance on reporting to senior management.

5.4 Prepare Individual Status Report

An individual status report is a scheduled report that is created by each project team member on a periodic basis, typically weekly or biweekly. This report captures the effort (time) spent on the work that is in progress, an estimate of the effort required to complete the work in progress, and a list of comments for the project manager. The purpose of the individual status report is to provide the team leader or project manager of the sub-project or overall project with an accurate account of time spent on work by each individual and an assessment of the effort that remains to complete the assigned work.
  1. Reviews previous project status report in order to understand major issues or concerns facing the project as a whole.
  2. Reviews the project procedures description to understand the organization or project's instructions for preparing an individual status report
  3. Reviews the operational schedule to determine the tasks, components, deliverables, or milestones the project manager or the team were responsible for completing.
  4. Reports completion status on tasks, components, deliverables, or milestones, using the mechanism defined by the project manager requesting the data (for example, percent complete, effort spent, or Not Started/ Started/ Completed).
  5. Reports effort remaining (work still to be completed) on tasks, components, deliverables, or milestones.
  6. Provides comments that need to be brought to the attention of the project manager or input on:
    1. Pending issues
    2. Risk evolution
    3. Findings and defect trends
    4. The status of previous decisions
    5. Project technical environment effectiveness
    6. Effectiveness of procedures
    7. HR availability changes
    8. The status of purchase orders and costs
  7. Submits the individual status report to the project manager at the next level of the project hierarchy.
