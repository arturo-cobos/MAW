Description
Main Description

As a work product, in general, requirements are the project team's to-do list.  This work product defines what is needed and it's used to focus the project team, as it is the primary method used to communicate the functional goals of the project to everyone on the team.

Requirements define:

  • What the stakeholders need; and
  • What the system must include to satisfy the stakeholders' needs.

Detailing functional requirements may take different forms, including stories, use cases and scenarios, unstructured text, structured text, or a combination, and they may be stated at different levels of granularity. At the highest level of granularity, features define the services that the system must provide to solve the customer's problem. These are captured as structured or unstructured text in the project vision. At the next level of granularity, stories or use cases can be used to more specifically define the functionality that the system must provide to deliver the required features. 

A system must perform according to the behavior specified in stories or use cases. However, there are other categories of requirements that do not represent a specific behavior, also known as system-wide or non-functional requirements, that affect system behavior including:

  • Legal and regulatory requirements, as well as application standards.
  • Quality attributes of the system to be built, including usability, reliability, performance, and supportability requirements.
  • Interface requirements to be able to communicate with external systems
  • Design constraints, such as those for operating systems and environments and for compatibility with other software.

There are a number of techniques to assist the team in identifying requirements, including Business or System Process Modeling.  In cases where Business or System Process Modeling is necessary, the typical agile project kicks off after the modeling activities are complete, or those activities may be planned into definition phase facilitated workshops.

On an Agile With Discipline project, the functional requirements, decomposed as stories, allow for small pieces of the requirements to be estimated, planned and verified by the stakeholder. In addition, most non-functional requirements are planned as part of the fulfillment of the story (or story of a story) that they first relate to in accordance with business priority.
