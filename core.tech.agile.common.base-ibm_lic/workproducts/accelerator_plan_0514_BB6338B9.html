Deliverable: Accelerator Plan (DEL 0514)
This plan is developed throughout the Solution Delivery phase, and is the day-to-day plan used by the project team during the Solution Development phase.
Purpose

It is used to plan and manage the “Agile with Discipline” approach for each project, which allows the team to clearly communicate expectations with stakeholders on a weekly basis, while empowering them to elaborate on the details each week. Portions of it may be supported through automated Agile tooling, such as Rational Team Concert or another agile tool. When baselined on a weekly basis, it allows the team to communicate the progress the team has made against the planned stories / functions, and informally what changes were made (adds/deletes or elaboration to scope) to stakeholders.

The Accelerator Plan will consist of the following as appropriate:

  • User types / personas – Describes the list of users, groups, their roles and personas (when applicable).
  • Prioritization rules - Using “MoSCoW” (Must, Should, Could, or Want) rules approach.
  • Functionality List – A list of identified and categorized functional requirements, written in story format, with assumptions, estimates and actual effort in person days, and planned iteration for that function.
  • Dropped from scope list – Lit of functions that the stakeholder has decided to move out of scope for the current iteration.
  • Timeboxes – Fixed start and end dates for each iteration over the duration of the project.
  • Reuse Plan – Description of components that are being used instead of built from scratch (e.g. Websphere Commerce components, Existing Services).
  • Accelerator Usage – Usage rating and description of how each AWD technique will be used.

Main Description

This includes taking an iterative approach while performing the following activities:

  • Further refine the high-level solution requirements that were developed in the Agile Project Definition and Charter, Project Management System Summary and first-draft Accelerator Plan. Specifically:
    • Profile, verify, clarify and reprioritize, if necessary, each user group, i.e., users with unique functionality in the resulting system (e.g., separate screens, reports, processes).
    • Further identify, detail, describe and prioritize each requirement. For example:
      • Organizational and cultural profiles
      • Business needs
      • Business functions
      • Components or system descriptions
      • Deficiencies
      • Goals
      • Input and output specifications
      • Data specifications
      • Functional specifications, e.g., use case models or test descriptions specifying how users in specific roles, from their perspective, will use the system and what functions the system will provide.
      • Usability specifications, e.g., navigation, screen layout, look-and-feel, consistency, keystroke sequence, aesthetic factors and user performance or productivity goals.
      • Nonfunctional specifications, e.g., run-time service level requirements on performance, capacity, availability, system management; or portability and maintainability requirements.
      • System and communication specifications
      • Backup and recovery specifications
      • Support specifications
      • Hardware specifications, including functionality and characteristics.
      • Software specifications, including functionality, characteristics and usability.
      • Technical specifications, including development information and design constraints.
      • Project tasks, e.g., develop end user training materials and develop deployment plan.

    • Note that in the beginning of the project, the list of requirements is primarily business needs, business functions and project tasks. Later in the project, the list of requirements includes identified modules/components and detailed technical specifications.

  • Leverage Joint Application Development/Design/Requirements (JAD/JAR) techniques, where appropriate. JAD/JAR is a mechanism in which business customers, subject matter experts and technical experts collectively attend intense sessions to collaborate and focus on the business problem, rather than the technical details. This includes:
    • Leveraging an experienced session facilitator
    • Using a variety of visual aids and a facilitated meeting style to maximize effectiveness.
    • Empowering the workgroup to make decisions and approve results
    • Establishing clear objectives for each session
    • Arranging rooms/facilities for each session, e.g., visual aids and scribing.
    • Preparing/organizing documentation for each session
    • Running sessions according to agreed agenda items and times
    • Ensuring session pace is fast
    • Ensuring tight communications between the facilitator and scribe in each session
    • Ensuring session output is complete, validated and approved.
    • Controlling discussions by limiting their length and ensuring relevant points and decisions are captured
    • Controlling participation of the group for each session

  • Refer to the Conducting Joint Application Development (JAD) Sessions Technique Paper.

  • Use the Iteration column of the AWD Accelerator Plan to group prioritized-requirements into three equal-sized iterations, effectively specifying the Release Plan. Have the Business Customer, Architect and Lead Developer estimate sizing and validate with existing function point estimates. Ensure estimates account for refactoring.
  • Allow for variable scope. Note:
    • Projects are generally thought to contain three primary dimensions of delivery: scope as defined by requirements, schedule as indicated by time, and cost of resources.
    • AWD differs from most traditional projects in that it fixes the dimensions of schedule and cost, and plans to allow the scope to vary. Most delivery approaches strive to fix all three dimensions and end up varying schedule and cost by default, i.e., delivering late and/or over budget.
    • Achieving variable scope in an AWD project is possible due to multiple AWD Accelerators. AWD breaks solution development into multiple, time-boxed iterations, usually three. The functional content of each of these iterations is determined and prioritized throughout the AWD project by the business customer, who is a co-located, empowered, member of the delivery team. This prioritization process ensures highest-priority functionality is always delivered first. Any functionality not delivered by the third iteration is lowest priority in the eyes of the business customer. This “thrifted scope” may be delivered later as a separate project or by an application maintenance and support effort, if found to still be necessary.

  • Rearrange the list of requirements and modules as necessary to ensure timebox end dates are met
  • Further define completion criteria for each timebox, i.e., describe work products required by the Timebox End Date
  • Further describe how each AWD Accelerator/Best Practice is to be used on the project
  • Search for reusable work products in intellectual capital repositories and via other sources repeatedly, and update the Reuse Plan in the AWD Accelerator Plan to reflect this information.
  • Trace requirements by (a) numbering requirements and (b) indicating for each component/module listed in the AWD Accelerator Plan which requirements it addresses. Refer to Requirements Traceability Matrix for more information.
  • Review the AWD Accelerator Plan regularly with stakeholders and update it based on feedback
  • Ensure the AWD Accelerator Plan is updated and signed by the by the Business Customer and Lead Developer weekly

In general, developing requirements has these benefits:

  • Ensures team understands the business context of the solution
  • Provides a consistent definition of the customer’s business as it pertains to the solution
  • Determine the complexity and scope of the solution
  • Helps customer make informed investment decision
  • Allows team to get in and out rapidly, collecting and developing only the information absolutely needed.
  • Identifies changes to business processes, technologies, and organizations that occur through the introduction of new ways of doing business
  • Focuses on visible properties/components and their functional/operational context
  • Provides a view of the solution's impacts
  • Provides team with "due diligence" regarding the project feasibility
  • Ensures required organization change is planned for and addressed in later phases

The process to fully develop requirements may include the following:

  • Review the current customer environment – Define all facets of the “as is” customer environment to facilitate mapping to the “to be” and identifying existing constraints. This includes:
    • Business processes – Document steps, roles, inputs, outputs, rules, problems, constraints and impact on the solution for in-scope business processes.
    • Data – Document existing logical data entities and their relationships, as well as primary and secondary access keys, database physical locations and constraints and impact on the solution for in-scope business processes.
    • Organization – Document the organizational chart and constraints and impact of the solution for in-scope organizations
    • Technology – Diagram current systems, interfaces and technical infrastructure and document purpose, technology, constraints and impact of each on the solution for in-scope business processes.

  • Outline the solution requirements – Scope the major functional (Use Case Model), usability and non-functional requirements of the solution to define the "breadth" of the requirements.
  • Outline the application model – Create a quick, high-level draft of the application model that will address the solution requirements and provide a solid indication of the size and complexity of the application.
  • Outline the architectural model – Create a quick, high-level draft of the architectural foundation that the application resides on to indicate the size and complexity of the required infrastructure.
  • Assess business impact - Gauge the level of organizational change likely with the new solution, the organization's ability to cope with the change, the customer’s understanding of affected business processes, and the solution’s contribution to business goals. The “to be” business process model helps identify report requirements, use cases and end-user support requirements, among other advantages.
  • Outline the solution strategy – Specify how to construct the solution and achieve business goals
  • Confirm the solution requirements – provide details and approvals, as required
