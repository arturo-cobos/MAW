Purpose

The purpose of this practice is to further elaborate the Service Model and to specify services in terms of dependencies, service operations, service messages, and non-functional requirement and to make realization decisions regarding implementation of services.

Main Description

Service Specifation

SOMA Service Specification (SS) defines the dependencies, composition, service policies and rules, service operations, service context, service messages, non-functional requirements pertaining to services, context aware attributes and decisions (including management of state within a service) for the services. This practice essentially updates the remainder of the Service Model that was initially created during identification. Service Specification essentially transforms an identification level Service Model representing business oriented perspective on SOA to a specification level Service Model representing IT concerns.

Service granularity helps in transforming identification level Service Model representing business oriented perspective on SOA to specification level Service Model representing IT concerns.

Service Dependencies

The first step in Service Specification is to model the service dependencies.  Service Dependencies has two kind 1) dependency on other services i.e. service (logical) dependency 2) dependency on existing databases and systems i.e. physical dependencies.

There are two types of service dependencies, Functional dependency and Processing dependency.

Functional dependency is a tightly coupled dependency, where Processing dependency is a loosely coupled dependency. In Functional dependency, a service requires the functionality of one or more services to fulfill its functionality.

Processing dependency has three sub-types. First, pre-condition dependency, where another service invocation must have executed successfully before the current invocation can begin execution; Second, processing dependency, where another service invocation is required to complete the successful execution of current service; and Third, post-condition dependency, which appears in cases where a service requires another service invocation after its execution; for example, to complete a business transaction.

In Service Specification, we also capture dependency of services on components and operational systems such as databases, and so on.

Service Composition and Flow

The next step in Service Specification is to model the service composition and flows. Services are composed to realize business processes. This activity realizes the business processes through the choreography of services. There are two types of service compositions. They are: First, macro-flow and second, micro-flow. A macro-flow is a type of composition that denotes a long-running flow and is interruptible, meaning it contains one or more long-running, interruptible tasks. Such service composition must be placed in the Process layer in SOA Reference Architectures. On the other hand, a micro-flow is usually a short-running composition with a deterministic latency between the request and the response and is non-interruptible. In micro-flow, a higher level composite service is created or composed out of other services. A micro-flow service composition is placed both at the Service layer and the Process layer in the SOA Reference Architecture. The type of composition determines the placement of the services in the proper layer in the architecture stack.

Service Operations

Recall Service Model is a hierarchical tree of services with parent-child relationship. As you we go deeper in the hierarchy, granularity of the services is finer grained. We define service operations by specifying nodes in the service hierarchy that will become service operations, Based on service granularity, usage scenarios and non functional requirements service operations are identified.

Service Messages

Messages between communicating services are a critical part of an SOA. This includes not only the input and output messages of a given service invocation, but also the internal message format to be used within the enterprise as the flow of information passes through the layers of the Solution Stack. In many cases, a common message format is recommended. The specification of messages for the service model should take into consideration perspectives from enterprise architecture, data architecture, and integration architecture. This includes message standards defined at an enterprise or application level, appropriate meta model or data model that are part of a data architecture, and message transformation standards that are part of an integration architecture. During the Specification phase in SOMA, it is important to understand the standards established in an organization, if available, in each of the three areas of architecture. Information Specification focuses on defining common message format and message model. In Service Specification, while specifying service interfaces, we capture at least description of the messages associated with a service and its operations and a set of message attributes. We specify message for service operations leveraging the common message model that was developed as part of Information Specification. Common message model defines structural of business entities such as Claim, Member, Account. We use these common definitions of the business entities to define specific request and response messages for each of the service operations of a service.

Non Functional Requirements for Services

SOA provides the opportunity to choose a service provider based not only on the functionality it provides, but also on the Quality of Service it guarantees. One of the reasons for changing a service provider may often be the result of a change in non-functional requirements, necessitating a new level of quality of service not currently supported by the existing provider. It may also result from the degradation in quality of service expected by the service consumer. SOA allows this agility at a lower cost, in general, than other architectural styles. Thus, it is very important to clearly specify the non-functional requirements required by the consumer such as: cost of transaction, performance, availability, security, and so on. While specifying service interface, we identify the non-functional requirements for the desired quality of service. The non-functional requirements will be used to commit resources for service components that offer the services and to fund the realization and maintenance of service components that will ensure the delivery of the quality of service over time. Key architectural decisions must be made to ensure that the promised quality of service based on the non-functional requirements is achieved.

Service Realization

Service Realization is the binding of a component and the services it provides to actual instances of implementation. Example of such bindings are whether the service will be realized through a newly developed EJB that we will expose as a Web Service or whether the service operations will be realized through the wrappering of a set of CICS transactions after we have componentized the legacy system to be able to expose these CICS transactions and have separated out the COBOL copybooks. We make such kind of Realization decisions as part of Service Realization in SOMA. In Service Realization, we also decide what kind of interactions between layers of the architecture or the solution stack are needed to realize the services. We also assign or designate service integration mechanisms to mediate, route, and transform service requests between service consumers or requestors and service providers. Enterprise Service Bus, ESB, is one example that fulfills this responsibility. This is a key enabler for SOA. In case where a service is realized by legacy applications and systems, we map service operation messages to legacy system interface for those service operations that are implemented by leveraging existing system interfaces. The parameters of the service operations are mapped to the parameters of existing system interfaces.
