Artifact: Content Sourcing Plan (BUS 333)
The Content Sourcing Plan addresses content availability, access, and the relationship between one or more internal and external providers and the publisher/distributor of content.
Domains: Knowledge Management
Work Product Kinds: Plan
Purpose

To assure access to and delivery of all content and data required to support the site’s navigational, informational, technical and transactional design and business objectives, and to develop the working documents and processes necessary to sustain and expand the web content delivery operations.

It must address:

  • Access to multiple repositories, with different owners (both internal and external)
  • Access to content in disparate and/or inconsistent forms (data base, document, etc.)
  • Integration of dissimilar content from multiple sources (internal business units, merged/acquired business units/contents, externally sourced content)
  • The business issues associated with access to externally sourced content, and
  • The consumption of and compensation for use of content via the Web.

Competition over the web, for example between the leading Portal sites and internet start ups is driven by differentiation around features such as, brand, programming appeal, ease of use, security and choice, in terms of ordering traditional products, or of downloading content via the web. Securing the rights to acquire, distribute and use content over the web could be critical to the success of the e-business transformation initiative. Therefore, the plan for acquiring and securing the rights for secure web distribution of content should be identified before performing the application design.

The Content Sourcing Plan must identify and resolve internal sourcing issues resulting from content ownership by multiple business units or organizations. These content repositories may have been developed independently over time and may (will probably) lack common vocabulary, design, business objectives, staffing and support requirements.

Description
Main Description

The Content Sourcing Plan needs to take into account:

  • Identification of content and associated requirements
  • Ownership of the material (foundation elements for a trusting relationship)
    • Responsibilities of the owner (maintenance, availability, etc.)
    • Rights of the owner (restriction, frequency of change, etc.)
    • Responsibilities of the user (payment, volume, attributions, etc.)
    • Rights of the user (based on agreement or contract)
  • Access to the content
  • Permission vs. Entitlement
  • Security
  • Performance and service/support levels
  • Maintenance/Update (static vs. dynamic) and Change Management
Brief OutlineA Content Sourcing Plan may include the following components:
  • Internal Content Catalog
  • Content Sourcing “Document of Understanding” detailing Owner and User rights & responsibilities
    • Access to the content is controlled by the owner, and is defined as the ability to find and retrieve the content for publication to a site. It may be based on an internal agreement between different business units within the same organization; or it may be a formal contract between an organization and an external provider.
    • The owner may reserve the rights to make the content unavailable during updates, or to change it without notification; they may reserve the rights to withdraw it without notice.
    • The user or publisher of the content will conversely, want to assure access with minimal disruption and “surprises.” Additionally, the user may want to hold the provider liable for specific failures (e.g., informational failures), and service levels.
    • The mechanics of access must also be negotiated. Will the user have direct access to the source repository? Or, will access be provided through a duplicate or replicate production server, or set of indices? What are the performance parameters? What is the frequency of update, if it is “batch” updated? Will the user be advised of changes? How? How much in advance (time) of the change? Who will define the testing required as part of the change process, and who will perform (and pay) for it?
    • The terms and conditions should be formally spelled out, after negotiations are complete.
  • Content maintenance and change control plan (processes, resources, service level commitments, etc.). For example, what are the notification commitments of the owner in the case of a supplied content element which is infected with a virus, and what is the required response time of the user (and resulting liability) in removing the content until the issue is resolved?)
  • Content issues and mitigation summary, addressing (but not limited to) performance (factor of volume of access, volume of content, growth, peak period analysis, benchmarks, testing, etc.), missed commitments, escalation of high-risk situations, cross-organizational reporting, etc.
  • External Content Catalog
  • Content Sourcing Requirements and Issues - must detail the requirements, recommendations to address them; samples of, or templates for “Terms & Conditions” between the owner and the user; and, identification of issues/risks, and anticipated mitigation plans.
  • Scope and Approach for Commercial Agreement

Key Considerations
  • When creating the Content Model, it is worth identifying any high level issues that may surface as a result of a requirement to use external content, any potential showstoppers identified at this stage may change the overall scope and approach to developing the content sourcing plan.
  • With internally sourced content, especially in organizations undergoing re-organization, merger, or acquisition, it is vitally important to establish clear ownership and escalation procedures, and to articulate, document and gain consensus on specific roles and responsibilities. The risk of not doing this is that a given site may “break,” and the time to resolution can extend unmercifully while internal political or organizational issues are resolved.
  • It is highly advisable to schedule regular sourcing plan performance and planning reviews with all owners/users of content on a regular, periodic basis, to coordinate plans and changes. It may be of particular importance, where similar content (e.g., customer information) is held in multiple repositories, to establish clear, content strategic ownership, and develop content convergence and integration plans.
  • It is extremely dangerous to make the assumption that all necessary content will be available and accessible, and consistently defined and managed simply because it is all owned by the same company. The larger the company, and the broader the types of content, the greater the likelihood of content sourcing inhibitors.
  • A common vocabulary (see other Content WPDs) is a core element that can reduce many of the risks associated with content sourcing.
  • Benchmarking, growth and performance is frequently overlooked, and may require detailed data base reviews, re-designs, additional copies (which add to operational support complexity and delivery costs). Particular care must be given to reviews of transactional and dynamic publishing environments (vs. informational and static publishing environments) to assure that content will be delivered with integrity and “at speed” to meet the expectations of the users.
  • Finally, particular care must be paid to data-type content typically used in a transactional environment, which may result in direct legal liability via an explicit or implicit online contract between the provider and the user.
Tailoring
Impact of not having

Without this work product, the client may be unable to realize objectives originally intended for the Web Site, or may inadvertently breach Intellectual Property and other laws.

Additionally, key pieces of content or data may be unavailable, inaccurately maintained (i.e., synchronized change/update of a pricing file). The risk levels of inaccurate, inconsistent, out-of-date, or inaccurate content because of a failure in content sources varies with the general type of content:

  • Navigational failure - primary risks are user inability to complete desired activity, and a loss of customer confidence in the provider’s ability to deliver the sought-after content. The underlying cause may well not be the navigational mechanism, and is more typically an underlying content failure in indices, linkages, or cross-references that must be maintained to support the navigational paths.

There is, however, the possibility of a flawed navigational tool as well. For example, some current and publicly available Search Engines that have been tested have provided unreliable and inconsistent results. Specifically, they return different results lists when the same search is executed repeatedly. In this case, the user could -do everything right- to retrieve a piece of specific content, and the underlying classification, indexing, links, etc. could be correct, but the user would still be unable to complete the activity because the Search Engine itself failed to find and present the content.

Whether the root cause is content-based or truly navigational, the primary symptom is that the web activity is not successfully completed, and the user "quits" in frustration. A common feedback that would be symptomatic of this type of failure would be variations on "I couldn't get there from -- For example, "I couldn't get to the document from my results list." Or, "When I clicked on 'Go to Top,' I went to a product information page."

  • Information failure - risks range from mis-information level to legal liability for actions based on incorrect content (still to be tested in the courts.) In this case, the actual content is incorrect or is incorrectly identified to the user. This failure may reflect a lack of content review and control within the creation process; or, it may reflect a failure in the content maintenance process.

For example, if the content is a "Chemical Composition and Safety Sheet", which is required by federal law, an information failure could be content accuracy, i.e., incorrect chemicals are listed, or have the wrong percentages, or have incorrect safety or emergency procedures; or, the information failure could be that obsolete copies were not correctly updated on duplicate, replicated, or downstream servers (that may be in place for performance reasons) when the source was updated.

Whether the root cause is the actual accuracy of the content, or maintenance of the content, the effect on the user is potentially action based on inaccurate or obsolete content. A common feedback that would be symptomatic of this type of failure would be "Your information is wrong." Or, "I followed the instructions, but they didn’t work."

In the example of a "Chemical Composition and Safety Sheet," this type of failure is potentially catastrophic, in that property and personal harm may result.

  • Technical failure - primary risk is inability to access the site, or information and the resulting failure to meet business purpose. This implies an underlying technology based failure: a network component is down, the server is down, there is a problem with the browser, etc.

For example, a presentation may be planned, but severe weather at the hosting facility may have taken the power out; the net effect is that an attempt to bring up the site will fail.

  • Transactional failure - risks range from loss of any individual transaction (and resulting revenue, etc.), to direct financial exposure and liability for incorrectly executed transactions (incorrect pricing, terms, conditions, etc.)

A common transactional failure example is related to sales tax on web purchases: if a vendor fails to collect sales tax as required by law, based on current and correct legislation, who is responsible for payment of that tax when the problem is identified?

Reasons for not needingThis work product will not be required if internal content source(s) are owned by the user of the content, and all available elements are clearly documented (complete data dictionary), and there are appropriate processes and resources to manage and support the content maintenance; and, if there is no externally sourced content.
