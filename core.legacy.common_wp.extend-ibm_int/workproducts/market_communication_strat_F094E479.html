Artifact: Marketing Communications Strategy (BUS 424)
This Marketing Communications Strategy work product description outlines the steps taken to produce a plan to communicate effectively with internal and external audiences, such as employees, customers, partners and suppliers, using a variety of marketing communications disciplines, formats and channels.
Domains: Marketing
Work Product Kinds: Strategy
Purpose

The purpose of a Marketing Communications Strategy work product is to:

  • Enable effective communication of information to key audiences
  • Provide a framework for tactical efforts; e.g., loyalty programs, sponsorships, third-party promotions, etc.
  • Ensure consistency of messages across marketing communications disciplines.

The communication or message may or may not be as simple as telling customers about a new product or service, or asking them to do something they already do in a different way. If, for example, a brand’s values have changed, its new values will need to be clearly communicated in order to effectively re-position it within the marketplace.

If an existing product, service or process has been revised or upgraded, it may offer the customer or user additional benefits or require a change in actions or behaviours. Clear communication is essential to ensure that change and new processes are accepted and implemented in the way that the client requires.

Description
Main Description

The Marketing Communications Strategy provides the framework for an integrated and consistent delivery of messages across disciplines, channels and formats - from the look and tone of advertising and direct marketing, to the content of company brochures and executive speeches, to the way customer service responds to a complaint. The general thinking is that an integrated marketing communications effort has a greater persuasive impact because of the synergies it creates. Taking a holistic view of marketing communications also allows a review of how a company uses each discipline and in what proportion.

The Marketing Communications Strategy also defines the specific message(s) to be delivered to each target audience. The content of the message may communicate the value proposition, support the brand, educate and guide behaviours or a combination of all three. The message itself should be engaging, outline the benefits and in some instances provoke an action (e.g. visit a Web site, tell a friend or clients, phone the call centre, realign partner communications strategies, etc.).

But a Marketing Communications Strategy is not just about delivering the right message to the right audience - it’s also about learning from the market. Communication should be a two-way process - where target audience opinions (feedback), behaviours and requirements not only influence the initial messages and activities, but also provide a baseline upon which to measure the success of the communications effort.

The Marketing Communications Strategy should be aligned with the client’s overall Marketing Plan; indeed it should be a critical driver of many initiatives within this plan. While it is likely that the strategy is owned the client’s marketing area, a successful Marketing Communications Strategy has buy-in across the business arena, including the client’s entire internal audience, suppliers and partners.

Brief Outline

This work product is normally a report that consists of the following information:

  • Situation analysis and objectives for the plan
  • Recommended messages and strategic marketing communications activities by target audience
  • Recommended sequence for execution of these activities

Key Considerations
  • It is important not to undertake this type of work without referring to internal specialists in the Marketing Strategy or Digital Brand & Marketing practice.
  • The key dependencies stated earlier may be considered showstoppers.  To undertake a commitment to deliver this work product without access to this type of information could be considered a risk to the success of the initiative and the client brand.
  • Ensure that the Marketing Communications Strategy is built around a clear set of objectives.  The client should have a clear idea what it will be achieved, how and by when.
  • Manage expectations.  Ensure that the client understands what can and cannot be achieved by a Marketing Communications Strategy. Areas where particular attention should be given include:
    • Promises of increased sales 
    • Competitor activity and the context of the market
    • Cultural change issue
    • Cost and pricing/budgetary constraints, 
    • Resource issues
Tailoring
Impact of not having

Without a consistent Marketing Communications Strategy, the client risks miscommunicating with its intended target audiences, which may have negative results, such as:

  • Disjointed communications leave the audience with mixed messages about the brand and its products such that they "take away" completely the wrong message.
  • The messages from the brand are ambiguous and open to interpretation by staff, customers, partners, etc., and provide no clear signals of direction or positioning
  • Unclear or unfocused messages dilute the brand - sending out no clear signals of direction or positioning.
  • Lack of understanding. The audience may not be sophisticated enough to interpret the client’s actions alone. "Don’t expect everyone to guess correctly - spell it out for them."
Reasons for not needing

There may be valid reasons for omitting the Marketing Communications Strategy work product from IBM engagements. Some example situations have been outlined below:

  • IBM has been engaged to perform a strategic opportunity analysis and marketplace assessment. No opportunity has yet been identified to warrant a Marketing Communications Strategy. This however, would only delay the use, not rule it out.
  • IBM has been engaged to deliver a technical solution only. There is no scope for moving up into the business environment, and/or the team is engaged with entirely the wrong client audience. However, approaching the client with business intelligence solutions, such as data mining or CRM could open the door to this type of project.
