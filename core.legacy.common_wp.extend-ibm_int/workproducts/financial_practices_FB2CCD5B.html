Artifact: Financial Practices (BUS 428)
The Financial Practices work product contains information about how financial management is currently undertaken in the client, within the scope and relevance of the engagement at hand.
Domains: Business Value & Financials
Work Product Kinds: Standard
Purpose

The Financial Practices work product provides information about the current position within a client and is broadly used as the baseline from which a subsequent assessment of a clients financial practices can be made and appropriate improvement / development conclusions and recommendations determined. The information that makes up this work product can be varied in both depth and breadth depending upon the context of the engagement. Examples of content and use are illustrated below.

  • Understand current cost and value linkages to both business and IT strategy ensuring that client activities and performance is in sync with strategic direction and underpinning requirements (desired strategic framework). Information that would uncover the current status in this area would be:
    • Portfolio contents and WIP Vs strategy
    • Interfaces to the project portfolio and current services portfolio
    • Linkages to an underpinning pipeline management process
    • Tools and metrics linking strategic fulfillment with performance
    • Organisational setup to perform such alignment activities
  • Appreciate current costs from budgets and interviews that can subsequently feed into a cost model in order to drive enhanced efficiencies
  • Appreciate current IT accounting tools, techniques, practices and organization in order to appreciate feasibility and complexity involved in improving or developing service pricing and charging to the clients of IT
  • Understand current cost management tools, process and automation to allow for continual efficiency monitoring and improvement
  • Appreciate tools and techniques used in prioritizing potential investments to ensure that the most likely outcomes are reflected and appropriate investments chosen
  • Obtain financials associated with the current project portfolio to ensure that it is both appropriately prioritized and optimized for return on investment
  • Understand portfolio and pipeline management process and organization to ensure that on-going investment decisions are effective and efficient throughout the lifecycle of both projects and service currently in production

In essence information required from the current organization is taken to drive forward assessments and ultimate conclusions and recommendations that can positively impact one or a number of the following areas:

  • To be cost efficient without compromising the organizations ability to deliver appropriate products and services
  • To invest in projects, services and technologies that will optimize both fiscal and non-fiscal returns to the organization
  • To be aligned to strategy in terms of cost and value management and have an ability to be both pro-active and to react quickly to changing environmental circumstance
Description
Main Description

Financial Practices work product provides a baseline by which conclusions can be drawn and improvement / development recommendations made.

The breadth and depth of this work product can vary considerably depending upon the engagement. Listed below are key elements that can form a part of this work product.

  • Operational plans and budgets
  • Inventory and asset management tools that can automatically collate and process information for financial use (e.g. Tivoli, Openview, SMS, NetCensus, CA-Unicentre, Tally systems, Tangram Enterprise Solutions Inc, JDE)
  • Inventory and asset management documentation, processes / roles & responsibilities and organization
  • Accounting and cost management tools (e.g. Acorn EPS, Cognos Finance, Sage, Hyperion Financial Management, Oracle Financials etc) techniques, practices and organization
  • Investment / development budgets
  • Business case templates
  • Investment appraisal / decision support / portfolio selection tools (e.g. Expert Choice, InfoHarvest, AliahTHINK!, various DCF tools and the use of spreadsheets etc) and techniques
  • Project portfolio and management documentation, processes / roles and responsibilities and organization
  • Strategic alignment and business performance management tools (Hyperion, SAS, SAP, Oracle, Cognos, Performance Soft and many others) and strategic feedback and update processes
Brief Outline

Budget
In business this generally refers to a document that contains quantitative and financial information forecasting what the organization expects to spend on operational and / or investment activities over the next twelve months. This tends to be defined at variable levels of granularity, generally across organizational units. Often the budget is wrapped into an overall operational plan that details policies, resources, activities and plans in more detail.

Financial and management accounting
Financial accounting concerns measurement and collation of financial data for audit, legal and formal external reporting purposes. Management accounting concerns internal measurement and reporting to help better plan, improve and control an organization from a fiscal perspective.

Historic costs
Accounting generally utilizes historic costing rules, basically this means that when monetary measurement is taken it records transactions at their original cost, and ignores subsequent price or value changes such as say those resulting from inflation.

Variable and fixed costs
Costs behave in different ways. This is one way of classifying costs. Variable costs are those that vary as business activity increases or decreases. (E.g. sales commission, expenses). Fixed costs on the other hand remain constant over variation of business activity. (E.g. salaries and insurance) Note that over time all costs eventually become variable.

Direct and indirect costs
Another way of classifying costs. Direct costs are those that can be physically traceable to a product or service Indirect costs are costs that cannot be traced to specific services or products.

Accruals and prepayments
An accrual is an amount owed at the end of the period for services supplied during that period. A prepayment is an amount paid for a service that is to be provided in a future period.

Opportunity and sunk costs
Opportunity and sunk costs are investment considerations. Opportunity costs consider the cost of making a project investment compared to placing the same funds in a ‘safe’ investment, generally some form of savings account or government bond. A sunk cost is an investment that has already been made and cannot be considered as part of a future investment decision.

FIFO, LIFO and weighted average
These are methods of identifying costs related directly to production. FIFO – first in first out is an accounting pricing method, which considers charging the oldest stock to production first. Alternatively LIFO – Last in first out, the latest prices are used to charge goods out to production. Weighted average uses an average price of stock to charge to production, this could be either a continuous method or fixed as a periodic weighted average.

Revenue
Revenues are the actual funds received for goods and service sold.

Gross and net profit / margin
The gross profit is overall sales revenues minus the cost of goods sold. Net profit is the gross profit minus operating expenses. In essence the net profit is that which the company actual makes. The margins are versions of gross and net profit converted to % terms.

Capital and revenue expense
Capital expenditure is regarded as expenditure that provides a benefit in more than one accounting period. Revenue (or operating / on-going) expenditures are costs associated with only one accounting period.

Profitability ratios
ROI (return on investment) and its variants are examples of profitability ratios. These are various styles of ratios that accountants and analysts use to understand how much profit a company makes, allowing them to be able to trend and compare with previous periods and other organizations. Examples of other common profitability ratios are current asset ratio that considers a companies level of debt i.e. liquidity, stock turnover ratio that gives an indication of company operating efficiency and earnings per share which is one example of many investment ratios that puts profits into a context of the markets view of its stock.

Payback
Payback is an investment decision making method that determines at what point in time the initial investment in say a project is completely covered by subsequent returns made as a result of the investment. I.e. when returns minus investment costs = 0. Payback is therefore expressed in terms of time e.g. months, quarters, years etc.

Discounted cash flow
Discounted cash flows are those that consider the time value of money. In effect a dollar today is worth more than one tomorrow as it can start earning interest immediately. Discounting and using discounting factors takes this fact into account.

Portfolio Management
Portfolio management can be confusing. It is most commonly used to refer to a portfolio of investments within a money market context. Within our area of interest portfolio has two references. The first is that of the pool of projects / developments that a company is investing in or proposing to invest in, to enhance the value of the corporation and its operations. The second is that of the list of services or products that a company currently provides in order to generate funds and revenues for the ongoing existence and profitability of the company.

Pipeline management
Pipeline management is the proactive management of ongoing investment made in a) projects as they progress from initiation and concept through to design and implementation; and b) the ongoing running, improvement and retirement of products and services in production.

Portfolio scoring
These are techniques applied to determining the actual and relative value of one investment versus other investments. A well-accepted method used is that of AHP (Analytical Hierarchy Processing) incorporated in a number of automated tools that perform this activity.

Profit and loss account
A periodic, (usually annual) reported / published statement of structured and granular operating revenues and expenses for that period illustrating the periods profit or loss.

Balance sheet
A periodic, (usually annual) reported / published statement of a company’s ongoing assets and liabilities.

Relevance and prudence
Relevance is critical in finance and accounting, where a plethora of information exists that could be collected. However only that data relevant to the users of the information should be collected. Materiality is also associated with this, an example of the latter is that clearly reporting the cost of a single pen in an aggregated $10m service procurement is absolutely not material or relevant. Materiality considers reporting on items that are significant. Prudence on the other hand considers being conservative, something we all need to do when considering the returns on an investment compared to the cost of that investment. These last terms are really only included to act as a principles guide when undertaking any cost and value work.

Key Considerations

As with all data collection activities it is critical to collate the correct data and appropriate volume of data required for the subsequent assessment activities.

When determining the type and quantity of data required, the following should also be considered and reviewed during data collection activities:

  • Client requirements and objectives of the engagement.
  • Scope of the engagement.
  • Timescales to engagement completion.

The easiest thing to do is collect too much data, by doing this you run the risk of delaying engagement completion and potentially impacting customer satisfaction and / or reducing effective profit margins on your engagement. Data collection is a very time consuming activity.

It is better to identify and collect data in an initial data collection pass knowing that as you progress through assessment and data synthesis you will need to obtain more focused data as unforeseen information requirements invariably occur during engagements. So long as a client is made fully aware of this potential upfront, this should not provide major issues downstream.

Often a client does not have all the data required. This needs to be firmly investigated upfront. As such you need to have a good understanding of data required for the engagement at the proposal stage. Clearly define what data the client will provide you and the implications if they cannot do so. After all data creation (which is what you have to perform if data is not available) is a much more time consuming event than data collection. Data creation (i.e. measurement and in-depth interviews to obtain required data) may significantly increase time to engagement completion and therefore cost to client OR alternatively losses to you if this is not factored in.

There is a plethora of terminology covering the area of finance. Such terminology can also vary greatly by country and region. Some specific language related to accounting is described within the IT Accounting work product. There is also a list of books within the references section of this work product that can help in this area. Some terminology as it applies to basic cost and value is included in this section but even this combined with IT Accounting work product is simply a flavor of the language of finance that you may come across. As such it is critical that only professionals that are IT Cost and Value trained or have a financial background undertake the development of this work product.
Tailoring
Impact of not havingIf you or the client does not know how they currently manage costs, make decisions on and drive forward investments or how such undertakings tie in to the strategy, then there is no basis from which to understand what changes and improvements could or should be made. This is equally true if you cannot capture through appropriate tools and subsequently measure financials in terms of costs or benefits. In essence you cannot improve what you can’t control or measure. Without this work product you and the client will not know how the organization currently performs cost and value management and therefore do not have a baseline by which to assess and develop conclusions and recommendations in this area.
Reasons for not needingThis work product is generally produced when the engagement has a very business / commercially oriented perspective irrespective of the content of the engagement, or if the engagement is actually about how a client improves or develops their ability to manage cost and value. This work product should not be produced if:
  • This information is already known or;
  • The client has confidence that such information or subsequent assessment is not needed or;
  • The cost of obtaining the information outweighs the benefits.

The last point covers a danger in the application of cost and value. It is critical to ensure that the client appreciates the benefit and desires the application of cost and value elements to an engagement where cost and value is not the main topic (i.e. e-business transformation, service delivery improvement, systems integration etc).

Failure to appreciate the client’s position with respect to this places IBM’s reputation, your reputation and client satisfaction in jeopardy. For instance:

  • Speed of implementation may be a big factor and client does not want to be sidelined from this by undertaking any activities that could hinder progress.
  • Politically and fiscally a decision may have already been made compromising this would be counter to the clients requirements.
  • The cost of the engagement may be a major factor and the client may not be prepared to add to the cost by performing cost and value activities.
By all means always explore the potential and need for cost and value analysis but only pursue if there is a substantiated client requirement in this area.
