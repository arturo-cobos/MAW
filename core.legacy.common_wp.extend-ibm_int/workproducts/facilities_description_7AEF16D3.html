Artifact: Facilities Description (OPS 321)
The Facilities Description is a diagram of the current and/or planned state of the facility environment with supporting text.
Domains: Facilities
Work Product Kinds: Inventory
Purpose

Used to document the current or planned facilities in order to understand the integrity and viability of the facility and to establish a baseline for facility selection, design, enhancement, and relocation decisions. Provides the basic data and information that will be used to:

  • Identify the client’s physical facilities and environments in order to assist the client in determining risks associated with the physical and/or geographic locations. 
  • Identify gaps between the current facilities capabilities and those required for planned capabilities.
  • Ensure working conditions are conducive to quality performance by evaluating the internal and external environmental factors of the facility.
  • Maximize the usage of the capital investment in facilities, by reviewing consolidation, relocation or outsourcing opportunities. 
  • Provide insights into subsequent transition planning activities.
     
     
Description
Main Description

This work product will provide an inventory and description of everything within the current or planned client sites and facilities, and its location, as well as a description of a number of key external or environmental characteristics of the facility. This inventory and schematic are gathered for later evaluation against industry best practices in relation to the client’s facility requirements to support facility selection, design, enhancement, and relocation decisions.

The requirements should cover all facility components, effecting both man and machine. These requirements maybe internal to the site, such as:

  • Real Estate Statistics (i.e., square footage, cost per square foot, etc.)
  • Lease vs. Own Conditions 
  • Physical Plant and Layouts  
  • Climate control/HVAC Conditions  
  • Equipment Conditions
  • Fire Protection, Safety and Security Conditions
  • Power, Telecommunication and System Infrastructure and Connectivity
  • Telecommunication Costs

    
Or, external to the site, such as:

  • Geographic Locations
  • Labor Pool Availability (i.e., labor costs, unemployment rates) 
  • Cost of Living 
  • Transportation 
  • Weather
  • Political and Social Potential Hazardous Conditions
  • Government Support (i.e., tax relief, incentives, etc.)
  • Business Recovery Conditions  

  
These details are summarized into a form that can be readily used as input to many future architecture, design, and infrastructure delivery and management decisions, along with observations on current known facilities deficiencies, concerns, and user satisfaction. This work product’s scope is intended to be flexible enough that it can be adjusted so as to be applicable for multiple engagement types and facilities areas. (i.e., data centers, call centers... development environments, research labs, etc..)
Brief Outline

This work product is normally produced as a text document with associated tables and diagrams, and should document all relevant which includes:

  • Space Planning
    • Space Requirements
    • Business Office Area and Related Spaces
    • Space Utilization
  • Architectural Systems
    • Building Structure
    • General Construction
    • Interior Partitions
    • Ceiling System
    • Raised Floor
  • Fire Protection, Safety and Security Systems
  • Mechanical Systems
  • Electrical Systems
  • Real Estate Information
    • Cost per Square Foot
    • Ownership Conditions
    • Geographic Location
    • Weather
    • Political and Social Potential Hazardous Conditions
    • Government Support
  • Labor Information
    • Labor pool availability 
    • Cost of living
    • Transportation

The diagram is typically done using Visio or other computer assisted design tools with the supporting text written in a word processing or spreadsheet tool.  The drawings should include all of the components in the facility.  Key external and environmental characteristics across all facilities are often documented in a matrix, table, or spreadsheet to support further analysis and assessments.

Key Considerations

Always make a softcopy of the drawing.  Marking up the client's existing hardcopy documentation or doing only a hardcopy drawing makes it difficult to share this information.  It cannot be easily sent to other team members, architects, etc. that may need to look at it.  Putting it in softcopy also gives you a start on additional work you might do on the facility such as an assessment or future design.

Make sure that you do not accept any client documentation without performing a walkthrough yourself.  Facility components are often updated on a regular basis and client documentation can become quickly out of date.  If you do not do the verification yourself, you cannot guarantee that the client's documentation actually represents what is in the facility. 

For engagements that are supporting systems architecture and design deliverables, make sure that you document the exact location of each component.  Placement is an important issue for these engagements and it is not sufficient to just understand what components are in the facility.  For selection, relocation or outsourcing decisions specific location information is not required. 

The local Chamber of Commerce and/or Real Estate agents can be of assistance in gathering the environmental and external information points. 

A person with a strong facilities background, should write the major portions of the description.  Many of the sections relate to how the facility was originally built.  As-built drawings may have to be thoroughly reviewed in this effort.           
           
         

Tailoring
Impact of not having

Without a comprehensive understanding of the client’s current or planned facilities, follow-on assessment, definition, transition planning, and investment decisions activities will not be soundly based. The specific impacts of not developing this work product may include:

  • The client may be paying too much for facilities operations.
  • The client may not have proper working conditions. 
  • Information pertinent to developing alternatives and planning work may not be considered.
  • Issues pertinent to facilities may not be discovered until the cost of addressing them has increased. 
  • A clear understanding of the integrity of the facility would not be possible. 
  • No baseline facilities information would exist to support assessments, investment and/or relocation decisions.


     
     
     
Reasons for not needing

A description of an existing facility is always required when the facility is within the scope of the engagement to establish the need for enhancements and/or improvements.   

Although this work product can deliver significant value, it may not be necessary in the following situations:

  • The client does not have any existing facilities or has stated that the current facilities can be completely disregarded. 
  • The information has already been gathered. In this instance, time should still be allowed for reviewing the completeness of this material.
