Artifact: Capability Description (BUS 414)
The Capability Description Work Product contains the information describing a capability and its attributes.
Domains: Business Strategy
Work Product Kinds: Model
Purpose
This work product defines and documents existing and new business capabilities. The information in a Capability Description is necessary for a variety of subsequent analyses including assessing capabilities, identifying the leverage across a set of new business initiatives, identifying extra-firm sourcing, refining business model design, defining the business models that can implement a new business initiative and other kinds of analyses.

Description
Main Description

A Capability identifies any combination of knowledge, organization, process, and technology (Capability Enablers/Resources or KOPT) [We will use the shorthand KOPT to refer to these in the remainder of this document]. Also note, Capability Enabler and Resource both refer to KOPT.) that enables a firm (or business unit) to deliver a specific value proposition directly to a defined segment [A Segment is defined as a collection of firms or individuals that have similar wants and needs from the perspective of the focal (client’s) firm.] of stakeholders, e.g., customer segment or supplier segment, or indirectly through the support of other capabilities.

The internal components of a given capability can vary from firm to firm. For example, several firms may be observed to be capable of delivering the same value propositions; therefore, each of these firms may be described as having the same capability. When analyzed in more detail and compared across firms, each of these firms may have developed or configured a very different set of KOPT in its own unique capability. This identifies an important use and characteristic of capabilities: they provide a scheme whereby the similarities across firms or business models can be identified while the details of the differences among firms can be preserved.

More specifically, a capability’s title provides a label for a firm’s collection of specific KOPT commissioned to support the delivery of specific value propositions. These labels or titles should be assigned so as to allow for identification of similarities. The capability description adds the details of a particular firm’s KOPT, identifying more detailed similarities and differences among specific capabilities that each support the delivery of the same value propositions.

Since in subsequent analyses we may need to know those capabilites which directly support value propositions, we refer to these as Externally Focused Capabilities. These Capabilites are externally focused and directly support a value proposition to one or more segments. Capabilites that indirectly support value propositions through other capabilites are referred to as Internally Focused Capabilities. These capabilites are internally focused and support other capabilities. Occassionally, capabilities will be observed to support value propositions directly and support value propositions through other capabilities. These capabilities are labeled as both External Focused and Internally Focused.

Capabilities are usually described in the format:

For example: "Ability to access information"

Examples of capabilities that enable all major Value Net Players [A Value Net Player is defined as one of the following - Customer, Supplier, Competitor/Substitute or Complementor]. For more information on Value Nets, refer to the Value Exchange Technique Paper:

  • Ability to access customer information
  • Ability to generate customer awareness
  • Ability to source high quality components
  • Ability to create and maintain partnerships and alliances
  • Ability to quickly identify and analyze competitor actions in the marketplace
  • Ability to dynamically source capacity from competitors

Numerous combinations of KOPT can deliver capabilities as indicated by the diversity in management systems, skills, intellectual capital, organizational design and process implementations across firms. In general, there is no "one-way" of delivering a capability. Indeed, there may be no "best-way" of delivering a capability for all firms. What is best for any given firm may depend to a large degree on the other capabilities (and supporting KOPT) that are available in that firm. This flexibility is part of the strength of using capabilities versus other more detailed and specific representations. The primary advantages of Capabilities are:

  1. Avoidance of the tactical detail that may cloud a strategic decision, since capabilities are an abstraction of KOPT,
  2. Avoidance of the myopic (restricted) focus on delivering value in one and only one manner. It allows the client to explore multiple options for delivering the value.

In the most simplistic usage, capabilities identify the essential components of a proposed new business initiative. In this instance, the minimal description of a capability is needed, including only the value produced and not requiring the details of its Capability Enablers (KOPT). If additional information on the Capability Enablers (KOPT) is available, it should be captured during the current task or later in follow-on tasks. In general, many analyses involving capabilities will employ the details describing the Capability Enablers.

The most detailed capability descriptions contains information about Capability Enablers including:

  • Processes that are in place or would need to be in place to deliver the value produced by the capability
  • What knowledge and skills the organization must have, including any critical information or data
  • The organization required in terms of roles and responsibilities and critical management practices and policies
  • Technology deployed or necessary to enable this capability including classes of applications that would be required and any necessary technical infrastructure
Brief Outline

There are a numerous attributes of a capability that can be identified or developed and documented. The following table describes the attributes, some of which are optional.


Attribute
Definition
Examples

Name:
One sentence that describes the capability, usually stated as, “Ability to…”
· Ability to observe and gather detailed information on customer behavior and preferences.
· Ability to ensure adequate inventory of full product line while keeping costs low

ID #:
Unique number used for identification
· C1
· C3

Capability Level:
External or Internal. External Capabilities are externally focused and directly support a value proposition to one or more segments. Internal Capabilities are internally focused and support other capabilities. Capabilities can be both External and Internal. Note: this should be consistent with the Value Proposition Supported and Supported Capabilities fields below.
· External Capability: Ability to provide accurate and up-to-date product information
· Internal Capability: Ability to manufacture the lowest cost product

Description:
Brief statement of the capability – including the value produced and any other relevant descriptive information.
· The ability to ensure adequate inventory of full product line while keeping costs low will result in more impulse buying, higher sales volumes and greater profits.

Function/ Department/ Organization Ownership:
The entity within the firm that is ultimately responsible for the delivery of the capability. It may or may not be the group that owns the capability enablers that support the capability.
· Marketing
· Manufacturing
· Research and Development
· Corporate Strategy (Competitive Intelligence)

Assessment/ Performance:
This can include current performance/ importance for existing capabilities and target performance/importance for modified and new capabilities.
Importance / Performance
· H / L: Improvements needed
· H / H: Leverage and maintain
· L / H: Extend outside current usage, e.g., in-source
· L / L: Ignore, eliminate or outsource

Initiative(s) Supported:
The initiatives that contain the supported value propositions (see Value Proposition(s) Supported). This only applies to External capabilities.
· Customer Portal
· Employee Portal
· Call Center/Web Integration
· Supplier Collaboration Extranet
· Wireless Services

Value Proposition(s) Supported:
The value propositions that are supported by this capability. This only applies to External capabilities.
· Self selection by customer
· Low manufacturing cost
· Supply chain visibility

Supporting Capabilities (Input):
Other capabilities required to deliver this capability (input capabilities).
· Ability to provide in house design focused on low cost manufacturing

Supported Capabilities (Output):
Other capabilities supported by this capability.
· Ability to support self transport by customers

Degree of Core Capability Support:
The capability can 1) be a core capability itself*, 2) support (strengthen) a core capability, 3) be supported by a core capability or 4) conflict with (reduce the effectiveness of) a core capability.
* If it is a Core Capability, describe what is rare or unique about the capability.
· Patented business model
· Patented “one click” selection and purchase facility
· Specialized and proprietary production processes and know-how in making product XXX at Y% percent below competitor’s cost.

Cost Drivers:
The variables that drive the overall cost of the providing the capability.
· Number of shipments
· Number of SKUs

Capability Enabler – Knowledge:
The specific knowledge required by the firm to design, deliver and operate the capability.
· Knowledge about the industry and its products to describe standard templates for product types, including attributes that are relevant for selection and pricing.
· Knowledge of industry protocols or workflow for executing transactions.
· Knowledge of current industry practices for terms negotiation.
· Knowledge of effective enablement and participation in cross functional teams
· Knowledge of effective implementation of ERP applications

Capability Enabler – Organization
The elements of the management system required by the firm to design, deliver and operate the capability.
· Roles - Centralize planning and dispatch management, centralized supplier management, inventory management at individual warehouse

Capability Enabler – Process
The business processes required by the firm to design, deliver and operate the capability.
Inventory Management Processes
· Auto replenishment ordering based on POS and inventory information
· Emphasis on low stock levels but wide range with daily replenishment
· Replenishment levels set up based on POS information & forecast of sales trends
Production & Distribution Management Processes
· Products dispatched as completely knocked down kits (CKDs)
· Production schedules for CKD units released daily for the next day
· Routing is done to optimize cost & time of transportation taking into account load for each warehouse and urgency
Material Management Processes
· Single sources with long term contracts
· Suppliers manage component replenishment based on production schedules
· Multiple consignments of components delivered directly to shop floor based on production plan
· Supplier performance monitoring

Capability Enabler – Technology
The specific technology enablers required by the firm to design, deliver and operate the capability.
· ERP application with distribution optimization and EDI enabled
· Communication infrastructure based on the web as well as leased lines

Capability Enabler - Other
Any other tangible enablers such as physical facilities, distribution networks or intangible enablers such as brand equity
· Local Bank Branches
· Strong Brand Image

Sourcing & Design:
The source of the capability:
· As is - currently exists in the firm and can be used unchanged
· Upgrade - Currently exists in the firm but must be improved, increased, or otherwise modified (need to determine upgrade cost and time)
· Build - does not currently exist and must be built (need to determine design and implementation cost and time)
· Buy - does not currently exist and must be bought (need to determine purchase and implementation cost and time)
· Outsource (Ally) - does not currently exist and can be provided by a partner (need to determine partnering costs)
· For example, the capability, “Ability to drop-ship product overnight to customers facilities” should be outsourced. The capability would take too long to build, is to risky to focus on (outside core competency) and would cost too much.

Overall Risk, Issues & Impacts:
A description of any risks, issues or impacts that will affect the design, implementation or operation of the capability.
· If outsourced, the company will lose control of the “last mile” to the customer and the MOT at the customers shipping dock. Negative performance by the outsourcer could harm our brand image

Key Considerations

Since capabilities can be described at varying levels of detail, it is important to understand what subsequent analyses will be performed with the capabilities and their descriptions.  This should dictate the level and detail of the description.  For example, capabilities identified during an e-Design engagement will normally comprise a far more detailed description of a firm or business initiative than those defined for a strategy engagement.  The difference in detail arises because when we are working to define an e-business strategy, we are interested in the commonality and differentiation among a set of e-business initiatives.  In this situation, we need only identify the most obvious and most significant capabilities that comprise the initiatives.  However, when we are optimizing the design of one of those initiatives, it is necessary to work from a much more detailed decomposition of an initiative into its constituent capabilities.

When defining capabilities for a firm, confusion can arise over the level of detail at which the capabilities should be defined.  The capability, “Ability to generate awareness” describes a very general characteristic of most firms.  In contrast, “Ability to access market demographics” is a more detailed capability that often appears in support of the former capability.  It need not be identified unless it also supports other capabilities.  The level detail of the capability should be dictated by the needs of the engagement and the agreed to scope.  Obviously, the more general capabilities can be identified far easier than the more detailed capabilities.  A careful review of identified capabilities, whether new or existing, should be performed to ensure that a “common level” has been achieved.

Describing capabilities should be an iterative process.  As stated above, a capability may be described at a high-level during the initial activities within a strategy (sufficient amount of detail to make the relevant decisions) and then further refined and described in later activities and fully detailed during e-Design (to support the design and eventual implementation of a supported initiative).
Tailoring
Impact of not havingThe impact of not having this work product could be one of the following:
  • No analysis and documentation of capabilities will make the strategy extremely difficult to execute, e.g., implement
  • Costly redundancy and opportunities for synergy will not be uncovered
  • Analysis performed at a lower level, e.g., Processes, may cloud strategic insights at this point in an end-to-end customer solution, e.g., "Cart before the horse."
  • A client’s options for delivering value propositions will be severely restricted if lower level capability enablers are used
Reasons for not needingThe work product may not be needed if the client has already performed an equivalent analysis (with or without IBM) and is able to share the results. However, in most cases clients operate at a process level and do not usually have documented capabilities.
