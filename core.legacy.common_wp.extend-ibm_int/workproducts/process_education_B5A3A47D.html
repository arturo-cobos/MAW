Artifact: Process Education (BUS 403)
The Process Education work product provides focused training to the Steering Committee, Senior Management, Project Management and Project Team of an engagement on the desired future processes and process enablers of a business solution. It also begins the planning and development of the training materials required to implement the solution.
Domains: Business Process
Work Product Kinds: Realization
Purpose

The Process Education Work Product focuses on developing learning materials needed to implement education-related support processes for targeted stakeholders to realize a planned future business solution. The delivery of educational materials occurs throughout the implementation of the project.

The purpose of this work product is:

  • To identify the project team roles and existing skills available to the implementation
  • To ensure that the implementation team understands the desired future business processes and application functionality of a solution
  • To ensure that the project team understands the required capabilities and performance of the future business processes
  • To establish initial understanding of the future management and control systems around the business solution
  • To ensure that key process owners understand the impact that the planned solution has on the company's organization, data processing disciplines and work structures
  • To ensure that the implementation team is fully trained in the relevant components of a package or asset, when part of the business solution
  • To provide an introduction to the proposed technologies planned to enable the business solution
  • To ensure that skills required for a successful implementation are developed
  • To ensure that key process owners understand their role in the success of the project
  • To provide an initial input and general framework for End User Training

Course Module Definitions

Course modules are independent, reusable components used in conjunction with one another for building courses. Course module definitions briefly describe course modules by specifying the following information:

  • Module name. This is the unique identifier for this course component.
  • Focus. This is the subject of the module, such as an elementary business process, derived logical process, skill, unit of knowledge, or behavior.
  • Objective. This is the performance learners should exhibit as proof that learning has taken place.
  • Delivery strategy. This is the mechanism for offering the module; that is, training, process documentation, or help materials.
  • Learning measure. This determines whether the module achieves its objective. Common ways for gauging the degree to which stakeholders learn are through tests, observation, exercise results, and rating scales.

Course Ingredient Definitions

These identify the following elements a course must address in order to transfer skills, knowledge, and behaviors to its learners:

  • Facts. These are terms related to the specific skill, unit of knowledge, or behavior being addressed. For example, a Course Ingredient Definition might define the term "wholesale order."
  • Concepts. These state the relationships between facts, such as cause and effect. For example, a Course Ingredient Definition might define retail pricing versus wholesale pricing.
  • Principles. These are the implications of facts and concepts. The course must convey these principles in order to make course content meaningful. For example, a Course Ingredient Definition might include the following principle: "The mark-up is less for wholesale orders than for retail orders because there are no distribution channels to maintain."

Course Ingredient / Course Module Matrix

This identifies the ingredients for which each module is responsible and documents module scope by recording involvement indicators in the intersecting cells.
Using a similar “matrixing” technique shown above, tie course ingredients to course modules. This will help in the development of the course content.

Course modules

Course modules include the following components:

  • Training modules. These include:
    • Lesson plan. This is an instructional blueprint describing instructor and learner activities for reaching module objectives. The "instructor" may be a person, computer program, or whatever is appropriate to the delivery method.
    • Student guide. The student guide typically contains presentation images, learner notes, and practice instructions. It may be printed, recorded, or presented for viewing on a screen.
    • Instructor guide. Given that the "instructor" may be a person, computer program, or whatever is appropriate to the delivery method, the instructor guide specifies the instructor's role in module delivery. This will include special instructions, solutions to practice exercises, use of visual aids, likely issue areas, probing questions for audience, anecdotes and references. It usually follows the structure of the student guide.
    • Process Documentation and Help Modules. These are devices for extending the learner's memory beyond training or for providing information in lieu of training. Examples include procedure manuals (printed, recorded, or computerized), quick-reference materials, telephone help lines, application help screens, coaching specifications, and electronic information exchanges. These modules are not intended to be works of art. Meeting specifications, being useful, and providing control over the business process are more important than appearance.

Education Case Studies

A Case Study is a complex exercise that is reality-based and offers practice using multiple skills, units of knowledge, and behaviors together. One or more sample solutions are included for learners to review. This work product expands as new courses are developed and existing ones are modified.

The standards for developing case study material vary with the medium used-a Case Study might be paper-based, conversational, or human-machine interactive.

Key Considerations

Many ingredients go into determining the approach and sequence for developing initial education and education materials. First, recognize that there is no such thing as the correct education plan. Your choice of learning options will vary with such factors as:

  • Learner Demographics.  Learner demographics are the individual and group characteristics over which you have little or no control, such as stakeholder age and years of service. You compiled stakeholder demographics when assessing the current organization. Review them now to determine how they might affect your education plan. For instance, you may want to identify learners’ current experience and their average and median levels of education if they need to acquire new and challenging skills.
  • Number of Learners. The learning mechanisms you choose may depend on how many people you must reach in the given time period.
  • Frequency of Use.  The number of times course content will be used within a given time period determines the appropriate level of access to educational materials.
  • Time Constraints. Limitations on development and delivery time may eliminate some learning options.
  • Targeted Level of Expertise. The value of each learning option varies with the level of competency you are aiming for.
  • Project Budget. Certain learning options are more expensive to develop than others. There is a trade-off, however, between short-term and long-term costs.
  • Shelf Life. You may wish to use fewer resources to develop educational materials that will quickly become obsolete or no longer necessary. When estimating shelf life, consider anticipated learner turnover.
  • Available Education Facilities. Some learning options may require a development or delivery environment currently unavailable.

The learning options themselves are affected by each other and by the technologies chosen for use in the solution implementation. Some options may have a greater resource of education than others, such as product training and documentation.  A comprehensive instructional program may reduce the need for reference material in general.

Remember that there is more to learning than sitting through a regimen of prescribed courseware. Learning also involves informal education, such as coaching and mentoring.

Instead of striving for a particular balance or number of course graduates, aim for getting targeted populations to behave competently in their assigned roles. Consider all ingredients together and make high-level trade-offs between them. Where possible, provide quantitative guidelines to aid decisions about the level of investment to be made in any category. For example, you may want to spend more on training for a process that will be used by one person 10,000 times a month than on one that will be used by 100 people 20 times a month.

In addition, the following guidance is recommended:

  • Focused training approach means selecting the necessary functions from the standard package modules, assets or technologies that pertain to the specific client, rather than giving standard training for all available standard functionality.  Later some configuration of the training system and data migration has to be done in order to ensure that the training is focused on the client's business. 
  • The focused training should be conducted by the Solution Architects.
  • The training must cover the required capabilities and performance of the future business processes.
  • The functionality that the training focuses on should be limited to the business processes that will be impacted by the package or technology and the specific functions that the package or technology will be enabling. This allows the client to focus only on the functionality necessary for his company, making the education of the project team more efficient and goal oriented.
  • To conduct the focused training it is necessary that time is allocated to do the necessary system configuration and user representative data set up.
  • To plan the focused training, collect the following information: 
    • How many users require training?
    • Do supervisors and managers need training?
    • How many trainees can attend at one time?
    • How long will the course be?
    • Where will the training be held?
    • Have similar courses been given before?
    • Is some course material already available?
    • Is job documentation already available?
    • How many instructors are available?
    • How skilled are the instructors in effective training techniques?
    • What budget is available for training?
    • Can outside resources be called in to develop training materials or lead the training courses?
    • Who needs to have input into the course content and design?
    • Who is the "sponsor" for the training?
    • Is there enough equipment to handle the training?
    • Are there classrooms/conference rooms/training labs available for the project?
    • Will trainees have to come in after normal work hours for training?  If so, does overtime apply?
    • Are in-house training designers available to assist with the course design?
Tailoring
Impact of not having

Without a proper understanding of the business process enablers and the technologies planned for use in the solution implementation, it will be difficult for the Client's team members to participate in the development of the future design of the business processes and the application (package/asset/custom). This education helps the project team understand the opportunities and limitations of the planned technologies and business process enablers (people/technology).

As the solution implementation progresses, it may be difficult for Client’s team members to understand to what extent the future design fulfills the required capabilities and performance as well as the impacts on the roles and skills required to operate with the new business solution.

Reasons for not needing
  • When the Client's team members already have the required knowledge of the package, asset or technology to be utilized
  • When it is a more or less a 100% replacement of existing applications and no new design of processes, organization etc., will take place
  • When the Client's team members for other reasons will not become involved in the future design
