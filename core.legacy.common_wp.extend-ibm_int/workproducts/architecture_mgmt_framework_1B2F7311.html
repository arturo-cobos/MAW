Artifact: Architecture Management Framework (ARC 312)
An Architecture Management Framework defines the processes, roles and responsibilities required to manage and implement an enterprise-wide Architecture.
Domains: Enterprise
Work Product Kinds: Model
Purpose
The Architecture Management Framework is used to show how changes to the desired business and IT environment will be implemented, managed and updated in response to changes in business needs and available technologies. One of the key uses of this work product, is the identification and definition of new processes, roles and responsibilities which may not currently exist in the client environment, but are fundamental to the effective implementation of the strategic aims of the business. These new processes are used to enable the business to make conscious decisions about IT, the acquisition of IT assets and the design and implementation of new systems/solutions to meet business needs.

Description
Main DescriptionAn Architecture Management Framework covers business processes, applications, data and technology within a given client environment. The Architecture Management Framework defines how the Enterprise Architecture (and all its component parts) will be implemented, managed and updated in response to changes in business needs and available technologies. The processes defined within this framework are fundamental to enabling the business to make conscious decisions about IT.
Brief Outline

An Architecture Management Framework can take several forms, but it should contain the following: 

  •  A textual description of the desired management processes, with appropriate process flow diagrams 
  •  A defined set of roles and responsibilities - normally textual, but diagrammatic / foil based versions are a valuable aid to communication and gaining agreement 
  •  An organization chart showing the reporting structure for the newly defined roles (optional).

Key Considerations

Depending on the scope of the management processes to be defined, a business or IT process consultant may be of help here. However, the IT Process Model does not specifically address this area in any detail and the consultant will find the guidance contained in the associated ‘Architecture Management Framework’ Technique Paper will be more than sufficient to successfully address architecture management processes - it has been used as a skeleton on many engagements.

It is critical to remember that these processes will normally only be defined during an enterprise-wide Architecture engagement - an engagement focussed on the IT Process Model will not normally cover this area in sufficient detail. It is wise to include some planning for implementation of the processes during the engagement to increase the chance that they will indeed be of value to the client.

Tailoring
Impact of not havingWithout a proper set of management processes, and agreed roles and responsibilities, any architecture will fail and will become simply a set of reports and recommendations gathering dust on a shelf. This work product is key to the future long-term survival and implementation of an enterprise-wide Architecture.
Reasons for not needingArchitecture management processes, roles and responsibilities are well understood and already implemented within the client environment.
