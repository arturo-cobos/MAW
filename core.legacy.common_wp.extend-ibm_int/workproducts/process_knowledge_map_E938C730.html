Artifact: Process Knowledge Map (ORG 412)
A Knowledge Map describes what knowledge is used in a process, and how it flows around the process.
Domains: Knowledge Management
Work Product Kinds: Model
Purpose

The purpose of a process knowledge map is to:

  • Understand the current core processes and the knowledge resources needed to execute them
  • Identify the "focal points" of initiatives and projects.

Focal points are process steps and knowledge resources with a great deal of variability or vulnerability to breakage. The output forms the basis for a gap analysis.

Description
Main DescriptionA process knowledge map is the basis for determining knowledge commonality, or areas where similar knowledge is used across multiple processes. It is the first step in defining a high-level corporate taxonomy and common language. Fundamentally, a process knowledge map contains information about the organization’s knowledge. It describes who has what knowledge (tacit), where the knowledge resides (infrastructure), and how the knowledge is transferred or disseminated (social). Tacit knowledge is expertise in people’s heads. Social capital is the level of trust among a group, their interpersonal relationships and shared cultural norms. Infrastructure refers to the processes, tools, roles and responsibilities, and incentives.
Notation

The process knowledge map should be created following a standard format. The format should include the type of knowledge represented, specifically tacit, social, or infrastructure knowledge. It should include the process name and the objective of the process.

Key Considerations

If possible, use subgroups to divide the work.  This will greatly reduce the amount of time required for the work.  Additionally, make sure the client and consulting team have a clear understanding of the business issues (sometimes referred to as business drivers) that will be the basis for determining what knowledge is needed and where the knowledge resides.  From the onset, it is essential that the consulting team focus on the people, documents, and processes that will be the basis for the process knowledge map.

The real key here is a thorough understanding of the business issues.  These business issues (sometimes referred to as business drivers) will set the stage for directing the consultant towards what knowledge is needed and where it resides.

Tailoring
Impact of not having

Without this work product:

  • Inexperienced project members will take longer to learn the process knowledge mapping technique
  • The organization will necessarily have to relearn lessons previously learned causing rework and time constraints
  • It will be harder to improve the standard approaches and methodologies for specific problem and engagement types
  • The implementation may fail because key knowledge sources and needs are not identified
  • Recipients of a knowledge management system may not realize improved decision making effectiveness if core knowledge processes go undetected by the consulting
Reasons for not needing
  • The information already exists in an acceptable form such as IDEF models
  • Artifacts that are inputs to engagements do not need this level of description
