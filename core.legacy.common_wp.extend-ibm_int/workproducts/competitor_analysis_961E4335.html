Artifact: Competitor Analysis (BUS 418)
Competitor Analysis involves the assessment of emerging or existing competitors and their potential impact on an enterprise’s strategy.
Domains: Business Strategy
Work Product Kinds: Assessment
Purpose
The purpose of the Competitor Analysis work product is to:
  • Assess the client’s market positioning against one or more competitors/substitutors.
  • Assess the competitors internal capabilities as well as their competitive strengths and weaknesses.
  • Understand the how competitors are exploiting strategic relationships or alliances to achieve competitive advantage.
  • Identify new players who may emerge as competitors in the future.
  • Use the competitive intelligence gathered to evaluate the client’s competitive options in formulating strategy.
  • Identify specific initiatives to address competitive threats and opportunities.
Description
Main DescriptionThe analysis encompasses understanding the market positioning, internal capabilities and strategic relationships or alliances of specific competitors or substitutors.

Key Considerations

By reviewing industry trends, it is sometimes possible to identify new threats that the client may not have had in their radar. Indeed, in some cases the client may have little information on competitors e.g. when attempting to enter a new market or to develop a new capability/product/service.

Examine the industry trend of another branch in which parallel situations regarding your client have occurred or will take place.

Be creative and investigate whether it is better to join a competitor than beating him. This may look rather odd or not a part of the engagement but will bring much better discussion into the steering committee or project group. It also shows you’re an expert in the business your client is in.

It is impractical to research and analyze more than 6 competitors.  Narrowing this number done to 3 or 4 focuses the research even more and allows an even more in depth review and analysis.  In some cases, however, it may be necessary to look at many different competitors because they each have unique capabilities or products which the client may want to understand or benchmark against.

Ensure that the client is engaged through every point of the analysis.  The client should participate in the selection of competitors and even more so in the selection of areas of focus for the analysis.  Many companies are often willing to provide dedicated resources to assist in researching and synthesis.  This provides an opportunity for some skills transfer. 

Competitor analysis may sometimes be a tough sell.  A few clients may believe they have a good handle on their competitors. Others may already have a dedicated market intelligence team.  In such cases, practitioners can still bring value to the table by presenting new frameworks for analysis, graphical representations and maybe even new information not known to the client.  The value also comes in the identification of strategic initiatives to address threats and achieve competitive advantage.

Do not rely too much on press releases or other publicly available data.  One Competitive Intelligence professional notes that only about one third of press releases are reliable.  By using industry sources or reports by research firms like Gartner or Forrester, practitioners can often get a clearer view of a competitor’s true capabilities.

Ethical issues. Be careful that the information you have gathered during other engagements will not mix with a competitor analysis engagement. Your client expects confidentiality but will ask you everything about his competitor!

First mover. For more than 80 % of the E-business companies a company being first on the market was of first interest. Nowadays (June 2001) a competitive analysis helps venture capitalists, board members and all other interest groups to make solid decisions.

If your client claims a competitor analysis is too much, to expensive you should consider the appetizer technique. In this it means you can offer the opportunity of conducting a benchmark by using scanning techniques. By using a small questionnaire (let’s say 10 important, meaningful questions) the outcome could be that devastating the client wants to know more. Examples could be the implementation of a customer friendly portal and the connection with the back office or the use of CRM and organizational impact in the insurance market.

Gather information about branches that have gone trough similar changes like your client branch does. Try to find out what mistakes competitors have made, learn from that, and translate it to your customers’ branch.

Tailoring
Impact of not havingCompetitor analysis is an essential input in formulating any strategy. Without benchmarking itself against its competitors, an enterprise may fail to identify key competitive threats to its current market positioning. In addition, without first understanding in detail what competitors are doing, an enterprise has no basis from which to formulate its strategy to achieve competitive advantage.
Reasons for not needingThis work product may not be required if the client has already conducted a detailed competitor analysis and can make this information available. In some cases, large enterprises have dedicated market intelligence resources whose job it is to perform targeted analysis on competitors. However, the client can still benefit from analysis frameworks and graphic comparisons that help them visualize the where they are positioned. Indeed the real value comes from transforming the competitor knowledge into actionable initiatives to address identified threats and opportunities.
