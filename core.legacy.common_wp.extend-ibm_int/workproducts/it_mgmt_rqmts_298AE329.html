Artifact: IT Management Requirements (OPS 303)
The IT Management Requirements work product documents all the requirements needed to deliver the in scope IT management services.
Domains: IT Management & Monitoring
Work Product Kinds: Requirement
Purpose

This work product is used for evaluating the existing client IT management environment and determining what changes to that environment are required to support the IT management services in the scope of the engagement. It encompasses the gathering of the detailed IT management requirements for process, tools, organization, and data.

This work product is used to identify:

  • what modifications will need to be made to existing processes and what new processes will need to be implemented.
  • what already-installed tools are sufficient and what new tools should be acquired. (By arranging the tool requirements in generic tool definitions, the tool requirements can be more easily compared against the functionality of tools already in use by the client. In addition the tool requirements will become the functional requirements for selection of new tools).
  • what types of data interfaces should be developed. (By mapping the data requirements to sub-processes, the data requirements can be more easily integrated in the solution design).
  • what types of skills are already available and what new skills should be developed. (By arranging the skill requirements in generic role definitions, the role requirements can be more easily compared against the jobs already in existence).
Description
Main Description

IT management services are defined based on the customer’s perception of services, and scoped according to the requirements of the engagement.The IT Management Requirements work product includes:

  • an overview of the in scope IT management services
  • a mapping of the in scope IT management services to the processes and sub-processes required to delivery them
  • a mapping of the managed IT elements to their associated sub-processes to structure the base matrix for assembling the requirements
     

IT Management Requirements are composed of four sections:

  • IT management process requirements
  • IT management tool requirements
  • IT management data requirements
  • IT management organization requirements

IT management process requirements are requirements on the sub-processes involved in delivering the in scope IT services. These may take the form of requirements for modification of existing processes, or depending on the state of the IT management environment, requirements for new processes.

IT management processes and sub-processes are used as the basis, in conjunction with the specific IT elements, for organizing tools, skills and data requirements gathering exercises.

The IT management process requirements should include:

  • An overview of the existing processes involved
  • A list of requirements for each process
  • A list of new processes that will be required

The IT management tool requirements define the generic tool profiles for tools needed to perform the sub-processes involved in delivering a desired service.

Generic tools can be defined as: “any technology that will be used in the management of the target system.” Defined generically, the tools become descriptions of types of tools available for systems management and should not be associated with actual products until they are fully defined. The generic tool should represent the "to-be" tool, instead of merely a description of an existing product. In this way, generic tools that are defined by requirements that cannot be met by any existing product can sometimes be realized through development or acquisition of several tools that, when combined, satisfy the generic tool requirements. Tool requirements consist of functional, non-functional as well as interface requirements to enable performance of the systems management sub-processes.

The IT management tool requirements should include:

  • An overview of technology elements involved
  • A list of functional requirements for each tool
  • A list of generic tools that will be required

Optionally, it may also contain:

  • Where the tool will reside
  • A cross-reference of the tool to the sub-processes and/or capabilities using the tool

The IT management data requirements define data elements that are necessary to support the performance of the sub-processes.
Process teams and subject matter experts develop the data requirements.
The IT management data requirements should include:

  • An overview of sub-processes and IT-elements involved
  • A list of data requirements for each sub-process

The IT management organization requirements define the generic skill profiles for roles needed to perform the sub-processes involved in delivering a desired IT management service.

Generic roles are defined by the set of skills required and the set of systems management sub-processes performed, the generic role is a "hat" worn by an IS resource. The term "generic" is used during the design to prevent tying a role to an actual job existing in the client organization before the role can be defined.

The IT management organization requirements should include:

  • An overview of sub-processes and IT elements involved
  • A list of professional, technical (tool related, applications) skill requirements for each role
  • A list of generic roles that will be required
Brief Outline

This work product will generally be comprised of several components including:

  • A document defining the scope of the process evaluation (organized according to in scope IT management services).
  • process requirements list.
  • A document defining the scope of the tools evaluation (organized according to sub-processes and managed IT elements).
  • Tool Requirements List.
  • Generic Tools List.
  • A document defining the scope of the data evaluation (organized according to sub-processes and managed IT elements).
  • List of input and output data mapped to sub-process activities. This will either be in the form of a Business Process Model (BPM) or a Requirements Collection Form.
  • A document defining the scope of the organization evaluation (organized according to sub-processes and managed IT-elements).
  • Skill Requirements List.
  • Generic Roles List.

These documents will generally be in the form of a lists or matrixes.

Key Considerations

Experience has shown that all discussions concerning "tools" in one way or another tend to be very subjective and sometimes even emotional. It is advisable to define tool requirements and generic tools first. This will provide the foundation for the evaluation of the current tool environment, the identification of gaps and the tool selection in later phases of the engagement.

Note: The term "tool" in this work product denotes any technology that will be used in the management of the target system. Most tools are applications specifically designed for systems management; however tools may also be a built-in feature of the target system (e.g., Telnet for remote access, native SNMP agents).

Likewise, discussions concerning "roles" often can become political. It is crucial to define skill requirements and generic roles first. This will provide the foundation for mapping the generic roles against the current organization to identify gaps.

The collection of data requirements is considered a critical success factor for downstream engagement activities. Nevertheless, it is not advisable to put too much effort into the details. It is sufficient only to collect data groupings e.g. "Employee Information" instead of "surname, first name, telephone #, personal identifier, date of birth". During the implementation these data categories will then be detailed and again reviewed with the client.

In order to successfully complete this task it is necessary to,

  • Structure the work. Thought should be given to the type of expertise required to cover specific sub-processes or managed IT elements.
  • Quickly review each sub-process prior to capturing the requirements to remind team members of the definition, scope and objectives of the sub-process in question.
  • Use the templates as starting points to promote discussion. Capture the requirements that are independent of the elements for each sub-process. The process teams should attempt to prioritize the requirements as they are collected (i.e. High, Medium, or Low Priority). This will be of great help to the design team in attempting to satisfy specific requirements on tool mapping and selection.
  • Divide the work and assign responsibility to process team members. Subject Matter Experts (SME) are useful here to ensure the capture of specific detailed technical requirements that might be otherwise overlooked by the teams due to lack of detailed technical. Client process team members typically show reluctance to complete the work in this way; however, due to the amount of data that must be captured, it should be stressed that this approach will be required in order to stay on schedule.
  • Begin with a quality set of template requirements.
  • Maintain the process team focus.
  • Ensure that requirements are collected with as much detail as possible.
  • Document requirements as they are collected.
  • Ensure that process teams include in their work only those requirements from templates that are applicable and make sense.
IT Management Requirements are validated against IT enabling characteristics and IT process definitions.
Tailoring
Impact of not having
  • Without well defined process requirements, the tool, data and organization requirements that are developed based on the sub-processes may be incompletely and/or incorrectly defined
  • Without well-defined tool requirements the client may not realize that some already installed tools may fit the requirements or that additional tools may have to be acquired.
  • Without well-defined data requirements the client may not realize that the solution design developed may not include the required data. Data availability is a key criterion for end-user acceptance of technical solutions. Poor data requirements definition can lead to insufficient data availability later on during the implementation of a project.
  • Without well-defined organization requirements the client may not realize that the existing skills within their organization may or may not fit the requirements of the IT management solution, and overlook a significant factor for future solution implementation.
Reasons for not needingThis work product is not required if IT management is outside the scope of the engagement.
