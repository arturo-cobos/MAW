Artifact: IT Infrastructure Management Model (OPS 302)
The IT Infrastructure Management Model work product is a preliminary logical model or architecture representing the basic shape of the IT management framework.
Domains: IT Management & Monitoring
Work Product Kinds: Model
Purpose

The objective of this work product is to select an appropriate model (or models) for structuring the implementation of the client's IT management organization and tool architecture. The model should be relevant to the client's IT and business environments and meet the client's requirement for management control. 

Selecting a model provides a vision that will foster consistency between future organizational design, assignment of management responsibilities and management tool selection and design. Tool and organization characteristics will vary significantly among the different management models.

This work product is used by the consultant to describe the management model alternatives and to generate client discussion as to the model that best fits their environment.

Description
Main Description

This model describes:

  • The distribution, location and scope (domains) of management control and responsibility.
  • The distribution and location of management function.
  • The flow of information and style of communication.
  • The mechanisms for governing this model and ensuring compliance, review and vitality.

A management model may be applied both to the IT management organization/governance model and to the IT management technology model. The management model is not the same as the organization structure however. Nor is it the same as the systems management tool architecture. The organization structure and tool architecture are directed by the management model and represent the way the IT management model is physically implemented.

Model selection is a key architectural decision that defines the direction and shape of the subsequent framework design. Choice of one or more models is dependent upon the client's style and philosophy, organizational aspects, and the existing IT management framework.

Brief Outline

The IT Infrastructure Management Model work product will often contain a graphical representation that is usually based on one of the following model types, but may also be a hybrid of these.

  • Enterprise Central 
  • Workgroup 
  • Distributed 
  • Enterprise Hierarchical

There may well be multiple versions or representations of the IT Infrastructure Management Model. The model may have different physical and logical views that require separate representations. Additionally there may be separate models for the distribution of process or organizational roles and responsibilities and for the distribution of management tool control and execution functions.

The IT Infrastructure Management Model may also include a matrix to show the broad distribution of management responsibilities. This will typically show primary and secondary responsibilities for ownership and execution of key management processes.

The controls necessary to ensure that the model is appropriately implemented and applied consistently will generally be documented as formally defined management processes.

Key Considerations

A management model may be applied both to the management organization/governance model and to the management technology model. The management model is not the same as the organization structure however. Nor is it the same as the systems management tool architecture. The organization structure and tool architecture are directed by the management model and represent the way the management model has been physically implemented.

The organization/governance and technology views of the management model do, however, influence one other. Therefore, before reviewing the current management model or selecting a new one, take the time to understand which view of the model you are dealing with and why.

Ordinarily the first choice should be to determine the future organizational/governance model and this choice will usually be made during the “strategy” stage of the solution life cycle. The way the client prefers to distribute management responsibilities, process execution and control should then place requirements on the design and shape of the resulting technical infrastructure, rather than vice versa.

The management model is an important element of the client's desired future state. As with the guiding principles, it describes the future management style and will directly influence the subsequent organization design and the design of the supporting management tools infrastructure. If the "to be" model is significantly different to the current model it is likely to drive one or more corresponding transition initiatives necessary to transform the required aspects of the management framework. These initiatives could be quite large or lengthy and far-reaching in their impact. Therefore it is important to consider the future management model as part of the strategy stage of the life cycle, where the broad implications of a change in management model can be appropriately handled.

Key Criteria for Selecting a Management Model

Control -Where are decisions made?

  • Single or Multiple
  • Single - A single point of control will be established for the target environment
  • Multiple - Multiple points of control will be established for the target environment

Execution - Where are management functions executed?

  • Single or Multiple
  • Single - A single point of execution will be established for the target environment
  • Multiple - Multiple points of execution will be established for the target environment

Communication - How will communication of information flow in the target environment?

  • Vertical and/or Peer-Peer
  • Vertical - Communication flows vertically from managers/management servers to clients
  • Peer-Peer - Communication flows peer-peer between managers/management servers

The selection is validated through this discussion.  Selecting a model provides a vision that will foster consistency between future organizational development and tool selection.  Technology and organization characteristics will vary significantly among the different management models.

Tailoring
Impact of not havingWithout this work product, the locations of control and execution points for IT infrastructure management will not be well defined. Future design decisions may not reflect the client's IT and business requirements.
Reasons for not needingIf the client organization is very small and simple, (for example, all of the control and execution will occur from a single point in the organization) this work product may not be needed. There may also be instances where prior work has already determined the future model, in which case the chosen model will be used as input in lieu of creating this work product.
