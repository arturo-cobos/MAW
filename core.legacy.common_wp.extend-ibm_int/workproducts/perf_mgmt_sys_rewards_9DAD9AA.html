Artifact: Performance Management Systems and Rewards (ORG 011)
Performance Management and Rewards Design provides the performance management systems to support the new organization design, new processes, and associated information technology.
Domains: Organization Design
Work Product Kinds: Specification
Purpose

To establish effective systems that will set, track, evaluate, reward, reinforce, and provide feedback on the skills, activities, and behaviors required in the new organization. Aligned, effective rewards and performance management systems are vital for a business transformation to succeed.

Performance Management:

  • Serves as a tool for continuous improvement that will ultimately improve individual and business performance
  • Empowers and enables self-management of contribution and career development
  • Emphasizes measurement, feedback, and training
  • Provides employees with clear goals

It sets conditions, encourages required contributions and behaviors, enhances and builds employee affiliation and loyalty, and encourages a more productive work environment.

Description
Main Description

This artifact:

  • Identifies measures and measurement methods to be used
  • Contains implications for a reward structure
Brief Outline
Notation

Performance Management System: an iterative process where performance is set, monitored, assessed, and linked to consequences or outcomes.

Total Rewards System: all mechanisms by which people in an organization feel rewarded or through which an organization communicates what it values.

Balanced Scorecard: The balanced scorecard approach takes the words that express strategy and goals and translates them into statements of "what we must do" and then into clear numeric key performance indicators (KPIs). KPIs are readily understood and are the basis for consistent communication, performance management, and initiatives across a large organization. The Balanced Scorecard complements financial measures with operational measures on customer satisfaction, internal processes, and the organization's innovation and improvement activities -- operational measures that drive future financial performance. It enables executives to prioritize investments and make operational decisions using proactive metrics.

Key Considerations

Performance appraisals and bonuses are sensitive issues. Therefore, anticipate resistance and be vigilant about reward systems that may run counter to your aims.

It may be necessary to dismantle measures that encourage local optimization at the expense of the rest of the organization.
Involve middle managers and supervisors early in the process and seek their buy-in. Begin by actively listening to their concerns and addressing the ones you can as quickly as possible before you ask for their support.

In many redesigns it will become less possible and less necessary to hold individuals specifically accountable for overall outcomes. The greatest gains will often come from interdependence and teamwork.

An effective Performance Management process is regularly scheduled and timely, and it is developmental. It reinforces superior performance and recognizes the achievement of goals. It corrects inferior performance and establishes performance improvements, and it ensures appropriate levels of training.

With measures, some of the indicators will be very soft, while many will not be sourced from existing systems. Some data may come from external sources. Measures should be linked to SMART objectives, and goals should cascade down the organization. A good measure:

  • Is accepted as being meaningful to the customer
  • Is part of a set of balanced metrics
  • Shows a trend, not a snapshot
  • Is economical to collect and maintain
  • Is simple, logical, and repeatable
  • Drives appropriate behavior

Once they are designed, validate the systems in a series of presentations and workshops with the interested communities:

  • HR
  • Management
  • Staff representatives
  • The executive management of the organization or division, who should be the final approving body
  • With the business in implementing the business measures through the Business Information model

The final test of success will be if the systems accomplish the client organization's goals and are accepted by employees. The best way to ensure this is to involve employees as much as possible from the start and to ensure that the results and reactions to the implementation of new performance management, measures, and rewards systems are monitored and the results fed back to amending the system if necessary.

Tailoring
Impact of not havingIf the client does not address the rewards and performance management systems to support changes to the business, processes, and organization, the changes probably will not "stick," will be ineffective, or will not achieve anticipated benefits. Tension and conflict in the business are likely, as employees at all levels will do the things for which they are rewarded and praised, not those that make the organization effective and successful.
Reasons for not needing

This work product is essential if the client is making changes to the process or organization, and it can be produced at a level to suit the nature and focus of the changes, size of the organization, and groups affected. For example, for small changes to individual jobs the work product would include only the measures and rewards for the changing jobs, rather than a design for the whole performance management system.   

It should be omitted only if there is no organization change, or if the planning and execution of the change are outside the scope of the engagement; in which case the client should be advised to ensure alignment of performance management, measures, and reward systems.
