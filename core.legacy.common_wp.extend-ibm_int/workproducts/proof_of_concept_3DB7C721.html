Artifact: Proof of Concept (BUS 313)
The Proof of Concept (PoC) work product documents the validation of the process design.
Domains: Business Process
Work Product Kinds: Model
Purpose

This work product is produced to prove and validate the process design through various simulation techniques. The simulation activity looks to:

  • Provide a facility for the redesign and integration of the business process in an iterative manner while validating the expected results. It enables users, developers, and managers to reach design decisions quickly
  • Assess the appropriateness of enabling technologies and software solutions to the process design. It provides a better understanding of the package solutions capabilities against the desired business requirements
  • Identify and evaluate the impacts of the process design to the organization (i.e. roles, resources, etc.)
  • Minimize the overall risk to the project through the validation of the process design prior to engaging in the implementation activities of the solution
  • Identify additional requirements to fill package deficiencies
  • Validate hypothesized performance levels and results (i.e. volumes, cycle time, performance metrics, etc.). Determine acceptable limits and results
  • Validate the business benefit measurements and technology costs in the business case (i.e. estimated solution costs, expected process benefits, etc.)
  • Provide better information for planning the development and deployment efforts
  • Document the issues, results, and findings of the simulation order to set and evaluate future performance levels
  • Achieve customer acceptance and buy-in
  • Reduce the risk of implementation by validating the "to be" process in an early stage
Description
Main Description

The Proof of Concept work product describes the objectives of the Proof of Concept effort, techniques used, and the results from the simulation of the new process.

This validation is a simulation, which can be a visualization, animation, role-play, prototype or use of an automated tool.

Results may identify issues and action items for refinement of the process design.

Brief Outline

The Proof of Concept is broken out into three main sections:

  • Overview: Provides the general background information, purpose, scope of effort, techniques used, tool requirements, setup details, etc. on the proof of concept.
  • Execution Scripts: Contains the proof of concept scripts, detailed execution results,
  • Presentation of conclusions and recommendations: Contains a summary of the result of the proof of concept with summarized findings from the proof of concept execution and includes improvement areas and recommended changes.

Overview section:

  • Proof of Concept: Name of the Proof of Concept(s)
  • Description of the current situation
  • Objective: A statement of goals for the PoC. This should include an indication of the identified risks that are present, and how the exercise will address those risks. The objective clearly describes what the PoC should prove e.g. 20% reduction of lead time of the in take process. Based on the objectives appropriate measurements and evaluation criteria will be set.
  • Scope: An indication of the breadth and depth of the test with respect to the process areas; the logic behind the setting of these boundaries; an assessment of the ability to extrapolate the results beyond these boundaries; the known limitations of the test.
  • Techniques: A description of the method of testing, and why it is appropriate for the POC.
  • Results: An overall description of the expected results of the POC.
  • Execution Schedule: An overall master plan and schedule for the execution of the proof of concept scripts. It includes the resources, location, times, etc. to coordinate the proof of concept.
  • Guidelines and assumptions
    Guidelines during the proof of concept, e.g. no adjustments, etc.
    Assumptions: E.g. assumptions on lead time and other key figures

Execution Scripts:

This section is best described in a table format containing the following details:

  • Proof of Concept Script Name: Name given to the individual Proof of Concept script.
  • Description: Description of the script and scope of processes covered.
  • Special Requirements/Resources: Identifies and special setup requirements, facilities, and resources for the script execution.
  • Execution Path: A listing of the execution steps or activities.
  • Expected Results: A brief description of the expected results.
  • Actual Results: Descriptive summary of the results of the script execution.
  • Impact Type: An indicator of what was impacted: Process, Organization or Technology.
  • Impact Description: Documents the issues, gaps and other considerations on the process design and requirements identified during the script execution. This includes impacts to the process design, organization roles and policies, and technology/software and may be described with a value of: high, medium, low that can indicate the degree of impact.

Key Considerations

Simple simulations, such as a paper-based walk-through of a process design, will often suffice.  Computer simulations are usually justified when the process is complex, with many events and variations coming into play defining the specific "Proof of Concept" needs for the project, consider the following:

  • What needs exist for visualization of the new business system e.g., to build organizational buy-in or support validation efforts?
  • How might computer simulation help validate the proposed technology approach? Resolve performance uncertainties?
  • The value versus the cost/time investment required of creating a scale model prototype of the new business system to enable demonstration, study or testing

Whichever method is used to prove the concept, there is likely to be feedback on potential improvements to the process. These should be incorporated and if necessary the Proof of Concept has to be run again.

In rare cases, it may happen that the stakeholders are not satisfied with the future process concept (i.e. the concept is not proven).  If this happens, it may be necessary to go 'back to the drawing board' with the process.  This may require revisiting the business objectives, the process design principles and objectives, the selected process enablers and the design of the business system (process, organization and technology).

Advice and guidance can be found in the simulation game reference guide.

It makes sense to involve stakeholders and to ask them to devise potential disruptions to the normal process that can be evaluated in the simulation.
Tailoring
Impact of not having

If this work product is not produced, then:

  • Issues with the new process design may not be uncovered or corrected before the new process is implemented
  • Expected benefits of the process design and enabling solutions will not be validated
  • Expected performance levels will not be confirmed and refined
  • Customer buy-in to the process design may not be achieved
  • Appropriateness of the supporting technologies and software solution options that are being considered in the process design will not be confirmed
  • The risk of implementation of the processes is significantly higher
Reasons for not needing

This work product should not be produced when:

  • The process design has low risk or minimal impact to the organization (non-critical)
  • Process changes are limited and consequences can be easily understood
  • Validation of the business case is not required
  • Performance levels and measurements are not issues
  • Client does not feel that performing a simulation would add value to the visualization or understanding of the process design
  • Sufficient functionality exists that will satisfy most of the business solution
  • The customer is willing to accept a high risk in the implementation of the processes
