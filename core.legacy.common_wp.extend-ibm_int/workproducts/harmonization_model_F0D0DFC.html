Artifact: Harmonization Model (BUS 052)
The Harmonization Model is a description of how the organization will look in the future.
Domains: Business Process
Work Product Kinds: Model
Purpose
The purpose of this document is to ensure that company process and procedures are implemented on a consistent basis across the organization. It will provide the framework for aligning local procedures to overall corporate goals and provide the foundation for the ultimate configuration of the software solution.
Description
Main DescriptionThe description of the Harmonization Model covers the processes - i.e., what is going to be computerized, finance, materials management etc. the systems - what systems will be in place and the interfaces between them, the structure - shared services and the job roles - the activities that people will be involved in which will support the structure.
 
This work product describes the issues that should be considered when designing a harmonized corporate model on a multi-site project. The output of this work product is a document that shows what can be harmonized across the sites including processes, systems, structure and job roles.
  • It is done on a corporate wide basis. There is not a Harmonization Model for each site.
  • It follows the development of harmonization principles and the description of the business architecture. Corporate "as-is" information as well as an assessment of the definition of common corporate procedure supports the model as well.
The content of this work product is centered on a matrix where the areas relevant for harmonization are represented.
 
The model is once again designed on the basis of top down approach. It is designed to deliver the corporate business needs, and it is not an aggregation of the needs of each of the sites.
Brief Outline

The multi-site Harmonization Model document will consist of four major sections: Business Process harmonization, Systems harmonization, Organization Structure harmonization and Job Roles.  Each section may use a different form of notation to effectively communicate the model.

Business Process multi-site harmonization

A typical notation for the future business processes is a Process Hierarchy diagram showing the Level 1(Business Process Area), level 2(Sub-process), level 3 (Procedural or Action) and level 4(Transactional or work step) processes.  This process hierarchy would include a composite of all required future business processes.  Notation in the harmonization model would indicate which processes apply to which locations (corporate, divisions, regions, geography, country, site, etc.) For example, if shared services for Finance is part of the future design, then certain financial processes would be required only at the corporate level. 

Systems multi-site harmonization

This notation builds upon the Future I/T Infrastructure work product and incorporates any changes to the future system architecture as a result of the Business Process harmonization model.  Changes are typically driven by local legacy systems interfaces, 3rd party packages, that support local unique requirements, etc.

Organization structure multi-site harmonization

The notation for Organization structure could be a Visio ™ diagram(s) showing the organizational and reporting hierarchy.  For example, level 1 in the hierarchy could be Corporate, level 2 = division, level 3 = region, level 4 = country.  Regions and countries may have representation in more than one division, therefore a notation that color codes regional and country linkages to division may be appropriate.

Key Considerations

The Harmonization Model is the vision of what the company will look like in the future.  This will define the structure, in terms of the package, i.e., shared services center, the systems, the interfaces and the databases.

When collating the above information it will be necessary to note the following points.

  • Harmonization principles and future business architecture design considerations:
    • The related Core Competence's will impact which skills and processes are performed within the company and which skills and processes should be acquired with partners.
    • The management and financial principles required to support the business scope will determine degree of centralization and local autonomy that will have an impact on the support processes.
    • The company culture and change ability will be factors to consider when a decision for harmonization is taken.
