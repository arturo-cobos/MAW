Artifact: Content Assessment (BUS 336)
The Content Assessment establishes a baseline understanding of the elements of a content-based work effort, the customer's current status for each of those elements, and correlates the client's customers' content "needs and wants" (i.e., customer perceived content value) to the business needs and directly measureable business results.
Domains: Knowledge Management
Work Product Kinds: Assessment
Purpose
To identify, define and document and assess the baseline "as-is" information required to scope and size all subsequent work efforts, and to establish the success criteria by which this and all subsequent content work efforts will be evaluated. It is the basis for the core customer expectations (and satisfaction), and clearly outlines the expected measurable results, both for the client and the client’s customers. It has two primary phases:
  1. Information gathering; and
  2. Analysis and Assessment.
It addresses 4 primary areas of review, although the depth of assessment and granularity of the review may vary. Those 4 primary areas are:
  1. Content Value, as determined by business need and customer perception, and defined in terms of specific, measurement points that can be directly correlated to business results;
  2. Content (Information) Architecture, as determined by the current information publication hierarchy or classification structure(s) and the defined presentation "layout map", standards, formats, and associated style definitions which combine to provide the logical view(s) of content at the site, page, and content level;
  3. Content Availability, as determined by identification of content sources, formats, owners, strategic repository plans, content elements, and access requirements, and which is the basis for the physical management of the content.
  4. Content Processes, as determined by the current processes to select, assign, create/acquire, publish, measure and maintain content, as well as the operations processes (See the "Content Management Plan Work Product Description" for a complete discussion of the process).
Description
Brief Outline

A Content Assessment includes all or parts the following components:

  • Client Customer Segmentation, content correlation, correlated Metric Plan Summary - Who are the customers? What are their purposes and characteristics? Which customer sets or types are strategic? What content or information is needed to make them successful? In what formats? What are the specific attributes of content that have value (e.g., timeliness, accuracy, customer specificity (personalization), access (search), etc.)? Which of those purposes drive specific business objectives? What will be measured, against what specific targets to demonstrate that the desired results are being achieved? This may be laid out in a series of matrices and/or charts, with accompanying customer/content need profiles and recommendations.
  • Information Architecture Summary - How is content currently organized, and is that structure adequate to support the needs identified by the previous customer content needs analysis? How is the content currently classified? Is there a common vocabulary across the business units? Are terms cross-referenced in indices and/or thesauri? What are the specific content elements? Are they fully defined as entities, with defined descriptive attributes and relationships? Are there established formats and styles for presentation of specific types of content? Are these used consistently and effectively?
  • Database / Content Repository Summary – What content is currently available (Data Dictionary information)? Where is it, and in what format? Who owns it? Is it accessible? How frequently is it updated? At what rate is the volume of content growing? What are the access performance considerations? Does its presentation vary based on classification, external configuration, or contractual relationship (e.g., product campaign or special promotion vs. product information, customer profiling, customer paid subscription/entitlement)?
  • Process Review Summary – summarizes the processes that are established (defined, documented, implemented and managed) to support the business objectives. This includes identification of roles, responsibilities, policies, procedures, guidelines, and regulations or business rules that will govern the delivery of content.
  • Readiness Summary – summarizes the assessment and analysis of the current environment, and identifies any core enabling or ramp-up activities that must occur to support the engagement objectives. These issues and inhibitors directly affect the schedule of follow-on activities, skills and resources required, and business case for the effort. It may identify key tactical efforts that should or must be executed as pre-requisites. This should include a summary of any recent site customer surveys or studies that reflect on any element of content effectiveness or any elements which directly relate to the quality, navigation, organization or depth of the content.

The Readiness Summary should include summary recommendations, with an associated project plan, resource plan and schedule estimates to implement those recommendations. Optionally, possible available resources (internal and external) may be identified at this time.

Key Considerations

Thought:  Web automation of a current business process is NOT business transformation. 

The single largest impediment to successful completion of a Content Assessment is a tendency to “boil the ocean.”  Where new areas are identified, they should be captured, but listed separately for future review.

These “emerging and ancillary issues” may generate new discussions and additional assessments, and presenting them in summary format as an addendum to the Readiness Assessment Review (as Emerging and Ancillary Issues) is recommended.  If the list is extensive, the Readiness Assessment Review may recommend that the plan be reviewed in context of the entire list for prioritization by the sponsoring executive.  However, the focus must be maintained on the scope as defined, based on the sponsor’s business objectives and purpose.

There is a constant and ongoing tension between “Webspeed” and “completeness.”  Management of this tension must be keyed and constantly calibrated against the business purpose that is identified “up front.”  If the activity, question, content, application, issue, etc. is not absolutely vital to execution of the business purpose, then it should be noted and deferred to the “Emerging and Ancillary Issues” list.

These items should be treated as business opportunities!  This list provides the primary means to expand and enhance the engagement.  It should be reviewed with the overall Project Executive regularly, who may choose to escalate the issue(s) separately with the sponsoring executive.  Examples include: Site Performance benchmarking, proof-of-concept site delivery, usability studies, disaster recovery, etc.

It is the responsibility of the consultant leading this effort to forward these opportunities appropriately, and in a timely fashion.  Additionally, new or inexperienced consultants should hold regular internal team review checkpoints with their team and leadership, to discuss the progress and identify new opportunities.  In a 4-week engagement, this should happen at least twice: at midpoint, and at the end of the effort.

These discussions may not only serve to clarify opportunities, but also risks.  They may also be educational to the team on the capabilities of IBM services and its partners.

Tailoring
Impact of not having

Without this work product, the client will not be able to maintain a clear line-of-sight between their investment in content and content delivery and business results. It may result in a lack of focus on pre-requisite enabling activities, and unnecessarily increase the expenditure of time and resources on activity and content that do not directly correlate to the "critical path" business objectives.   

Resulting implementations may lack consistency in presentation, format, purpose and underlying design and style standards, which directly (negatively) impact a high-quality consistency in content presentation "look and feel" and customer satisfaction and "comfort" with the site. These symptoms may already be present, and reflected in existing site studies or surveys.   

Investments in "reactive" implementations may be made without proper underlying logical and physical infrastructures, which results in slower implementations, higher than necessary maintenance costs, slower growth and expansion, and eventually, costly re-work. It will impede the client’s ability to transform existing e-business static publishing environments to dynamic, transaction - supporting environments.

Reasons for not needingThis work product will not be required if the customer can provide:
  1. Documented customer segmentation analyses, correlated and clearly prioritized definitions of customer-validated content requirements, with associated measurement points and correlated downstream measurable results.
  2. Documentation of their existing (logical) information architecture and defined common vocabulary. This includes current classification of their content at the site, page and content element levels, including core indices, cross-references, and thesauri, as well as defined styles, templates and standards.
  3. Documentation of their existing (physical) data structures (data dictionaries), sources and processes for accessing that information.
  4. Documentation of the governing processes is available, and has undergone a "transformation review" that optimizes those process for the web environment (rather than simply automating a pre-existing manual process).
  5. Current studies indicate that the site effectiveness (results and customer satisfaction) is high against designed objectives.
