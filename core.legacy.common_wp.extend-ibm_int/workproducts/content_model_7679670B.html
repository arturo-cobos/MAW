Artifact: Content Model (BUS 332)
The Content Model Work product defines three core elements of content — requirements, information architecture and design and delivery mapping.
Domains: Knowledge Management
Work Product Kinds: Model
Purpose

The purpose of the Content Model is three-fold:

  1. It defines customer-task driven bases for content identification and selection, and establishes boundaries that will enable organizations to focus (only) on that content which is "necessary and sufficient" to direct business objectives and results. Additionally, this focus and bounding enables organizations to deliver the selected content at accelerated "web-speed."
  2. It establishes a flexible underlying foundation, via the Information Architecture, to:
    • Enable task and results targeted static web publishing
    • Support the transformation to dynamic, transaction e-enabled publishing environments 
    • Leverage the value of content via definition of a framework that supports the extended delivery of content via alternate and/or complementary channels, such as print, broadcast, or other media (e.g., CD’s, Video’s, consumer products) 
    • Provide a framework that supports development and deployment of global functional requirements (e.g., search, personalization, language translation, print services, download, etc.
  4. It transforms the presentation of content from the previous traditional delivery format(s), to formats that are optimized for the user characteristics, tasks, media (web), usability, effectiveness (usability, customer satisfaction) and business objectives (measurable business results).
Description
Main DescriptionThe three core elements of content are:
  1. Content Requirements, which defines and correlates the audience tasks, wants and needs to clearly defined business objectives, and then identifies actual content required to meet both the audience and business needs;
  2. Content Information Architecture, which defines the actual content elements or entities, their attributes, their relationships, their primary and secondary classifications and indexing, the resulting navigational components, and a common vocabulary. In addition, Information Architecture may define common functional requirements that interact across the content, such as search, personalization, printing, downloading, etc. This may also be called the "Content System View," or the "Logical Content Model."
  3. Content Design and Delivery mapping, fully defines the content delivery "look and feel" and establishes (identify and define) the base delivery design structures, templates, and the explicit placement of content elements within that framework. The design provides the critical linkage between the Content Requirements and the Content Information Architecture, and renders the purpose and value of the content delivery visible, measurable and actionable.

Key Considerations

The Content Model has two co-requisites for actual implementation. Those are a Content Management Plan, and a Content Sourcing Plan.  Without those (or their equivalents), the Content Model alone will not have any direct functional value. The customer must be able to (a) provide the content; and (b) manage the content as defined in the Content Model.

In the case where a site already exists, it may be appropriate to create an AS-IS ‘user view’ hierarchy and set of maps. The user tasks should be reviewed against the AS-IS model. Often, updates with regrouping, consolidation of content, deletion of unused content will provide the correct ‘user views’ for the Content Model.

Very often, however, with sites that have evolved over time, placing information without regard to the user requirements will require starting the Content Model from scratch. Ensuring that the Content Model is user focused versus internal organization, existing database structure, etc. focused will lead to the development of a more successful site.

Tailoring
Impact of not having

Often companies want to start their strategic site planning with an assessment and installation of technologies without regard to user requirements or an understanding of what is required to manage the content.

The Content Model establishes a solid foundation to identify and select the valued content and tools to design, build and maintain site that meets both the users and business objectives. Without the ‘user view’ in this work product, the site runs the high risk of containing content that does not meet the user’s needs, is redundant, is impossible to locate, and/or is placed on the site because it is available, not because it is required.

The Content Information Architecture of the Content Model must be developed to ensure that all critical information about the content is captured and supports accurate development, publication and maintenance of the content. Without this framework, transformation to a dynamic publishing environment, business transformation of processes via the web, best-of-breed delivery practices (e.g., personalized sites) may be severely slowed, if they are possible at all. The costs, effort and time for delivery of content via alternate and/or complementary channels will increase, and the overall speed and responsiveness will slow.

Finally, there is a risk that the design of the web site may be "accidental" or "inherited" from different delivery media that are not designed for effectiveness for the specific targeted audience(s), their characteristics, their tasks and/or the web. This results in a loss of web traffic, poor business results, low customer satisfaction, etc.
