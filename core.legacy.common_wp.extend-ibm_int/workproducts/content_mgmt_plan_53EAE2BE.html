Artifact: Content Management Plan (BUS 335)
The Content Management Plan establishes (identifies, defines, and documents) the sets of processes, organizational structures and technologies which are required to manage content across the content life cycle in support of business objectives.
Domains: Knowledge Management
Work Product Kinds: Plan
Purpose

To establish (identify, define, and document) the sets of processes, organizational structures and technologies required to support a dynamic, transaction-capable Web content publishing environment. Specifically,

  • Processes - include management processes, publishing workflow processes (core content management), and operational delivery processes
  • Organizational Structures - include policies, guidelines, personnel, roles, responsibilities, supporting education
  • Technologies - include information and data architectures, application architecture interfaces, tools and technical infrastructure(s)
Description
Main Description

The Content Management Plan includes all elements that are critical to a business in order deliver accurate, high-impact, timely, and consistent content to their Web channels. This content management plan must encompass navigational, informational, transactional or technical content, and delivery of the content as well as a closed loop measurement and analysis plan that drives iterative and ongoing improvement.

Core to the Content Management Plan is the linkage between the plan and specific, measurable, actionable business results.

Brief Outline

An end-to-end Content Management Plan addresses three primary areas: Processes, Organizational Structures, and Technology (see Content Management Plan Detailed Outline for more details).

Key Considerations

Content for the web site may be pulled from existing processes. Ensure that processes and requirements are in place to provide the content in a style using verbiage that is acceptable to the Web and the audience.

Often each source will have a different process for the creation and approval of the content. Document all of these and determine where processes can be standardized and simplified.

Beware of semantic confusion. Multiple groups may sink into an adversarial stance without realizing that the differences are in definitions and role assignments/staffing, not content or processes.

Develop a glossary and common vocabulary.

The process should also address content encoding, compression, encryption, national language translation and transmission where necessary.

In both the process and policy arena, ensure that user management, relative to rights and permissions, is addressed. Verify any internal audit procedures that may apply to any published content, and review the processes to assure that the audit points are addressed and an acceptable audit trail is maintained.

When creating an initial repository development of a user/task driven information architecture is key. This includes the definition of the content entities, the attributes, the relationships, if and how content can be broken into smaller data elements, and how multiple sites and pages can reuse it. It also drives style library definitions, and must be linked to the organization’s branding and/or image plan.

Where content resides in multiple repositories and is in multiple (legacy formats), a convergence plan must be developed to a) identify only the “necessary and sufficient” entities; b) migrate them to a common repository. This requires that the “strategic” or destination repository(ies) be clearly identified by management, and the owners of “tactical” and “legacy” repositories support the convergence.

The plan should factor in the requirement to separate the actual content from the format and/or layout, the “look and feel” (template) of the site. Templates may be required at multiple levels: e.g. Site, Masthead/Nav Bar/Footer; Page, Section, Sub-section, content element. This allows for easier updates and simplified reuse of the content, but requires information architecture to establish the user-driven enabling design points, and maintain consistency.

Content may be sourced from one location, but may be used in multiple places. Ensure that this content is being published from the same location to ensure consistency and quality. This includes verifying that change control and maintenance procedures adequately support update of any downstream, sibling, duplicate, replicate or progeny pages.

Verify that all approvers are identified and that their roles and participation are agreed to (e.g. legal, communications, brand management, etc.).

Content Management tools are evolving at a very rapid rate. Check the latest offerings to determine current best of breed and its applicability.

Tailoring
Impact of not having

Without this work product, the client jeopardizes their ability to meet the business objectives set for their-e-environment, and runs the risk of publishing inappropriate content, leaving content on the site beyond its usefulness, creating and editing content multiple times, having redundant personnel and skill sets, or inadequate available personnel skills, etc. Inefficiencies faced by ad-hoc, non-automated sites lengthen lead times, increase costs, and negatively impact the market perception of the client.

The inability of these sites to process content in multiple formats discourages potential content partners, and inhibits clients’ ability to cost-effectively complete any business transformation from a static publishing environment to a robust, high-volume, transaction capable dynamic web e-business environment.

Growth in a manual, poorly end-to-end managed web environment is difficult at best and maintenance costs for systems that lack automation, process, or strategic information and data architectures increase at a geometric rate. In addition, the risk of re-work costs and delayed new implementations increases dramatically if technologies and tools are not reviewed, selected and implemented with consistency, standards and guidelines.

Lack of attention to the operational infrastructure can result in poor performance, "broken" sites/links, lost customers and revenues, and poor customer satisfaction. Without a closed loop assessment process, a direct correlation between web delivery and attainment of business results cannot be executed, and no direct cost to value effectiveness ratio can be managed.

Reasons for not needingThis work product will not be required if a client has already established effective Content Management processes, organizational structures and technologies to support their web objectives, or if the customer has a successful and operational total environment and does not wish to extend or transform their current e-environment into a dynamic publishing or transactional model.
