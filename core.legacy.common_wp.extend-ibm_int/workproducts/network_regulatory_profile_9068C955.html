Artifact: Network Regulatory Profile (ARC 314)
The Network Regulatory Profile contains information regarding wide area telecommunications services in countries where a client requires a networking presence.
Domains: Networking
Work Product Kinds: Assessment
Purpose

Understanding the state of wide area telecommunications services in each country leads to design recommendations with a realistic expectation of successful implementation. Network Regulatory Profiles help to identify and describe services from which the network designer can select in designing the network.   

For a given country, the design is not valid if the client cannot implement it due to restrictions or limitations in that country.

Description
Main DescriptionThe Network Regulatory Profile contains information regarding wide area telecommunications services in countries where a client requires a networking presence. The regulatory environment for each country is described and the major market players are listed. For each required service, availability, cost, lead times and quality are profiled. The Network Regulatory Profile provides a snapshot of wide area telecommunications services available in the client’s geography.

Key Considerations

Try to select the services and characteristics to research you believe will be significant in your recommendations. You may find you need to research additional services or characteristics after initial data collection but before your recommendations are finalized. Remember that you want to make recommendations that can be implemented! If the requirement is to provide a service that is not available in a given country, recommending it will not assist the client in meeting business objectives.

Some typical research techniques are document review, online searching and e-mail or telephone inquiries. Research resources include commercial data providers, international telecommunications organizations, carriers, alternative telecommunication providers, market research firms and trade publications.

One commercial data provider, Lynx Technologies, collects and disseminates national and international telecommunications tariff information for over 350 carriers in more than 200 countries. Subscriptions to Lynx Technologies database can be as short as three months. In addition, the Lynx Technologies homepage provides links to other sources of related information on carriers, global alliances, regulatory sites, satellite sources and international organizations.

The International Telecommunications Union (ITU) is an international organization that provides a vehicle for governments and the private sector to coordinate telecommunications networks and services worldwide. This organization also publishes background information and statistics on member countries. The ITU is part of the United Nations. The ITU homepage is http://www.itu.int.

Many governments use the Internet to disseminate information about their countries including telecommunications information. Two approaches to find government information are (1) using diplomatic links and (2) locating country information through hierarchical country lists. For comprehensive research, a combination of these approaches can be used.

A diplomatic mission may keep a list of government Internet sites for its country or serve as a gateway to information from the country. In either case, finding a link to a Ministry of Foreign Affairs may provide a URL that ultimately leads to the telecommunications information you require. Web homepages with lists of embassies and diplomatic missions include:

The Embassy Page - http://www.embpage.org

Embassy World - http://www.embassyworld.com/embassy/directory.htm

Permanent Missions to the United Nations -- New York - http://www.undp.org/missions

Permanent Missions to the United Nations -- Geneva - http://www.itu.int/OTHER/diplomatic/index.html

Another approach to locating information about a country is using an Internet site that lists information geographically. Keep in mind that you will find a mix of government and non-government URLs but you probably will find something to start with for any country. Two useful lists are:

Worldwide Governments on the WWW - http://www.gksoft.com/govt

Yahoo - Country Links Section – http://dir.yahoo.com/Government/Countries

Searching by technical topic may uncover information you need. For instance, the ADSL Forum (http://www.adsl.com) homepage includes information on ADSL service providers throughout the world.
Market research firms also examine the worldwide telecommunications infrastructure and related services. In particular, Datapro offers a global networking service that looks at the commercial and regulatory telecommunications environment in countries and regions around the world. A list of Datapro global networking reports is located at http://www.gartnerweb.com/public/static/datapro/toc/tocgns.html.

You may be able to borrow market research reports from IBM’s Marketing Analysis Information Center (MAIC) in White Plains, NY. If the MAIC has a second copy of a report, you can sometimes arrange to borrow it for a brief period of time. You can also visit the MAIC to use their collection.

The MAIC collection is indexed in the IBM Technical Information Retrieval Center’s (ITIRC) MDOC database. Selected market research reports are included, in full text, in ITIRC’s COMP database. To access the ITIRC databases use http://w3.itirc.ibm.com. COMP access is IBM Confidential. Access via ITIRC requires management approval. COMP is also available to registered users of ViewBlue.

You can search ITIRC’s other business and engineering databases for magazine articles. Some of the articles are reproduced in entirety. ITIRC also offers a fulfillment service for magazine articles. IBM libraries and university libraries are other good sources of magazine articles.It is a good idea to consider if IBM is a member or has a business relationship with an organization that may have information you need. For instance, IBM has a sector representative to the ITU. Sometimes, IBM’s organizational representatives can be very helpful in pinpointing specific information or referring you to sources. Also, if IBM has a business relationship with a marketing research firm, you may be able to talk to an analyst or receive additional information or reports.

For critical information, you may want to ask a networking trained individual from IBM in the target country to review and comment on your findings.

If you are using data collected more than three months in the past, check details on any critical wide area network services for currency.

Tailoring
Impact of not havingDesigning a network without knowing that the wide area services specified in the design are available creates risks that the recommendations that cannot be successfully implemented. For instance, if the client has short timeframes to implement and private circuits take a year or more to obtain, a design calling for private circuits may not be feasible.
Reasons for not needingA Network Regulatory Profile is not needed if the engagement does not include wide area network aspects. Also, a new Network Regulatory Profile may not be required if a recent (three months or less) Network Regulatory Profile for a country is available. In this case, details on any critical wide area network services should be verified for currency.
