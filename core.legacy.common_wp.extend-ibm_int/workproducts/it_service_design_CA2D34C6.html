Artifact: IT Service Design (OPS 305)
An IT service is a specific IT function or output, performed on behalf of or in support of a customer, that provides measurable value.
Domains: IT Management & Monitoring
Work Product Kinds: Specification
Purpose

This work product is the result of high-level design activities for an IT service. It ties together the process, organization and technology pieces of the solution that will provide the IT service.   

This work product is extremely valuable in communicating the solution design to the client. It illustrates all of the activities that must be performed to delivery an IT service, and shows how the roles, tools, and data support the execution of the activities. It can be used to test various client scenarios to ensure that the design meets the client’s needs.

Description
Main Description

Services are a basis for doing business with the customer (consumer of IT services), require an understanding of their needs, and are delivered through a series of implemented activities.   

An IT service brings together elements of process, organization and technology. The detailed service definitions show:

  • The activities (or sub-processes), from one or more processes, that are executed in delivering the service;
  • The service roles or participants responsible for executing those activities;
  • The tools or information technology used to automate the execution of activities and deliver their results.

From the customer’s perspective, the service definitions provide the service customer with the basis for describing the results they expect and the levels of performance they require. From the corresponding service provider’s perspective, they provide the basis for detailing the conditions under which the service will be provided and the characteristics of the enabling IT processes, organization and tools that need to be in place.   

The IT service design ties together the processes (actually, the sub-processes) together with the roles, tools, and data which will be used in the execution of the sub-process. Usually shown in part in a graphical format, the design also shows the flow of the activities in a time sequence.

Notation

The notation used includes both graphics and text. The flow of the service, including the roles, tools, and data used in the delivery of that service is depicted in a LOVEM (Line of Visibility Enterprise Modeler) type of flow diagram. The actual service definition (including: measurements, objectives, etc.) is described in an accompanying text document.

Service Definition - Customer View

From a customer’s perspective, the definition of a service should include:

  • Name - Description
  • Scope
    • In scope
    • Out of scope
    • Triggers (begins with)
  • Assumptions
  • Policies
  • Principles
  • Identification of responsibilities
    • Customers
    • Consumers
    • Business Owner (key individual representing the customer)
    • Contacts
  • Success Criteria
  • Levels of Delivery
    • Quality Items
    • Timing (when, how often)
    • Availability
    • Performance
    • Business Volumes
  • Deliverable/product

Service Definition - Provider view

From a customer’s perspective, the definition of a service should include:

  • Identification of responsibilities
    • Service Owner
    • Provider (organization(s)
    • Contacts
    • Support Groups
  • Objectives
  • Measurements
  • Participating processes and activities
  • Supporting services required
  • Procedure links (physical implementation level only)
  • Flows
    • Activities
    • Tasks
    • Roles
    • Timing
    • Sequence
    • Interfaces
  • Required Infrastructure
    • Tools
    • Information stores
  • Dependencies

Key Considerations
  • Develop the flow diagrams first.  Once they have been agreed upon, the text surrounding the diagrams can be developed easily. 
  • Rely extensively upon work done earlier in the engagement.  Do not invent new generic roles or generic tools at this time – this will invalidate the work done during organizational or tool design. 
  • Take careful notes when reviewing the diagrams.  The finalization of the diagrams will only come after several iterations, so it’s important to capture all the changes during each review.
Tailoring
Impact of not havingThis work product bridges the gap between high-level design and detailed design and development. Without this work product, it will be extremely difficult to implement an IT service.
Reasons for not needingThis work product is not needed if the engagement does not have an IT service focus.
