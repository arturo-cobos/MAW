Main Description
Content Sourcing Plan sample headings or table of contents: -
  • Definition of content “owner” and “user” rights and responsibilities
    • The rights, liabilities and responsibilities of both the owner and the user must be defined, regarding:
      • The specific identification, format, and characteristics of the content and the allowable uses of the content
      • Access to the content (time, scheduled unavailability, number of times to be accessed, whether the content can be moved/replicated and if so, to many times and for what specific purposes,
      • Maintenance of the content, frequency, notification to the user, lead time for change, maintenance of consistency of content naming (document/field) to avoid broken links, etc.
      • Support and response times w/ committed service levels for correction of failures associated with the content or access to the content, linked to severity of the problem and potential risk/liability. For example, representative severity and service levels are:
        • Severity 1: Catastrophic failure – the user is unable to access and provide content necessary to support core business functions, e.g., the web customer is unable to complete a “Buy” transaction because the pricing repository is not available; or, a file available for download is infected with a virus.
        • Severity 2: Serious failure – the user is unable to access and provide content to support business functions as designed, although core functions can be completed, e.g., the web customer can complete a “Buy” transaction, but is unable to see/print an order confirmation screen and/or tracking number because the order repository is not accessible;
        • Severity 3: Operational failure – the user is unable to access and provide content to support business functions as designed, although all core and support functions can be completed, e.g., the web customer can browse a catalog, search, buy, etc., but cannot access the product thumbnail or full-size photos of the items.
        • Severity 4: Emerging failure – the user has received reports of failures, but is unable to replicate it at this time. These failures should be tracked and analyzed on an ongoing basis to identify patterns and assist in problem determination.
For example, a foreseeable “Severity 1” issue/risk is that a file intended for download could become infected with a virus. In that circumstance, the following will be defined within the Terms and Conditions, in advance:
    • The owner is responsible for providing virus free source files. They are required to execute xyz virus scan on all new files prior to release, and are required to update a current virus “siglist” on a weekly (daily, monthly, etc.) basis.
    • Additionally, they must run a virus scan (updated as above) on each working repository on a weekly (daily, monthly, etc) basis.
    • The user is responsible for notifying the owner of any virus-infected file within 30 minutes of identification of the infection. This includes escalation and requires confirmed receipt of the notification from the owner.
    • The owner is responsible for removing any infected files within 30 minutes of notification by the user.
    • The user is responsible for identifying and notifying any customer who accessed or downloaded the infected content in the interval.
    • Neither the owner nor the user will be liable for any resulting legal actions, penalties, etc., provided that a) the owner can demonstrate that the scan procedures were followed; and b) the user has appropriately posted Limitations of Liabilities on the download site AND requires each customer to accept this limitation of liability as a transaction prior to download.
  • Internal Content Catalog (i.e. Data Dictionary, Glossary and/or Common Content Index)
    • Content Type (Video, Audio, Image, Site)
    • Content Characteristics (Volatility, Security, Attribution needs, Content Providers Policies & Guidelines for Display)
    • Content relationships (permissions, entitlements, pre-requisites, etc.)
    • Content Format (XML, MPEG, JPEG)
  • Content Production Style, Standards and Template Repositories
    • Content format descriptions and usage guidelines (i.e., News Release format, Product Information format, News Brief format, catalog format, etc.)
    • Associated Style reference files (DTD’s, Style libraries, etc.)
    • Defined standards for content formats and templates
    • Templates of content formats (for use by internal and/or external content sources)
  • Content maintenance and change control plan
    • Identification of change/maintenance cycles, manual and automatic change triggers
    • Modification to content requirements
    • Change testing, staging and production processes and cycle times
    • Service level commitments (availability of content 24/7, with live support, for example)
    • Content problem reporting, with committed response times based on severity
  • Content issue and mitigation plan
    • Identification, reporting and communication of emerging content issues (for example, regular content repository maintenance or “scheduled downtime” which occurs at 3 a.m. MST on Mondays results in unavailability to content at start of the business day/week in European marketplace)
    • Established escalation plan, based on risk assessment and severity of issue
    • Regular reviews of resolved issues to generate mitigation plans which enable sites to “anticipate and avoid” reoccurrences, rather than “identify and resolve” repetitively.
  • External Content Catalog (i.e. Data Dictionary, Glossary and /or Common Content Index)
    • Content Type (Video, Audio, Image, Site)
    • Content Characteristics (Volatility, Security, Attribution needs, Content Providers Policies & Guidelines for Display)
    • Content relationships (permissions, entitlements, pre-requisites, etc.)
    • Content Format (XML, MPEG, JPEG)
  • Content Sourcing Requirements and Issues
    • Rights management, including copyright
    • Integrated Content Management System (s) to address, optionally:
      • Definition of governing or managing processes associated with identification, selection, acquisition, delivering and/or payment for content
      • Ongoing daily workflow publishing processes (create, review, approve, maintain, test, distribute content, etc.) Note: this is the most commonly understood definition of “Content Management.” The discipline is emerging and evolving, and requires careful clarification in each environment.
      • Execution of operational technical processes (as defined as part of the management processes): physical access; transfer or replication of content (usually for performance reasons); testing, staging, “promotion” and publication processes; measurement and analysis, etc.
      • Maintenance of the access, delivery and distribution technical infrastructure (capacity, growth (in content and/or access volumes or “hits”), performance, etc.)
    • Re-distribution
    • Transcoding of material
    • Indemnification
    • Trademark and Copyright usage
    • Use of External Logo
    • Attribution
    • Embargo
    • Jurisdiction
    • Privacy
    • Security
    • Entitlement and Permissions/Permitted uses
    • Compensation
      • Flat fee
      • Percentage of subscription revenue
      • Percentage of transaction revenue
      • Percentage of advertising revenue
    • Liability
