  • Information Architecture Summary - Note: this is an assessment of the current information architecture, which may or may not be structured in an effective manner for e-Business and transformation. For example, current information might be categorized by product, where the e-business need might require categorization by specific audience. In some cases, the finding may be that the Information Architecture is “accidental,” as opposed to designed. If so, and particularly if there are no surveys or studies available to assess current site effectiveness, then the Information Architecture should be evaluated.
Information architecture is not new, but is emerging as a web-specific discipline. Quite simply, it focuses organizing information so users can satisfy their information or content needs. In many cases, existing “Information Architectures” are either “accidental” or “legacy architectures. The classification, indexing, etc. may have been assigned simply because that was how the information was presented or identified; or, it may be a legacy from the previous publishing format, e.g., the indices on the web map directly to a previous hard-copy publication’s indices as “Back Matter.” (“Back Matter” is information routinely found in the back of publications – glossaries, indices, cross-references, etc.)
The emerging Information Architecture discipline encompasses investigation, analysis, design and implementation, and may be approached from the “Bottom-up” (which is based on a deep understanding of available content, its attributes, relationships and uses); or from the “Top-Down” (which is based on the context in which a user requires the content.) In general, this methodology is a “Top-Down” approach; however, this content assessment is critical to establish that there is either: a) an adequate ‘Bottom-up” foundation of Information Architecture to support and sustain our efforts or b) resources and skills available to create an adequate Information Architecture to support (at least) our specific efforts and enable us to achieve our objectives.
Information Architecture establishes the foundation for usable and user-acceptable systems, and is based on knowledge and methods that are not only multi-disciplinary, but are also typically beyond skills available in the average IT development organization. The disciplines that are involved include: Interaction, Task Analysis, User Cognition, Organizational and Social Impacts of Technology, User Centered Design, Usability, Education and Communication.
- Overall content topology – Identifies current content base as being oriented by “breadth,” “depth,” or some combination of the two (usually on a business unit-unit basis). The degree of content breadth and depth that is required will directly affect the skills, time, and complexity of integration and/or implementation. As a rule of thumb, integrating independently developed, extremely deep content “wells” is significantly more complex and expensive than linking shallower content “ponds or puddles.”
The depth of content is typically not consistent across the breadth of the content base; typically, some areas will have great depth, and others will have varying degrees of maturity and development. These may be a reflection of a recent merger or acquisition; differences in staffing or funding; historical business focus(es); differing business models, measurements, objectives or rules for separate business units; or, an overall planned implementation by phases/focus.
A key to driving direct, rapid business results is to establish and maintain a focus on only the high-value customer facing information initially. The challenge is that the customer may be unable to differentiate between what is “necessary and sufficient,” vs. what is “available.”
- Navigation – may also be called browsing, contextual browsing, hierarchical browsing, searching, supplemental browsing, guide, index, etc. It is a direct reflection of the underlying classification and labeling plans.
It occurs on multiple levels, and describes how a user may retrieve specific content. Current navigation methods tend to reflect existing information access patterns, but new navigational constructs will continue to emerge as our understanding of the e-environment, usability and effectiveness deepens.
  • Global navigation – is usually common at a site level, is available consistently across all progeny or sibling sites. Examples abound, usually as tabs or buttons across a site masthead.
  • Hierarchical navigation– is the path that the user is expected to follow through a site to access content, and it is highly task (and therefore results) oriented. It may alternatively be called the ‘Site directory, taxonomy, ontology.” Hierarchical navigation directly reflects content grouping and organization.
It is extremely important, if the primary navigation of a site is hierarchical (in other words, there are limited alternate paths to the content), to determine whether there was customer involvement in the design and implementation. If there has not been, then the recommendations should include user studies of the current interface/construct.
  • Contextual navigation – offers one alternate path to content, and is usually entered once a user has identified at least one piece of content of interest, either via a search, or other path. The context may be synonymous, or similar material; or, it may be complementary, or related material.
This is frequently visible on a web site as a function called “Show me more like this,” or “See also…” or “See Related…”
  • Supplemental navigation – offers alternate paths to content , and may be specialized by audience segment. These paths include Site Maps, Indexes, Tables of Content, and audience specific guides.
Some of these are analogous to supplemental navigation aids that are commonly found in printed materials (particularly textbooks). For example, chapters may begin with a visual chapter overview or outline (map); they include both a Table of Contents, and at least one “Back Matter” Index by topic or keyword; they may include a Figure list, a Summary page or section at the end of each chapter, and alternate indices in the “Back Matter,” by author, date, or some other significant grouping.
They all serve to provide alternate paths to the content that may be more efficient for the user. However, the effectiveness and user-focused web-appropriate designs for supplemental navigation aids is clearly just emerging.
- Classification and labeling – may also be called grouping , organizing, indexing, schema’s, etc. and is frequently confused with hierarchical navigation. It reflects the attributes and relationship of content elements, in a collection that is understandable by the user and which supports business needs.
Examples of classification include: alphabetical, chronological, geographical, topical, task-oriented, audience-oriented. Indexing or labeling may be automated, based on content value, or it may be manual, based on expert contextual evaluation. These methods of grouping or classification can be reflected in site navigation, although investigation of the effectiveness of these constructs, for specific types of contents, audiences and tasks in the web environment is ongoing.
Integral to successful e-environment classification is the development of a common vocabulary, consistent labeling of content, as well as indices which establish cross-references between the labels. Thesauri enable users to access content across classification and navigation constructs via definition of content relationships: hierarchical (parent-progeny), associative (e.g. related or similar), or equivalent (synonymous).
- Style definition, standards and templates – may be referred to by a wide range of labels, ranging from very technology-specific terms such as “DTD’s” to very general terms such as page, document, section, subsection, or document type, layout, structure, format, fragment, or element, etc.
Lack of a common lexicon for these associated style definitions can result in confusion, and extended discovery periods if not resolved early in the assessment period.
Style definitions, standards & templates are established externally to the actual content, and define the presentation look & feel of every content entity that is presented. These styles are merged with the content at the point of publication, and result in a consistent look & feel in delivered content.
Examples of specific formats could include News Release, News Brief, Product Information, Technical Specifications, Catalog Pages, Order Confirmation, Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ’s), Hints and Tips (HaT’s), etc.
  • Data Base / Content Repository Summary
- Overall content format topology summary – establishes the breadth and depth of the physical content environment. Significant content percentages in multiple legacy formats will directly affect content readiness assessment, and has direct implications for both resource needs and projected schedule.
If there is a significant volume or percentage of Legacy content is required (e.g., hard-coded content only accessible at a document level, such as hard-coded HTML documents in a Lotus Notes ™ database), then an entire segment of tasks and activities must be included in the Readiness Assessment project plan. This must address selection, Transcoding and/or conversion, automated vs. manual “scraping,” and indexing.
Careful investigation is recommended to identify legacy content that is “required” vs. “available” in order to correctly assess its scope and impact on overall readiness. High percentages of legacy content needs may be mitigated by phasing in availability over time, as appropriate in support of the business objectives.
The overall content format topology summary should identify:
  • Overall approximate volume of content, broken down by estimates of Strategic, Tactical, Legacy and Supporting pages or percentages.
  • The number and types of legacy content affected
  • Percentages of legacy formats, by legacy types (e.g., hard-coded HTML documents, Host or Mainframe formats, data bases by data base type, etc.)
  • Percentage available in dynamic format, with available supporting style libraries (includes standards, style sheets, templates)
- Identification of current content repositories/sources (The “Blueprint”)
  • Repository name, purpose, owner
  • Repository status (Strategic, Tactical, Legacy, Supporting)
  • Repository format (DB2, Notes, Oracle, HTML, etc.)
  • Repository size, change rate (frequency, percent), growth rate (percent, time interval)
  • Repository control (security), access and performance considerations
  • Repository indices (views) available
  • Current Review frequency (last review, next and future scheduled reviews)
- Identification of individual content repository contents (Data Dictionary)
  • Content Category/Element (example, Product Description, Catolog entry)
  • Actual field name, description, length, format (text, numeric, etc.) allowed values
  • Field required (Y/N) or restricted (referenced values:business rules)
  • Defined field attributes, indexing parameters, relationships
- Identification of Strategic repositories, technologies, tools and their status
  • Is there an agreed upon “Common Vocabulary” that is available to establish the context for this review and discussion?
The “Common Vocabulary” is developed as part of the overall Information architecture, and establishes the working definitions and terms that will enable discussions and progress to be made without the risks of semantic confusion. This is critical in the emerging web and e-environment.
For example, the following terms should all have commonly agreed upon definitions: Strategic, Legacy, Tactical, Portal, Site, Page, Section, sub-section, content element, content description, document, meta-data, format, style, template, attribute, relationship, entity, repository, data base, access, security, navigation, hierarchy, browsing, searching, profile, personalization, etc.
  • Where will the definitive versions of the content be created, stored, maintained?
For example: There will be one Customer Information (CI) data base to contain all customer specific information; there will be one Product Information (PI) data base to store all product specific information; there will be one Support Information (SI) data base to store all support specific, experiential information such as FAQ’s, Hints & Tips, Case Studies, etc.; there will be one Contract Entitlement (CE) Data Base to contain all contract information for entitlement purposes
  • What indices or constructs will be used to provide scalable access to this content?
For example, the Contract Entitlement database will be cross-referenced to the CI database via the following specific fields: Customer name, Customer number, etc. The primary database will be replicated in x locations at x intervals to provide adequate response time globally.
  • The technologies, down to a version level, should be called out.
For example: All strategic DB’s will be DB2, Ver. X, Rel., Y, running in the xyz operating environment. Content will be XML tagged, using the Style sheets as defined in x., etc.
  • Tools to be used to support these repositories should be identified down to a version level, and called out.
For example, All data dictionaries will be created and maintained using product abc, Ver. X, Rel Y, running in the xyz environment.
  • The status of these repositories should be explicitly identified.
For example: the CI data base is in production and available for access; the PI database is designed, and in test with dummy data; three repositories must be transcoded and merged into the PI database, and the schedules for each are abc.
