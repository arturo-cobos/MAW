The following has been included to show the 5 maturity rating levels for business processes and needed actions.


Level
Rating Criteria
Needed Actions

Undefined and Unmanage
Little or no process documentation exists.
Little or no measurements exist.
No process goals have been set, nor is ownership assigned.
Provide process management and re-engineering education for management & process coordinators.
Assign process ownership.
Define the processes and put goals in place.
Define and agree on a process improvement plan.
Institute performance tracking.

Defined and Monitored
All needed actions, as defined at level 1, have been accomplished.
A strategic process goal (content and scope) has been defined.
Responsibilities for the process and sub-processes have been assigned.
Critical success factors (CSFs) have been defined.
Customer and supplier relationships and performance requirements have been agreed upon.
High-level process documentation is in place. Some sub-processes have been documented.
Standards for measurements, such as key indicators and critical measurement points (CMPs), and their initial level have been established for:
Customer satisfaction
Number of errors
Cycle time
Process costs
Existing dependencies on IT have been described and data owners have been assigned.
Some operational problems exist, but the resulting weaknesses can be corrected in a reasonable amount of time.
Goals for improvement over a specified period, such as 12 months, have been set.
Establish rudimentary statistical control and predictability of plans, schedules, and costs.
Establish and agree on a plan for process re-engineering and ongoing process improvement.
Establish quality assurance plans and procedures.
Extend performance tracking.

Stable and Managed
All level-2 criteria have been fulfilled over a certain period of time (to be defined by the rating team).
The goals that were established for level 4 have been reached.
The process meets customers' expectations.
The process is effective and efficient.
No significant operational problems exist.
Goals for improvement over a certain period of time have been set.
Cost and quality are still variable but are managed.
Reasonable control over schedules and process improvement methods and procedures are in place.
Necessary I/T support has been specified and agreed to by management.
Plans for regular performance comparisons with similar processes are in place.
Develop process standards and definitions.
Assign process resources.
Establish process improvement methods and procedures.
Work towards achieving the set goals and execute the approved plans and schedules.

Competitive and Repeatable
All level-3 criteria have been fulfilled over a certain period of time (to be defined by the rating team).
The goals that were established for level 3 have been reached.
Major process improvements have been made and measurable results realized.
Goals for improvement from a customer's point of view have been set with a specific schedule in place.
Reliable process costs and improvement schedules are in place.
A quantitative plan for systems and network development and specified IT requirements is in place.
The process is improving, but there is still some unpredictable quality performance in certain areas.
Performance comparisons with other similar processes have been made, and the gaps between this process and the best of breed are quantified.
Establish quantitative process quality goals and plans.
Establish a measurement and tracking system.

Best of Breed
The goals that were established for level 4 have been reached.
Comprehensive process measurements and error prevention methods are in place.
The process surpasses all competition and is most economical.
The output from the process is viewed by customers as free of defects.
Quantitative statistical control over process quality is established.
A quantitative basis for capital investment for continuing process improvements, including automation, exists.
Continue to emphasize process measurements and error prevention.
Focus on quantitative productivity plans and tracking, including economically justified technology investments.

The lowest level of Process Work Flow

A Work Flow is the lowest level Process Flow. It represents the sequences of activities from starting event to ending event. It indicates which individual role is performing each process and shows the decision logic used to navigate the flow.
