Main Description


Management Model

1. Using information learned during the assessment workshops and client interviews, document the current IT organization structure, the distribution of IT management responsibilities and the current management tool structure.
2. Review existing management philosophies or principles. Review existing management philosophies (or guiding principles) with regard to organizational, geographical, and technical aspects. Note: many organizations will not have existing documented guiding principles but do have a unique management philosophy.
3. Evaluate potential IT management models. After gathering the information necessary for the decision process and analyzing the existing management philosophy, a future management model should be selected. Consider all necessary geographical and organizational aspects, and reflect this consideration in the management model topology. When a single systems management model will not address the needs of a design, a "hybrid" model may be necessary. This may require an iteration of steps 2 and 3.
4. Document selection of the management model.
5. Gain sponsor approval. The client sponsor must sign off the selected IT management model as it has potentially far-reaching consequences for any subsequent organizational design or management technology architectures.

Distribution of Management Responsibilities

1. Agree on the process or service framework to be used for describing responsibilities. Preferably this should be based on work performed earlier in the engagement. Ensure that the level of granularity is appropriate to this engagement and will provide the required insight into any broad groupings of responsibility or shifts in responsibility. Excessive or unnecessary detail should be avoided if possible.
2. Identify the broad organisational groupings to which responsibility will be assigned (e.g. Corporate IT, Business Unit, Cross-Business Unit Council, External Partner, etc). At this stage the groupings should probably not be mapped to actual organisational entities since we are trying to establish a high level governance framework which should have greater longevity than any specific organisation structure.
3. Assign management responsibilities in accordance with the agreed management model and any relevant guiding principles. In completing the Management Responsibilities Matrix, think both about where the management responsibility and the management authority reside. Where responsibility is shared between more than one group denote this by checking both cells in the matrix and indicating whether there is a primary/secondary or a control/execution relationship.
4. Identify any broad groupings or domains of responsibility within the matrix and crosscheck that these are consistent with the management model and the guiding principles.
5. Identify and highlight any significant shifts in responsibility between the "as is" and "to be" environments. These gaps will require subsequent projects or initiatives defined to effect those shifts in responsibility.
6. Review the resulting Management Responsibility Matrix with the client sponsor and ensure you have concurrence before proceeding.

Management Controls

1. Determine whether the client already has a quality management program. This, or other mechanisms for ensuring continuous improvement, can provide a useful starting point for defining the management controls that are required and implementing them.
2. In the absence of any existing processes, identify any required overall governance processes and define the overall ownership and responsibility for implementing them.
3. Identify the control metrics and performance indicators that will allow the IT management system and processes to be monitored and improved.
4. If necessary, establish new policies on the types of management controls that are required and when or where they are to be implemented. This may include establishing broad guidelines on the triggers or levels of risk beyond which management controls become necessary. It may also include defining the checkpoints where management controls will be implemented.
5. Identify the compliance processes, incentives or communication plans necessary to ensure compliance with the IT management model and guiding principles. These will often be similar to the management processes and reviews put in place to ensure compliance with an enterprise IT architecture. (Examples of architecture management processes can be found within the Enterprise Architecture method or the associated IT Architecture and Design data base on ICM AssetWeb.)
