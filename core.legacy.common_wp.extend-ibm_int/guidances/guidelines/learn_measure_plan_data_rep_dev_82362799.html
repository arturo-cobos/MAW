Guideline: Developing Learning Measurement Plan and Data Repository
Migrated development approach from existing IBM Global Services Method work product.
Relationships
Related Elements
  • Learning Measurement Plan and Data Repository
Main Description
  • Create the Measurement Menu. The Measurement Menu should be derived from a Causal Chain or other measurement inventory. It is recommended that measures be selected from the Business Results, Organizational Performance Indicators, and Individual/Team Performance Indicators from the Causal Chain. Select the indicators that appear to be good measures of the intervention. This selection is based on linkage with the Key Leverage Points on the Causal Chain
  • Prioritize the proposed measures. Identify priority list of measures based on subject matter expert review in terms of business linkage and availability of data. Business measures should be rated in terms of the strength of the linkage of the measure with the intervention – Strong (S), Moderate (M), or Weak (W) and the ability to measure (accessibility or availability of the data for the proposed measure) – High (H), Moderate (M), Low (L). Once the strength of the links and the ability to measure are rated, it is up to the consultant and the subject matter experts to determine the highest priority measures for inclusion on the Learning Measurement Plan and Data Repository. However, it is recommend that only strong to moderate links be considered acceptance and ability to measure should also high or moderate.
  • Identify Other Potential Measures (optional). If needed, identify and inventory other measures that might be appropriate for learning measurement. This should be done if the Measurement Menu is not perceived to be comprehensive enough.
  • Produce the populated Learning Measurement Plan and Data Respository, including measures, but not data. This plan should include both Business and Learning Outcome Measures. The list of Business Measures should be based on the prioritization of the measures from the Measurement Menu. The Learning Outcomes Measures selection should be made based on the information needs of the client in terms of Levels 0-3 learning measurement.
  • Perform Predictive Measurement quantification and compute ROI (return on investment) or use some other valuation method, if appropriate. This task is optional. This should be done using the following procedure:
    • Determine the appropriateness of quantifying the learning and business measures. The decision to do Predictive Measurement Quantification should be based on a number of considerations. The most important one is whether credible estimates of the measures can be achieved. If the estimates are not credible, any effort invested in this task will be wasted. Identify the most appropriate measures for quantification. The critical path measures will not all be readily quantifiable. Select the ones that lend themselves to quantitative estimation.
    • Determine the estimating methodology and assumptions. Predictive estimates are developed using a methodology with underlying assumptions. Measures typically need to be converted to dollar terms in order to use valuation methods. One important cautionary note before you begin: Because quantitative analysis is based on estimating assumptions, these assumptions must be credible.
    • Estimate the quantifiable factors. One or more experts are typically used for estimating the effects on learning measures. The selection of the experts is critical to the credibility of the estimates. Estimates can be weighted by asking estimators for their confidence levels (between 100% confidence and 0% confidence).
    • Verify the credibility of the estimates with stakeholders (optional, but recommended). Once the estimates have been made, estimates should be checked with the stakeholders to assess their credibility.
    • Apply appropriate valuation methodology to each intervention option. Use the valuation decision and method(s) determined in the previous steps.
    • Determine the most appropriate measures to use as baseline (pre-intervention) measures.
  • Identify the target for each proposed baseline measure. The target is the goal level, or standard, for the measure, if has not already been determined earlier. The target provides a critical reference point for interpreting the data. This provides a basis for understanding measurements during other phases. Without a target, there would be no basis for interpreting the progress being made. Establishing evaluation objectives or targets has several advantages. It provides measurable goals for assessing the progress of all training. It focuses attention on accountability and communicates a strong message about the need for measurement and evaluation.
  • Collect the baseline data. Enter the data into the appropriate spreadsheet or database for later comparison with in-process and retrospective data. As part of this process the quality of the data needs to be evaluated. The measurement plan should be updated to take into account the number of in-process measurements to be taken. It is important to determine the method and schedule for ongoing data collection.
  • Collect in-process data according to the plan. Most of the data should be available through existing operational data collection methods. Make sure to assess the cost of continuing in-process data collection if there is no budget for incremental change or large-scale improvements to the intervention that are detected by the analysis of the in-process data.
  • Collect retrospective data, which typically occurs after the intervention is completed. Its primary purpose is to make final judgments about the intervention. ROI analysis can be done at this point, if the client desires.
