Main Description

The following are the guidelines for developing Performance Management Systems;

1. Identify measures required for organization, divisions, teams, and individual jobs
2. Design future performance management system
3. Construct measurement system
4. Review total reward system
5. Summarize recommendations and implementation requirements

Identify Measures Required for Organization, Divisions, Teams, and Individual Jobs

To be effective, the system needs to be based on measurements that are relevant to the organization's goals; closely related to the jobs performed by individuals and teams; specific, defined, and well understood; and measured. These measurements should be identified for all three levels of the organization: enterprise or SBU; team, function, department, or team, function, department, and process; and individual.

Enterprise or SBU Level

If this is part of the engagement, the organization's measures should be developed with its senior management. Draft a set of measures with senior management and then review and agree on them in a workshop or series of workshops. Scope the measures initially from the organization's strategy, mission, and vision, goals and critical success factors, business plans, and key processes.
Construct these measures using the balanced scorecard approach to show both financial and non-financial measures of success and to cover both business-as-usual and the process of change. A default industry standard template that may be useful to start with is available on the Business Intelligence database (see Tools).

Team, Function, Department, or Process Level

Now identify appropriate measures for the remaining levels of the organization. Objectives should cascade down the organization, including to the executive team, so that overall goals are reflected in the goals of the departments and functions below it, but in greater detail and specificity for each level. Identify the measures in draft and agree to them in meetings or workshops with the areas concerned to ensure buy-in. Key sources of information are:

  • Organization-level goals and measures
  • Roles defined in the Future Organization Design
  • Divisional sub-strategies or plans (e.g., IT strategy, HR strategy)
  • Critical success factors
  • Key processes: where the organization is adopting a process or process matrix structure this will be a principal component, but it should be considered in all cases.

Individual Level

Measures are also needed at the individual level to measure individual performance of roles. Cascading from the team and organization level, these will also be derived from, and checked against, the new job descriptions. This process may be iterative, ensuring that measures are correctly aligned to the new jobs. Draft the measures with client staff and management, and where possible have employee groups review them to ensure buy-in. Key sources of information are:

  • Team-level goals and measures
  • Job descriptions
  • Process measures

The key work product inputs to this process will come from:

  • Strategic Direction work product
  • Process Benchmarks / Process Enablers work products
  • Future Organization Design
  • Jobs, Roles, Responsibilities, and Competencies
  • Program Management Framework (for the change process)

Design Future Performance Management System

The four steps in designing performance management systems are shown in PMGTMDL.PRE under Notation.

Review Existing Performance Management Approaches

Evaluate existing approaches for completeness and the degree to which they support the organization vision, mission, and strategy and the future organization design. This is a review and update of the work started in the Current Organization Assessment work product.

  • Collect performance approach descriptions for the firm and by department, if applicable
  • Interview HR resource for clarification and insight into approaches and the client organization's performance management philosophy. Clarify the objectives of the transformation from an organizational effectiveness perspective.
  • Conduct employee focus groups, interviews, or surveys with staff from areas affected by the transformation and seek input on the operation of the performance management framework in practice. Probe for understanding of the extent that employees use the performance management program as required or if ad hoc programs are used instead. The Performance Management Questionnaire (PMQ) can be used in this information gathering.
  • Create criteria for the performance management system to complete the evaluation of the current system and provide the basis for redesign.

Design the Future Performance Management System

Base the design of the system on the organization's strategy and business priorities, and on the future design characteristics and future organization design completed in the Organization Scope and Requirements and Future Organization Design work products. The design will need to cover the different kinds of jobs the redesigned organization will have, for example call center staff, finance executive, and production staff. Although the details for each job family will be specified later, define the overall framework or frameworks to cope with all the job families.
New or revised performance management approaches and processes should be designed and documented, including all four elements of the process:

  • Setting performance goals and expectations. objective-setting approach: team or individual goals; objective timelines (short, medium, or long term); cascaded objectives
  • Monitor performance and give and receive feedback and coaching.
    • Distinguish roles among participant, manager, or coach, and review and ensure that the roles are well defined and understood
    • Appraisal process: annual, quarterly, or more frequent; 360-degree appraisal or more traditional management assessment
  • Assess performance. Link to measurement, relative or absolute measures, fact-based and objective
  • Link performance to consequence. Link into the organization's rewards and recognition mechanisms

Identify Gaps

Identify gaps between existing and desired performance management in each area and identify approach to implement the redesigned system.

Implement and Construct a Review Process

Construct Measurement System

Once the measures have been identified and agreed to and the performance management approaches have been defined, it is essential to link these to the organization's management information systems and reporting processes to ensure that the measures are in place and reported to the appropriate structure. This will depend on the organization's overall management system (structure, committees, functions, etc.) that will have been reviewed in the Future Organization Design. It may be a separate assignment to revise the management reporting process to reflect the balanced scorecard. Implementation will be through the Balanced Scorecard offering framework and the Business Information Model work product.
Targets for each measure should be set with the business.

Review Total Reward System

Note: The design of rewards and remuneration is a specialist HR role. This work product reviews existing reward frameworks and identifies opportunities for improvement, but does not redesign the rewards system.

Review the organization's existing rewards frameworks and identify gaps and changes required by the revised organization design and measures. Rewards are both formal and informal. Formal rewards include bonuses, pay schemes, contribution awards, discounts, holiday days, and company cars, while informal rewards include promotion, time off in lieu of pay, invitations to management events, and selection for development.

Identify Reward Objectives

Identify the objectives of the rewards system and ensure that the results are linked to the to desired behaviors and measures for each role.

Identify and Evaluate Guiding Principles of the Total Reward System (excluding incentive plans)

Review the principles of the total reward system and identify high-level issues and gaps in the following areas:

  • Total pay positioning is pay benchmarked against that of competitors (median, above, below)
  • Internal equity and consistency is the degree to which principles and incentive design are consistently applied throughout the organization
  • Prominence is the importance of rewards to the group's business needs
  • Non-cash awards include canteen subsidies, childcare, and company cars
  • Portfolio plans allow employees to choose from a menu of benefits and evaluate them against the desired culture and organization goals.
  • Recognition approaches

Evaluate Design of the Incentive Plans

Determine where among the three types the organization or organization areas currently are and where the desired culture and performance requirements suggest that they should be for each team and job type.

  • Gainsharing, such as rewards for gains in productivity or sales
  • Goal sharing, rewarding multiple performance categories
  • Cash profit sharing, rewarding based on a single profit measure
  • None -- no incentive scheme

Evaluate the appropriate award size relative to other employee reward opportunities. Consider what award size will send the appropriate signal about the value of the performance improvement.
Evaluate how the awards are funded and how they will plan finance payouts. Consider the impact of the system on the incentive of staff and management to achieve or recognize the achievement. (Will awarding a large bonus to staff reduce the cost-based bonus of the awarding manager?)

Produce an Overview of the Rewards System and Identify Key Gaps for Closure

One all areas have been evaluated, summarize the evaluation, identify the critical issues, and develop recommendations for changes to the system to close those gaps.

Summarize Recommendations and Implementation Requirements

From the evaluations, identify key areas for improvement and ensure that activities required to implement the performance management, measurement, and reward systems are included in the implementation plan. Set up a process to ensure that the effectiveness of the systems is reviewed on a regular basis.
