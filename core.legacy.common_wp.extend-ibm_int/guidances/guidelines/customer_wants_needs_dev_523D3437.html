Main Description

The CVM specialist class provides detailed techniques for a Customer Wants and Needs engagement  including unique CVM focus group facilitation methods, that probe each MOT (moment of truth) interaction within the engagement scope to identify not only WHAT customers want, but WHY.

The "why" (or value/benefit they can receive) is then made the focus of visioning exercises that identify creative new ways to deliver that value and differentiate the client’s product/service.

  1. Define the Project Objectives and Data Sources to be Used
    The first step of any project of this type is to ensure the objectives are clear and then to build issue-based hypotheses, which will be proved or disproved. With the hypotheses articulated, the practitioner should map out the questions that must be asked to validate the hypotheses and identify the appropriate sources. In the case of customer wants and needs, customers and potential customers should always be included in the sources.
  2. Identify All Customer Touchpoints
     In order to ensure that all customer wants and needs are explored, all touchpoints with the customer should be identified (think about the interactions customers may have) and make a first cut at prioritizing their importance.
  3. Collect Data from Customers
    This data will come from focus groups and/or surveys. In some cases, focus groups may have to be the extent of data collection. This is acceptable, but it is important to ensure that all customer groups are represented through data collection, including those with disabilities. It is also very important that those customers and potential customers selected to participate are representative of their segment as is possible and that more than one focus group is conducted with each customer segment.  The special needs (large print, sign language interpretation, etc.) of the participants may need to be considered.
    A more statistically sound method of determining the importance of wants and needs is conducting a survey and draw information from hundreds of customers and potential customers. With this amount of data, the responses can be statistically tested to discover which wants and needs drive customers up the loyalty curve. The collected data can be prioritized by level of importance.  Responses on the extreme ends of the priority scale should not be discarded.  Instead, these responses should be carefully considered.
    3A. Collect Industry or Best Practice Data (if necessary)
     In some cases, the practitioner will want to measure the differences or gaps between the client’s performance on each want and need longitudinally or in comparison to the industry leader or perhaps the industry average. These data should be located while the survey is in the field.
  4. Check the Strategic Alignment of the Wants and Needs with Business Drivers
    With the data (from focus groups or surveys) in hand, compare the alignment of the customer’s wants and needs with the elements that are considered the Business Drivers. This exercise will illuminate where business processes are not supporting meeting customer wants and needs.
  5. Identify Business Process Characteristics and Compare with the Ideal, Identify Gaps and Prioritize
     List the few things that MUST be done and which your business processes must be capable of doing. Define the dependencies or prerequisites that must be present in the business infrastructure to support or enable the essential capabilities, such as organizations/people, skills/resources, policies/practices, information or information technology, and management/measurement systems. Having identified these elements, the practitioner can identify and measure the gaps between the current and ideal or desired state; then, the priorities of which gaps to address can be set.
  6. Knowing Priorities and Capabilities, Make Recommendations
    With all the information collected through this process, the team can help the client see all the opportunities which exist to address customer wants and needs and improve what the company provides to the customer. It is then the job of the IBM team to help the client make the investments that will give the greatest return.
