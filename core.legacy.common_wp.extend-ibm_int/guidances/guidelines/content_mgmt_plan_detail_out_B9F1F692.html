Main Description

1. Processes

Content Management Processes generally fall into three primary areas (Managing or Governing Processes, Publishing Workflow Processes, Content Distribution (Operations and Infrastructure) Processes). For each of the areas, the following must be addressed:

What are the tasks and workflow within each process? Are the inputs, outputs, dependencies and performers defined for each process? Have external providers of content been identified?

The processes specified below must first be established – (identified, defined, and documented). Once established and implemented, they must be regularly reviewed and adjusted in support of the business objectives, staffing, etc. The actual execution of the processes occurs as part of the ongoing business activities.

  • Managing or Governing Processes include creating the processes and policies required for enabling and controlling the Content Management processes.

Enabling Processes must be initially established, and then reviewed on an ongoing, periodic basis to assure that they still meet the business objectives they were designed to support, and include:

- Selection and Identification of processes to create the Content Model - establish the critical link between the audience definitions and requirements, and the content. In many organizations, there may be a significant body of legacy content, and the tendency is to focus funding and effort on making all “available” content ‘web-ready.’

However, a core element (and differentiator) of the Content Management Plan is the focus of funding and effort on only that content which is “necessary and sufficient” to meet the specific audience needs and drive business results.
The focus and criteria for content selection and identification in the Content Model clearly requires management involvement. It is here that the foundation for measurement and success are established. It is also here that the relationships between different business units are first defined and/or negotiated. For example, where there are separate Marketing analysis and segmentation teams, and product teams, this is where any conflict in business measures and models must be identified and resolved.

- Acquisition and/or creation process and policies that allow for the enabling of the information architecture.

  • Indexing and description of content – how will it be handled, what is the framework to manage?
  • Storage and Access of content – what are the underlying processes and assumptions that must be defined?
  • Re-purposing and usage of content – who will be able to reuse content, what process will be used to approve the reuse of content?
  • Distribution and governance of content - who sets the rules and determines where and when processes should be updated?

          - Administration and Publishing Workflow definition

    • Collaboration and resource sharing for content – will team rooms be used, how will scarce resources be allocated across divisions?
  • Workflow Publishing Process – Ongoing performance
This is the most common understanding of “Content Management,” and it represents the actual steps and activities that are executed to deliver content on a day-to-day basis.

- Select and Identify Content within audience requirements criteria and selection parameters

- “Assign” specific responsibility (to Create, Acquire) to the content owner

  • Create/Acquire
  • Submit or “Turn in”
  • Edit
  • Review/Approve – may link to external and/or governing processes
    • Compliance with business rules, policies, guidelines
    • Layout and Design Integrity (Fonts, Colors, placement, logo integrity, navigation, etc.)
    • Architecture Integrity (Information, Data, Application interfaces, especially for common functions such as print, search, download, link, etc.)
    • Quality Control
      • Content Accuracy, Source
      • Styles or formats (writing style including capitalization, phrasing, voice, spacing, etc.; picture style (border, description, etc); other styles or formats as appropriate for unique content types.)
    • Language translation and/or globalization (cultural content review)
  • Release for Publication

- “Publish” – is the actual process of preparing all of the content objects and elements for delivery or publication. The tasks include:

  • Define Content Placement
  • Define Content Format (layout/design)
  • Define Timing (schedule/life cycle)
  • Identify Storage (Indexing/Description, links and thresholds or triggers for maintenance.)

- Measure content effectiveness, impact or results -- executes an iterative, “closed loop cycle,” to maintain alignment of desired results with actual results.

    • Define/refine measurement plan (control/measurement points or streams)
    • Collect results
    • Analyse
    • Define/refine Adjustment/Change
    • Trigger Re-active Change(s)

- Maintain

      • Schedule
      • Trigger Updates and affiliated maintenance (downstream, upstream, peer/replica sites)
      • Remove/Replace end-of-life content
      • Notify owner
  • Content Distribution (Operational Delivery and Infrastructure) Processes (a.k.a. “Technology Model”) – is the actual ‘hands-on’ management of the infrastructure and operations processes that result in publication (as defined above). This set of processes is broken out because this represents ‘Web Hosting,’ and may be out-sourced as a self-contained scope of work. There must be a strong interlock between the Workflow Publishing Processes and the Operational Delivery and Infrastructure Processes, with clearly defined roles, responsibilities, escalation methods and contacts, etc.
The tasks that must be coordinated and executed include:
  • Collect, review and manage infrastructure technology requirements and standards
  • Infrastructure request (e.g., infrastructure to support a new site, or expansion of an existing)
  • Content submission
  • Content testing
  • Content staging and “release”
  • Content “promotion”
  • Content maintenance and support (add/change/delete)
  • Core IT processes
    • Daily Operations
    • Site problem determination and escalation processes
    • Site change management processes (request; assess need, risk, value; plan, test, implement)
    • Site backup and recovery processes
2. Organizational Structures
  • Policies and Guidelines Definition and Update – What current policies and guidelines exist that need to be updates or revised to support the Web environment? What new policies and guidelines need to be established? This might include how deal with cataloging externally sourced content, how to standardize on a common ‘masthead’, how to ensure consistent use of terminology, advertising, and graphics, and a definition of what constitutes inappropriate content.
  • Personnel, Roles and Responsibilities - Within the content management arena, what are the roles and the associated responsibilities for each participating group? Who is currently fulfilling each of these roles? What new roles need to be established?
  • Education – for needed, “short-supply” and/or core content skills, as well as processes, business rules, guidelines, policies, procedures, etc., how are personnel educated? How are the common processes, vocabulary and standards communicated and taught and maintained?
3. Technologies

Within the content management arena, what are the technologies required to automate the process? What are the high level requirements of this technology? How and where does it integrate with existing systems and processes?

  • Information Architecture
  • Data Architecture
  • Application Architecture Interfaces
  • Tools evaluation and selection (multiple toolsets, unique to individual process streams)
  • Technical Infrastructures - Core IT and Network/Communications Infrastructures
