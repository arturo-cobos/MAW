Determine and Secure Access to the Information Sources, and Review the Current Organization Description

Before the assessment can take place, you should determine and access all relevant information sources. Your analysis must remain focused on the elements of the organization within the scope of the engagement. Some examples of information sources are:

  • Current organization design scope and past work products
  • Executives, middle management, junior managers, other employees (including employees with special needs)
  • Support functions
  • People in key administrative roles
  • Internal customer satisfaction surveys
  • External customer satisfaction surveys
  • Financial statements
  • Mission statements
  • Benchmarking reports

Assess Organization Alignment against the Future Business Context

Assess Strategic Alignment
The most fundamental organization design principle of all is that “structure should support strategy.” Elements of the structure should support competitive positions by concentrating management attention on strategic imperatives, while allocating resources and accountabilities in terms of business priorities, by considering people’s competencies and abilities. Behaviors will determine whether the corporate culture is aligned with the strategic goals and capabilities of the organization. This also includes considerations on how the corporation is capable of facing the external requirements in terms of accessibility. At a higher level, the company should be able not only to satisfy a customer’s request, but to promote accessible solutions and products to its customers. The Enablers of an organization should support the business imperatives through performance management, motivation systems, and training and development.
Map out the key strategic imperatives for the organization, for example, reduce overhead costs, increase speed of response to market, or identify opportunities for exploiting employees’ competencies and abilities. Once you understand what the strategic goals are, conduct workshops or interviews with key clients to assess the extent to which the organization structure, behaviors, and enablers are aligned with the strategy. Use the Strategic Alignment Matrix Tool to map out your findings.

Assess Bureaucracy Alignment
Bureaucracy refers to an organization model that defines jobs, responsibilities, and relationships in a hierarchical, systematic framework, and it has neutral connotations in this work product. The circumstances of a business should drive decisions on the nature and extent of bureaucracy or corporate staff. The need for bureaucracy is proportional to the needs of the business for economies of scale, consistency, adherence to standards, and centralized control.
Assess Process Alignment
The most effective organizations are aligned with core process goals and emphasize horizontal work and information flows. You should have an understanding of whether your client’s teams, performance management system elements, competencies, communication structures, and behaviors are aligned with and support process-defined needs:
  • Does the structure facilitate the direct flow of work and information? Is this flow of work and information accessible?
  • Are resources allocated on the basis of process needs?
  • Are resources allocated on the basis of their abilities, competencies, and skills (utilize Human Capabilities Assessment work product)? This implies an understanding of disabled people’s needs and abilities and allows for better fit of their capabilities to tasks.
  • Are communication support systems designed to facilitate a process-oriented environment by aligning workflows with information flows?
  • Have considerations been explored based on the need for special process considerations for employees with disabilities? For example, have the software or web applications been enabled so that skilled employees with disabilities can still perform the tasks?

Assess Resource Allocation Alignment
In an aligned organization, there should be consistency between business goals and the allocation resources. Functions and units with key process goals should be appropriately resourced. Resources should be allocated by process as well as by function and should be measurable against customer-defined objectives. Use the Financial Resource Allocation to Business Goals table to help in understanding which resources are allocated by function and process.

Assess Organization Efficiency against the Future Business Context

Organization efficiency is a factor of how well the organization is able to achieve its performance expectations with a minimum amount of resources. Resource usage is measured in two ways:
  1. The value of physical resources required, that is, facility/operational costs, overhead, communication systems costs
  2. Costs associated with achieving coordination, that is, costs of decision making, cost of organization inefficiencies

Remember that an efficient organization is effective in minimizing both the absolute number of resources it requires to operate and the extent to which organization structures, behaviors, and enablers stand in the way of conducting business. The more resources are allocated by considering their competencies, the higher the savings in time and resource cost for a particular task. In addition, the quality of the final product or service will be greatly elevated. This demonstrates that a skill map of employees’ competencies is one of the best means of improving efficiency. Accessibility may positively affect efficiency. For example, if employees with disabilities are employed in processes that engage suppliers or customers who also have disabilities, the barriers will have already been dealt with and sensitivities to special needs will be inherent.

Assess Efficiency Measures
Efficiency measures indicate whether the elements of the organization are cost-effectively designed and managed to achieve the goal of the business. Obvious efficiency measures are operating and managing costs and what the company does with the cost data. Less obvious are the non-financial costs, and these may be difficult to access. These include measures such as speed of decision making and efficiency in information sharing. Use the Efficiency Measures Checklist to help you understand what efficiency measures are in place and where the gaps are.

Assess Human Resource Efficiency
An efficient organization uses the minimum amount of human resources and other assets to achieve a given level of coordination and communication. You can then see if there are resources that are under- or over-allocated.

Assess Bureaucratic Efficiency
Bureaucracies are created to coordinate activities and to facilitate communication. Examine the bureaucracies of the organization and see if the amount of bureaucracy exceeds or does not meet the demand. Remember that the need for bureaucracy varies depending on the requirements of the organization.
Assess Efficiency of Coordination and Communication
Coordination and communication systems are often a time and resource drain on organizations. Make sure you identify any barriers or redundancies in these systems.

Assess Performance Effectiveness against the Future Business Context

Effective organizations have a systematically defined and managed set of programs designed to foster high levels of human performance. A key element of employee retention and productivity is providing opportunities for workers to grow skills. To assess performance effectiveness, you must determine where the weaknesses in the development of employees exist and where the opportunities to provide capabilities exist. Refer to the Human Capabilities Assessment work product.
Assess Appropriate Levels of Performance
The performance of an organization depends on the performance of its people, both collectively and individually. The first area to assess is whether the levels of human performance are right and if a system is in place to support the requirements. Performance can be influenced by such things as:
  • Environment (Is it accessible to everyone? Lighting? Space?)
  • Processes
  • Motivation
  • Definition of performance requirements
  • Leadership
  • Incentives

Examine these factors, and any other you can identify, to see where the strengths and weaknesses lie.
Assess Human Performance Support Systems
The human performance requirements need to be backed up with an equally clear support system (including tools and technology) that is responsible for removing barriers and that is relevant to all employees, including employees with disabilities.
Assess Competency and Skill Development Process
Competencies and skills should be managed as a corporate asset and employee competencies and skills must be kept current with the business goals. Make sure you examine the process of employee development and how the culture, strategies (including accessibility), and priorities either support or undermine this process.
Assess Organization Learning Processes and Practices
Use the Learning Process Checklist tool to understand how learning and knowledge sharing is promoted, facilitated, and enabled throughout the organization. Keep in mind that employees in an optimal organization should have the skills and knowledge required to perform and the learning process should be continuous, cumulative, accessible to those with disabilities and collaborative. Learning requirements should be linked to both human performance and business imperatives.

Assess Human Performance Measurement
Use the Human Performance Measurement Process Checklist to review how measurements are used to facilitate growth and learning across levels of the organization. Remember that measures are how the organization communicates to the employees about what is important and valued and they are an important driver in human and organization performance.
Assess Appropriate Accessibility Needs
Ensure that the performance assessment considers barriers to people with disabilities. For example, some assessments incorrectly identify a blind employee as poorly or unable to perform a database administrator job, but the problem isn’t the person’s capabilities or skills, it is the software that has barriers in it that prevent the assistive technology used by the database administrator from working or working efficiently.

Determine the Priorities for Redesign

Once you have gathered and assessed all the relevant information and discovered the issues, it is necessary to prioritize the redesign areas to best meet the future business goals.
Use the Issue Prioritization and Presentation tool to map issues according to the severity of impact and the need to be addressed. The tool itself is a checklist and a spreadsheet for scoring each issue and plotting them on a two-over-two matrix.
The results of the issue prioritization then yield a simple guide to action, based on four possible quadrant placements:
  • Redesign should include these items
  • A separate program is needed to address these items
  • Optional opportunities exist in these items
  • No action
