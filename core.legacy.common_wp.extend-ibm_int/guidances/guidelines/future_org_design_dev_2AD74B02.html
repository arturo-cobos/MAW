Main Description
The high level steps for performing future organization design are as follows;

1. Review and validate
2. Develop detailed future organization design - Structure
3. Develop detailed future organization design - Behaviors
4. Develop detailed future organization design - Enablers
5. Document and validate

Review and validate

1. Review existing information

Collect and review the current organization description, current organization assessment, future state vision/definition, and future organization scope and requirements, and confirm that it is still accurate and represents the current agreed approach to the organization design. Put special emphasis on the design points defined in the scope and requirements to ensure that they represent the client's desired direction for the redesign. If the future state vision/definition and future organization scope and requirements are not available, do not proceed with this work product until they or their equivalents have been produced.

2. Obtain further detailed information

 Detailed information about roles (including new process roles and new IT roles), current position responsibilities, skills, compensation, and organization structure must be gathered for departments that will be significantly impacted by the new organization. Before creating a new design, one must have a complete inventory of the work done in the current organization, because changes in even one job or department will likely impact others. This information will also be crucial to defining the activities required to implement the project successfully in a supportive environment.

Develop detailed future organization design - STRUCTURE

1. Based upon the design points, design a new organization structure which defines structural elements.  Depending on the scope, these may include:

  • groupings of new process roles and responsibilities, along with unchanged, existing roles, into new jobs (for this work product, this is a high-level view only)
  • groupings of work activities into organizational units, with a mission and recommended name for each
  • vertical reporting relationships
  • horizontal and inter-firm linkages
  • staffing levels and spans of control

Typically the first iteration of the structural design is done at a conceptual level. The conceptual model is like the architecture of the organization structure. Most organizational models that IBM consultants have designed are hybrids of various "pure" organization models (e.g., functional, geographic, customer- based, etc.). A matrix model is a hybrid that is frequently employed in today’s designs. See the tools at the end of this section for detailed instruction on various models. Exploring the various models is done in a workshop setting that is designed to allow the client team to imagine the alternatives for their organization and test them in "what-if" situations. Once consensus is reached among the team on the model, the sponsor’s agreement should be obtained

2. The next step is to apply the model to the overall organization at the strategic level. This shows how the model, at a high level, encompasses strategic business units, lines of business, functions, outsourced functions, business partners, geographies, and executive management groups.

3. This model can then be fleshed out with all the detail. In a large, multi-site redesign, the following sequence can be useful: in-scope strategic business units; in-scope functions; lines of business; geography. This sequence proceeds from the broader to the narrower of the existing units. Apply the model at each level and figure out what the groupings of work activities will be. Be sure to include any outsourced functions and show clearly where the linkages to the extended enterprise (alliances, business partners) lie. Also indicate where process leadership and management reside in the organization. The design may also include elements that support process in other ways, such as centers of competency or cross-functional process teams

4. The lowest level of detail is the final step--team design. This may include process teams, other cross-functional teams indicated by the model, and project or program teams. When designing, test the new structure for its ability to clarify the linkage needed among the teams and their integration with the organization’s decision-making and management system

Develop detailed future organization design - BEHAVIORS

The current organization assessment will highlight existing behaviors, which will no longer contribute to the organization's future direction nor success in implementing its initiatives. The structural redesign may make it necessary to change behavior at the enterprise, business unit, or individual level. Behavior models for high-performance organizations--the behavior equivalent of best practice--can provide descriptions of target behaviors (see tools below). With these inputs, the behaviors required in the new organization design can be detailed using the following approach.

  1. Envision and design the target organization behaviors - Facilitate dialogue to envision how the future organization structure and changes caused by other initiatives will make it important to behave differently (focus only on the in-scope levels--enterprise, business unit or work group, individual). At a high level, define and reach consensus regarding ideal behaviors which are related to success of the initiative.
  2. Define and size gaps - Draw out the contrast between the current organization culture and the target culture (i.e., "we want more of this behavior and less of that behavior"). Identify existing cultural strengths that can be fitted easily into the target culture. For each target behavior, assess the relative difficulty of making the transition.
  3. Prioritize target behaviors - Assess the relative importance of achieving each targeted behavior based on its expected contribution to success of the initiative. Using both the importance and difficulty ratings, extract a prioritized list of specific target behaviors to be addressed.
  4. Plan actions to foster target behaviors - Actions may include training, coaching, and incentives. Work with the human resources department to ensure alignment with existing programs and policies.
Remember that in multinational projects, where local cultures may be stronger in influence than the corporate culture, there may be a requirement to overlay local cultures onto the target design.

  • Denison Organization Culture Survey - cultural traits of high-performing organizations

Primarily an assessment tool, this survey is based on a culture model built from studies of many companies, which linked behavior and culture to business results. The four traits are:

  • Involvement: building human capability, ownership, and responsibility
  • Consistency: defining the values and the organization's systems that are the basis of a strong culture
  • Adaptability: translating the demands of the business environment into action
  • Mission: defining a meaningful long-term direction for the organization

Develop detailed future organization design - ENABLERS
The current organization assessment will highlight inadequacies in the current enablers. The structural redesign and targeted new behaviors will generate new requirements for enablers, as well (e.g., a process management framework with service level agreements for process teams). With these inputs and best practice information, the enablers can be redesigned to meet the organization design points. These will include mechanisms for:
  • Communication
  • Compensation
  • Recruitment and induction
  • Education and development
  • Performance management

Appropriate members of the corporate and division human resource units should be involved in developing recommendations for these enablers.
