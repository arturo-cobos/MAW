Guideline: Developing Process Benchmarks

Main Description

The Process Benchmarks work product can be developed with the following steps:

1. Identify a process that has to be benchmarked.

2. Identify aspects of the process that could be benchmarked and select or define related measurements.

Which aspects of the process should be benchmarked could be derived from the business measures that currently are already being tracked or from interviews and workshops to be conducted with business managers and workers in the process itself or in related processes. Furthermore suggestions for measurents could be derived form success stories in publications and, last but not least, the organizations joining in the benchmark could have suggestions as well.

3. Identify and invite organizations against which a benchmark on this process would be useful and feasible.

  • he benchmark is useful if it is against organizations in the same or other industry segments carrying out a comparable process, preferably in a way that is considered as “best-in-class”, for example,
    • Competitors,
    • Companies that have won quality/business awards,
    • Recognized industry leaders and companies with superior financial results,
    • Companies about which success stories were published,
    • Companies about which you received feedback from internal and external experts, clients, suppliers or business partners, and, 
    • Internal organizations which carry out the same value-added processes.
  • The benchmark with a specific organization is feasible if the the client will be able to reach that organization and will be able to get it supportive to the benchmark, for example by,
    • Common interest in the benchmark results.
    • Good contacts at executive level.

4. Measure the client’s current performance with respect to the chosen measurements.

5. Undertake performance comparisons between the organizations that join in the benchmark.

6. Analyze the reasons behind the performance differences by doing a gap-analysis between the client and the best-in-class organization for the compared aspects of the process.

This is a real detailed analysis. It will require contacts with managers and workers in both the client’s and the best-in-class organization, which is not necessarily the same organization for each of the aspects of the benchmarked business process. Moreover, because of likely differences in the process for different kinds of commodities, it probably has to be executed at commodity group level. The analysis should clarify how the specific activity is organized and conducted in both the client’s and the best-in-class’ organization and why and how the best-in-class process gets to the better performance.

Be aware of differences that originate from the business itself, specifically if the benchmarked organizations are not operating in the same industry.

7. Define, based upon the results from the gap-analysis and the client’s ability to change, potential process improvement actions that might be taken to narrow the gaps.

8. Propose, in line with the defined potential process improvement actions, redefined goals and future targets for the chosen measurements. The future targets can be spread out across time to show incremental benefits over a longer period.

It will not always be necessary to spend much effort on the steps 2 and 3 in above list. For several business processes success stories about best-in-class performers are published regularly in special literature and on Internet. A benchmark then can be simplified by just using the performance measurements of the best-in-class organization to compare the client’s process to that ‘best-in-class’ and the real effort can be spent in steps 6, 7 and 8.
