Main Description

The following are the high level steps for developing Job, Roles, Responsibilities and Competencies;

1. Review current jobs
2. Determine tasks to be performed in the future
3. Design new job roles and responsibilities
4. Validate design feasibility
5. Document and review with client

The steps above should yield formatted job descriptions and documentation of enablers required for successful implementation.  In addition to these deliverables is a client report. It should revisit the purpose of the initiative and articulate how the job designs will contribute to achieving the project’s intended business outcomes.

Note: Most of the tools listed below apply to more than one step.

Review Current Jobs
  1. Review the future organization scope and requirements to identify the jobs in scope. The client is likely have an existing set of job descriptions that outlines at least the basic tasks performed in the affected jobs. Work with the human resources department to understand how current and accurate these descriptions are, and whether any additional information should be gathered. Some information can also be gathered from the current organization description and current organization assessment. If necessary, update them to reflect changes since their completion. These work products may also provide insight into the client’s culture (how it typically approaches and responds to job change), and whether employees are unionized.
  2. In the absence of either of these sources, create a template to inventory existing job tasks. Identify a representative sample of people in each job to fill out the template. Try to involve those whose performance is considered outstanding. Compile the information.
Determine Tasks to Be Performed in the Future
  1. Prepare a list of all tasks needed to execute the future business processes. Obtain information about the new processes and the tasks and roles that make them up. Some current tasks may still be needed in the future, while others that are done today may no longer be needed.
  2. Also identify the information requirements for each task, including whether access to data should be read-only or whether authorization to update data is required.
Design New Job Roles and Responsibilities
  1. Collaborate with a small client team to design new job roles, responsibilities, and competencies. It is important to involve the human resources or organization development department and take into account the client’s usual approach to job design. These client team members can also ensure that the job redesign is consistent with and supported by HR policies (particularly in respect to job levels, compensation, benefits, etc.).
  2. The future organization design will illustrate at a very high level how some of the process activities should be clustered into jobs. This work product will refine that high-level model. The design points used for the organization redesign will also apply to job design. In the absence of organization redesign and design points, work from an existing set of design principles that are based on research and IBM Consulting Group experience (available on ICM AssetWeb and listed in the tools section below).
  3. A workshop setting is most effective in developing job designs. It also helps with building buy-in to the task of defining the level of detail that the design should have.

5.     Next enrich the role definition to cover the following categories:        
                  - Work tasks/responsibilities ("perform the task")         
                  - Accountabilities ("own the task")        
                  - Objectives        
                  - Competencies required        
                  - Behaviors that should be demonstrated within the role        
                  - Measurements for role performance        
                  - Security authorizations required for access to date        
         These should be documented within the client’s standard job description format, unless it does not adequately define the roles; if it
         does not, a revised template will need to be developed.
6.     Group jobs with the same security requirements into profiles. The same profile may apply to multiple jobs. Profiles will simplify the  
        structure of the security trees in any information technology applications used in job roles.
7.     Next define any changes to the management system that would be required to support the new designs (often referred to in
        Organization Change intellectual capital as "structural enablers"). Some examples are: HR practices, compensation programs,
        performance evaluation, skills management, career planning, hiring strategy, security strategy, recognition, and incentives. The
        physical environment, such as office layout and equipment, may also need to be changed.
8.     Also consider the implications that implementing the new job designs might have for the organization transition plan, and document
        them.

Validate Design Feasibility

1.     Check for consistency in the mapping of tasks to jobs. Ensure that the design does not produce unnecessary overlap or process
        inefficiencies.
2.     Revisit the design points and evaluate how well the new designs meet them. Some refinement may be required as a result of this test.
3.     Authority levels are an important business control. Review security profiles and look for consistency between the work being performed
        and access to data. Test them on a wide variety of job titles. If the profiles have been designed well, the number of profiles should not
        grow.
4.     Ask yourself whether the job description would give employees a clear understanding of their roles and job with respect to:

- Organization (team, department, business unit)
- Processes (that is, contribution as part of the value chain)
- IT user profile (access to what kind of information, authorizations, responsibilities)
- Individual contributions, behaviors, measurements, objectives, and outcomes

5.     A highly recommended approach to validation is to test the designs with an employee focus group. Various scenarios or process
        walkthroughs could be used to illustrate the real-life context of the job. For example, the job can be modeled in its process context by
        using process modeling tools such as LOVEM (see references).
6.     Because all of these jobs will have to be filled, ensure that the difficulty of doing so has been accurately gauged. Review the client’s
        approach to recruitment, selection, in-placement, outplacement, reskilling, and severance, and identify any additional
        recommendations that will support implementation of the redesign.
