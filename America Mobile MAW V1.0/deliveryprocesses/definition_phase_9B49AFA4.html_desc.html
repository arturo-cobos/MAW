Description

During the sales cycle, these activities should have been planned at a high level, as facilitated sessions over a period of time-boxed weeks, depending on the scope and scale of the initiative. Typically, one iteration of Definition is planned for a stand alone project, and some level of a proof of concept, may be included. Larger programs / complex projects should identify early business milestones, decompose the definition and following phases, so that incremental value can be delivered to the client. These three groups of activities should be conducted in parallel, in workshops with the core project team participating, and including others that are impacted by the scope of the project.

1.

Prepares the initial draft of the Agile Project Definition and Charter for the Project.

The charter is extremely important and serves as the single blueprint for the approach forward and as a unifying contract between stakeholders. It is used to open, manage, implement and close the effort. It is the single point of reference for goals, objectives, deliverables, estimates, resources, and budget commitments.

Ensure all AWD Accelerators/Best Practices are listed within the document.

Obtain approval of the document from key stakeholders to pursue business needs utilizing AWD Accelerators/Best Practices, such as variable scope management, which is typically a major change from their past project experiences.

Develop initial drafts with the Business Customer, and/or via brainstorming sessions with additional stakeholders. Ensure the Business Customer drives the initial sections.

In specifying the project and general AWD approach at a high-level, ensure:

  • Large or complex projects have been decomposed according to Guideline: Partitioning Large Projects for Agile.
  • Each project consists of a small, dedicated core project team of 5 to 10 professionals.
  • Use of dedicated facilities and equipment for the AWD Project.

Use AWD Roles and Responsibilities for standard roles when specifying resources.

Refine the Agile Project Definition and Charter in conjunction with drafting the AWD Plan.

Review with stakeholders and update to reflect their feedback.

Obtain all approvals, including from the business customer, from business and executive I/T Sponsors and from other key stakeholders. To support audits, ensure all approvals are kept in the AWD Control Book.

Ensure the final version is accessible to the team, password-protected and version controlled.

2.

Determines maintenance requirements and plan.

After migration to production, system responsibility is transitioned to the Maintenance Team and, if an application is to be deployed to multiple sites, to a Rollout Team (for simplicity the term “Maintenance” is used below for both). This requires:

  1. Identifying the Maintenance Team (MT) and the MT Transition Leader for the project
  2. Educating the MT Transition Leader on the scope/timing, using the draft Agile Project Definition and Charter and Project Management System Summary (PMSS).
  3. Working with the MT Transition Leader to perform the following:
  • Develop a MT resource plan for transition, e.g., staff, equipment and facilities
  • Determine deliverables and activities required for turnover. Note: the most efficient way to knowledge transfer or train MT members is to have them involved in key project activities.
  • Determine the disposition of the project’s hardware and software, and establish a strategy for building the MT environment
  • Estimate the total maintenance costs for the current plan year, including the costs of transition, infrastructure, training, production support and enhancements (if any)
  • Document maintenance and transition risks and assumptions
  • Incorporate all maintenance transitions tasks, resources, costs, etc. into the AWD Charter and AWD Plan

Knowledge transfer activities may include participation in:

Team status meetings

Peer reviews (e.g., code inspections and document walkthroughs), either as a reviewer or as an observer

Facilitated sessions

Education sessions, scheduled specifically for knowledge transfer

Building documentation

Application development

In some cases MT members may serve full-time on project delivery. For example, if:

  • MT members possess deep knowledge of the existing system critical to project success
  • The project uses technology not presently supported by the MT
  • The complexity/criticality of a new system poses a high risk to the business unless resources that participated in the AWD project are transitioned to the Maintenance/Rollout Team
  • MT members can benefit from a development assignment to an AWD Project Team

Refer to AWD Maintenance Transition Documentation Checklist for more information.

3.

Completes Agile Project Definition and Charter and PMSS.

Starting with the initial drafts, finalize impact of recent information, including sizing from the initial function point estimate, maintenance/rollout transition, resources obtained, launch strategy, quality plan, technology architecture, deliverables, gaps, timing, costs, reuse, qualitative requirements (e.g., service levels, security, response time), productivity assessments, risks, resource calendar changes, newly identified tasks, and contingency.

Consider use of the Accelerator Plan to add WBS tasks within iteration timeboxes for prototyping and development and then generating bottom-up estimates for those added tasks.

Perform final detailed and integrated review of all components, including all hours/costs.

Review with stakeholders and update to reflect their feedback.

Obtain all approvals, including from Sponsors and other key stakeholders.

Baseline the Agile Project Definition and Charter and PMSS to allow full tracking.

Ensure the final versions are accessible to the team, password-protected and version controlled.

4.

Starts AWD Project.

Relocate core resources together in the teamroom, review the final Agile Project Definition and Charter and review and begin executing the PMSS.
