Deliverable: Architecture Management (DCD 036)
The Architecture Management deliverable is normally produced as part of an enterprise-level architecture engagement.
Purpose

The Architecture Management deliverable is used to document how changes to the desired business and IT environment will be implemented, managed and updated in response to changes in business needs and available technologies.

The content of this deliverable is used in several different ways:

  • In organizations where the outsourcing of certain capabilities is being considered, this deliverable will provide a direct input to the documentation of partner responsibilities, roles and interface requirements. It will also heavily influence the definition of joint architecture management processes and responsibilities.
  • Provides a complete framework of processes to manage and evolve both enterprise and solution-level architectures, iteratively, over-time, as further levels of detail are defined during solution design and implementation activities. This includes processes, which all business, application development and IT operations staff will need to use, conform to and implement. It also includes processes to manage and evolve the chosen set of one or more ‘reference architectures’, which provide known, proven ‘default’ patterns for the design and implementation of business systems, application systems, packages and their associated infrastructure. These reference architectures drastically improve the opportunities for implementing rationalized architectural components and the architecture management framework provides processes for their effective reuse and promotion within the IT organization.
  • As a framework for documenting and managing the key resources (process, information/knowledge, organization and technology) required to implement the desired business model and its associated capabilities. The detailing of these component resources will take place over time, as various business, architectural and organization projects/activities are undertaken. The deliverables from these engagements will be documented in a component of this Architecture Management DCD called an ‘Architecture Delivery Environment’ (Note; that the name of this deliverable will change, dependant on client focus and preferred nomenclature), which will be accessible to all business and IT stakeholders.
  • Provides a central source of rules and guidelines for how the various resources in general and the architectures in particular, will be used and implemented in practice.
Main Description

This DCD is produced either as an output from phase 6 of the Business Architecture - Quickstart engagement model, or phase 4 of the IT Architecture - Quickstart engagement model. The content of this deliverable should include:

  • A reminder of the overall scope, objectives and deliverables of the engagement
  • An overview of the agreed architecture management processes and how these will be linked into the business and IT planning cycles
  • An overview of the new architecture management roles and responsibilities and how these have been/will be incorporated into the current organization
  • An overview of the architecture delivery environment, which contains content produced by all DCDs defined in these engagement models. For example: Business Architecture Context DCD, Business Architecture DCD, Application Architecture DCD, Architecture Management DCD, Business architecture Transition Considerations DCD, Business architecture Final Report DCD, IT Architecture Context DCD, IT Architecture DCD, IT Architecture Transition Considerations DCD
  • Whilst we strongly recommend that all deliverables from enterprise-level architecture engagements be incorporated into an asset like the architecture delivery environment, some clients may still choose not to take this option. If this is the case, all deliverables will be made available as base documents and presentation materials.
  • An overview of the new decision model and how this will improve the future decision-making capabilities of the organization
  • A summary of any key issues relating to the above architecture management components, which require senior management decisions and attention

The previously defined guiding principles (business, application, data and technology) will influence the overall style and focus of the architecture management processes, roles and responsibilities defined. The detailed content of these architecture management roles, responsibilities and processes are defined using the ARC 312 Architecture Management Framework WP.

External Description

The above description and purpose statements provide a summary of suitable words for inclusion in any client documents. The rest of this section contains additional wording, which may be of use in proposals, etc.

Description: An Architecture Management deliverable documents how changes to the desired business and IT environment will be implemented, managed and updated in response to changes in business needs and available technologies. Experience has shown that most architectures fail, due to the lack of a robust architecture management framework. Without a proper set of management processes, and agreed roles and responsibilities, any architecture will become simply a set of reports and recommendations gathering dust on a shelf. Implementing and managing new roles, responsibilities and management processes is not easy, especially when they challenge ingrained management and cultural styles.

There are a number of CSF’s to implementing effective architectures:

  • Sponsorship of the architecture - must be at the correct level in the organization. Experience has shown that the minimum should be a Chief Information Officer (CIO), Chief Finance Officer (CFO) or Chief Executive. The sponsor must be in a position to ensure all business and IT users are motivated to conform to the architecture and will contribute positively to its successful implementation.
  • Stakeholders and participants who come from both business areas and IT – must come from all parts of the company, not just the strategists – this includes business users, application development and service delivery. Many architectures fail because of a lack of direct line management involvement and responsibility.
  • Clear ownership of the architectures – someone who will drive the definition and implementation of these enterprise-level architectures and who will leave a ‘successful, living legacy’ behind them when they move on – which they always do!
  • Clearly defined processes, roles and responsibilities to manage these enterprise-level architectures as they grow, evolve and change (to reflect changes in both the business requirements and advances in technology). This includes processes for review, change, conformance, deviation and communication of the content of the architectures.
  • Skilled senior support staff – people with a broad skill base, with no technology bias, who are highly respected across the organization and have good communications skills. These are the individuals who will form the core architecture team and work directly with development teams helping future solutions and systems to conform to and implement the chosen architectures.
  • Easy access to the content of these enterprise-level architectures and the dissemination of information – document in a tool or delivery environment which is easy to access and maintain, for example, as a Lotus Notes database, or HTML pages on a company-wide intranet. All business and IT users must have a positive reason to use the information available.
  • Clear communication of the content to both business and IT users and stakeholders – as the architectures are defined, clear, concise models will be key to getting acceptance, and business investment to ensure the they are implemented and managed on an ongoing basis. Visibility drives culture and organizational change.
  • When companies decide to outsource all or part of an IT organization, particularly application development or service delivery, the impact of these CSFs is multiplied.

The deliverable would normally include the following:

  • Architecture Management principles (optional) if appropriate· Defined and agreed Architecture Management processes, integrated into the business, IT planning and procurement cycles
  • Defined and agreed Architecture Management roles and responsibilities
  • An overview of the Architecture Delivery Environment within which the contents of these enterprise-level architectures will be maintained
  • List of any architecture management initiatives including a more detailed definition of appropriate processes, roles and responsibilities – part of the Business Architecture Transition Considerations DCD
  • Architecture Management transition issues (may be captured as part of Critical Issues, Opportunities and Recommendations WP) – part of the Business Architecture Transition Considerations DCD

Packaging Instructions

The Architecture Management deliverable assembles the work product outputs into a format, which is suitable for the client. The format typically includes three parts:

  • The Architecture Delivery Environment – provides a complete environment (Notes database, web application, or external tool) for capturing and disseminating all the deliverables and outputs produced from one or more engagements, or internal client projects.
  • The architecture management processes, roles and responsibilities components, stored in the Architecture Delivery Environment
  • Presentation material, which summarizes the content of each of the component parts and the structure, benefits and objectives of the Architecture Management DCD.

The content of the deliverable and its (associated) presentation material is used to educate and inform all client business and IT stakeholders and users of:

  • The detailed outputs which have been produced, both in the course of this engagement and other associated projects.
  • The processes, roles and responsibilities involved in managing the architectures over time, as individual solutions are designed and implemented and further levels of detail are added
  • The structure of the database/web application which holds the outputs, how it is used and how to access the information it contains

In practice, key business users and IT stakeholders will have been involved in defining requirements for the architecture delivery environment and will have provided feedback as it develops. In addition, some key stakeholders will have been involved in defining and agreeing the new processes, roles and responsibilities captured in the Architecture Management Framework.

Combine the work product inputs/outputs as follows using the following structure and guidance:

  • Use ENG 315 to provide an overview of the overall scope, objectives and deliverables of the engagement.
  • Use a summary of the Business Architecture Context, IT Architecture Context, Business Architecture, Application Architecture and IT Architecture DCDs previously produced, to remind stakeholders of the work that has been completed so far. Confirm their understanding of what the enterprise is and aspires to become, how it intends to achieve its goals and make the desired transition, and by what means.
  • Show how the ARC 309 Principles, Policies and Guidelines have been incorporated into the Architecture Management Framework and will directly underpin how these assets are used and evolved over time.
  • Use a summary of the ARC 312 Architecture Management Framework and ORG 006 Future Organization Design outputs, to highlight the new processes, roles and responsibilities, which have been defined and how these will be implemented across the organization structure. Organizational and process changes always take a long time to assimilate effectively, and the sooner you can get senior management support for the changes which have been agreed, the sooner the new management framework can be effectively implemented. 
  • Use the Architecture Delivery Environment, which has been defined and implemented as part of this engagement, to demonstrate the contents, usability, access and benefits of the technical environment, which has been established (originally defined in ENG 359 Technical Environment Inventory).
  • In particular, stress how this deliverable will support the business goals - remind key stakeholders of the value of these activities and the benefits of providing/implementing a governance framework, which will evolve over time as business and IT requirements evolve and change.
  • Use BUS 005 Critical Issues, Opportunities and Recommendations to highlight any major issues, which have become apparent during these architecture management activities. Highlight any assumptions made which need to be validated by senior business and IT executives, or any key observations/insights which are already apparent and which senior management need to start considering, in their day-to-day business management/decision making activities.
  • Summarize the results and detailed content of this deliverable in BUS 321 Executive Briefing Package, ready for presentation to senior executives and key stakeholders.

The inputs used to provide the component parts of this Architecture Management deliverable include:

  • Outputs from phase 3 of either the Business Architecture – Quickstart engagement model, or the IT Architecture – Quickstart engagement model, specifically the Initiate Business Architecture/Initiate IT Architecture activities.
  • General knowledge and awareness of the outputs from previous phases, which could include the contents of the Business Architecture Context DCD, the Business Architecture DCD, the Application Architecture DCD, IT Architecture Context DCD and the IT Architecture DCD. Note that the activities and tasks that produce this deliverable are run in parallel with other phases in the engagement model (s.)
  • An understanding of all relevant, existing client roles, responsibilities and processes.
  • IBM IC assets - for example, default architecture management processes, roles and responsibilities. These IC assets can be found in:
  • - IBM Global Services Method Release 3, attached to the ARC 301 Architecture Management Framework WP - The IBM Enterprise Architecture (EA) Method database, which is available to EA trained practitioners – provides generic samples and templates of architecture management frameworks defined for multiple industries.
  • - The Enterprise and IT Architecture (E&ITA) KN ICM database – has multiple examples of different architecture management frameworks.
  • Other IBM IC assets - The Enterprise and IT Architecture (E&ITA) KN ICM database also has multiple examples of architecture delivery environments, in the form of Lotus Notes databases, or HTML pages for a web application. In addition there are entries relating to the use of external tools as a documentation environment. These can all be found in the ‘Tools’ section of the E&ITA KN ICM database and include:
  • - E-Design Delivery Environment – provides a default notes database, with sample deliverables for each component part. This includes samples of all the DCDs defined in the Business Architecture – Quickstart and IT Architecture – Quickstart engagement models.
  • - Architecture Repository Tool – a notes database with a focus on documenting existing application, data and technical architectures, with linkages to the appropriate business architecture components. This database has been revised and extended over many client engagements and is now considered to provide ‘best practice’ scope and detail.
  • - Experiences with the Ptech Framework – a set of HTML pages, based on using the Ptech Framework Tool to document and define enterprise-level architectures
  • - Experiences with the Popkin System Architect Tool.
  • - Experiences with the Adaptive Enterprise Architecture tool/environment.
