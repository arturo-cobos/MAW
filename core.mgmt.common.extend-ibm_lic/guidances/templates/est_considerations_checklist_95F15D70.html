Main Description

The attached Estimating Considerations Checklist Template contains a list of suggested considerations divided into the following areas:

  1. Considerations for Estimate Development

    This category includes general project considerations such as estimating team, methodology, tools, technical experts, estimating subject matter experts, estimating approaches, historical data and assumptions.

  2. Considerations for Estimating Size

    Quantification of scope, often called size, is required against which the rest of the estimate will be made. Size comes in many guises and more than one ‘unit’ might be used. Size may be a parametric (Function Points, Lines of Code), Project output artefacts (Screens, reports) or design artefacts (classes, objects) or as an overall size based on a delivery process or Work Breakdown Structure (WBS). It is important that the size be related to effort. This is best done via historical data, but expert assessment is also commonly applied.

    This category includes considerations for estimating size.

  3. Considerations for Estimating Effort

    An effort estimate should be based on the size estimate (in whatever guise).  The initial effort estimate is commonly (but not always) of technical effort (Design, Code, Unit Test, or DCUT).  All other project effort must be added to this DCUT estimate for a project estimate to be complete.

    This category includes considerations for estimating effort.

  4. Considerations for Estimating Schedule

    Schedules are created based on effort estimates by allocating resource roles against the work breakdown structure and leveling the effort to provide a realistic individual work effort per day.

    This category includes considerations for estimating schedule.

  5. Considerations for Estimating Cost

    Resourced schedules provide an ability to accurately assess resource costs. Indicative costs can be obtained from effort figures and assumptions about average resource costs. There are additional resource-based costs, for example travel expenses, and costs from procurement of hardware, software, services from 3rd party vendors and so on.

    This category includes considerations for estimating cost

  6. Final Considerations

    Having completed your estimate, you should review its content and assumptions, considering factors such as accuracy of costs versus appropriate skill levels.

  7. Miscellaneous Project Management Considerations

    In addition, considerations need to be made for project management tasks and on-going project management activity when estimating effort and duration.  These considerations do not solely relate to management roles, but may also relate to the roles of team members.
